  1. "MD's don't LIE" — Medial = Direct, Lateral = Indirect (hernias relative to inferior epigastric a.)
  2. "Hands in pockets / up & in / across" — EO / IO / TA fiber directions
  3. "Above = Split, Below = Slip" — Rectus sheath above vs. below the arcuate line
  4. "DIP on a C" — Omental foramen boundaries: Duodenum, IVC, Portal triad, Caudate
  5. "Left Sock Hides" — Celiac branches: Left gastric, Splenic, common Hepatic
  6. "SAD PUCKER" — Retroperitoneal organs
  7. "I 8 10 EGGs AT 12" — Diaphragm openings: T8 IVC, T10 esophagus + vagal, T12 aorta + thoracic duct
  8. "GFR Makes Good Sex" — Adrenal cortex zones & products (Glomerulosa→Mineralo, Fasciculata→Gluco, Reticularis→Sex)
  9. "PIL-Q" — Posterior wall muscles medial→lateral: Psoas, Iliacus, Lumbar plexus, QL
  10. "Interested In Getting Laid Friday? Obviously!" — Lumbar plexus from top: Iliohypogastric, Ilioinguinal, Genitofemoral, Lateral cut., Femoral, Obturator

Active Learning Suggestions for the Practical

1. Draw it from memory. Each lab tab includes mnemonics — after reading once, close the guide and sketch the structures (especially the celiac trunk, SMA tree, kidney hilum, and inguinal canal cross-section).

2. Trace the arteries with your finger. On a cadaver or anatomy app, follow each artery from origin to its first branch. The act of tracing burns it in.

3. Quiz yourself with practical-style "tag prompts." Cover the structure name and ask yourself: "What is this? What does it supply or drain? What's clinically important about it?"

4. Use spaced repetition on weak structures. Italicized structures in your list (less likely to be tagged but testable) are still high-yield for written exam — flag the ones you don't recognize and revisit each day.

5. Pair structures with stories. Every clinical box (e.g., Pringle maneuver, nutcracker syndrome, Kehr's sign) is a memory hook. Attach the structure to the story to remember both.

6. Test before you study, not just after. Take the Practice Questions tab BEFORE you finish reviewing each lab — discovering what you don't know is a powerful encoding event for ADHD brains.

▼ 1. Osteology & Surface Anatomy

⭐ The abdomen is bounded above by the costal margin and xiphoid process, below by the iliac crests, ASIS, pubic tubercles, and pubic symphysis. These are the landmarks you'll palpate to define quadrants and regions.

🦫 Upper Landmarks: Xiphoid & Costal Margin

  • Xiphoid process: Tip of the sternum, at the level of the T9 vertebra. Marks the upper midline of the abdomen.
  • Costal margin: The inferior edge of the rib cage — formed by the costal cartilages of ribs 7–10 (which join to form this arch) plus the tips of ribs 11 and 12 floating free.
  • The costal margin is the superior boundary of the anterior abdominal wall.

The inguinal ligament runs like a hammock from the ASIS (lateral) to the pubic tubercle (medial). If you can feel both anchors, you can trace the ligament with your finger.

⚡ Lightning High-Yield Vertebral Levels:
  • Xiphoid = T9
  • Transpyloric plane (halfway xiphoid ↔ umbilicus) = L1 (celiac trunk, SMA, pancreas neck, pylorus)
  • Umbilicus = L3–L4 disc (also aortic bifurcation)
  • Subcostal plane = L3 (bottom of rib 10)
  • Transtubercular plane = L5 (IVC formation)
  • Iliac crest = L4 (used for lumbar puncture)
⚠ TRAP: ASIS vs. iliac tubercle — they are NOT the same point ▼
  • The ASIS is the anteriormost point of the iliac crest. It anchors the inguinal ligament.
  • The iliac tubercle is on the outer lip of the iliac crest, about 5 cm posterior to the ASIS.
  • The iliac tubercle defines the transtubercular plane (L5), not the ASIS. Questions will try to conflate them.

▼ 2. 9 Regions & 4 Quadrants

⭐ Two planes + two horizontal lines make the grid. Midclavicular lines run vertically; subcostal (top) and transtubercular (bottom) planes run horizontally. This creates 9 regions. For quadrants, the median + transumbilical planes split the abdomen into 4.

⚠ TRAP: Subcostal plane vs. transpyloric plane ▼
  • Subcostal plane = bottom of rib 10, L3 — used in the 9-region scheme.
  • Transpyloric plane = halfway xiphoid to umbilicus, L1 — not in the 9-region scheme BUT crosses pylorus, pancreas neck, SMA origin, fundus of GB, hila of kidneys, duodenojejunal junction.
  • Questions love to mix these up. If a question mentions "pylorus level," think L1 transpyloric, not subcostal.

▼ 3. Superficial Fascia & Cutaneous Nerves

⭐ Below the umbilicus, superficial fascia splits into Camper's (fatty, superficial) and Scarpa's (membranous, deep). Scarpa's matters clinically because it continues into the perineum as Colles' fascia — urine from a ruptured urethra can track here.

🥫 Camper's Fascia (Fatty)

  • Composition: Loose fatty tissue. Continuous with superficial fascia of thigh.
  • Function: Energy storage, insulation, cushioning.
  • Clinical: This is what you cut through first during a C-section or laparotomy.

🧹 Scarpa's Fascia (Membranous)

  • Composition: Thin fibrous membrane, deep to Camper's.
  • Attachments: Blends with fascia lata of the thigh (just below inguinal ligament); continues inferomedially into the perineum as Colles' fascia; into penis/scrotum as Dartos/Buck's fascia.
  • Key clinical: In a ruptured urethra below the perineal membrane, extravasated urine tracks into the scrotum, penis, and up the anterior abdominal wall deep to Scarpa's but NOT into the thigh (fascia lata blocks it).

⚡ Cutaneous Nerves (T7–L1)

  • Anterior cutaneous branches: From the anterior rami of T7–T11 intercostals, subcostal (T12), and the iliohypogastric (L1). Supply midline skin.
  • Lateral cutaneous branches: From the same intercostals + subcostal; pierce the lateral wall to supply flank skin.

⚠ TRAP: Camper's vs. Scarpa's position ▼
  • Camper's is Cushiony & Closer to skin (superficial)
  • Scarpa's is Strong & Subdeep (membranous, deeper)
  • Mnemonic: "CS" = Camper's then Scarpa's, top to bottom. Don't reverse them.

▼ 4. Anterolateral Wall Muscles (3 Flat + 1 Strap)

⭐ Three flat muscles layered in an "X–Y–X" pattern (External oblique → Internal oblique → Transversus abdominis) plus the midline rectus abdominis. Their aponeuroses form the rectus sheath, meeting at the linea alba.

🦫 External Oblique (Most Superficial)

  • Fiber direction: Inferomedial (like hands in pockets / "down & in"). Same direction as external intercostals.
  • Origin: External surfaces of ribs 5–12.
  • Insertion (aponeurosis): Linea alba, pubic tubercle, anterior iliac crest. Inferior free edge = inguinal ligament (between ASIS and pubic tubercle).
  • Action: Compress abdomen; flex & rotate trunk to the opposite side.
  • Innervation: Thoracoabdominal nerves (T7–T11) + subcostal (T12).

🦫 Internal Oblique (Middle)

  • Fiber direction: Superomedial ("up & in"). Perpendicular to EO.
  • Origin: Thoracolumbar fascia, iliac crest, lateral 2/3 of inguinal ligament.
  • Insertion: Ribs 10–12, linea alba (via aponeurosis). Contributes to conjoint tendon with transversus abdominis (inserts on pubic crest — reinforces posterior inguinal canal).
  • Action: Compress abdomen; flex & rotate trunk to the same side.
  • Innervation: T7–T12, iliohypogastric, ilioinguinal (L1).
  • Special: Gives rise to cremaster muscle in males (covering of spermatic cord).

🦫 Transversus Abdominis (Deepest)

  • Fiber direction: Horizontal (like a weightlifting belt).
  • Origin: Internal surfaces of costal cartilages 7–12, thoracolumbar fascia, iliac crest, lateral 1/3 of inguinal ligament.
  • Insertion: Linea alba (via aponeurosis), pubic crest (via conjoint tendon).
  • Action: Compresses abdomen only (no trunk rotation).
  • Innervation: T7–T12, iliohypogastric, ilioinguinal.

🏋 Rectus Abdominis (The "Six-Pack")

  • Origin: Pubic crest and pubic symphysis.
  • Insertion: Xiphoid process and costal cartilages 5–7.
  • Tendinous intersections: 3 fibrous bands crossing the muscle (at xiphoid, umbilicus, halfway between) — create the "six-pack" appearance. Firmly attached to anterior rectus sheath only.
  • Action: Flex trunk (bring ribs to pelvis); tense abdominal wall.
  • Innervation: T7–T12.
🧠 Mnemonic: Flat Muscle Fiber Directions ("Hands In Pockets")
▼

Stand up. Now:

  • Put your hands in your front pockets → fingers point inferomedially = EO direction.
  • Flip hands so fingers point up & toward midline = IO direction.
  • Wrap your hands horizontally around your waist = TA direction.

Or: "Down–Up–Across" (EO → IO → TA, superficial to deep).

⚠ TRAP: Rotation direction — same side or opposite? ▼
  • External oblique rotates to the opposite side (e.g., right EO rotates trunk left).
  • Internal oblique rotates to the same side (e.g., right IO rotates trunk right).
  • Memory hack: "I = Ipsi, E = Extra/opposite." Or: EO + IO rotate together — right EO + left IO both rotate the trunk to the left.
  • Transversus abdominis does NOT rotate — it compresses. Questions often ask this.

▼ 5. Rectus Sheath, Linea Alba & Arcuate Line

⭐ The 3 flat-muscle aponeuroses form the rectus sheath that envelops rectus abdominis. But there's a twist: above the arcuate line the sheath wraps both sides of rectus; below the arcuate line, ALL aponeuroses go in front — there's NO posterior sheath below the arcuate line (only transversalis fascia). This is the single highest-yield concept of the abdominal wall.

📍 ABOVE the Arcuate Line (upper 3/4)

  • Anterior sheath = External oblique aponeurosis + anterior half of Internal oblique aponeurosis.
  • Posterior sheath = Posterior half of Internal oblique aponeurosis + Transversus abdominis aponeurosis.
  • So the IO aponeurosis splits to sandwich rectus abdominis.

📍 BELOW the Arcuate Line (lower 1/4)

  • Anterior sheath = ALL 3 aponeuroses (EO + IO + TA) pass in front of rectus.
  • Posterior sheath = NONE. Only the thin transversalis fascia lies behind rectus.
  • The arcuate line is the curved lower edge of the posterior sheath, ~1/3 of the way between umbilicus and pubis.

⤴ Linea Alba

  • Midline fibrous raphe from xiphoid to pubic symphysis where the aponeuroses of both sides interdigitate.
  • Avascular — surgical incision plane for rapid midline laparotomy.
  • Widens at the umbilicus (explains umbilical hernias in infants/adults with raised intra-abdominal pressure).
🧠 Mnemonic: "Above = Split, Below = Slip"
▼
  • Above arcuate: IO splits → half in front, half behind rectus.
  • Below arcuate: all aponeuroses slip in front.
  • Or picture a bucket: the arcuate line is the lip of the bucket. Above the lip, there's a wall on both sides. Below the lip, the back has fallen away.
⚡ Clinical: Rectus Sheath Hematoma
  • Rupture of the inferior epigastric artery (branch of external iliac) or a vein inside the rectus sheath.
  • Above arcuate line → hematoma is contained within the sheath (sharp, unilateral pain).
  • Below arcuate line → hematoma can spread through transversalis fascia into the pelvis / retropubic space.
⚠ TRAP: Where does the arcuate line sit? ▼
  • The arcuate line is between the umbilicus and the pubis, approximately 1/3 of the way above the pubic symphysis (i.e., 2/3 of the way down from umbilicus to pubis, or equivalently, about halfway).
  • It is NOT at the umbilicus — a common distractor.
  • The posterior sheath terminates here in a curved, concave-down edge.

▼ 6. Wall Vessels, Transversalis Fascia & Parietal Peritoneum

⭐ The rectus abdominis is fed from above and below by two epigastric arteries that anastomose inside the rectus sheath. These anastomoses form collateral circulation when the aorta or subclavian is occluded.

🔴 Superior Epigastric Artery

  • Origin: Terminal branch of internal thoracic artery (from subclavian).
  • Course: Enters the rectus sheath at the costal margin and runs inferiorly behind rectus.
  • Anastomoses with inferior epigastric near umbilicus.

🔴 Inferior Epigastric Artery

  • Origin: External iliac artery, just above inguinal ligament.
  • Course: Runs superomedially, enters rectus sheath below the arcuate line (piercing transversalis fascia), ascends behind rectus.
  • Forms lateral umbilical fold of peritoneum (important landmark for direct vs. indirect hernias).
  • Clinical: Risk of injury during paracentesis, laparoscopic port placement, or rectus hematoma.

🫂 Transversalis Fascia

  • A continuous endoabdominal fascia lining the entire abdominopelvic cavity, deep to all muscles and fascias.
  • Deep to transversus abdominis, superficial to extraperitoneal fat + parietal peritoneum.
  • Evaginates through the deep inguinal ring to form the internal spermatic fascia of the cord.

💥 Parietal Peritoneum

  • Innermost lining of the abdominal wall; innervated by somatic nerves (sharp, well-localized pain).
  • Contrast with visceral peritoneum — autonomic innervation (dull, poorly localized).
  • Clinical: When inflammation reaches parietal peritoneum (e.g., rupture of appendix), pain shifts to localized location (RLQ in appendicitis).
⚡ Lightning High-Yield: The superior + inferior epigastric anastomosis is a major collateral pathway. In coarctation of the aorta (distal to left subclavian), blood detours: subclavian → internal thoracic → superior epigastric → inferior epigastric → external iliac → descending aorta. This drives rib notching on chest X-ray from dilated intercostal collaterals.

▼ 7. Inguinal Region: Ligament, Rings & Canal

⭐ The inguinal canal is a 4-cm tube in the lower abdominal wall that transmits the spermatic cord (M) or round ligament of uterus (F). It has 2 rings (deep & superficial), 2 walls, a roof, and a floor — the walls are made of different muscle/fascia layers.

🔗 Inguinal Ligament

  • The inferior free edge of the external oblique aponeurosis, curled under.
  • Runs from ASIS (lateral) to pubic tubercle (medial).
  • Forms the floor of the inguinal canal.
  • Clinical: Separates abdomen from thigh; a femoral hernia goes below it, inguinal hernia goes above.

🔖 Deep (Internal) Inguinal Ring

  • Entry to the inguinal canal from the abdominal side.
  • An outpouching of the transversalis fascia.
  • Located lateral to the inferior epigastric artery, midway between ASIS and pubic symphysis.
  • The spermatic cord/round ligament pass through it.

🔔 Superficial (External) Inguinal Ring

  • Exit from the inguinal canal to the scrotum/labia.
  • A triangular defect in the external oblique aponeurosis, just above and lateral to the pubic tubercle.
  • Bounded by medial + lateral crura of EO aponeurosis.

🏠 Walls of the Canal (memorize these — exam gold)

BoundaryWhat It's Made Of

⚡ Ilioinguinal & Iliohypogastric Nerves (both L1)

  • Iliohypogastric (L1): Pierces IO above the deep ring, runs between IO and EO, becomes cutaneous just above the superficial ring → supplies skin over pubic region (suprapubic).
  • Ilioinguinal (L1): Pierces IO near the deep ring, travels through the inguinal canal (but NOT through deep ring — it enters canal directly), exits through superficial ring → supplies skin of anterior scrotum/mons pubis + upper medial thigh.
  • Clinical: Both are at risk during hernia repair and appendectomy incisions → numbness or neuroma in the groin.
🧠 Mnemonic: "MALT — 2M, 2A, 2L, 2T" (Inguinal canal walls)
▼

From superficial → deep, lateral → medial:

  • Muscle (2 + 1): EO, IO, TA contribute
  • Aponeurosis: EO = anterior wall
  • Ligament: Inguinal = floor
  • Transversalis fascia: posterior wall

Or simpler: "The canal is a muscular tunnel whose FLOOR is a ligament, ROOF is arching muscle, ANTERIOR is aponeurosis, POSTERIOR is fascia."

⚠ TRAP: Which nerve traverses the canal? ▼
  • Ilioinguinal nerve runs INSIDE the inguinal canal (but enters through the side, not through the deep ring). It exits through the superficial ring with the cord.
  • Iliohypogastric nerve runs ABOVE the canal, between IO and EO; becomes cutaneous above the superficial ring.
  • Both carry L1 fibers (T12/L1 trunk). Both supply skin — the ilioinguinal reaches more inferior/medial (anterior scrotum, labia, upper medial thigh).

▼ 8. Spermatic Cord, Testis & Round Ligament

⭐ The spermatic cord is a bundle of structures traveling from the deep ring → through the canal → out the superficial ring → to the testis. It has 3 arteries, 3 nerves, 3 "other" structures, wrapped in 3 fascial layers. That's the "rule of 3s."

🥩 Spermatic Cord Contents — Rule of 3s

GroupStructures
3 ArteriesTesticular artery (from aorta ~L2), cremasteric artery (from inf. epigastric), artery to ductus deferens (from sup./inf. vesical)
3 VeinsPampiniform plexus → drains to testicular vein (R → IVC; L → L renal vein), cremasteric vein, vein of ductus deferens
3 NervesGenital branch of genitofemoral (innervates cremaster — efferent of cremaster reflex), sympathetics (from T10/T11), ilioinguinal (only on top of the cord, not inside it technically)
3 OtherDuctus deferens, lymphatics (drain to paraaortic nodes at L2!), remains of processus vaginalis

Note: Transversus abdominis does NOT contribute a layer — its arching fibers form the roof of the canal and simply arch over the cord.

🥪 Testis & Its Coverings

  • Tunica vaginalis: The derivative of the processus vaginalis (peritoneum that descended with testis). Has a parietal layer (outer, lining scrotum cavity) and a visceral layer (inner, hugs testis). Fluid between = hydrocele.
  • Tunica albuginea: Tough fibrous capsule of the testis proper. Sends septa inward dividing the testis into lobules.
  • Lobules: Each contains 1–3 seminiferous tubules where sperm are made.
  • Seminiferous tubules → rete testis → efferent ductules → head of epididymis.

🧠 Epididymis (Sperm Maturation & Storage)

  • Head: Superior pole of testis; receives efferent ductules.
  • Body: Runs along the posterior testis.
  • Tail: Inferior pole; continues as the ductus deferens.

💍 Round Ligament of the Uterus (Female Analog)

  • Derived from the gubernaculum (same embryologic origin as the male gubernaculum that pulls testis down).
  • Runs from the uterus → deep inguinal ring → through inguinal canal → superficial ring → labium majus.
  • Much thinner than the spermatic cord; harder to find on dissection.
  • Clinical: Can be a site of endometriosis; ilioinguinal neuralgia in females after pelvic surgery.
⚡ Lightning High-Yield — Venous Drainage:
  • Right testicular vein → directly into IVC.
  • Left testicular vein → into left renal vein → IVC.
  • Varicoceles are more common on the LEFT because: (1) left vein is longer, (2) drains at 90° into renal vein (higher resistance), (3) risk of compression by "nutcracker" (SMA & aorta squeezing left renal vein).
  • A NEW left varicocele in an older man = renal cell carcinoma until proven otherwise (tumor thrombus obstructs left renal vein).
🧠 Mnemonic: "3 Arteries, 3 Nerves, 3 Other in the Cord"
▼

"Have 3 Veins, 3 Arteries, 3 Nerves, 3 Ducts/Lymph/Remnants"

  • And 3 fascial coats = External spermatic (EO), Cremasteric (IO), Internal spermatic (TF).
  • Think "4 × 3 = 12 things in the cord."

▼ 9. Hernias — Clinical Application

⭐ The inferior epigastric artery is the landmark dividing indirect (lateral to it) from direct (medial to it) inguinal hernias. Femoral hernias are a separate beast — they pass below the inguinal ligament through the femoral ring.

🟢 Indirect Inguinal Hernia (MORE COMMON)

  • Mechanism: Persistent processus vaginalis — congenital.
  • Path: Through deep ring → canal → superficial ring. Often enters the scrotum.
  • Relationship to inferior epigastric artery: LATERAL to it.
  • Covered by all 3 fascial layers of the cord (because it goes through the canal with the cord).
  • Who: Young males, infants. Most common hernia overall.

🟠 Direct Inguinal Hernia

  • Mechanism: Acquired weakness of the posterior wall of the canal (transversalis fascia in the Hesselbach's triangle).
  • Path: Bulges directly through the posterior wall, then may exit through superficial ring. Rarely enters scrotum.
  • Relationship to inferior epigastric artery: MEDIAL to it.
  • Covered by external spermatic fascia only (doesn't traverse deep ring).
  • Who: Older men (weak abdominal wall).

💠 Hesselbach's Triangle (Direct Hernia Site)

Borders (medial → lateral → inferior):

  • Medial: Lateral border of rectus abdominis
  • Lateral (superior): Inferior epigastric artery
  • Inferior: Inguinal ligament

Direct hernias push through this triangle.

🔴 Femoral Hernia

  • Path: Through the femoral ring, into the femoral canal, below inguinal ligament, medial to femoral vein.
  • Who: Female > male (wider pelvis).
  • Danger: Highest risk of strangulation because of the tight femoral ring.
🧠 Mnemonic: "MD's don't LIe"
▼

In hernias, the relationship to the inferior epigastric artery:

  • Medial = Direct (MD)
  • Lateral = Indirect (LI)

"MDs don't LIE."

⚠ TRAP: Direct goes through deep ring? NO! ▼
  • Indirect = through deep ring (follows cord path).
  • Direct = through Hesselbach's triangle (posterior wall of canal, NOT deep ring).
  • Both can exit through superficial ring — but only indirect goes through ALL parts of the canal.

▼ 1. Peritoneum: The Double-Layer Serous Sac

⭐ The peritoneum is a serous membrane with two layers: parietal (lines abdominal wall) and visceral (covers organs). Between them is the peritoneal cavity, a potential space with a thin film of fluid. The cavity has two parts connected by the omental foramen.

🎂 Parietal vs. Visceral Peritoneum

FeatureParietalVisceral

InnervationSomatic (T7–L1) — sharp, well-localized painAutonomic (sympathetic/parasympathetic) — dull, poorly localized pain, referred

💌 Greater & Lesser Peritoneal Sacs

  • Greater sac: The main peritoneal cavity. Everything you see when you open the abdomen first.
  • Lesser sac (omental bursa): A smaller pocket behind the stomach and lesser omentum, in front of the pancreas. Acts as a "sliding space" so the stomach can move against the posterior wall.
  • Communicate via the omental (epiploic) foramen.

🔒 Omental Foramen (of Winslow) — Gateway to the Lesser Sac

Located posterior to the free edge of the lesser omentum (hepatoduodenal ligament). Bounded by:

  • Anterior: Hepatoduodenal ligament (contains the portal triad).
  • Posterior: IVC (covered by peritoneum).
  • Superior: Caudate lobe of liver.
  • Inferior: Superior (1st) part of duodenum.

Clinical: In bleeding from the cystic or hepatic arteries during cholecystectomy, the surgeon can use the Pringle maneuver — compress the hepatoduodenal ligament between finger and thumb (finger in the foramen) to occlude portal inflow.

🧠 Mnemonic: Boundaries of the Omental Foramen — "DIP on a C"
▼

Imagine doing a "DIP on the Caudate":

  • Duodenum (1st part) — inferior
  • IVC — posterior
  • Portal triad (in hepatoduodenal lig.) — anterior
  • Caudate lobe — superior
⚡ Pringle Maneuver High-Yield:
  • Pinching the hepatoduodenal ligament stops inflow from the hepatic artery proper and portal vein.
  • Persistent bleeding despite Pringle = blood from hepatic veins (drain directly into IVC, not part of triad).
  • Can also suggest an accessory/aberrant hepatic artery not in the ligament.

▼ 2. Mesentery & Mesocolons

⭐ A mesentery is a double layer of peritoneum that suspends an organ from the body wall and carries its vessels/nerves/lymphatics. "Mesentery" (no qualifier) = the mesentery proper of small intestine. Mesocolons suspend parts of the colon.

🌕 Mesentery Proper (Small Intestine)

  • Fan-shaped double layer suspending the jejunum + ileum from the posterior abdominal wall.
  • Root runs obliquely from duodenojejunal flexure (L2, upper left) to ileocecal junction (R iliac fossa) — about 15 cm in length, but the free edge is 6 m long.
  • Contains SMA branches (intestinal arteries + arcades + vasa recta), lymphatics (lacteals), autonomic nerves.

🍃 Transverse Mesocolon

  • Suspends the transverse colon from the pancreas (anterior surface).
  • Contains the middle colic artery (SMA branch).
  • Divides the abdominal cavity into supramesocolic (above — stomach, liver, spleen) and inframesocolic (below — SB loops) compartments.

🍌 Sigmoid Mesocolon

  • Suspends the sigmoid colon from the pelvic wall.
  • Inverted-V shape; apex overlies left ureter & common iliac vessels.
  • Contains the sigmoid + superior rectal arteries (IMA branches).

🥮 Mesoappendix

  • Small mesentery that suspends the appendix from the mesentery of the terminal ileum.
  • Contains the appendicular artery (end artery from ileocolic → appendicular). Appendicitis risks ischemia → gangrene because there is no collateral supply.

📊 Intraperitoneal vs. Retroperitoneal Organs

Intraperitoneal organs are suspended by a mesentery and covered on (almost) all sides by visceral peritoneum:

  • Stomach, 1st part of duodenum, jejunum, ileum, cecum + appendix, transverse colon, sigmoid colon, liver, spleen, tail of pancreas.

Secondarily retroperitoneal (originally intra, then pressed against wall):

  • Duodenum (parts 2–4), pancreas (except tail), ascending colon, descending colon.

Primary retroperitoneal (always behind peritoneum):

  • Kidneys, adrenal glands, ureters, bladder, rectum (lower 1/3), aorta, IVC.

  • Aorta / IVC

  • Pancreas (except tail)

  • Colon (ascending & descending)
  • Kidneys
  • Esophagus (lower 2/3)

▼ 3. Greater & Lesser Omentum

⭐ An omentum is a double fold of peritoneum extending from the stomach. The greater omentum hangs down from the greater curvature like an apron; the lesser omentum runs from the lesser curvature to the liver.

🥥 Greater Omentum — "Policeman of the Abdomen"

  • Four layers of peritoneum: descends from greater curvature, loops down, folds back up, attaches to transverse colon.
  • Contains fat, lymph nodes, and gastroepiploic (gastroomental) arteries.
  • "Policeman": Can migrate to sites of inflammation (e.g., wrap around a perforated appendix) to wall off infection.

🥥 Lesser Omentum — Two Parts

Connects lesser curvature of stomach and 1st part of duodenum to liver. Split into two ligaments:

  • Hepatogastric ligament: Liver → lesser curvature of stomach. Contains L & R gastric arteries.
  • Hepatoduodenal ligament: Liver → 1st part of duodenum. Forms the free right edge of the lesser omentum. Contains the portal triad (bile duct, hepatic artery proper, portal vein).

The free edge (hepatoduodenal lig) bounds the anterior aspect of the omental foramen.

⚡ High-Yield Layers in the Portal Triad (in hepatoduodenal ligament):
  • Anterior left: Hepatic artery proper (A)
  • Anterior right: Bile duct (D)
  • Posterior: Portal vein (V)
  • Mnemonic: "A-D-V" or "Mickey Mouse" face — bile duct and artery are the ears, portal vein is the head.

▼ 6. Liver, Gallbladder & Spleen

⭐ The liver has 4 anatomic lobes but 8 Couinaud segments (based on portal vein branching). The porta hepatis is the "gate" where the portal triad enters and the bile/hepatic ducts leave. The spleen is the largest lymphoid organ — an ODD organ that comes from dorsal mesogastrium but behaves like an intraperitoneal foregut organ.

🥬 Four Anatomic Lobes of the Liver

LobePositionKey Fact
Right lobeLargest, right sideMost hepatocyte mass
Left lobeLeft of falciform ligamentSmaller than right (anatomically); functionally it's ≈ 50% of liver
Caudate lobePosterior, between IVC (right) and ligamentum venosum (left), above porta hepatisDrains directly into IVC — spared in Budd-Chiari!
Quadrate lobeInferior, between gallbladder (right) and ligamentum teres (left), below porta hepatisAnatomically with right lobe, functionally with left

❓ Bare Area of the Liver

  • Posterosuperior region of the right lobe not covered by peritoneum; directly attached to the diaphragm.
  • Bordered by the coronary ligament (peritoneal reflection).
  • Clinical: Direct communication between liver lymphatics and thoracic lymphatics — explains pleural effusions in liver abscess (right subphrenic).

🔓 Porta Hepatis — The Gate

  • Transverse fissure on the inferior surface of the liver between the caudate (above) and quadrate (below) lobes.
  • Structures: Portal vein (posterior), hepatic artery proper (anterior-left), common hepatic duct (anterior-right), plus lymphatics and autonomic nerves.

🥤 Gallbladder

  • Pear-shaped sac on the inferior surface of the liver (between right + quadrate lobes).
  • Parts: Fundus → body → neck → cystic duct.
  • Fundus contacts the anterior abdominal wall at the tip of the 9th costal cartilage (right MCL) — where Murphy's sign is elicited in cholecystitis.
  • Blood supply: Cystic artery (from right hepatic artery — in the triangle of Calot).

⚠ Triangle of Calot (Cystohepatic Triangle)

  • Borders: Cystic duct (inferior), common hepatic duct (medial), inferior surface of liver (superior).
  • Contents: Cystic artery — critical landmark for safe cholecystectomy to avoid bile duct injury.

🫁 Spleen

  • Location: LUQ, against ribs 9–11 on the left.
  • Mesogastrium origin: Develops in the dorsal mesogastrium. Although it's mesoderm (not gut epithelium), its blood supply is foregut (via splenic artery off the celiac trunk).
  • Rule of 1-3-5-7-9-11: Weight 7 oz (200 g), length 11 cm, width 7 cm, thickness 3 cm, ribs 9–11. (Old mnemonic — roughly correct.)
  • Ligaments: Gastrosplenic (anterior) + splenorenal (posterior); these bound the lesser sac.
🧠 Mnemonic: Caudate & Quadrate — Which is Which?
▼
  • Caudate = Caval side (borders IVC). Sits posterior, above the porta hepatis.
  • Quadrate = Qloser to gallbladder (between GB and round ligament). Sits inferior, below the porta hepatis.
  • On the inferior surface of the liver: porta hepatis runs horizontally, the caudate is above it (like a tail curling up), the quadrate is below it (like a square block sitting on the porta).

▼ 7. Celiac Trunk — Foregut Blood Supply

⭐ The celiac trunk exits the aorta at T12 and supplies all foregut derivatives: distal esophagus, stomach, proximal duodenum, liver, gallbladder, pancreas (proximal), and spleen. It has 3 main branches: Left gastric, Splenic, Common hepatic.

🔴 Left Gastric Artery

  • Smallest branch of celiac trunk.
  • Runs to the lesser curvature; anastomoses with right gastric artery.
  • Gives off esophageal branches to distal esophagus — these form a porto-systemic anastomosis with the azygos system (site of esophageal varices in portal HTN).

🫁 Splenic Artery

  • Largest branch of celiac trunk; tortuous (runs "like a snake" along upper pancreas).
  • Branches:
    • Short gastric arteries (via gastrosplenic ligament) — to gastric fundus.
    • Left gastroepiploic (gastroomental) artery — runs along greater curvature.
    • Pancreatic branches (dorsal, magna, caudal) to body + tail of pancreas.
  • Clinical: Splenic artery aneurysm = 3rd most common intraabdominal aneurysm; rupture common in pregnancy.

🥬 Common Hepatic Artery

Splits into gastroduodenal + hepatic artery proper.

▼Gastroduodenal artery (GDA)
  • Descends behind the 1st part of duodenum.
  • Branches:
    • Right gastroepiploic (gastroomental) artery → greater curvature (anastomoses with left gastroepiploic).
    • Superior pancreaticoduodenal arteries (anterior + posterior) → head of pancreas + C-loop of duodenum. Anastomose with inferior pancreaticoduodenal from SMA — this is the main celiac ↔ SMA collateral.
  • Clinical: Posterior duodenal ulcer can erode GDA → brisk upper GI bleed.
▼Hepatic artery proper
  • Runs in the hepatoduodenal ligament (portal triad) toward the porta hepatis.
  • Branches:
    • Right gastric artery → along lesser curvature, anastomoses with left gastric.
    • Left hepatic artery → left + quadrate lobes.
    • Right hepatic artery → right + caudate lobes.
    • Cystic artery (typically from R hepatic) → gallbladder (in triangle of Calot).
🧠 Mnemonic: Celiac Trunk 3 Branches — "Left Sock Hides"
▼

Left Sock Hides = Left gastric, Splenic, Common Hepatic.

Or: "LSH → three branches of the celiac trunk."

Further: The 3 branches feed their respective regions — Left gastric feeds stomach's lesser curve + distal esophagus; Splenic feeds spleen + pancreas + greater curve via gastroepiploic/short gastrics; Common hepatic feeds liver + duodenum + parts of stomach.

⚡ Foregut Portosystemic Anastomoses (from celiac):
  • Esophageal varices: Left gastric vein (portal) ↔ esophageal veins → azygos (systemic).
  • Caput medusae: Paraumbilical veins (portal) ↔ small epigastric veins of wall (systemic).
  • Hemorrhoids (internal): Superior rectal vein (portal) ↔ middle + inferior rectal veins (systemic).
⚠ TRAP: Cystic artery — is it always from R hepatic? ▼
  • In ~75% of people, the cystic artery arises from the right hepatic artery within the triangle of Calot.
  • Up to 25% have anatomic variants (from left hepatic, gastroduodenal, or common hepatic). Surgical injury during cholecystectomy is common if the surgeon doesn't clearly identify Calot's triangle.

▼ 8. Porta Hepatis & Biliary Tract

⭐ Follow the bile backwards from liver → duodenum: Right + Left hepatic ducts → Common hepatic duct → (joins) Cystic duct from GB → Bile duct (common bile duct) → ampulla of Vater (with pancreatic duct) → 2nd part of duodenum.

💍 Portal Triad (Hepatoduodenal Ligament)

  • Bile duct — anterior right (drains bile out of liver).
  • Hepatic artery proper — anterior left (brings oxygenated blood, ~25% of liver flow).
  • Hepatic portal vein — posterior (brings nutrient-rich, low-O2 blood from gut, ~75% of liver flow).

⚡ Hepatic Portal Vein Formation:
  • Superior mesenteric vein (SMV) + splenic vein unite behind the neck of the pancreas (at ~L1, transpyloric plane) to form the portal vein.
  • The inferior mesenteric vein typically drains into the splenic vein before this union.
  • Mnemonic: "SMV + Splenic = Portal; IMV joins Splenic."
⚠ TRAP: Ampulla of Vater — 2nd duodenum, NOT 1st ▼
  • The ampulla of Vater (hepatopancreatic ampulla) opens into the second (descending) part of the duodenum, on the posteromedial wall, via the major duodenal papilla.
  • A gallstone lodged at the ampulla blocks both bile AND pancreatic juice → obstructive jaundice + gallstone pancreatitis.
  • The accessory (Santorini) duct opens at the minor duodenal papilla ~2 cm superior to the ampulla of Vater.

▼ 1. Foregut, Midgut, Hindgut — The Big Picture

⭐ The gut tube is divided into 3 embryologic regions based on blood supply. Each region has its own artery, vein, lymphatic drainage, and autonomic innervation — and knowing the "border" between them explains referred pain patterns, tumor spread, and vascular compromise.

MidgutSMAL1Distal 2nd duodenum → 2/3 of transverse colonPeriumbilical
HindgutIMAL3Distal 1/3 transverse colon → upper rectumHypogastric/suprapubic
🧠 Mnemonic: Gut Boundary Points
▼
  • Foregut ↔ Midgut transition: At the ampulla of Vater in the 2nd part of duodenum. The duodenum above the ampulla = foregut; below = midgut.
  • Midgut ↔ Hindgut transition: At the junction of proximal 2/3 and distal 1/3 of transverse colon (near splenic flexure).
  • Autonomic nerve levels: Foregut = T5–T9 (greater splanchnic); Midgut = T10–T11 (lesser splanchnic); Hindgut = T12 (least splanchnic) + L1–L2 (lumbar splanchnic).

▼ 2. Superior Mesenteric Artery (SMA) — Midgut Supply

⭐ The SMA arises from the aorta at L1 (just below celiac), passes behind the pancreas, over the uncinate process, and emerges between the pancreas (above) and 3rd part of duodenum (below). It supplies the entire midgut via 5 branches (radiating right + straight ahead).

🥮 Appendicular Artery

  • Branch of ileocolic; runs in the mesoappendix.
  • End artery with NO collateral supply → appendicitis rapidly progresses to ischemia, gangrene, perforation if untreated.
⚡ SMA Syndrome:
  • The 3rd part of duodenum is squeezed between SMA (anterior) and aorta (posterior).
  • Rapid weight loss or spinal surgery reduces the fat pad → compressed duodenum → postprandial vomiting, early satiety.
⚠ TRAP: SMA embolism presents like a silent catastrophe ▼
  • Classic triad: "pain out of proportion to exam" (visceral ischemia → severe pain but soft belly initially), elderly patient with atrial fibrillation, and metabolic acidosis (lactate).
  • Collateral flow is limited; infarction of entire midgut possible within hours.
  • Contrast with chronic mesenteric ischemia = postprandial pain + fear of food + weight loss ("intestinal angina") from atherosclerosis of all 3 gut arteries (celiac, SMA, IMA).

▼ 3. Inferior Mesenteric Artery (IMA) — Hindgut Supply

⭐ The IMA arises from the aorta at L3 (just above the aortic bifurcation at L4). It has 3 branches that supply from the distal 1/3 of the transverse colon down to the upper rectum.

🍋 IMA Branches

BranchSupplies
Left colic arteryDistal 1/3 transverse colon + descending colon; anastomoses with middle colic (SMA) via marginal artery of Drummond
Sigmoid arteries (2–4 branches)Sigmoid colon; form arcades
Superior rectal arteryTerminal branch of IMA; upper rectum. Anastomoses with middle + inferior rectal arteries (internal iliac) — porto-systemic anastomosis (hemorrhoids)

🔴 Rectal Arterial Supply (3 arteries)

  • Superior rectal: From IMA (hindgut). Supplies upper rectum.
  • Middle rectal: From internal iliac. Supplies middle rectum (and pelvic side).
  • Inferior rectal: From internal pudendal (of internal iliac). Supplies lower rectum + anal canal below dentate line.

This 3-way supply is why rectal tumors spread in multiple directions lymphatically and why the dentate line divides systems (visceral vs. somatic).

▼ 4. Marginal Artery of Drummond & Watershed Areas

⭐ The marginal artery of Drummond is a continuous arterial arcade running along the mesenteric border of the entire colon — it's the life-line that connects SMA and IMA territories. Where it's weak, the colon is vulnerable to ischemia.

🔗 Marginal Artery (of Drummond)

  • Runs along the colon's mesenteric border.
  • Connects the ileocolic, right colic, middle colic (SMA branches) with left colic, sigmoid (IMA branches).
  • The larger arc of Riolan is a more central anastomosis (middle colic ↔ left colic) — present in some people only; also called the "meandering mesenteric artery."

⚜ Watershed Areas (Ischemia-Prone)

  • Splenic flexure ("Griffith's point"): Junction of SMA territory (middle colic end) and IMA territory (left colic end). Weak collateral → ischemia in hypoperfusion.
  • Rectosigmoid junction ("Sudeck's point"): Junction of IMA territory (last sigmoid artery) and internal iliac territory (rectal arteries).

Clinical: Ischemic colitis classically affects watershed zones in hypotensive elderly patients; they present with cramping abdominal pain and bloody diarrhea.

🧠 Mnemonic: Watershed Points
▼
  • Griffith = Great bend of colon = splenic flexure (SMA↔IMA).
  • Sudeck = Sigmoid area = rectosigmoid (IMA↔iliac).
  • Both are "G.S. — Gray Spots on the colon's blood supply map."

▼ 5. Portal Venous Drainage — SMV, IMV, Splenic V.

⭐ All gut venous blood goes to the hepatic portal vein, NOT directly to IVC. This is the "first pass" through the liver. Three veins feed the portal vein: SMV + splenic vein unite behind the pancreatic neck; the IMV usually joins the splenic vein before the union.

🫂 Superior Mesenteric Vein

  • Drains everything SMA supplies (midgut: duodenum 2nd/3rd/4th, jejunum, ileum, cecum, appendix, ascending colon, 2/3 transverse colon).
  • Runs on the right side of the SMA in the root of mesentery.
  • Unites with splenic vein behind the neck of the pancreas → hepatic portal vein.

🫁 Splenic Vein

  • Drains spleen, pancreas (most), parts of stomach (via short gastrics + L gastroepiploic), plus IMV.
  • Runs behind the body/tail of pancreas; receives IMV from below.

🍌 Inferior Mesenteric Vein

  • Drains hindgut (distal 1/3 transverse colon, descending colon, sigmoid, upper rectum).
  • Ascends to the left of the IMA, hooks under pancreas body, joins splenic vein.
  • Note: There is no "inferior mesenteric artery's paired vein going to portal directly" — the IMV joins splenic first.
🧠 Mnemonic: "SMV + SV = PV; IMV hitches a ride"
▼

Picture 3 friends meeting at the neck of the pancreas:

  • SMV comes up from below (midgut).
  • Splenic vein comes from the left (spleen, stomach).
  • They marry at the back of the pancreatic neck → Portal vein.
  • The IMV (hindgut) is the "+1" who hitches a ride with the splenic vein before the wedding.

▼ 6. Duodenum — 4 Parts, C-Shape, Vital Relations

⭐ The duodenum is C-shaped, ~25 cm long, and cradles the head of pancreas. The first 2 cm is intraperitoneal; the rest is secondarily retroperitoneal. Each of its 4 parts has a unique relation/function — and the junction between parts 2 and the foregut/midgut is where the ampulla of Vater opens.

2nd (Descending)L1–L3; right of vertebraeAmpulla of Vater enters here (major duodenal papilla); minor papilla ~2 cm aboveForegut→midgut transition at ampulla; ERCP target

🎓 Ligament of Treitz (Suspensory Muscle of Duodenum)

  • A fibromuscular band from the right crus of diaphragm to the duodenojejunal flexure.
  • Marks the transition from duodenum to jejunum, and clinically divides "upper" from "lower" GI bleeds.
  • Melena = bleeding proximal to Treitz; hematochezia typically distal.
⚡ Blood Supply of Duodenum (Dual Supply):
  • Above ampulla of Vater (foregut): Superior pancreaticoduodenal (from GDA/celiac).
  • Below ampulla of Vater (midgut): Inferior pancreaticoduodenal (from SMA).
  • The two anastomose around the head of pancreas — critical celiac↔SMA collateral.
⚠ TRAP: Which part is intraperitoneal? ▼
  • Only the first 2 cm of the 1st part (duodenal cap) is intraperitoneal (connected to hepatoduodenal ligament).
  • The rest of the duodenum (most of 1st part, all of 2nd, 3rd, 4th) is secondarily retroperitoneal.
  • This is why duodenal perforations of the posterior wall often leak into retroperitoneum (not free peritoneum) and can be subtle on imaging.

▼ 7. Pancreas — 5 Parts, 2 Ducts

⭐ The pancreas lies retroperitoneally at L1–L2, stretching horizontally from the duodenal C-loop (right) to the splenic hilum (left). The neck overlies SMA + SMV; the uncinate process hooks BEHIND them.

🍍 Five Parts (right → left)

PartLocationKey Relation
HeadNestled in C-loop of duodenum; right of SMVCommon bile duct passes through it → pancreatic cancer of head → obstructive jaundice
Uncinate processHook-shaped extension of head behind SMA + SMVCan compress SMA (SMA syndrome) or trap vessels in pancreatic cancer
NeckNarrow, overlies SMA/SMV anteriorly; SMV+splenic v. unite BEHIND itCommon site of pancreatic transection in blunt trauma (compressed against L1)
BodyCrosses midline over L1/L2 vertebraeSplenic artery runs tortuously along its top
TailExtends to splenic hilum in splenorenal ligamentONLY intraperitoneal part; at risk during splenectomy

🌵 Pancreatic Ducts

  • Main pancreatic duct (of Wirsung): Runs through length of pancreas. Joins bile duct at the hepatopancreatic ampulla (Vater) → opens at major duodenal papilla in 2nd duodenum. Controlled by sphincter of Oddi.
  • Accessory duct (of Santorini): Drains upper head + uncinate. Opens at minor duodenal papilla (~2 cm above major).
⚡ Embryology Connection:
  • Pancreas develops from 2 buds — dorsal (larger, most of pancreas + accessory duct) and ventral (uncinate + head + main duct).
  • Ventral bud rotates posteriorly and fuses.
  • Annular pancreas: failed rotation → ring of pancreas encircles 2nd duodenum → duodenal obstruction.
  • Pancreas divisum: fusion failure → main duct drains only head/uncinate via accessory papilla → recurrent pancreatitis.

▼ 8. Colon-Specific Features

⭐ Three features distinguish colon from small intestine on the practical: teniae coli, haustra, and omental (epiploic) appendages. Learn them because exam and practical will ask "How do you tell colon from jejunum at first glance?"

🍡 Teniae Coli

  • Three longitudinal bands of outer muscle running the length of the colon (cecum → rectum).
  • Represent the condensed outer longitudinal muscle layer.
  • Shorter than the colon → bunch the wall into haustra.
  • All 3 teniae converge at the base of the appendix — surgical landmark.
  • Teniae disappear at the rectum (outer longitudinal muscle becomes uniform).

🍜 Haustra

  • Sacculations of the colonic wall formed because the teniae are shorter than the colon.
  • On abdominal X-ray, haustra DON'T cross the full width of the lumen (contrast with small bowel valvulae conniventes, which do).
  • Clinical: Loss of haustra on imaging = "lead pipe colon" in ulcerative colitis.

🪴 Omental (Epiploic) Appendages

  • Small fat-filled peritoneal tags hanging off the colon (NOT on small intestine or rectum).
  • Clinical: Epiploic appendagitis — torsion/inflammation mimics appendicitis or diverticulitis.

Testing Your Understanding — Lab 3, Question 1

A 78-year-old man with atrial fibrillation presents with severe periumbilical pain, vomiting, and bloody diarrhea. CT angiogram shows an occlusion of the SMA just beyond its first branch. Predict which structures will become ischemic and which will be spared. Why is the pain periumbilical?

Consider: SMA branch order, embryologic gut regions, visceral referred pain levels

✓ Answer: Midgut structures distal to the inferior pancreaticoduodenal artery are at risk — most of jejunum, all of ileum, cecum + appendix, ascending colon, and proximal 2/3 of transverse colon. Foregut (celiac) and hindgut (IMA) are spared. Pain is periumbilical because midgut visceral afferents refer to T10 dermatome (umbilicus).

The first SMA branch is the inferior pancreaticoduodenal, which anastomoses with the superior pancreaticoduodenal from the celiac trunk — so the duodenum + head of pancreas are often spared in occlusions beyond this branch. The middle colic, right colic, ileocolic, and intestinal arteries, however, are downstream and supply an area with only limited collateral (via the marginal artery of Drummond at the splenic flexure — which may or may not be sufficient). Embryologic origin dictates referred pain: midgut = T10 (periumbilical), foregut = T5–T9 (epigastric), hindgut = T12–L2 (suprapubic).

Consider: If instead the patient had chronic progressive atherosclerosis of all 3 gut arteries (celiac, SMA, IMA), what syndrome would develop? (Chronic mesenteric ischemia → postprandial pain, fear of food, weight loss.)

Testing Your Understanding — Lab 3, Question 2

A pancreatic head cancer encases the portal vein. During surgical staging, the surgeon notes the pancreatic uncinate process wraps around a major artery, compromising resection. Which artery does the uncinate process encircle, and what is the positional relationship between the uncinate, SMA, SMV, and pancreatic neck?

Consider: pancreas embryology, SMA origin, venous formation behind pancreas

✓ Answer: The uncinate process hooks BEHIND the SMA + SMV. The pancreatic NECK lies ANTERIOR to these vessels. So from posterior → anterior: uncinate → SMA/SMV → pancreatic neck.

The uncinate is a posterior extension of the head of the pancreas derived from the ventral pancreatic bud, which rotates during development. As SMA emerges from the aorta and SMV crosses the 3rd part of duodenum, they pass ANTERIOR to the uncinate but POSTERIOR to the neck. Pancreatic head cancer (the most common pancreatic cancer) can: (1) obstruct the bile duct passing through the head → obstructive jaundice, (2) encase SMA/SMV → unresectable, and (3) encase the portal vein (formed behind the pancreatic neck from SMV + splenic v. union).

Consider: Which enzyme-producing ducts must be identified for a successful Whipple procedure? (Main pancreatic duct of Wirsung + bile duct; both open at the ampulla of Vater.)

▼ 2. Inferior Vena Cava & Its Tributaries

⭐ The IVC forms at L5 (transtubercular plane) from the union of the common iliac veins, lies to the right of the aorta, and ascends through the caval hiatus (T8) to the right atrium. Asymmetry rule: the left side drains into the LEFT RENAL VEIN first (extra step), while the right side drains directly into IVC.

🌀 Left Renal Vein — A Highway & a Choke Point

The left renal vein is the LONGEST vein in the abdomen (crosses midline anterior to aorta, behind SMA). It receives:

  • Left gonadal vein (testicular/ovarian)
  • Left suprarenal vein
  • Often a hemiazygos/lumbar communication

Nutcracker syndrome: Compression of left renal vein between aorta (behind) and SMA (in front) → ↑ pressure in left kidney → left flank pain, hematuria, proteinuria, and LEFT varicocele in males.

🧠 Mnemonic: "Left Tributaries Detour, Right Go Direct"
▼
  • The IVC lives on the right side, so the right gonadal + right suprarenal veins drop in directly.
  • The left gonadal and left suprarenal veins have farther to go → they use the left renal vein as a shortcut.
  • Practical consequence: Left-sided varicocele is far more common (↑ back-pressure via long left gonadal + tricky renal vein junction + nutcracker risk).
⚠ TRAP: Left gonadal vein pathway ▼
  • The left gonadal vein drains into the left renal vein, NOT directly into IVC. This is THE classic USMLE trap.
  • The right gonadal vein drains directly into IVC.
  • Don't confuse with arteries: both gonadal arteries arise from the aorta at L2 (symmetric origin).

▼ 3. Kidneys — Fascia, Vasculature & Internal Architecture

⭐ The kidneys are primarily retroperitoneal, at vertebral levels T12–L3. The right kidney is slightly lower (pushed down by the liver). Each is wrapped in 3 concentric layers: renal capsule (innermost) → perirenal fat → renal fascia (Gerota's) → pararenal fat → posterior abdominal wall.

🌲 Kidney Fascial Layers (inside → out)

Perirenal (perinephric) fatAdipose tissue just outside capsule
Renal fascia (Gerota's)Fibrous envelope; encloses kidney + adrenal + perirenal fat; fuses superiorly, open inferiorly
Pararenal fatOutside Gerota's fascia, against abdominal wall

Clinical: Kidney abscesses can spread inferiorly along the open-bottom fascia (to pelvis) but not superiorly (blocked above).

🟢 Renal Arteries & Veins

  • Right renal artery: Longer; passes posterior to IVC.
  • Left renal artery: Shorter (kidney is closer to aorta).
  • Both arteries give off inferior suprarenal arteries before entering hilum.
  • Right renal vein: Short; drains directly into IVC.
  • Left renal vein: LONG; crosses midline anterior to aorta, under SMA → IVC. Receives left gonadal + left suprarenal veins.

🔑 Renal Hilum (Anterior → Posterior)

Structures enter/leave the kidney through the hilum. Memorize this order:

  • Vein (anterior)
  • Artery (middle)
  • Ureter (posterior)

Mnemonic: "VAU" (front to back) — or "Vad, Ayarna, Ureter" — or "Vein → Artery → Ureter" as you move from anterior to posterior.

🛠 Ureter Course & 3 Constrictions

~25 cm long; runs retroperitoneally from renal pelvis to bladder. 3 constrictions where stones lodge:

  1. Ureteropelvic junction (UPJ) — kidney → ureter
  2. Where ureter crosses the pelvic brim — over the common iliac bifurcation
  3. Ureterovesical junction (UVJ) — enters bladder (narrowest)

Key relation: "Water under the bridge" — ureter passes under the uterine artery in females (risk of injury during hysterectomy) and under the ductus deferens in males.

🧠 Mnemonic: Kidney Hilum — "Front Door to Back Door"
▼

Hilum structures anterior → posterior:

  • Vein (most anterior — "welcome sign")
  • Artery (middle)
  • Ureter (most posterior — "back exit")

Alternative: "V-A-U" or think "Venom Always Ultimate" — V in front.

⚠ TRAP: Kidney stones lodge at 3 sites, not 1 ▼
  • Common trap: Question asks "most common site" — the answer is usually the ureterovesical junction (UVJ), the narrowest point.
  • Classic pain referral: "Loin to groin" — ureteric colic radiates from flank (T11–L1 dermatome) to groin (L1–L2, genitofemoral) as the stone descends.
  • Hematuria + severe colicky pain + flank tenderness = triad of renal colic.

▼ 4. Suprarenal (Adrenal) Glands

⭐ Small pyramidal glands on the superomedial aspect of each kidney, within Gerota's fascia (but separated from kidney by a fat septum). The right adrenal is more pyramidal (tucked against IVC); the left is more crescent-shaped. Triple arterial supply, single venous drainage.

🟢 Triple Arterial Supply

  • Superior suprarenal artery: from inferior phrenic artery.
  • Middle suprarenal artery: directly from abdominal aorta (~L1).
  • Inferior suprarenal artery: from renal artery.

🔴 Single Venous Drainage (asymmetric!)

  • Right suprarenal vein → IVC directly (short; high risk of injury during right adrenalectomy).
  • Left suprarenal vein → LEFT RENAL VEIN → IVC.

🌊 Layers (cortex → medulla)

"GFR Makes Good Sex" — zones from outside to inside + their products:

  • Glomerulosa → Mineralocorticoids (aldosterone) — "salt"
  • Fasciculata → Glucocorticoids (cortisol) — "sugar"
  • Reticularis → Sex hormones (androgens, DHEA)
  • Medulla → Catecholamines (EPI/NE) — neural crest origin (chromaffin cells)
⚡ Pheochromocytoma & the Medulla:
  • Catecholamine-secreting tumor of adrenal medulla → episodic HTN, headache, sweating, palpitations ("the 5 P's": Pressure, Pain, Palpitations, Perspiration, Pallor).
  • 24-hr urine metanephrines & vanillylmandelic acid → dx.
  • Rule of 10s: 10% bilateral, 10% malignant, 10% extra-adrenal, 10% familial (MEN-2A/2B, VHL, NF-1).

▼ 5. Posterior Abdominal Wall Muscles

⭐ Four muscles line the posterior wall (medial → lateral): psoas major, psoas minor (variable), iliacus, quadratus lumborum. Plus the transversus abdominis from the lateral wall extending posteriorly. The lumbar plexus emerges from within psoas major.

💪 Psoas Major

  • Origin: Transverse processes + bodies of T12–L5.
  • Insertion: Lesser trochanter of femur (joins iliacus → iliopsoas tendon).
  • Action: Main hip flexor.
  • Innervation: Direct branches from ventral rami L1–L3.
  • Clinical: Psoas abscess → positive psoas sign (pain on hip extension) in appendicitis (retrocecal appendix inflames overlying psoas).

💪 Psoas Minor (Variable — absent in ~40%)

  • Thin muscle on anterior surface of psoas major.
  • Origin: T12–L1.
  • Insertion: Pectineal line of pelvis.
  • Weak trunk flexor.

🪗 Iliacus

  • Origin: Iliac fossa (of ilium).
  • Insertion: Lesser trochanter (joins psoas → iliopsoas tendon).
  • Action: Hip flexion (with psoas).
  • Innervation: Femoral nerve (L2–L4).

▼ 6. Diaphragm & Its 3 Openings

⭐ The diaphragm is a dome-shaped muscle dividing thorax from abdomen. It has a central tendon, muscular edges (including the right + left crura), and 3 main openings at T8, T10, T12. Innervation = phrenic nerve (C3, C4, C5). "C3, 4, 5 keeps the diaphragm alive."

  • Right crus: Longer; arises from L1–L3 vertebrae; forms the esophageal hiatus (fibers sling around esophagus).
  • Left crus: Shorter; arises from L1–L2.

▼ 8. Sympathetic Trunk & Splanchnic Nerves

⭐ The sympathetic trunk runs along the vertebral column as a chain of ganglia. In the abdomen, it sits medial to psoas, on the bodies of lumbar vertebrae. The splanchnic nerves (presynaptic sympathetics) pass through the trunk without synapsing to reach prevertebral ganglia (celiac, SMA, IMA), where they synapse and then distribute to gut.

Testing Your Understanding — Lab 4, Question 1

A 58-year-old man presents with sudden-onset left flank pain radiating to the groin + scrotum, gross hematuria, and on exam a new, non-pulsatile mass in the left scrotum that does not reduce with lying down. CT abdomen shows a left renal mass extending into the renal vein. Explain the venous anatomy that links these three findings.

Consider: left gonadal v. drainage, nutcracker geometry, renal cell carcinoma venous invasion

✓ Answer: Renal cell carcinoma is obstructing/invading the left renal vein. The left gonadal vein drains into the left renal vein — any obstruction backs up pressure into the pampiniform plexus → left varicocele. Hematuria is from tumor bleeding into the collecting system; flank pain is from capsular stretch.

The left gonadal (testicular) vein empties into the left renal vein (NOT directly into IVC, unlike the right). This makes left varicoceles uniquely sensitive to renal vein issues: nutcracker compression, renal cell carcinoma thrombus, or even physiologic increased venous pressure. A new left varicocele in an older man should always prompt a renal evaluation — RCC until proven otherwise. "Renal cell carcinoma triad" = flank pain + hematuria + palpable mass (≤10% of patients have all 3), but a left varicocele is a classic paraneoplastic/mechanical sign.

Consider: Why is a right-sided varicocele much less commonly caused by renal vein pathology? (Right gonadal vein drains directly into IVC — bypasses the renal vein highway.)

Testing Your Understanding — Lab 4, Question 2

A morbidly obese 55-year-old presents with constant right shoulder tip pain 3 days after emergency laparotomy for a perforated peptic ulcer. Her labs show elevated WBCs. Abdominal CT reveals fluid collection under the right hemidiaphragm. Explain the anatomy behind her referred pain, specifying nerve roots and the path of pain signal.

Consider: phrenic nerve, diaphragm innervation, dermatome referral

✓ Answer: Subphrenic abscess is irritating the central diaphragm, which is innervated by the phrenic nerve (C3, C4, C5). Afferent fibers travel via the phrenic nerve back to the C3–C5 cord segments. Pain is referred to the dermatomes at those levels — the shoulder tip (C4 dermatome) — a phenomenon called Kehr's sign.

Central diaphragm = phrenic nerve only (C3-4-5 keeps the diaphragm alive). When inflammatory irritation from a subphrenic abscess reaches the central dome, the brain cannot distinguish visceral afferent pain signals from skin inputs entering the cord at the same level. Peripheral parts of the diaphragm, in contrast, are supplied by T7–T11 intercostal nerves, so peripheral irritation refers to the thoracoabdominal wall. Kehr's sign is also classic after splenic rupture (blood tracks to under left hemidiaphragm, refers to left shoulder).

Consider: What other structure passes through the caval opening at T8 alongside the IVC? (Right phrenic nerve — clinically relevant if IVC is clamped or divided.)

B. Processus vaginalis

Q4 — Dermatome Referral

A 10-year-old with early appendicitis complains of vague periumbilical pain. Over the next 12 hours, pain shifts to the right lower quadrant. Which statement best explains the initial periumbilical pain?

D. Somatic afferents from the umbilicus

Early appendicitis causes visceral peritoneal inflammation; pain is poorly localized because visceral afferents converge centrally at the same spinal levels as midgut sympathetics (T10–T11). Only when the inflamed appendix contacts the PARIETAL peritoneum in the RLQ does pain localize (sharp, well-localized somatic pain via lower intercostal nerves).

(A) This explains LATER localized RLQ pain, not initial periumbilical. (B, D) Somatic mechanisms cannot explain the diffuse/referred nature. (E) Phrenic refers to shoulder, not umbilicus.

Q8 — Muscle Fiber Direction

Q22 — Appendicular Artery

A 14-year-old with perforated appendicitis goes to surgery. The appendicular artery is a branch of which artery and travels in which mesentery?

A. Right colic artery; transverse mesocolon

B. Ileocolic artery; mesoappendix

C. Middle colic artery; mesentery proper

D. Superior rectal artery; sigmoid mesocolon

E. Inferior mesenteric artery; mesentery proper

Correct: B — Ileocolic artery, traveling in the mesoappendix.

The appendicular artery is an END artery — no collateral supply. This is why appendicitis rapidly progresses to gangrene and perforation if untreated. The ileocolic artery is the lowest right-sided branch of the SMA; it also supplies the terminal ileum, cecum, and proximal ascending colon.

(A, C) Wrong parent artery. (D, E) IMA territory is hindgut; appendix is midgut.

Q23 — Pancreatic Cancer Complications

A 65-year-old man with painless jaundice has a 3-cm mass in the head of the pancreas. Which structure passing through the pancreatic head is responsible for his jaundice?

A. Main pancreatic duct

B. Common bile duct

C. Superior mesenteric vein

D. Inferior vena cava

E. Cystic duct

Correct: B — Common bile duct.

The distal bile duct travels through the head of the pancreas before joining the main pancreatic duct at the ampulla of Vater. Cancer of the pancreatic head commonly compresses or invades the bile duct → obstructive ("painless") jaundice + a palpable, enlarged non-tender gallbladder (Courvoisier's sign).

(A) Obstructing the pancreatic duct alone → chronic pancreatitis, not classic jaundice. (C, D) SMV + IVC don't traverse the head; SMV passes behind the neck. (E) Cystic duct is in the hepatoduodenal ligament, not the pancreas.

Q24 — Colon Identification

A pathology resident is examining a resected bowel specimen. She identifies three longitudinal bands of outer muscle, fatty tags hanging off the serosal surface, and pouched-out sacculations. Which segment of bowel is this?

A. Jejunum

B. Ileum

C. Colon

D. Duodenum

E. Rectum

Correct: C — Colon.

The three distinguishing features of the colon: (1) teniae coli (3 longitudinal muscle bands), (2) haustra (sacculations between teniae), (3) omental (epiploic) appendages (fatty tags). All three are unique to the colon (none in small intestine or rectum).

(A, B, D) Small intestine has plicae circulares (valvulae conniventes) instead. (E) Rectum has NO teniae — the longitudinal muscle spreads to form a continuous outer layer.

Q26 — Nutcracker Syndrome

A 23-year-old thin woman develops intermittent left flank pain, microscopic hematuria, and a new-onset left-sided varicocele. CT shows compression of the left renal vein between two vessels. Which two vessels are responsible?

A. Aorta and IVC

B. Aorta and SMA

C. Celiac trunk and aorta

D. SMA and SMV

E. Left renal artery and aorta

Correct: B — Aorta (posterior) and SMA (anterior).

Nutcracker syndrome: the long left renal vein crosses the midline between the aorta posteriorly and the SMA anteriorly. In thin people with narrow aortomesenteric angle, the vein is compressed. Because the left gonadal vein drains into the left renal vein, back-pressure → LEFT varicocele. Also flank pain + hematuria from congestion.

(A, C, D, E) Other vascular relationships not causing classic Nutcracker.

Q27 — Left Varicocele Workup

A 62-year-old man presents with a newly discovered left-sided varicocele that does not disappear when he lies down. What is the most concerning underlying diagnosis?

A. Benign prostatic hyperplasia

B. Right-sided renal cell carcinoma

C. Left-sided renal cell carcinoma

D. Pancreatic tail tumor

E. Chronic pancreatitis

Correct: C — Left-sided renal cell carcinoma.

An acute or non-reducible left varicocele in an older man should prompt imaging to rule out RCC. The left gonadal vein drains into the left renal vein. Tumor thrombus invading the left renal vein obstructs venous return from the gonadal vein → pampiniform plexus dilates → varicocele.

(B) Right RCC would cause right-sided venous congestion — but right gonadal drains directly into IVC, so right varicocele from RCC is less classic and usually requires massive IVC invasion. (A, D, E) Do not cause this specific venous pathology.

Q28 — Kehr's Sign

A 24-year-old man kicked in the left flank during a fight develops severe left shoulder pain 2 hours later. Abdominal exam shows left upper quadrant tenderness. FAST ultrasound shows free fluid. Which nerve mediates the referred shoulder pain?

A. Intercostobrachial nerve (T2)

B. Axillary nerve (C5-C6)

C. Phrenic nerve (C3-C4-C5)

D. Thoracodorsal nerve (C6-C8)

E. Suprascapular nerve (C5-C6)

Correct: C — Phrenic nerve (C3-C4-C5).

Splenic rupture with hemoperitoneum irritates the left hemidiaphragm. The central diaphragm is innervated solely by the phrenic nerve (C3-C4-C5). Sensory afferents enter the cord at C3-C5, and pain refers to the C4 dermatome = shoulder tip = Kehr's sign. Same mechanism for right shoulder pain in subphrenic abscess or liver laceration.

(A) Upper medial arm — wrong dermatome. (B, D, E) Not involved in diaphragmatic sensation.

Q29 — Ureter Injury

During a hysterectomy, the surgeon clamps the uterine artery near the cervix. 48 hours later, the patient develops hydronephrosis on the clamped side. Why is the ureter at such high risk during this step?

A. The ureter runs alongside the uterine artery in the broad ligament

B. The ureter runs beneath the uterine artery ("water under the bridge") near the cervix

C. The ureter enters the bladder directly through the uterine wall

D. The ureter is adherent to the uterine artery at the pelvic brim

E. The uterine artery gives off ureteric branches that can be avulsed

Correct: B — "Water under the bridge": ureter passes UNDER the uterine artery ~2 cm lateral to the cervix.

The ureter is at its most vulnerable as it passes under the uterine artery near the cervix. "Water (ureter) under the bridge (uterine artery)." In males, the same relation exists with the ductus deferens passing over the ureter. This is why hysterectomy requires meticulous ureter identification. Clamped or transected ureter → obstructive uropathy.

(A, C, D, E) Incorrect anatomic claims; the critical relation is B.

Q31 — Psoas Sign

A 22-year-old with RLQ pain has worsening pain when the examiner extends the right hip. This "psoas sign" suggests inflammation in which location?

A. Anterior cecal appendix

B. Pelvic appendix

C. Retrocecal appendix (adjacent to psoas muscle)

D. Terminal ileum

E. Ascending colon

Correct: C — Retrocecal appendix (behind the cecum, overlying the psoas muscle).

A retrocecal inflamed appendix lies directly on the psoas major muscle. Extending the hip stretches psoas → pushes against inflamed peritoneum → pain. Similarly, internal rotation of the flexed hip (obturator sign) suggests a pelvic appendix against the obturator internus.

(A, B, D, E) Don't preferentially irritate psoas.

Q32 — Lumbar Plexus

A 48-year-old develops numbness on the lateral thigh and burning pain after wearing a tight belt. Which nerve is entrapped, and what is its root level?

A. Iliohypogastric nerve, L1

B. Ilioinguinal nerve, L1

C. Genitofemoral nerve, L1-L2

D. Lateral cutaneous nerve of thigh, L2-L3

E. Obturator nerve, L2-L4

Correct: D — Lateral cutaneous nerve of thigh (L2-L3) — "meralgia paresthetica."

The lateral cutaneous nerve of the thigh emerges from the lateral border of psoas, passes under the inguinal ligament just medial to ASIS, and supplies lateral thigh skin. Entrapment under the inguinal ligament (by tight belts, pregnancy, obesity) → meralgia paresthetica = burning/numbness of lateral thigh without motor deficit.

(A) Iliohypogastric — suprapubic skin. (B) Ilioinguinal — anterior scrotum/labia + medial upper thigh. (C) Genitofemoral — cremaster + femoral triangle skin. (E) Obturator — medial thigh adductors + skin.
