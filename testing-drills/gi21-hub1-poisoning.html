LO 2 · SOAP Mnemonic
What are the four top single-substance fatality classes reported to the NPDS?
Click to flip
Answer
SOAP: Sedatives/hypnotics/antipsychotics (#1, 299) · Opioids (#3, 244) · Acetaminophen (#2, 270) · Painkillers (alcohols #4, 231). Memorize the ranking — "APAP is #2."
LO 2 · Carbon Monoxide
Why does routine pulse oximetry fail to detect CO poisoning, and what test is required?
Click to flip
Answer
COHb has absorption similar to oxyhemoglobin at the wavelengths the pulse-ox uses → falsely normal SpO₂. CO binds Hb with 200–250× affinity of O₂. Diagnosis requires co-oximetry. Treat with 100% O₂; HBO for severe/pregnant/neuro findings.
LO 2 · Lead Poisoning
Which two heme-synthesis enzymes does lead inhibit, and what classic peripheral-smear finding results?
Click to flip
Answer
Lead inhibits δ-ALA dehydratase and ferrochelatase → microcytic sideroblastic anemia with basophilic stippling on smear. Chelate with succimer (oral, mild-mod) or EDTA + BAL (severe/encephalopathy). Also: burton's lines, wrist/foot drop, abdominal colic.
LO 3 · WARS History
What four history categories must be collected on EVERY poisoning patient?
Click to flip
Answer
WARS: What (agent + ALL co-ingestants) · Amount + route · Route/Reason (accidental vs intentional vs recreational) · Since when (time of ingestion → drives decontamination window + APAP level timing).
LO 4 · Red-Flag Presentations
Name four vital-sign or exam findings that should immediately raise suspicion for occult poisoning.
Click to flip
Answer
(1) Unexplained AMS / coma; (2) Seizure (especially first-ever, refractory, or in young adult); (3) Unexplained acidosis with ↑ anion gap; (4) Marked HR/BP abnormalities without cause. Also: pediatric arrhythmia, wide QRS, severe hypoglycemia, unique breath odor.
LO 5 · Labs
What is the universal "tox-screen quartet" ordered on every undifferentiated poisoning?
Click to flip
Answer
APAP level, salicylate level, ECG, bedside glucose. Add: BMP (anion gap, osmolar gap), lactate, VBG, β-hCG in reproductive-age women. Urine drug screen is low-yield (misses synthetics, long windows) — do NOT let it replace clinical toxidrome assessment.
LO 6 · Charcoal
What is the dose of single-dose activated charcoal, and which five substance classes does it not bind?
Click to flip
Answer
Dose: 1 g/kg PO (max ~50–100 g) within ~1 h of ingestion in an awake, airway-protected patient. LAME Ca exceptions (not adsorbed): Lithium · Alcohols · Metals (iron, lead) · Electrolytes · Caustics. Also poor: hydrocarbons, cyanide.
LO 6 · Decontamination
When is gastric lavage indicated, and when is it absolutely contraindicated?
Click to flip
Answer
Consider only for life-threatening ingestion within 1 h when charcoal is ineffective (e.g., metals). Contraindicated: caustics (re-expose esophagus), hydrocarbons (aspiration pneumonitis), unprotected airway. Ipecac/lavage are no longer routine.
LO 7 · SLIME Mnemonic
Which five toxins are classically dialyzable, and why does digoxin escape the list?
Click to flip
Answer
SLIME — Salicylates · Lithium · Isopropanol (and toxic alcohols: methanol, EG) · Metformin · Ethylene glycol. Digoxin has a huge Vd (~7 L/kg) and tissue distribution → not dialyzable; treat with digoxin-specific Fab.
LO 7 · MDAC
Which four agents benefit from multi-dose activated charcoal via enterohepatic / entero-enteric interruption?
Click to flip
Answer
CPSA: Carbamazepine · Phenobarbital · Salicylates · Antidepressants (TCAs — controversial; dapsone, theophylline, quinine also classic). MDAC = 0.5 g/kg every 4–6 h. Requires intact bowel + airway.
LO 8 · Anticholinergic Toxidrome
Give the five-part mnemonic for anticholinergic toxidrome.
Click to flip
Answer
"Hot as a hare, Dry as a bone, Red as a beet, Blind as a bat, Mad as a hatter." → hyperthermia, dry skin/mucosa, flushing, mydriasis, delirium. Plus: urinary retention, ↓ bowel sounds, tachycardia. Diphenhydramine, TCAs, atropine, Jimson weed.
LO 8 · Cholinergic Toxidrome
What are the muscarinic and nicotinic features of organophosphate poisoning?
Click to flip
Answer
Muscarinic (DUMBELS): Diarrhea · Urination · Miosis · Bronchospasm/Bradycardia · Emesis · Lacrimation · Salivation. Nicotinic (MTWHF): Muscle weakness/fasciculations · Tachycardia · Weakness · Hypertension · Fasciculations. Killer = respiratory failure.
LO 9 · Delayed Toxicity
Name four classic "rocks-in-the-stomach" ingestions with delayed peak toxicity.
Click to flip
Answer
(1) APAP — hepatotoxicity peaks 72–96 h; (2) Iron — phase 3/4 hepatic failure 24–96 h; (3) Methanol/EG — lag 6–24 h until toxic metabolites form; (4) Sustained-release / enteric-coated ingestions. Bezoar formers (Fe, aspirin, Li) → consider WBI.
LO 9 · APAP
What are the four phases of acetaminophen toxicity?
Click to flip
Answer
Phase 1 (0–24 h): N/V, malaise, LFTs normal · Phase 2 (24–72 h): RUQ pain, ↑ AST/ALT, latent deterioration · Phase 3 (72–96 h): peak hepatotoxicity, encephalopathy, coagulopathy, acidosis · Phase 4 (4 d–2 wk): recovery or death. NAC works best <8 h.
LO 10 · Antidotes
Match the antidote: opioids · benzodiazepines · β-blockers · CCBs · digoxin.
Click to flip
Answer
Opioids → Naloxone (caution: withdrawal) · Benzos → Flumazenil (avoid in chronic users / TCA co-ingest — seizures) · β-blocker → Glucagon · CCB → Ca²⁺, insulin/euglycemia, lipid emulsion · Digoxin → Digoxin-Fab.
LO 10 · Toxic Alcohols
What is the antidote for methanol/ethylene glycol poisoning, and what is its mechanism?
Click to flip
Answer
Fomepizole (preferred) or ethanol — both competitively inhibit alcohol dehydrogenase preventing formation of the toxic metabolites (formic acid → retinal; oxalic acid → renal). Add HD, folate (methanol), thiamine + pyridoxine (EG).
LO 10 · INH
An overdose of isoniazid produces refractory seizures. What is the antidote and how is it dosed?
Click to flip
Answer
Pyridoxine (vitamin B6) dosed gram-for-gram of INH ingested (or 5 g empiric). INH depletes pyridoxine → ↓ GABA synthesis → seizures refractory to benzos alone. Also corrects the lactic acidosis.
LO 10 · OP & 2-PAM
Why is pralidoxime (2-PAM) time-sensitive in organophosphate poisoning?
Click to flip
Answer
OPs phosphorylate acetylcholinesterase. Over hours the bond "ages" to an irreversible state. 2-PAM reactivates AChE before aging. Give ASAP with atropine. Atropine treats muscarinic; 2-PAM treats nicotinic (muscle weakness, respiratory failure).
LO 11 · Supportive Care
Name the three pillars of supportive care that save more poisoning patients than any antidote.
Click to flip
Answer
(1) Airway / ventilation (intubate for AMS, seizures, respiratory depression); (2) Hemodynamics — IVF → pressors (norepinephrine first-line for most); (3) Seizure & temp control — benzos first-line for tox seizures; aggressive cooling for sympathomimetic/anticholinergic hyperthermia.
LO 12 · Toxidrome Pearls
How do you differentiate anticholinergic from sympathomimetic toxidrome at the bedside?
Click to flip
Answer
Both have tachycardia, HTN, mydriasis, hyperthermia, agitation. Key discriminators: anticholinergic = DRY skin + absent bowel sounds + urinary retention; sympathomimetic = DIAPHORETIC + hyperactive bowel sounds + normal bladder. Cocaine vs. diphenhydramine — feel the skin and listen.

Drill 1 · Four Phases of Acetaminophen Toxicity
From earliest to latest in the untreated patient
⋮⋮Phase 3 (72–96 h): Peak hepatotoxicity — encephalopathy, coagulopathy, lactic acidosis, AST/ALT often > 1000
⋮⋮Phase 1 (0–24 h): Nausea, vomiting, malaise — LFTs still normal; patient appears deceptively well
⋮⋮Phase 4 (4 d – 2 wk): Recovery with hepatic regeneration OR progression to fulminant failure & death
⋮⋮Phase 2 (24–72 h): RUQ pain, rising AST/ALT & INR, falling symptoms — the "latent" window
Answer:
  1. Phase 1 (0–24 h): Nausea, malaise, LFTs normal
  2. Phase 2 (24–72 h): RUQ pain, rising transaminases
  3. Phase 3 (72–96 h): Peak hepatotoxicity, coagulopathy, encephalopathy
  4. Phase 4 (4 d – 2 wk): Recovery or death
NAC works best < 8 h post-ingestion; use the Rumack-Matthew nomogram at 4 h.
Drill 2 · Five Phases of Iron Toxicity
Place the phases in order
⋮⋮Phase 4 (2–5 d): Hepatotoxicity — ↑ AST/ALT, coagulopathy, fulminant failure
⋮⋮Phase 1 (0.5–6 h): GI corrosive phase — vomiting, hematemesis, diarrhea, abdominal pain
⋮⋮Phase 5 (2–6 wk): Gastric outlet / pyloric scarring & strictures
⋮⋮Phase 2 (6–24 h): Latent / "deceptive recovery" — symptoms improve, but free iron is still circulating
⋮⋮Phase 3 (6–72 h): Shock & metabolic acidosis — vasodilation, third-spacing, anion-gap acidosis
Answer:
  1. Phase 1 (0.5–6 h): GI corrosive — hematemesis, diarrhea
  2. Phase 2 (6–24 h): Latent / "deceptive recovery"
  3. Phase 3 (6–72 h): Shock + anion-gap metabolic acidosis
  4. Phase 4 (2–5 d): Hepatotoxicity
  5. Phase 5 (2–6 wk): Gastric outlet scarring
Iron is radiopaque on AXR. Antidote: deferoxamine IV — urine turns "vin rosé."

Classic poisoning exam traps. Decide True or False — each statement reflects a misconception Step 1 loves to test.
Trap 1 · LO 2
Cherry-red skin is a sensitive clinical sign of carbon-monoxide poisoning and should be used to rule it in at the bedside.
FALSE. Cherry-red skin is a late, insensitive, and usually post-mortem finding. CO poisoning most often presents with nonspecific headache/nausea/AMS — you must order a co-oximetry level. Don't wait for cherry-red; treat with 100% O₂ and HBO when indicated.
Trap 2 · LO 6
Syrup of ipecac is still recommended in the ED for pediatric ingestions within one hour of presentation.
FALSE. Ipecac is no longer recommended in any setting — delays charcoal, induces vomiting that risks aspiration, and is no longer manufactured. The AAP withdrew its endorsement in 2003. Gastric lavage is also no longer routine; activated charcoal is the decontamination of choice.
Trap 3 · LO 6
Activated charcoal is the preferred decontamination for a child who ingested a bottle of ferrous sulfate 30 minutes ago.
FALSE. Iron is one of the "LAME Ca" substances that charcoal does not adsorb (metals). Use whole-bowel irrigation with polyethylene glycol for GI decontamination, follow iron levels, and administer deferoxamine for severe toxicity.
Trap 4 · LO 6
Gastric lavage and activated charcoal are appropriate initial interventions for an intentional hydrocarbon (kerosene) ingestion.
FALSE. Hydrocarbons are high aspiration risk — emesis or lavage provokes chemical pneumonitis. Charcoal does not adsorb them and worsens aspiration risk. Treat with supportive care; intubate for respiratory failure; no routine decontamination.
Trap 5 · LO 10
Flumazenil is the first-line antidote for an unresponsive polysubstance overdose presumed to contain benzodiazepines and a tricyclic antidepressant.
FALSE. Flumazenil can precipitate benzodiazepine withdrawal seizures and, in TCA co-ingestion, unmask lethal TCA seizures by removing the GABAergic brake. Avoid flumazenil in undifferentiated poisonings and chronic benzo users. Supportive care + sodium bicarbonate for TCA QRS widening.
Trap 6 · LO 10
Physostigmine should be given empirically to a tricyclic-overdose patient with tachycardia and delirium because of the anticholinergic features.
FALSE. Physostigmine is contraindicated in TCA overdose — it can precipitate asystole and refractory seizures. Use physostigmine only for pure anticholinergic toxidrome (e.g., Jimson weed) with no QRS widening. TCA cardiotoxicity → IV sodium bicarbonate.
Trap 7 · LO 7
Hemodialysis effectively removes digoxin from an acutely overdosed patient due to the drug's small molecular size.
FALSE. Digoxin has an enormous volume of distribution (Vd ~7 L/kg) — almost entirely tissue-bound. HD cannot access it. Treat with digoxin-specific Fab fragments. Digoxin is NOT in the SLIME mnemonic.
Trap 8 · LO 12
Dilated pupils (mydriasis) make an opioid overdose extremely unlikely and should redirect the differential.
TRUE (mostly). Classic opioid toxidrome = miosis + respiratory depression + ↓ LOC. Mydriasis points away from opioids toward anticholinergic/sympathomimetic. Exceptions: meperidine, propoxyphene, and tramadol can produce normal or dilated pupils; post-anoxic injury from any opioid overdose will also dilate pupils. Still: pinpoint pupils is the classic test.
