LO 1 — Parietal Cell Targets
Three receptor types on parietal cells that increase acid?
Answer
H⁺/K⁺-ATPase pump: Target of PPIs (irreversible)
Histamine H₂: Target of H₂ blockers (reversible)
Gastrin/CCK-B: Activated by gastrin
M₃ muscarinic (ACh): Vagal stimulation
LO 1 — Antacid Comparison
CaCO₃ vs. Mg(OH)₂ vs. Al(OH)₃ — onset and side effects?
Answer
CaCO₃: Rapid, rebound acid, milk-alkali syndrome
Mg(OH)₂: Fast, DIARRHEA, ↑Mg toxicity in renal failure
Al(OH)₃: Slow, CONSTIPATION, hypophosphatemia
LO 1 — PPIs vs. H₂ Blockers
Potency, onset, and cimetidine side effect?
Answer
PPIs: Most potent, irreversible, slow onset (prodrug)
H₂ blockers: Less potent, reversible, faster onset
Cimetidine: CYP inhibitor + antiandrogen (gynecomastia)
LO 1 — P-CABs
What are P-CABs? Example and advantage?
Answer
P-CABs: K⁺-competitive antagonists (vonoprazan)
Mechanism: Reversible, faster onset/offset than PPIs
Advantage: More predictable pharmacokinetics
LO 2 — H. pylori Therapy
Quadruple vs. Triple — when preferred?
Answer
Quadruple (PPI+Bi+Metro+Tet): Preferred in clarithromycin-resistant areas
Triple (PPI+Clarith+Amox): Older, less effective now
Confirm: Urea breath or stool antigen 4 weeks post-Rx
LO 3 — GERD Triggers
Common triggers of transient LES relaxations?
Answer
Triggers: Fatty meals, chocolate, caffeine, nicotine, alcohol, pregnancy
Mechanism: Transient LES relaxations allow reflux
Stepwise Rx: Lifestyle → antacids → H₂ blockers → PPIs
LO 4 — Sucralfate Mechanism
How does it work and why co-dosing timing matters?
Answer
Mechanism: Polymerizes in acidic environment → sticky gel binds ulcer
CRITICAL: Requires acidic pH — do NOT give with PPIs/H₂ blockers simultaneously
Dosing: 1h before meals, separate timing
LO 4 — Misoprostol
Class, mechanism, and major contraindication?
Answer
Class: Synthetic PGE₁ analog
Effects: ↑mucus/HCO₃⁻, ↓acid, ↑blood flow
⚠ TERATOGENIC/ABORTIFACIENT: Pregnancy contraindicated
LO 5 — Vomiting Reflex: NTS vs. CTZ
Location and function of each center?
Answer
NTS: Medulla, central coordinator
CTZ: Floor of 4th ventricle, OUTSIDE BBB, senses blood-borne toxins (D₂, 5-HT₃, NK₁)
Vestibular: H₁, M₁ — motion sickness
LO 5 — Chemo Nausea Pathway
How does chemotherapy trigger nausea?
Answer
GI damage: Enterochromaffin cells release 5-HT
Vagal afferents: 5-HT₃ receptors → NTS
Treatment: Ondansetron (5-HT₃ antagonist) very effective
LO 6 — Ondansetron Pharmacology
Receptor, indications, side effects?
Answer
Target: 5-HT₃ antagonist
Uses: Chemo-induced, post-op, hyperemesis gravidarum
SE: QT prolongation, constipation, serotonin syndrome
LO 6 — Metoclopramide
Mechanism and major CNS risk?
Answer
Mechanism: D₂ antagonist + 5-HT₄ agonist → prokinetic
Crosses BBB: Extrapyramidal side effects, tardive dyskinesia
Contraindicated: Parkinson's, bowel obstruction
LO 6 — Other Antiemetics
Scopolamine, dimenhydrinate, aprepitant — uses?
Answer
Scopolamine: M₁ antagonist, motion sickness, transdermal
Dimenhydrinate/Meclizine: H₁ antihistamine, motion sickness
Aprepitant: NK₁ antagonist, delayed chemo nausea
LO 7 — ORS Mechanism
Why ORS works against cholera?
Answer
Cholera: Toxin → ↑cAMP → Cl⁻ secretion
ORS: 2% glucose + 90 mEq Na⁺
Key: SGLT₁ still works (not cAMP-dependent) → drives Na⁺+glucose absorption
LO 7 — Antidiarrheals
Diphenoxylate vs. Loperamide — BBB crossing?
Answer
Both: μ-opioid agonists
Diphenoxylate: + atropine to deter abuse
Loperamide: P-glycoprotein substrate — does NOT cross BBB, OTC safe
Avoid: Bloody diarrhea (megacolon risk)
LO 8 — Laxative Onset Times
Bulk, osmotic, stimulant, softener — speed ranking?
Answer
Bulk: 1-3 days (safest)
Osmotic: Hours
Stimulant: 6-12 hrs
Softener: Days
LO 8 — Lactulose in Hepatic Encephalopathy
Mechanism of NH₄⁺ trapping?
Answer
Mechanism: Bacteria ferment → lactic acid → ↓pH → traps NH₄⁺
Result: ↓systemic ammonia → improved mental status
Also: Osmotic diarrhea helps washout
LO 9 — 5-ASA vs. Sulfasalazine
Indications and sulfasalazine side effects?
Answer
5-ASA (mesalamine): First-line UC
Sulfasalazine: 5-ASA + sulfapyridine moiety → oligospermia, hemolysis (G6PD), rash
Budesonide: Corticosteroid, ileal-release, induction only
LO 9 — Anti-TNF Biologics
Infliximab vs. adalimumab — structure and route?
Answer
Infliximab: Chimeric IgG → IV
Adalimumab: Fully human → SC injection
Both: TB/fungal risk, lymphoma, worsen CHF, demyelination
LO 9 — Newer IBD Biologics
Vedolizumab, ustekinumab, risankizumab — targets?
Answer
Vedolizumab: Anti-α₄β₇, gut-selective
Ustekinumab: Anti-IL-12/23 (p40 subunit)
Risankizumab: Anti-IL-23 p19 (newer, selective)
Note: Crohn's anti-TNF preferred; UC 5-ASA first

MCQ 1 — Acid Suppression Potency
A patient with GERD is started on omeprazole. Compared to ranitidine (H₂ blocker), how does omeprazole differ mechanistically?
ABlocks histamine H₂ receptors reversibly
BIrreversibly inhibits H⁺/K⁺-ATPase pump
CBlocks M₃ muscarinic receptors
DNeutralizes gastric acid directly
EEnhances mucus production
B. PPIs irreversibly block the final step (H⁺/K⁺-ATPase), making them more potent than H₂ blockers (reversible, upstream block). Ranitidine blocks H₂ (A), not the pump. Pirenzepine blocks M₃ (C). Antacids neutralize directly (D). Misoprostol enhances mucus (E).
MCQ 2 — Cimetidine Side Effect
A 55-year-old man on cimetidine for peptic ulcer disease develops gynecomastia and erectile dysfunction. What is the mechanism?
ACYP450 inhibition + androgen receptor blockade
BDirect testosterone suppression
CEstrogen receptor upregulation
DProlactin elevation
EReduced hepatic testosterone metabolism
A. Cimetidine is a potent CYP inhibitor (↓drug metabolism) AND blocks androgen receptors (antiandrogen). Both effects → gynecomastia, impotence, erectile dysfunction. Ranitidine/famotidine don't have these side effects. Metoclopramide causes hyperprolactinemia (D), not cimetidine.
MCQ 3 — Sucralfate Timing
A patient on sucralfate for ulcer protection is also prescribed omeprazole. What is the key consideration?
AGive both drugs together for maximum effect
BSeparate dosing — sucralfate requires acidic pH to work
CSucralfate reduces PPI absorption by 50%
DPPIs are contraindicated with sucralfate
ENo interaction — both can be dosed simultaneously
B. Sucralfate polymerizes ONLY in acidic environment. If PPIs suppress acid, sucralfate cannot activate → ineffective. Give sucralfate 1 hour before meals (separate timing from PPI). Same issue with H₂ blockers.
MCQ 4 — Misoprostol in Pregnancy
A 32-year-old woman with NSAIDinduced ulcers is prescribed misoprostol for cytoprotection. What is the critical contraindication?
AIncreases gastric acid secretion
BTeratogenic and abortifacient — pregnancy contraindicated
CCauses severe diarrhea
DInteracts with NSAIDs
EReduces NSAID efficacy
B. Misoprostol is a PGE₁ analog that stimulates uterine contractions → abortifacient. Absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy. Also used therapeutically for medical abortion. Diarrhea (C) is a common SE but not the critical issue. Women of childbearing age must use contraception.
MCQ 5 — Ondansetron vs. Metoclopramide
A 45-year-old receives chemotherapy. Which antiemetic blocks 5-HT₃ and is ideal for acute chemo-induced nausea?
AOndansetron
BMetoclopramide
CScopolamine
DProchlorperazine
EDomperidone
A. Ondansetron (5-HT₃ antagonist) is the gold standard for chemo-induced nausea — blocks serotonin-mediated emesis from GI damage. Metoclopramide is a D₂ antagonist (different mechanism, CNS EPS risk). Scopolamine = motion sickness. Prochlorperazine = older antipsychotic antiemetic.
MCQ 6 — Loperamide BBB Crossing
Why is loperamide safe OTC while diphenoxylate is prescription-only?
ALoperamide is a P-glycoprotein substrate — does NOT cross BBB at therapeutic doses
BLoperamide has no CNS effects
CDiphenoxylate is more lipophilic
DLoperamide is not an opioid
EDiphenoxylate crosses the BBB
A. Loperamide is a μ-opioid but substrate of P-glycoprotein (efflux transporter) — does NOT cross BBB at therapeutic doses → minimal CNS effects, safe OTC. Diphenoxylate + atropine is prescription to deter abuse (atropine deters overdose). Both are μ-agonists, but loperamide has the BBB protection.
MCQ 7 — ORS Mechanism in Cholera
Why does oral rehydration solution (ORS) work despite cholera toxin causing massive secretory diarrhea?
AORS neutralizes cholera toxin
BORS blocks cAMP production
CSGLT₁ co-transporter still works — drives Na⁺+glucose absorption independent of cAMP
DORS contains antimicrobial agents
EORS increases intestinal peristalsis
C. Cholera toxin → ↑cAMP → Cl⁻ secretion + water loss. But SGLT₁ (Na⁺-glucose cotransporter) is NOT cAMP-regulated → still absorbs Na⁺ + glucose → water follows osmotically. ORS (2% glucose + 90 mEq Na⁺) exploits this pathway → life-saving. Does NOT neutralize toxin or block cAMP.
MCQ 8 — Quadruple vs. Triple H. pylori
In regions with high clarithromycin resistance, what is the preferred first-line H. pylori regimen?
APPI + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin
BPPI + Bismuth + Metronidazole + Tetracycline
CPPI + Metronidazole + Clarithromycin
DBismuth + Tetracycline monotherapy
EPPI monotherapy
B. Quadruple PBMT (PPI + Bismuth + Metronidazole + Tetracycline) is now preferred empirically due to rising clarithromycin resistance. Triple therapy (PPI + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin) was standard but is outdated in many regions. Confirm eradication 4+ weeks post-treatment.
MCQ 9 — Metoclopramide EPS
A 62-year-old on metoclopramide for gastroparesis develops tremor, rigidity, and bradykinesia. What is the mechanism?
A5-HT₄ agonism in basal ganglia
BD₂ antagonism in CNS (crosses BBB) → Parkinsonian symptoms
CAnticholinergic effects
DGABA antagonism
ESerotonin syndrome
B. Metoclopramide crosses BBB → D₂ antagonism in basal ganglia → extrapyramidal side effects, Parkinsonian features, tardive dyskinesia with chronic use. Contraindicated in Parkinson's disease. Ondansetron does NOT cross BBB significantly, so fewer CNS effects.
MCQ 10 — Infliximab Pre-screening
Before starting infliximab for Crohn's disease, what mandatory screening is required?
AHLA-B allele testing
BTuberculosis screening + hepatitis B screening
CHIV testing
DCardiac ejection fraction
EComplete metabolic panel
B. Anti-TNF agents (infliximab, adalimumab) carry risk of TB reactivation and HBV reactivation → mandatory TB screen (PPD/IGRA) and HBsAg/anti-HBc before start. Must also warn of lymphoma, fungal infections, CHF exacerbation, demyelination. HLA-B testing is for abacavir (HIV drug), not TNF inhibitors.
MCQ 11 — PPI Long-term Complications
A 70-year-old on chronic PPI therapy presents with a hip fracture from minor trauma. What is the mechanism of increased fracture risk?
A↓calcium and ↓magnesium absorption → osteomalacia/osteoporosis
BDirect inhibition of osteoblasts
CVitamin K deficiency
DIncreased parathyroid hormone
EDirect calcitriol antagonism
A. Long-term PPIs → ↓gastric acid → ↓Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ absorption (need acid for ionization) → osteomalacia/osteoporosis → fractures. Also ↓B₁₂, ↓K⁺. Monitor elderly on chronic PPIs; consider calcium/Mg supplementation. Secondary hyperparathyroidism (↑PTH) may occur.

Vignette 1 — NSAID Gastritis + Prophylaxis
Hx: 68-year-old with osteoarthritis on high-dose indomethacin develops epigastric pain. Endoscopy shows acute gastric erosion. Q: Which drug would be most appropriate for NSAID-induced ulcer prophylaxis?
ACimetidine (less expensive)
BMisoprostol (PGE₁ analog — ↑mucus, ↑HCO₃⁻)
CSucralfate (but NSAID inhibits prostaglandins anyway)
DAntacid monotherapy
EFamotidine (insufficient for high-risk NSAID)
B. Misoprostol is indicated specifically for NSAID-induced ulcer prevention in high-risk patients (elderly, prior ulcer history). It directly stimulates protective mechanisms (mucus, HCO₃⁻, blood flow) that NSAIDs inhibit. PPIs also effective but misoprostol is most directly indicated. Contraception required (teratogenic).
Vignette 2 — H. pylori Eradication Failure
Hx: 52-year-old from Southeast Asia with confirmed H. pylori PUD. Tried triple therapy (PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin) 2 months ago; urea breath test now positive (NOT eradicated). Q: What is the next regimen?
ARepeat same triple therapy
BQuadruple PBMT (assumes clarithromycin-resistant strain)
CContinue observation without antibiotics
DBismuth monotherapy
EMetronidazole monotherapy
B. Failure after triple therapy → likely clarithromycin resistance. Quadruple PBMT (PPI + Bismuth + Metronidazole + Tetracycline) is the empiric next step, covering resistant strains. Repeat eradication test 4+ weeks post-treatment. Bismuth/tetracycline avoid clarithromycin reliance.
Vignette 3 — Hepatic Encephalopathy + Lactulose
Hx: 61-year-old with cirrhosis + ascites develops confusion and asterixis. Serum ammonia 180 µmol/L (high). Q: Which medication would both treat acute encephalopathy AND reduce ammonia?
APPI (reduces ammonia-producing bacteria)
BLactulose (colonic bacteria → lactic acid → NH₄⁺ trapping)
CAlbumin IV
DRifaxomicin (antibiotic, less effective solo)
EL-ornithine L-aspartate (LOLA) only
B. Lactulose is first-line: nonabsorbable disaccharide → bacterial fermentation → lactic acid ↓pH → protonates NH₃ to NH₄⁺ (trapped, not absorbed) → osmotic diarrhea helps washout. Both reduces ammonia + causes "therapeutic" diarrhea. Effective prophylaxis + acute therapy. PPIs have minor role; rifaxomicin is adjunct.
Vignette 4 — Cimetidine Gynecomastia
Hx: 51-year-old on cimetidine for peptic ulcer develops tender bilateral breast enlargement + erectile dysfunction over 6 weeks. Testosterone and LH normal. Q: What mechanism explains both symptoms?
APrimary hypogonadism (↓testosterone production)
BCYP inhibition (↓hormone clearance) + androgen receptor blockade
CProlactin elevation
DDirect estrogen synthesis stimulation
EGonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) suppression
B. Cimetidine is dual-action: (1) CYP450 inhibitor → ↓clearance of endogenous hormones, (2) antiandrogen (blocks androgen receptors). Combined effect → relative estrogen dominance → gynecomastia + impotence. Normal testosterone because the problem is androgen receptor blockade, not hormone production. Switch to famotidine (no antiandrogen effect).
Vignette 5 — Chemo-Induced Nausea Management
Hx: 47-year-old receiving cisplatin chemotherapy suffers severe nausea/vomiting on day 2–3 post-infusion. Standard ondansetron pre-med (5-HT₃) + dexamethasone given but delayed nausea persists. Q: Which additional antiemetic targets delayed emesis?
AMore ondansetron
BAprepitant (NK₁ antagonist — standard for delayed)
CMetoclopramide monotherapy
DScopolamine patch
EDiphenhydramine alone
B. Aprepitant (NK₁ antagonist) is gold standard for delayed chemo-induced nausea (days 2–3, not acute). Ondansetron + dexamethasone cover acute phase; aprepitant covers delayed. Different pathophysiology: delayed involves substance P + NK₁ pathway, not pure 5-HT₃. Often given 3 days post-chemo as part of triple-agent regimen.
Vignette 6 — Motion Sickness Prophylaxis
Hx: 34-year-old preparing for a long ocean cruise knows from experience he develops severe motion sickness. Q: Which agent targets the vestibular pathway most effectively?
AOndansetron (more for chemo/post-op)
BMetoclopramide (prokinetic, not vestibular)
CScopolamine patch (M₁ antagonist in vestibular nucleus)
DAprepitant (NK₁, for delayed chemo)
EBismuth subsalicylate
C. Motion sickness involves vestibular apparatus (H₁, M₁ receptors) → NTS. Scopolamine (transdermal M₁ antagonist) is ideal for motion sickness prevention (can apply patch before travel). Also dimenhydrinate/meclizine (H₁ antihistamine) work well. Ondansetron is for chemo/blood-borne toxins (CTZ pathway), not vestibular.
Vignette 7 — Crohn's Flare + Biologics
Hx: 28-year-old with Crohn's disease refractory to 5-ASA + corticosteroids develops fever, RLQ pain, diarrhea. CT shows transmural inflammation with fistulas. Q: Which biologic is indicated for this Crohn's flare?
AVedolizumab (first-line for all IBD)
BInfliximab (anti-TNF — transmural/fistulizing Crohn's)
CUstekinumab (anti-IL-12/23)
DRisankizumab (anti-IL-23)
EMore corticosteroids
B. Crohn's = transmural + skip lesions + fistulizing → anti-TNF (infliximab/adalimumab) is preferred first-line biologic. UC = mucosal + continuous → 5-ASA first-line biologic (vedolizumab, ustekinumab are newer options for both, but anti-TNF best for Crohn's phenotype). Must screen TB/HBV before starting.
Vignette 8 — Loperamide vs. Flagyl in C. difficile
Hx: 71-year-old hospitalized with diarrhea; C. difficile toxin positive. Resident orders loperamide for symptom control. Q: Why is loperamide contraindicated here?
ALoperamide is ineffective against C. difficile
BRisk of toxic megacolon (↓peristalsis traps toxins)
CLoperamide causes diarrhea (paradoxical)
DInteraction with metronidazole
EAllergic cross-reactivity
B. Antiperistaltic agents (loperamide, diphenoxylate) are contraindicated in C. difficile-associated diarrhea → ↓motility traps toxins in colon → toxic megacolon, perforation, mortality ↑. Use metronidazole or vancomycin, NOT antidiarrheals. Same caution in bloody diarrhea (shigella, salmonella, etc.).
Step 1 Traps: Classic confusions and second-order thinking mistakes.
Trap 1
"Cimetidine and ranitidine are interchangeable H₂ blockers."
FALSE. While both are H₂ blockers, cimetidine has serious side effects: CYP450 inhibition (↑drug interactions) + antiandrogen (gynecomastia, impotence). Ranitidine, famotidine, nizatidine do NOT. Cimetidine is largely obsolete for this reason.
Trap 2
"You can give PPIs and sucralfate at the same time for maximum ulcer protection."
FALSE. Sucralfate requires ACIDIC pH to polymerize and form protective gel. PPIs suppress acid → sucralfate cannot activate. Must separate dosing (sucralfate ≥1 hour before PPI). Same issue with H₂ blockers.
Trap 3
"Misoprostol is safe in pregnancy to prevent NSAID-induced ulcers."
FALSE. Misoprostol is TERATOGENIC and ABORTIFACIENT — pregnancy is absolute contraindication. Women of childbearing age on misoprostol must use reliable contraception. Actually used for medical abortion. Use alternative (PPI instead).
Trap 4
"Metoclopramide is an antiemetic with no CNS side effects."
FALSE. Metoclopramide is D₂ antagonist that CROSSES BBB → extrapyramidal effects (tremor, rigidity, dystonia), tardive dyskinesia, parkinsonism. Contraindicated in Parkinson's disease. Ondansetron does NOT cross BBB significantly (safer CNS profile).
Trap 5
"Ondansetron is the best antiemetic for all causes of nausea."
FALSE. Ondansetron blocks 5-HT₃ (effective for chemo, post-op, vagal GI input). DOES NOT work for motion sickness (vestibular pathway = H₁/M₁). Use scopolamine or antihistamine for motion sickness. Aprepitant (NK₁) for delayed chemo nausea.
Trap 6
"Loperamide carries high abuse potential because it crosses the BBB like other opioids."
FALSE. Loperamide is a μ-opioid but P-glycoprotein substrate → does NOT cross BBB at therapeutic doses → minimal CNS effects → safe OTC. Diphenoxylate DOES cross BBB better (hence combined with atropine to deter abuse). This difference is KEY to their regulatory status.
Trap 7
"Antidiarrheals like loperamide are beneficial in C. difficile-associated diarrhea."
FALSE. Antiperistaltic agents are CONTRAINDICATED in C. difficile (and bloody diarrhea) → ↓motility traps toxins → toxic megacolon, perforation, mortality ↑. Use antibiotics (metronidazole, vancomycin), NOT motility slowers. Same caution applies to invasive bacterial diarrhea.
Trap 8
"5-ASA is the first-line biologic for both Crohn's and UC."
MISLEADING. 5-ASA is first-line medical therapy for UC (mucosal disease). Crohn's (transmural) often needs anti-TNF biologic earlier. Also: 5-ASA is not technically a "biologic" — biologics = antibodies/cytokine antagonists. Terminology matters on Step 1.
Trap 9
"Infliximab (chimeric) and adalimumab (fully human) have identical immunogenicity."
FALSE. Adalimumab (fully human) is LESS immunogenic than infliximab (chimeric = mouse/human hybrid). Less immunogenicity = less anti-drug antibodies = more durable effect. Both still risk TB reactivation, fungal infections, lymphoma, CHF. Pre-treatment TB/HBV screening mandatory for both.
