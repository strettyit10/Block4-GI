LO 1 — Embryonic Folding
How do cranial-caudal and lateral folding create the tube-within-a-tube body plan? What are the three germ layers involved?
Answer
Folding: Cranial-caudal folds bring head/tail ends toward midline; lateral folds bring side edges together → wraps visceral peritoneum around developing GI tract
Germ layers: Endoderm → epithelium of entire GI tract; Splanchnic mesoderm (visceral) → smooth muscle + mesentery; Somatic mesoderm → parietal peritoneum
Result: Peritoneal cavity (potential space), intraperitoneal organs, retroperitoneal structures
LO 2 — Mesentery Basics
What is the fundamental difference between ventral and dorsal mesentery? Which parts of the GI tract have each?
Answer
Ventral mesentery: Connects viscera to ANTERIOR abdominal wall; exists ONLY in foregut (esophagus to duodenum)
Dorsal mesentery: Connects viscera to POSTERIOR abdominal wall; spans ALL gut (esophagus to anal canal)
Key derivatives: Ventral → liver divides it into falciform ligament (between liver + anterior wall) + hepatoduodenal ligament (contains portal triad)
Dorsal: Becomes root of mesentery (small bowel), transverse mesocolon, sigmoid mesocolon, mesorectum
LO 2 — SAD PUCKER
What does SAD PUCKER stand for, and why is this clinically important?
Answer
SAD PUCKER: Structures that are Retroperitoneal
Suprarenals · Aorta · Duodenum (2nd–4th parts) · Pancreas · Urethra (proximal) · Colon (ascending + descending) · Kidneys · Esophagus · Rectum
Clinical: Retroperitoneal structures can bleed into the retroperitoneal space without immediate peritoneal signs (Cullen/Grey Turner signs in acute pancreatitis)
LO 3 — Foregut / Midgut / Hindgut Arteries
Which artery supplies each gut segment, and what does each develop into?
Answer
Foregut (celiac artery): Pharynx → duodenum (1st–2nd parts) · Also: liver, gallbladder, pancreas (dorsal bud)
Midgut (superior mesenteric artery): Duodenum (3rd–4th parts) → proximal 2/3 colon · Also: cecum, appendix, small bowel
Hindgut (inferior mesenteric artery): Distal 1/3 colon → anal canal above pectinate line · Also: rectum, sigmoid
Key: Pectinate line = ectoderm/endoderm boundary + vascular boundary (SMA/IMA watershed)
LO 3 — Stomach 90° CW Rotation
How does the stomach rotate, and what does this create? Why is this important?
Answer
Rotation: Stomach rotates 90° clockwise around its long axis → original anterior wall (left vagus) becomes LESSER curvature; posterior wall (right vagus) becomes GREATER curvature
Creates: Lesser sac (omental bursa) — potential space behind stomach
Clinical: Left vagus = anterior trunk (ALWAYS); right vagus = posterior trunk (ALWAYS); explains vagotomy effects; gastric ulcers on lesser curvature often bleed from left gastric artery
LO 3 — Pancreatic Buds (Dorsal vs. Ventral)
Where do each pancreatic bud originate, and what does each bud become? When do they fuse?
Answer
Dorsal bud: From dorsal aspect of duodenum (foregut) → becomes body, tail, and superior/anterior head
Ventral bud: From hepatic diverticulum (with bile duct) → becomes uncinate process + inferior/posterior head
Fusion: Wk 7 — ducts fuse (dorsal → main duct of Wirsung; ventral → accessory duct of Santorini)
Clinical: Annular pancreas (ventral bud encircles duodenum) → duodenal obstruction; tail of pancreas is always intraperitoneal (derived from dorsal bud in greater omentum)
LO 3 — Duodenal Atresia (Double Bubble)
What causes duodenal atresia, what is the classic imaging finding, and what genetic syndrome is associated?
Answer
Cause: Failure of duodenal recanalization (duodenum is initially solid, then hollow out in wks 8–12)
Imaging: "Double bubble" sign on X-ray (gas in proximal duodenum + pyloric stomach; NO distal gas)
Presentation: Neonate with bilious vomiting, no meconium passage within 24 hrs
Association: Down syndrome (trisomy 21) — strongest association
Management: Duodenoduodenostomy bypass
LO 3 — Midgut Rotation (270° CCW)
Describe the midgut rotation in three phases. What is the classic complication of failed rotation?
Answer
Phase 1 (wk 6): Midgut herniates out of abdomen, rotates 90° CCW around SMA
Phase 2 (wks 10–12): Herniated midgut returns to abdomen, continues rotating another 180° CCW (total 270°)
Phase 3: Cecum descends from right upper abdomen to right lower abdomen
Failed rotation: Malrotation + midgut volvulus → cecum midline, SMA may twist → neonate with acute bilious emesis, "whirlpool" sign on ultrasound, surgical emergency (Ladd procedure)
LO 3 — Pectinate Line (5-Column Comparison)
What are the five critical differences above vs. below the pectinate line?
Answer
ABOVE pectinate line: SMA/IMA · Superior rectal vein (portal circulation) · Internal iliac nodes · Visceral innervation (no pain from hemorrhoids/fissures) · Columnar epithelium
BELOW pectinate line: Inferior rectal artery · Inferior rectal vein (caval circulation) · Inguinal nodes · Somatic innervation (painful external hemorrhoids, fissures) · Stratified squamous epithelium
Clinical: Pectinate line is embryologic/vascular/innervation boundary; NOT the colonic watershed (that's at splenic flexure)
LO 4 — NCC Migration + MOANS C
Where do enteric neural crest cells originate, how do they migrate, and what can go wrong (MOANS C)?
Answer
Origin: Vagal neural crest cells (occipital somites 1–7) + sacral NCC (contributes to hindgut)
Migration: Vagal NCC migrate craniocaudally along entire GI tract (cranial→caudal), guided by GDNF (glial-derived neurotrophic factor) → RET receptor signaling
MOANS C (RET loss-of-function): Multiple endocrine neoplasia 2 · Oral mucosa · Axonal hypergangliosis · Nodules + Neurofibromas · Somatic mutations → Medullary thyroid carcinoma
Hirschsprung: RET LOF (loss-of-function) → NCC fail to migrate → absent ganglia in distal segment
LO 4 — Hirschsprung Disease Pathogenesis
What is the molecular defect, where does the aganglionic segment occur, and why does this cause obstruction?
Answer
Defect: RET LOF (loss-of-function mutation) → NCC migration failure in distal gut
Aganglionic segment: DISTAL colon (usually rectosigmoid; can extend higher)
Pathophysiology: Aganglionic segment cannot relax (no ganglia → no parasympathetic inhibitory neurons) → tonic contraction → obstruction
Proximal segment: Dilates (normal ganglia, but obstructed) → "transition zone"
Diagnosis: Biopsy of NARROW (aganglionic) segment shows absent ganglia; not the dilated proximal segment
LO 5 — Pyloric Stenosis
Presentation, age of onset, key laboratory findings, and pathophysiology?
Answer
Presentation: Infant 2–8 weeks old with nonbilious, projectile vomiting after feeding; "olive" mass palpable in epigastrium
Pathophysiology: Hypertrophy + hyperplasia of pyloric smooth muscle → gastric outlet obstruction
Labs: Hypokalemic, hypochloremic, metabolic alkalosis (loss of HCl in vomitus)
Imaging: Ultrasound shows thickened pyloric muscle (wall >3 mm)
Treatment: Fluid/electrolyte repletion FIRST, then pyloromyotomy (Ramstedt procedure)
LO 5 — Omphalocele vs. Gastroschisis
How do these two defects differ in location, coverage, and associated anomalies?
Answer
Omphalocele: Midline defect · Covered by peritoneum + amnion (sac) · Associated with Beckwith-Wiedemann, trisomy 13/18 · Gradual repair (silo, then closure)
Gastroschisis: Right of midline umbilicus · NO sac (exposed bowel) · NOT genetic · Acute surgical closure required
Key: Sac = omphalocele; no sac = gastroschisis · Location helps distinguish (midline vs. right)
LO 5 — Meckel Diverticulum (Rule of 2s)
What is Meckel diverticulum, the classic presentation, and how is it diagnosed?
Answer
Rule of 2s: 2% of population · 2 feet from ileocecal valve · 2 inches long · 2 types of ectopic tissue (gastric + pancreatic) · 2:1 male:female · 2% have complications
Presentation: Painless rectal bleeding (ectopic gastric mucosa secretes HCl → ileal ulceration)
Diagnosis: Tc-99m pertechnetate scan (technetium concentrates in ectopic gastric mucosa) — "Meckel's scan"
Treatment: Surgical diverticulectomy if bleeding
LO 5 — Biliary Atresia
Presentation, diagnostic workup, and what is the Kasai procedure?
Answer
Presentation: Neonate with prolonged jaundice >2 weeks · Direct hyperbilirubinemia · Acholic stools (pale/clay-colored, no bile) · Dark urine
Workup: Elevated direct bili + gamma-GT; HIDA scan shows uptake by liver but NO excretion into duodenum
Pathophysiology: Fibro-obliteration of extrahepatic bile ducts (etiology unknown)
Kasai procedure: Hepatoportoenterostomy — connects hepatic porta directly to small bowel (bypasses atretic ducts) · Success rates vary; may delay transplant or allow long-term function
LO 5 — Jejunal Atresia (Apple-Peel)
What causes jejunal atresia, and why is "apple-peel" volvulus more common in proximal jejunum?
Answer
Cause: SMA compromise in utero (vascular accident) → segment dies, leaving two blind pouches
Apple-peel volvulus: Proximal jejunum rotates around a small mesentery attachment (looks like apple peel when unrolled)
Presentation: Neonate with bilious vomiting, absent/delayed meconium
Imaging: Multiple dilated small bowel loops; "apple-peel" appearance on surgery
Treatment: Surgical anastomosis of blind pouches; intestinal length often reduced → short bowel risk
LO 5 — Congenital Diaphragmatic Hernia (CDH)
Most common location, presentation, and why does CDH cause pulmonary problems?
Answer
Location: Left > right (Bochdalek foramen on posterolateral diaphragm)
Presentation: Newborn with respiratory distress, cyanosis, scaphoid (hollowed) abdomen, bowel sounds in chest
Imaging: Bowel loops visible in thorax on CXR; confirm with antenatal ultrasound
Pathophysiology: Herniated abdominal organs compress lung during development → pulmonary hypoplasia (underdev of alveoli, airways) + pulmonary hypertension
Treatment: Gentle ventilation + surgical repair; high mortality if severe pulmonary hypoplasia present at birth
LO 5 — Tracheoesophageal Fistula (TEF) Types
What are the five types of TEF/EA, and which is most common?
Answer
Type A (85% — most common): Esophageal atresia + DISTAL TEF (proximal blind pouch; lower esophagus connects to trachea)
Type B: Isolated esophageal atresia (no fistula)
Type C: H-type TEF (no atresia, just fistula) — recurrent aspiration pneumonia, choking with feeds
Type D: Proximal TEF + atresia (rare)
Type E (rare): Double fistula (proximal + distal)
Presentation Type A: Excessive drooling, choking/cyanosis with first feed, "unable to pass NG tube"
LO 5 — Imperforate Anus + VACTERL
What is VACTERL, and how does imperforate anus fit? What is an H-type anorectal fistula?
Answer
VACTERL association: Vertebral · Anal · Cardiac · Tracheoesophageal · Renal · Limb anomalies (≥3 = VACTERL)
Imperforate anus: Failure of urorectal septum to divide cloaca → anal canal not patent
Types: High (above levator ani) vs. low (below levator)
H-type anorectal fistula: Fistula between rectum + urethra/vagina (shaped like H on cross-section); infant passes meconium through urine or fistula opening; requires surgical correction
Management: Screen all VACTERL patients for associated anomalies

Question 1 of 10 — Mesentery / Anatomy
A surgical resident is reviewing an anatomy case about peritoneal attachments. Which of the following is a derivative of the VENTRAL mesentery?
ARoot of mesentery for small intestine
BHepatoduodenal ligament (contains portal triad)
CTransverse mesocolon
DMesorectum
ESigmoid mesocolon
B — Hepatoduodenal ligament. The ventral mesentery exists ONLY in the foregut and is divided by the expanding liver into: (1) falciform ligament (between liver and anterior abdominal wall); (2) hepatogastric ligament (lesser omentum between liver and stomach); (3) hepatoduodenal ligament (between liver and duodenum, containing portal triad: portal vein, hepatic artery, common bile duct). All other options (A, C, D, E) are dorsal mesentery derivatives that span the entire GI tract or retroperitoneal folds.
Question 2 of 10 — Embryology / GI Arteries
A 7-week-old embryo undergoes normal GI development. Which of the following structures is supplied by the SUPERIOR MESENTERIC ARTERY?
AStomach and first two portions of duodenum
BDistal colon and rectum
CCecum, appendix, small intestine, and proximal two-thirds of colon
DEverything distal to the pylorus
EPancreas (dorsal and ventral buds only)
C — Midgut (SMA territory). The embryo is divided into three vascular domains: (1) Foregut (celiac) → pharynx through duodenum (D1–D2) + liver, pancreatic dorsal bud; (2) Midgut (SMA) → D3–D4 through proximal 2/3 colon; (3) Hindgut (IMA) → distal 1/3 colon through anal canal above pectinate line. Choice C captures the entire midgut domain.
Question 3 of 10 — Pancreatic Development
During week 7 of gestation, the dorsal and ventral pancreatic buds fuse. Which structures are derived from the VENTRAL pancreatic bud?
ABody, tail, and all of the head
BUncinate process and inferior/posterior head
CEntire pancreas (dorsal bud regresses)
DOnly the main pancreatic duct
EThe pancreatic islets (alpha + beta cells)
B — Uncinate + inferior/posterior head. The ventral bud originates from the hepatic diverticulum and contributes the uncinate process and the inferior/posterior portion of the pancreatic head. The dorsal bud (from dorsal duodenum) becomes the body, tail, and superior/anterior head. The two buds fuse in week 7, and their ducts also fuse: dorsal → main duct (Wirsung); ventral → accessory duct (Santorini). Clinically, if the ventral bud encircles the duodenum, annular pancreas results → duodenal obstruction.
Question 4 of 10 — Midgut Rotation
An embryo at week 10 of development is undergoing the physiologic midgut herniation and rotation. The midgut is rotating around which fixed point?
AThe pylorus
BThe superior mesenteric artery (SMA)
CThe ligament of Treitz
DThe ileocecal valve
EThe root of the dorsal mesentery
B — The SMA. During weeks 6–12, the midgut herniates out of the abdomen and rotates 270° counterclockwise around the superior mesenteric artery (the vascular axis and anatomic pivot). Phase 1 (wk 6): herniation + 90° rotation. Phase 2 (wks 10–12): return to abdomen + additional 180° rotation. The cecum, which starts at the midline, descends to the right lower abdomen as the rotation completes. Failure of this rotation leads to malrotation and potential midgut volvulus.
Question 5 of 10 — Pectinate Line / Posterior Boundary
Which anatomic and physiologic structures are found ABOVE the pectinate line of the anal canal?
AInferior rectal artery, inferior rectal vein, internal pudendal nodes, somatic innervation
BSuperior rectal artery, portal venous drainage, internal iliac lymph nodes, visceral innervation
CMixed vasculature (SMA + IMA), variable innervation
DTransitional epithelium (like bladder)
EStratified squamous epithelium with somatic sensation
B — Above pectinate line. The pectinate line marks three embryologic/vascular/innervation boundaries: (1) Above = SMA/superior rectal; (2) Below = inferior rectal; (3) Above = portal circulation; (4) Below = caval circulation; (5) Above = visceral (no pain sensation); (6) Below = somatic (painful hemorrhoids/fissures). This is a high-yield comparison — the pectinate line is NOT the colonic watershed (that's at the splenic flexure, SMA/IMA junction).
Question 6 of 10 — Enteric Neural Crest (ENS) / RET Signaling
Neural crest cells colonize the entire GI tract during embryonic development via guidance by GDNF (glial-derived neurotrophic factor) signaling through the RET receptor. Which pathologic finding would result from RET LOSS-OF-FUNCTION?
AExcessive ganglion cell proliferation in the distal bowel
BFailure of neural crest cell migration → absent ganglia in distal segments
CMedullary thyroid carcinoma and pheochromocytoma (MEN2)
DIncreased GDNF expression throughout the gut
EMucosal hypertrophy and increased acid secretion
B — RET LOF → NCC migration failure → Hirschsprung disease. RET is a receptor tyrosine kinase essential for GDNF-mediated neural crest cell migration. Loss-of-function mutations prevent NCC migration, leaving distal gut aganglionic. RET gain-of-function mutations (choice C) cause MEN2 with medullary thyroid cancer, pheochromocytoma, and primary hyperparathyroidism — but NOT Hirschsprung (which is LOF). The balance is critical: LOF → agangliosis; GOF → excessive innervation risks.
Question 7 of 10 — Gastroschisis vs. Omphalocele
A prenatal ultrasound identifies an abdominal wall defect in a 20-week fetus. The defect is located directly to the RIGHT of the umbilicus, and there is NO covering membrane. Which diagnosis and associated findings are most consistent?
AOmphalocele; genetic syndrome; covered by peritoneum and amnion
BGastroschisis; NOT genetic; no sac; exposed bowel; acute closure required
CTrisomy 21 association; defect at midline
DBeckwith-Wiedemann syndrome with covered defect
ECongenital diaphragmatic hernia with bowel in chest
B — Gastroschisis. The key differences: (1) Gastroschisis = lateral (right of umbilicus), NO sac, NOT genetic; (2) Omphalocele = midline, covered by peritoneum + amnion, associated with Beckwith-Wiedemann, trisomy 13/18. The "no covering membrane" is the diagnostic clue for gastroschisis. Both require surgical repair, but omphalocele may be staged (silo first), while gastroschisis often requires acute closure.
Question 8 of 10 — Meckel Diverticulum
A 3-year-old boy presents with painless rectal bleeding. Imaging with technetium-99m pertechnetate (Meckel's scan) shows focal uptake in the lower abdomen. At surgery, a diverticulum is found 18 inches from the ileocecal valve. Which component of Meckel diverticulum is responsible for the bleeding?
AMeckel diverticulum is always symptomatic and requires resection
BEctopic gastric mucosa secretes HCl → ulcerates adjacent ileum → painless bleeding
CEctopic pancreatic tissue causes inflammation and bleeding
DThe diverticulum itself is inflamed like appendicitis
EPortal hypertension from abnormal mesentery
B — Ectopic gastric mucosa. Meckel diverticulum follows the "rule of 2s": 2% population, 2 feet from ileocecal, 2 inches long, 2 types of ectopic tissue (gastric + pancreatic). The ectopic gastric mucosa is responsible for bleeding: it secretes HCl → ulcerates the adjacent ileal mucosa → painless rectal bleeding (cardinal feature). Technetium-99m pertechnetate concentrates in gastric mucosa (whether ectopic or normal), making the Meckel's scan the diagnostic test of choice.
Question 9 of 10 — Duodenal Atresia / Down Syndrome
A newborn with Down syndrome (trisomy 21) presents on day 1 of life with projectile vomiting and abdominal distension. Abdominal X-ray shows a "double bubble" sign with no distal air. Which embryologic process is disrupted to cause this finding?
AMidgut rotation (malrotation + volvulus)
BFailure of the pyloric sphincter to develop
CFailure of duodenal recanalization (solid → hollow transformation)
DExcessive villus development causing intussusception
EAnnular pancreas constriction of the duodenum
C — Failed recanalization. The duodenum is initially a solid cord (wks 4–8), then must hollow out (recanalize) during wks 8–12. Failure → duodenal atresia. The "double bubble" sign = gas in dilated proximal duodenum + gastric bubble; NO distal air (obstruction). Duodenal atresia is strongly associated with Down syndrome (trisomy 21). Management: fluid/electrolyte correction, then duodenoduodenostomy bypass. (Note: Annular pancreas is similar but allows some flow; atresia is complete blockage.)
Question 10 of 10 — Congenital Diaphragmatic Hernia / Pulmonary Hypoplasia
A newborn has a left-sided congenital diaphragmatic hernia (CDH) with herniation of abdominal organs into the chest. Which pathologic consequence of CDH leads to the primary cause of mortality in severely affected infants?
ABowel obstruction from compression by lung tissue
BPulmonary hypoplasia (underdevelopment of alveoli + airways) → respiratory insufficiency + pulmonary hypertension
CGastroesophageal reflux from displaced stomach
DSepsis from intestinal perforation
ECardiac compression and arrhythmia
B — Pulmonary hypoplasia. The herniated viscera compress developing lung tissue in utero → underdeveloped alveoli, airways, and pulmonary vasculature. This leads to respiratory insufficiency and pulmonary hypertension (the primary cause of mortality). CDH is left > right (Bochdalek foramen is posterolateral). Prenatal diagnosis allows delivery at a hospital with extracorporeal membrane oxygenation (ECMO) capability. Early gentle ventilation (avoid barotrauma) and surgical repair are key; prognosis depends on the degree of pulmonary hypoplasia.

Vignette 4 of 10 — Malrotation + Midgut Volvulus
A 2-week-old infant presents with acute-onset bilious emesis and inconsolable crying (severe pain). Abdominal exam shows guarding. Abdominal ultrasound shows the superior mesenteric vessels arranged in an abnormal "whirlpool sign" with SMA positioned to the RIGHT of SMV. No duodenal gas is seen.
AMalrotation with midgut volvulus — surgical emergency
BDuodenal atresia
CPyloric stenosis
DNecrotizing enterocolitis (NEC)
EJejunal atresia
A — Malrotation + midgut volvulus. Buzzwords: neonate with bilious emesis, acute pain, whirlpool sign (midgut twisted around SMA), SMA right of SMV (reversed). Failed midgut rotation (normal: 270° CCW around SMA) → ligament of Treitz not fixed → SMA/SMV can twist → arterial/venous compromise → necrosis. SURGICAL EMERGENCY (Ladd procedure: detorsion, division of Ladd bands, appendectomy). If bilious emesis in neonate: think malrotation until proven otherwise.
Vignette 5 of 10 — Meckel Diverticulum
A 5-year-old boy presents with painless rectal bleeding (bright red blood per rectum). Hemoglobin dropped from 13 to 9 g/dL. CT shows no masses. Technetium-99m pertechnetate scan (Meckel's scan) shows focal uptake in the lower abdomen distant from the stomach. At surgery, a diverticulum is found 15 inches from the ileocecal valve.
AMeckel diverticulum with ectopic gastric mucosa bleeding
BInflammatory bowel disease (Crohn or ulcerative colitis)
CJuvenile polyp
DIntussusception
EIntestinal tuberculosis
A — Meckel diverticulum. Buzzwords: painless rectal bleeding (cardinal feature), Tc-99m pertechnetate scan POSITIVE, 2 feet from ileocecal valve. Rule of 2s: 2% population, 2 types of tissue (gastric + pancreatic ectopia), 2 feet location, 2 inches long, 2:1 M:F, 2% symptomatic. Ectopic gastric mucosa secretes HCl → ulcerates adjacent ileal mucosa → painless bleeding (NOT painful like IBD). Technetium concentrates in gastric mucosa (ectopic or normal).
Vignette 6 of 10 — Gastroschisis vs. Omphalocele
Prenatal ultrasound at 19 weeks gestation identifies an abdominal wall defect. The defect is located to the RIGHT of the umbilicus, and there is NO covering membrane. Bowel loops are directly exposed to amniotic fluid. Chromosomal testing is normal. The pregnancy is otherwise uncomplicated.
AGastroschisis (right, no sac, not genetic)
BOmphalocele (midline, covered, genetic)
CBeckwith-Wiedemann syndrome
DTrisomy 13 or 18
ECongenital diaphragmatic hernia
A — Gastroschisis. Key distinction: location right of umbilicus, NO sac (exposed bowel), NOT genetic (normal karyotype likely). Omphalocele = midline, covered by peritoneum + amnion, associated with Beckwith-Wiedemann, Down, trisomy 13/18. Gastroschisis = lateral, bare bowel, no association. Both need surgical repair; gastroschisis requires acute closure; omphalocele may be staged with a silo first.
Vignette 7 of 10 — Biliary Atresia
A 3-week-old infant has persistent jaundice (total bili 8, direct bili 5 mg/dL), pale/acholic stools (no bile pigment), dark urine, and hepatomegaly. Ultrasound shows a normal gallbladder and patent biliary ducts. HIDA scan shows hepatic uptake but NO excretion into duodenum. Biopsy is pending.
ABiliary atresia (fibro-obliteration of extrahepatic ducts)
BNeonatal hepatitis
CCholedochal cyst
DAlpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency
EHemolytic disease of the newborn (Rh incompatibility)
A — Biliary atresia. Buzzwords: jaundice >2 weeks, direct hyperbilirubinemia, acholic stools, HIDA uptake but NO excretion. Etiology unknown; fibro-obliteration of extrahepatic bile ducts → no bile flow. Management: Kasai hepatoportoenterostomy (connects porta hepatis to jejunum, bypassing atretic ducts) — delays or prevents transplant need. Success rates ~50% for long-term function without transplant.
Vignette 8 of 10 — Congenital Diaphragmatic Hernia (CDH)
A newborn is delivered at term and immediately shows respiratory distress, cyanosis, and a scaphoid (sunken) abdomen. Bowel sounds are heard in the chest. Chest X-ray shows bowel loops in the left hemithorax and mediastinal shift. Prenatal ultrasound had shown this defect at 28 weeks.
ACongenital diaphragmatic hernia (left > right)
BEsophageal atresia with distal TEF
CTension pneumothorax
DMeconium aspiration syndrome
EGastroschisis
A — Congenital diaphragmatic hernia. Buzzwords: respiratory distress at birth, scaphoid abdomen (no abdominal organs), bowel sounds in chest, left side (Bochdalek foramen). Herniated abdominal organs compress developing lung → pulmonary hypoplasia (underdev alveoli + airways) + pulmonary hypertension. Mortality depends on degree of lung hypoplasia. Management: gentle ventilation (avoid barotrauma), ECMO if available, surgical repair (NOT emergency). Prenatal diagnosis allows tertiary care delivery.
Vignette 9 of 10 — Tracheoesophageal Fistula (TEF) / Esophageal Atresia (EA)
A newborn at 2 hours of life begins excessive drooling, choking with the first feeding attempt, and becomes cyanotic. An attempt to pass a nasogastric tube fails — the tube cannot advance past 10 cm into the esophagus. Chest X-ray shows the NG tube in a blind pouch and air in the stomach/bowel (distal gas present).
AEA + distal TEF (Type A, 85% of cases)
BIsolated esophageal atresia (no fistula)
CH-type TEF (fistula only, no atresia)
DPyloric stenosis
ETracheomalacia
A — EA + distal TEF. Buzzwords: excessive drooling, choking with feeds, cyanosis, unable to pass NG tube, air in stomach (distal gas). This is the most common type (Type A, 85%): proximal esophagus is blind, lower esophagus connects to trachea. The distal TEF allows air into stomach → distal gas seen on X-ray. H-type (C) would have no blind pouch and would present later with aspiration pneumonia. Type B (isolated EA) has no distal gas.
Vignette 10 of 10 — Imperforate Anus + VACTERL Association
A newborn with imperforate anus undergoes physical exam. Congenital anomalies are identified: vertebral abnormality, cardiac murmur, TEF, renal hypoplasia, and limb malformation. The infant has a fistula between rectum and urethra.
AVACTERL association (≥3 anomalies, including H-type anorectal fistula)
BDown syndrome (trisomy 21)
CEdwards syndrome (trisomy 18)
DPatau syndrome (trisomy 13)
ESingle gene mutation (autosomal recessive)
A — VACTERL association. VACTERL: Vertebral, Anal, Cardiac, Tracheoesophageal, Renal, Limb (≥3 = VACTERL diagnosis). The patient has 5 of 6 components. Imperforate anus = urorectal septum failed to divide cloaca. H-type anorectal fistula = fistula between rectum and urethra/vagina (shaped like H in cross-section); infant may pass meconium through urine or fistula opening. Requires surgical correction + screening for other VACTERL components.
Step 1 Traps: Common false statements that appear plausible but are incorrect. Choose the correct answer to reveal the trap explanation.
Trap 1 of 10
Gastroschisis has a protective sac covering the exposed viscera, just like omphalocele.
FALSE — this is a critical distinction. Gastroschisis = NO sac (bare, exposed bowel). Omphalocele = covered by peritoneum + amnion (has a sac). Gastroschisis is right of umbilicus, NOT genetic; omphalocele is midline, associated with genetic syndromes (Beckwith-Wiedemann, trisomy 13/18). The lack of sac in gastroschisis is the KEY distinguishing feature on ultrasound and at birth.
Trap 2 of 10
In Hirschsprung disease, the DILATED segment of colon is the aganglionic abnormal portion.
FALSE — this is a classic exam trap. The NARROW segment is aganglionic (abnormal). The DILATED segment proximal is actually NORMAL (has intact ganglia) — it is simply dilated from stool accumulation above the obstruction. When obtaining a rectal biopsy, ALWAYS biopsy the NARROW (aganglionic) segment, NOT the dilated one. This distinction appears on every Step 1 exam.
Trap 3 of 10
The ventral mesentery extends throughout the entire GI tract from esophagus to anal canal.
FALSE — ventral mesentery exists ONLY in the foregut (esophagus through duodenum). The dorsal mesentery spans the ENTIRE gut (esophagus through anal canal). Students often confuse the two. Remember: the liver expands into the ventral mesentery in the foregut, dividing it into falciform ligament and hepatic ligaments. Midgut and hindgut have ONLY dorsal mesentery.
Trap 4 of 10
Meckel diverticulum causes PAINFUL rectal bleeding, similar to inflammatory bowel disease.
FALSE — Meckel bleeding is PAINLESS (cardinal feature). Ectopic gastric mucosa in the diverticulum secretes HCl → ulcerates adjacent ileum → painless bleeding. This painless presentation helps distinguish it from IBD (Crohn) which presents with painful diarrhea. Key: painless rectal bleeding + Tc-99m pertechnetate uptake = Meckel.
Trap 5 of 10
RET gain-of-function mutations cause Hirschsprung disease.
FALSE — RET loss-of-function (LOF) causes Hirschsprung. RET gain-of-function (GOF) causes MEN2 (medullary thyroid cancer, pheochromocytoma, primary hyperparathyroidism). This is a commonly tested distinction: LOF = NCC migration failure = agangliosis = Hirschsprung; GOF = increased neuron proliferation + medullary thyroid risk = MEN2.
Trap 6 of 10
The pectinate line marks the colonic watershed area between SMA and IMA blood supply.
FALSE — the pectinate line marks the embryologic/vascular/innervation boundary (superior rectal above, inferior rectal below) but is NOT the watershed. The actual colonic WATERSHED is at the SPLENIC FLEXURE (junction of SMA and IMA territories). Ischemic colitis commonly affects the splenic flexure, not the pectinate line. Know the difference: pectinate = anorectal boundary; watershed = splenic flexure.
Trap 7 of 10
The pancreatic tail is retroperitoneal and never moves with the stomach during rotation.
FALSE — the pancreatic tail (derived from dorsal bud) is INTRAPERITONEAL and sits in the greater omentum, where it CAN move. Only the head, body (except tip), and uncinate process are retroperitoneal. The tail extends into the hilum of the spleen. This distinction is tested because students often assume "pancreas = retroperitoneal" — but the tail breaks that rule.
Trap 8 of 10
Double Bubble sign on X-ray indicates malrotation with midgut volvulus, not duodenal atresia.
FALSE — Double Bubble specifically indicates DUODENAL ATRESIA (complete obstruction = two bubbles of gas, no distal air). Malrotation/volvulus would have different findings (whirlpool on ultrasound, SMA right of SMV). Both present with bilious emesis in a neonate, but the imaging and embryologic defect differ. Students confuse these presentations because both are neonatal emergencies with bilious vomiting.
Trap 9 of 10
Recanalization failure causes malrotation; failure of rotation causes atresia.
FALSE — these are OPPOSITE. Recanalization failure (duodenum remains solid instead of hollowing out) → ATRESIA. Failure of midgut rotation (abnormal fixation of mesentery, abnormal vascular relationships) → MALROTATION. Both are embryologic failures but at different stages and with different mechanisms. Duodenal atresia = weeks 8–12 (recanalization). Midgut rotation = weeks 6–12 (herniation + rotation).
Trap 10 of 10
Annular pancreas is a congenital anomaly where the ventral bud completely encircles the duodenum with no gap.
FALSE — the clinical trap is that in annular pancreas, there IS often a small gap that permits some passage of chyme. This allows distal gas (unlike duodenal atresia). Annular pancreas can present with duodenal obstruction, but management is critical: NEVER simply divide the ring (divides pancreatic tissue → fistula risk). Always perform duodenoduodenostomy to bypass the constriction.

High-Yield Testing Notes (Optional): Key embryology patterns tested on Step 1, organized by concept and buzzword cluster.
KEY CONCEPT Embryologic Timeline → Clinical Presentation
GI12 requires understanding WHEN embryologic defects occur so you can predict clinical timing:

Wk 4–8 (foregut/hindgut start): Pyloric stenosis presents 2–8 wks old (smooth muscle hypertrophy).
Wk 6–12 (midgut rotation/duc recanalization): Malrotation 2–6 wks old; duodenal atresia day 1 neonate.
Wk 4–12 (NCC migration): Hirschsprung neonate failure to pass meconium (RET LOF blocks migration).

This timing pattern is tested with "which presentation matches this embryologic defect?" vignettes.
CLASSIC TRAP The Hirschsprung Narrow vs. Dilated Trap
Step 1's #1 Hirschsprung trap: students wrongly think the DILATED segment is abnormal. WRONG — the NARROW segment is aganglionic (absent ganglia on biopsy). The dilated segment is actually NORMAL gut distended by stool backup. Always biopsy the NARROW segment. This distinction appears in every board review and exam.
TESTING RET Mutations — LOF vs. GOF Changes Phenotype
RET Loss-of-Function: NCC migration fails → Hirschsprung (aganglionic colon).
RET Gain-of-Function: Excess neurons + MEN2 (thyroid cancer, pheochromocytoma).

The same gene gives opposite outcomes depending on mutation type. This is a high-yield testing concept because it shows how molecular biology determines clinical phenotype.
BUZZWORD CLUSTER Congenital Abdominal Wall Defects
Gastroschisis: Right lateral, NO sac, not genetic. Acute closure needed.
Omphalocele: Midline, covered by peritoneum/amnion, genetic (Beckwith-Wiedemann, trisomy).

Exam pattern: shows an ultrasound of an abdominal wall defect → asks to differentiate by location + sac presence. Know the differences cold.
KEY FACTS The "Rule of 2s" and Diagnostic Tests
Meckel Rule of 2s: 2% population, 2 feet from ileocecal, 2 inches long, 2 types tissue, 2:1 M:F.
Diagnostic Gold Standards:
• Hirschsprung → rectal biopsy (absent ganglia in narrow segment)
• Duodenal atresia → double bubble X-ray
• Meckel → Tc-99m pertechnetate scan
• Biliary atresia → HIDA scan (no excretion)
• Malrotation → whirlpool ultrasound sign

These diagnostic tests appear as "what's the next step?" board questions.
Clinical Correlates (Optional): Embryology-to-clinical connections and high-yield correlations for Step 1 embryologic defects.
Embryology → Pathology
From Embryonic Defect to Newborn Presentation
Understanding embryologic timing helps predict presentation:

Weeks 4–8 (early foregut): Pyloric stenosis (smooth muscle dysfunction) → 2–8 weeks old with projectile vomiting.

Weeks 6–12 (midgut rotation/recanalization): Malrotation (failed rotation) presents 2–6 weeks old; duodenal atresia (failed recanalization) presents day 1 neonate.

Weeks 4–12 (NCC migration): Hirschsprung (RET LOF) → NCC migration failure → neonate cannot pass meconium.

Week 7 (pancreatic fusion): Annular pancreas (ventral encircles duodenum) → varies in severity; biliary atresia (fibro-obliteration) → jaundice >2 weeks.
RET Signaling
RET Mutations Determine Phenotype — LOF vs. GOF
RET Loss-of-Function (LOF): NCC migration failure → absent ganglia in distal gut → Hirschsprung disease (aganglionic megacolon). Biopsy of narrow (aganglionic) segment shows no ganglia in myenteric or submucosal plexuses.

RET Gain-of-Function (GOF): Excessive neuron proliferation → MEN2 syndrome (medullary thyroid carcinoma, pheochromocytoma, primary hyperparathyroidism). NOT associated with Hirschsprung (which is LOF).

This LOF vs. GOF distinction is high-yield on Step 1 exams because both involve RET but produce opposite GI phenotypes.
Diagnosis
Key Diagnostic Findings by Anomaly
Each embryologic anomaly has a "gold standard" diagnostic test:

Hirschsprung: Rectal biopsy of NARROW (aganglionic) segment → absent ganglion cells (both myenteric + submucosal plexuses).

Duodenal Atresia: Double Bubble sign on plain X-ray (stomach + proximal duodenum); strong association with Down syndrome.

Meckel: Tc-99m pertechnetate scan (uptake in ectopic gastric mucosa = "Meckel's scan").

Biliary Atresia: HIDA scan shows hepatic uptake but NO excretion into duodenum; biopsy shows fibro-obliteration of extrahepatic ducts.

Malrotation + Volvulus: Ultrasound shows "whirlpool sign" (SMA/SMV twisted); emergency Ladd procedure required.
