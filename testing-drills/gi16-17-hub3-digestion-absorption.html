Q1 · Small Intestine Anatomy · Villus Identification
A 28-year-old woman undergoing diagnostic endoscopy for chronic diarrhea has small intestinal biopsies taken. On light microscopy, the pathologist observes prominent finger-like projections extending into the intestinal lumen, with an outer layer of tall columnar epithelial cells containing abundant mitochondria. Within the apex of each projection, the pathologist identifies capillaries and a central chylolacteal. Which structure is the chylolacteal most critical for absorbing?
AWater-soluble vitamins and monosaccharides
BSodium ions and potassium via active transport
CFat-soluble vitamins and chylomicrons
DAmino acids and dipeptides via PepT1
ECobalamin (vitamin B12) via IF-mediated uptake
Correct: C — Chylolacteal absorbs fat-soluble vitamins and chylomicrons
The chylolacteal is the central lacteal lymphatic vessel at the core of each small intestinal villus. It is the initial vessel for lymphatic absorption of lipids, fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K), and chylomicrons. These hydrophobic products cannot be directly absorbed into blood capillaries and must enter the lymphatic system via the chylolacteal for transport to the thoracic duct. The capillaries in the villus handle water-soluble nutrients (glucose, amino acids, monosaccharides).

A— Monosaccharides are absorbed via capillaries, not lacteals.
B— Electrolytes are actively transported across epithelial cells into capillaries.
D— PepT1 is an apical transporter for di/tripeptides; products exit via capillaries.
E— B12 is absorbed at the terminal ileum via IF receptors into blood.
Q2 · Lipid Absorption · Unstirred Water Layer
A 56-year-old man with cystic fibrosis reports steatorrhea (fatty stools) despite adequate pancreatic supplementation. His fat absorption remains suboptimal. A research study measures the diffusion gradient across the intestinal epithelium using stable isotope tracers. The investigators identify that the rate-limiting step for lipid absorption is NOT the enzymatic breakdown of triglycerides into fatty acids and monoglycerides, but rather something slower. What physiologic barrier accounts for this?
AUnstirred water layer (diffusion through aqueous barrier adjacent to epithelium)
BImpaired colipase activity limiting pancreatic lipase access to fat
CReduced intestinal mucosal mass due to villous atrophy
DDeficiency of apolipoproteins blocking chylomicron assembly
EBacterial overgrowth producing lipase-inhibitory substances
Correct: A — Unstirred water layer is rate-limiting for lipid diffusion
The unstirred water layer is a thin aqueous boundary layer adjacent to the intestinal epithelial surface that is not mixed by bulk fluid movement. Fatty acids and monoglycerides must diffuse through this layer to reach the epithelial cell membrane. This diffusion step is often rate-limiting for lipid absorption, especially when lipase activity or micelle formation is adequate. Bile salts enhance absorption by micellarizing lipids, which facilitates their movement through the unstirred layer.

B— Colipase is supplemented; not the issue here.
C— CF does not primarily cause villous atrophy.
D— CF has normal apolipoprotein synthesis.
E— Not a typical CF complication affecting lipid absorption.
Q3 · Digestive Enzymes · Source & Substrate Specificity
A 42-year-old man with chronic pancreatitis undergoes assessment of pancreatic function. His 72-hour fecal fat is elevated at 18 g/day, and serum levels of multiple pancreatic enzymes are diminished. Which pancreatic enzyme is most directly responsible for breaking down dietary fat into fatty acids and monoglycerides, and therefore its deficiency best explains his steatorrhea?
ATrypsin (serine protease cleaving peptide bonds)
BPancreatic lipase (hydrolyzing triglycerides at 1,3 positions)
CAmylase (breaking α-1,4 glycosidic bonds in starch)
DEnterokinase (activating trypsinogen to trypsin)
EColipase (enhancing lipase activity via cofactor binding)
Correct: B — Pancreatic lipase hydrolyzes triglycerides to fatty acids and monoglycerides
Pancreatic lipase is the primary lipase secreted by the pancreas that cleaves triglycerides, predominantly at the 1 and 3 positions, yielding two free fatty acids and one 2-monoglyceride. Its deficiency is the primary cause of lipid malabsorption (steatorrhea) in pancreatic insufficiency. Colipase is a cofactor that helps pancreatic lipase access triglycerides in the presence of bile salts.

A— Trypsin digests proteins, not fats.
C— Amylase breaks carbohydrates.
D— Enterokinase is an intestinal enzyme activating trypsinogen; doesn't directly digest fat.
E— Colipase is a cofactor, not the primary enzyme.
Q4 · Carbohydrate Digestion · Pancreatic Amylase Product
A 61-year-old woman with poorly controlled diabetes presents with postprandial abdominal bloating and diarrhea. Her fecal osmolality is high (400 mOsm/L). When she consumes a starch-rich meal, saliva and pancreatic amylase break starch down to shorter oligosaccharides and disaccharides. Which disaccharide is the PRIMARY product of pancreatic amylase action on starch?
AMaltose (glucose-α-1,4-glucose)
BLactose (galactose-β-1,4-glucose)
CSucrose (glucose-α-1,2-fructose)
DTrehalose (glucose-α-1,1-glucose)
ECellobiose (glucose-β-1,4-glucose)
Correct: A — Maltose is the primary disaccharide product of amylase
Pancreatic amylase cleaves α-1,4-glycosidic bonds in starch, producing the disaccharide maltose (and small amounts of α-limit dextrin, which has α-1,6 branches). Maltose is then further broken down by brush border maltase into glucose units for absorption.

B— Lactose is a dietary disaccharide (not a starch product); requires lactase.
C— Sucrose is a dietary disaccharide; amylase cannot cleave it (different bond).
D, E— Not products of amylase on starch.
Q5 · Monosaccharide Transport · Apical Transporter Mechanisms
A 19-year-old woman reports osmotic diarrhea after consuming fructose in soft drinks and candy. Her symptoms disappear when she avoids fructose. She has normal glucose and galactose absorption. A genetic test reveals a heterozygous mutation in GLUT5 (fructose transporter) causing reduced function. Meanwhile, a 35-year-old man has severe glucose malabsorption despite normal brush border enzymes. His intestinal biopsy shows markedly reduced SGLT1 expression in apical enterocytes. Which statement correctly distinguishes the transporters involved?
ASGLT1 is secondary active transport (Na+/glucose cotransport); GLUT5 is facilitated diffusion (fructose)
BSGLT1 transports fructose; GLUT5 transports glucose via active transport
CBoth are secondary active transporters requiring ATP
DSGLT1 operates on the basolateral membrane; GLUT5 on the apical membrane
EGLUT5 is inhibited by sodium-glucose cotransport
Correct: A — SGLT1 is secondary active (Na+ cotransport); GLUT5 is facilitated diffusion
SGLT1 (sodium-glucose linked transporter 1) is a secondary active transporter coupling glucose (and galactose) uptake to sodium influx down the Na+ gradient maintained by basolateral Na+/K+-ATPase. GLUT5 is a facilitated diffusion transporter specific for fructose; it does not require sodium or ATP. Both are apical transporters. Fructose deficiency explains the patient's fructose malabsorption (osmotic diarrhea).

B— Wrong assignment of substrates and mechanism.
C— GLUT5 does not require ATP.
D— Both are apical; GLUT2 is basolateral (export glucose from enterocyte).
E— GLUT5 operates independently.
Q6 · Indigestible Carbohydrates · Fiber & Cellulose
A 55-year-old woman on a high-fiber diet (focusing on cellulose-rich vegetables) experiences bloating and increased stool frequency. She consumes roughly 50 g of dietary fiber daily. Humans have no enzyme capable of breaking the β-1,4-glycosidic bonds in cellulose, yet ruminant animals (cows, sheep) digest cellulose efficiently via microbial fermentation in their stomachs. Why does the human intestinal microbiota NOT significantly ferment cellulose to absorbable short-chain fatty acids in most individuals?
AThe human colon is too acidic for cellulolytic bacteria
BHumans lack the microbial enzymes required to cleave β-1,4 bonds
CCellulose reaches the colon largely intact; transit time is too brief for significant bacterial fermentation
DBile salts inhibit cellulose-fermenting bacteria
EPancreatic lipase hydrolyzes cellulose in the small intestine
Correct: C — Brief intestinal transit limits cellulose fermentation
While humans have colonic microbiota capable of producing cellulases and fermenting fiber, cellulose transit through the small intestine is rapid (~2–4 hours). Most cellulose reaches the colon largely undigested. Although some bacterial fermentation occurs, producing short-chain fatty acids (acetate, propionate, butyrate), the amount is usually modest because: (1) transit time in the colon is only ~12–24 hours; (2) substrate availability may be limiting. Contrast with ruminants, which retain ingesta in the rumen for many hours, allowing extensive microbial fermentation.

A— The colon is not too acidic; pH ~5.5–7 is suitable.
B— Some humans do harbor cellulolytic bacteria.
D— Bile salts are mostly reabsorbed at terminal ileum.
E— Lipase does not digest cellulose.
Q7 · Lactose Malabsorption · Primary Lactase Deficiency
A 32-year-old woman of East Asian descent presents with osmotic diarrhea, abdominal bloating, and flatulence 30 minutes after consuming a large glass of whole milk. She has no history of gastroenteritis or celiac disease. Hydrogen breath testing after 25 g lactose load shows a rise in exhaled H2 from baseline 8 ppm to 48 ppm within 90 minutes. Which statement best explains both her clinical symptoms and hydrogen breath test findings?
ASecondary lactase deficiency from small intestinal bacterial overgrowth
BPrimary lactase deficiency: undigested lactose osmotically draws water; colonic bacteria ferment lactose producing H2
CLactose intolerance due to allergic reaction to milk proteins
DDisaccharidase brush border deficiency affecting only lactose, not maltose or sucrase
EImpaired SGLT1 function preventing glucose-galactose absorption
Correct: B — Primary lactase deficiency with osmotic diarrhea and bacterial H2 production
Primary lactase deficiency (decline in lactase activity after childhood in genetically predisposed individuals) is the mechanism here. Unabsorbed lactose in the small intestine: (1) acts as an osmotic agent, drawing water into the intestinal lumen → osmotic diarrhea (stops with fasting); (2) reaches the colon where commensal bacteria ferment it → hydrogen production detected on breath test. The positive H2 breath test (>20 ppm rise) confirms lactose malabsorption.

A— This is primary, not secondary deficiency.
C— IgE-mediated milk allergy would cause urticaria/anaphylaxis, not just osmotic diarrhea.
D— Only lactase is typically lost; maltase/sucrase preserved.
E— SGLT1 handles the glucose/galactose products of lactose hydrolysis by lactase.
Q8 · Protease Activation · Enterokinase & Trypsin Cascade
A 7-year-old boy is diagnosed with congenital enterokinase (enteropeptidase) deficiency following recurrent episodes of malnutrition and diarrhea. He has reduced ability to form blood clots and persistent hypoalbuminemia despite adequate dietary protein intake. Which mechanism best explains his protein malabsorption?
ALoss of peptidyl-amino peptidase activity at the brush border
BFailure to convert trypsinogen to trypsin, blocking the protease cascade
CImpaired PepT1 dipeptide transporter expression
DPancreatic elastase deficiency reducing protein digestion
EIncreased intestinal permeability allowing bacterial translocation
Correct: B — Enterokinase converts trypsinogen to trypsin, initiating protease cascade
Enterokinase (enteropeptidase) is the brush border serine protease that cleaves trypsinogen to activate trypsin. Trypsin then activates other pancreatic zymogens (procarboxypeptidases, proelastase, etc.), creating a proteolytic cascade. Without enterokinase, trypsinogen cannot be activated, and the entire cascade collapses, resulting in severe protein malabsorption despite adequate pancreatic enzyme secretion. His coagulopathy and hypoalbuminemia reflect poor protein/vitamin K absorption.

A— Peptidyl-aminopeptidase is separate and usually adequate.
C— PepT1 is present; the problem is earlier (protease activation).
D— Elastase is secreted; it's not activated.
E— Not the primary defect; leaky gut is a secondary consequence.
Q9 · Amino Acid Transport · Hartnup Disease & PepT1
A 14-year-old boy develops a photosensitive dermatitis rash, diarrhea, and ataxia after sun exposure. His urine shows massive excretion of neutral amino acids including tryptophan (often called "blue diaper disease" due to bacterial tryptophan metabolites). Stool tryptophan levels are also elevated. A diagnosis of Hartnup disease is confirmed. Which transporter dysfunction best explains his selective malabsorption of neutral amino acids while di/tripeptide absorption remains normal?
ADefect in basolateral amino acid exporter ASCT2
BLoss of apical neutral amino acid transporter; PepT1 still absorbs di/tripeptides
CDeficiency of aminopeptidase N preventing final amino acid release
DSelective tryptophanase deficiency in colonic bacteria
EReduced intestinal surface area from villous atrophy
Correct: B — Hartnup disease affects apical neutral amino acid transporter; PepT1 intact
Hartnup disease is an autosomal recessive defect in the apical neutral amino acid transporter (related to SLC6A19). It results in inability to absorb free neutral amino acids (including tryptophan) across the apical membrane, leading to: (1) malabsorption and diarrhea; (2) renal wasting (same transporter in kidney); (3) tryptophan deficiency → pellagra-like dermatitis, diarrhea, dementia (the "4 Ds" + ataxia). However, di- and tripeptide absorption via PepT1 remains intact, allowing partial nitrogen salvage if dietary protein is given as peptides.

A— Basolateral exporters are not impaired in Hartnup.
C— Aminopeptidases are still present.
D— Bacterial tryptophanase is secondary.
E— Intestinal architecture is normal in Hartnup.
Q10 · Lipid Digestion · Micelle Formation & Critical Micellar Concentration
A 48-year-old woman with primary sclerosing cholangitis and severely reduced bile acid secretion presents with steatorrhea, fat-soluble vitamin deficiencies (A, D, E, K), and lipid-soluble drug malabsorption. In-vitro lipolysis assays show that pancreatic lipase activity is normal, and fatty acids and monoglycerides are being produced, but their absorption is dramatically reduced. Which mechanism accounts for her malabsorption despite adequate lipase activity?
AImpaired gastric emptying reducing intestinal contact time
BInsufficient bile acids to form micelles and exceed critical micellar concentration
CDecreased pancreatic colipase reducing lipase activity
DBrush border lipase deficiency preventing final fat hydrolysis
EReduced intestinal alkaline phosphatase impairing nutrient absorption
Correct: B — Bile acids are essential for micellization above CMC
Micelle formation requires bile acids (bile salts) to reach and exceed the critical micellar concentration (CMC), typically ~2–5 mM. Bile salts surround the hydrophobic fatty acids and monoglycerides, forming small spherical structures that facilitate transport through the unstirred water layer to the enterocyte brush border. Without sufficient bile acids, products of lipolysis remain hydrophobic, poorly soluble, and cannot be efficiently absorbed. This explains the fat-soluble vitamin deficiencies (A, D, E, K) and steatorrhea.

A— Gastric function is not impaired.
C— Colipase is usually adequate.
D— Brush border lipase (phospholipase A2) is not the limiting factor.
E— Alkaline phosphatase is not critical for lipid absorption.
Q11 · Chylomicron Assembly · MTP & ApolipoproteinB-48
A 6-year-old girl presents with fat-soluble vitamin deficiencies (A, D, E, K), failure to thrive, and low serum triglycerides despite a high-fat diet. Intestinal biopsy shows lipid-laden enterocytes (abundant lipid droplets) but very low chylomicrons in the portal blood. Genetic testing confirms a mutation in the microsomal triglyceride transfer protein (MTP) gene. Which consequence of MTP deficiency best explains her clinical presentation?
AImpaired micelle formation reducing fat hydrolysis
BDefective pancreatic lipase secretion causing steatorrhea
CInability to assemble chylomicrons due to impaired triglyceride incorporation into apoB-48-containing particles
DReduced enterocyte mitochondrial function limiting ATP for active transport
EPremature apoptosis of intestinal enterocytes
Correct: C — MTP transfers triglycerides into apoB-48 chylomicrons
Microsomal triglyceride transfer protein (MTP) is essential for assembling chylomicrons, the lipoprotein vehicles for dietary lipid and fat-soluble vitamin transport. MTP catalyzes the transfer of triglycerides, phospholipids, and cholesteryl esters onto apolipoproteinB-48 (the 48 kDa N-terminal fragment of apoB in enterocytes). Without MTP, absorbed fatty acids and fat-soluble vitamins accumulate as lipid droplets within the enterocyte but cannot be packaged into chylomicrons, preventing their exit into lymph. This results in secondary malabsorption of vitamins A, D, E, K and low serum triglycerides despite normal lipase activity.

A— Micelle formation is intact.
B— Pancreatic function is normal.
D— Mitochondrial function is preserved.
E— Enterocytes do not apoptose.
Q12 · Abetalipoproteinemia · ApoB Deficiency & Vitamin E
A 9-year-old boy presents with progressive night blindness, peripheral vision loss (retinitis pigmentosa), and peripheral neuropathy. Blood smear shows acanthocytes (spiculated RBCs). Genetic analysis reveals a homozygous mutation in the APOB gene preventing synthesis of apolipoprotein B. Lipid panel shows nearly absent serum triglycerides and cholesterol. Which mechanism links his genetic defect to retinal degeneration and neurologic disease?
ADefective chylomicron synthesis preventing carbohydrate absorption
BImpaired VLDL secretion by the liver causing hepatic lipid accumulation
CInability to transport fat-soluble vitamins (especially vitamin E) → neurologic/ocular degeneration
DLoss of LDL receptors causing familial hypercholesterolemia
ESelective trypsinogen malabsorption due to protease deficiency
Correct: C — Abetalipoproteinemia prevents fat-soluble vitamin transport; vitamin E deficiency causes retinitis pigmentosa and neuropathy
Abetalipoproteinemia is a rare autosomal recessive disorder where mutations in APOB prevent synthesis of apolipoprotein B, the structural protein of both chylomicrons (intestinal) and VLDL (hepatic). This blocks transport of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) in blood. Vitamin E deficiency is particularly damaging to the nervous system and retina, causing spinocerebellar degeneration, peripheral neuropathy, and retinitis pigmentosa (progressive blindness). Acanthocytes result from abnormal RBC membrane lipid composition due to vitamin E deficiency and abnormal lipid distribution.

A— Carbohydrate absorption is normal.
B— VLDL cannot form (no apoB); hepatic lipid does accumulate but this is secondary.
D— LDL receptors are normal; lipid transport is blocked, not uptake.
E— Protease function is normal.
Q13 · Enterohepatic Circulation · Bile Acid Recycling & ASBT
A 52-year-old man with Crohn's disease undergoes resection of 2 meters of terminal ileum due to severe inflammation and strictures. Postoperatively, his bile acid pool becomes depleted (~50% loss), and he develops steatorrhea and chronic diarrhea. The terminal ileum normally reabsorbs ~95% of bile acids via an apical transporter, and the remaining ~5% is excreted in stool. After his resection, which mechanism best explains his malabsorption?
APancreatic lipase inactivation from acid reflux into the small intestine
BLoss of terminal ileum with ASBT transporters → impaired bile acid reabsorption → depleted bile acid pool
CBacterial overgrowth in the neoterminal ileum degrading bile acids
DReduced hepatic synthesis of bile acids in response to Crohn's disease
EIncreased ileal bacterial lipase converting bile salts to free steroids
Correct: B — Loss of terminal ileum removes ASBT, preventing bile acid reabsorption
Enterohepatic circulation of bile acids normally recycles ~95% of the bile acid pool back to the liver daily. The apical sodium-dependent bile acid transporter (ASBT) in the terminal ileum epithelium actively reabsorbs bile salts, returning them to the liver via the portal blood where they are re-secreted into the bile. Ileal resection removes the ASBT-expressing epithelium, reducing reabsorption to ~5%, causing the bile acid pool to become depleted. Fewer bile acids are available for micellization, resulting in steatorrhea and impaired fat-soluble vitamin absorption.

A— Pancreatic lipase is intact.
C— Bacterial overgrowth is not the primary mechanism.
D— Hepatic synthesis increases in response to losses, but cannot fully compensate.
E— Bacterial lipase is not a major factor.
Q14 · Ileal Resection Consequences · Vitamin & Mineral Absorption
A 58-year-old woman with a history of 3 meters of terminal ileum resection for Crohn's disease presents with megaloblastic anemia, recurrent nephrolithiasis, and bone pain. Laboratory studies show: serum B12 100 pg/mL (normal >200), elevated methylmalonic acid, macrocytic hemoglobin. Urine oxalate is elevated (>100 mg/24 hr, normal <40). Which combinations of malabsorptions occur as a direct result of ileal loss?
AVitamin B12 malabsorption only; other findings are unrelated
BB12 malabsorption (terminal ileum site); decreased bile acids → fat-soluble vitamin (ADEK) deficiency; increased colonic oxalate absorption → nephrolithiasis
CVitamin B12 and vitamin D deficiency only; oxalate stones unrelated to ileal resection
DDecreased intestinal calcium absorption causing secondary hyperparathyroidism and oxalate crystallization
EBacterial overgrowth producing urease, causing ammonia-based stone formation
Correct: B — Ileal resection causes B12 deficiency, ADEK deficiency, and hyperoxaluria
Terminal ileum resection has multiple consequences: (1) B12 malabsorption — the terminal ileum is the exclusive site of intrinsic factor (IF)-mediated B12 absorption via cubam receptors; loss → megaloblastic anemia. (2) Fat-soluble vitamin (ADEK) deficiency — loss of ASBT-expressing tissue → depleted bile acid pool → impaired lipid micellization. (3) Hyperoxaluria and oxalate stones — normally oxalate is bound by colonic calcium; bile acid loss → increased colonic fat malabsorption → colonic calcium binds fat instead of oxalate → oxalate hyperabsorption in colon and renal excretion.

A— Multiple deficiencies occur simultaneously.
C— All three malabsorptions are terminal ileum-related.
D— Calcium malabsorption is secondary to vitamin D deficiency, not primary.
E— Bacterial urease is not the mechanism.
Q15 · Intestinal Water Absorption · Osmotic & Active Coupling
A 41-year-old woman with chronic diarrhea undergoes assessment of intestinal water absorption. Her small intestinal epithelium is biopsied and shown to have normal villous structure with intact enterocytes. In-vitro Ussing chamber experiments demonstrate normal Na+ absorption and chloride absorption, yet water remains poorly absorbed. Which mechanism best explains the dissociation between electrolyte absorption and water absorption?
AAquaporin-2 water channel deficiency preventing osmotic water transport
BWater absorption is osmotically coupled to electrolyte absorption; without adequate osmotic driving force, water stays in lumen
CImpaired tight junction integrity allowing backflux of absorbed water and electrolytes
DBacterial acid production lowering pH and reducing water reabsorption
EDeficiency of brush border aquaporin-3 water channels
Correct: B — Water absorption is osmotically coupled to electrolyte absorption
The small intestine absorbs water largely through osmotic coupling: electrolytes (Na+, Cl−, glucose, amino acids) are actively transported out of the lumen into the bloodstream, creating an osmotic gradient that draws water passively into blood. Water-permeable tight junctions and basolateral aquaporins (AQP3, AQP4) facilitate transcellular water movement. Without adequate solute absorption, there is insufficient osmotic gradient to drive water reabsorption, and it remains in the lumen, causing osmotic diarrhea. This is the basis of osmotic laxatives (magnesium salts, PEG).

A— Aquaporin-2 is renal (ADH-responsive), not intestinal.
C— Tight junctions are stated to be intact.
D— pH effects are not the limiting factor.
E— AQP3 is usually present.
Q16 · Iron Absorption · DMT1, Ferroportin & Hepcidin
A 64-year-old man with long-standing rheumatoid arthritis presents with anemia, elevated serum ferritin, and low-normal serum iron despite normal dietary iron intake. His hepcidin levels are markedly elevated (>100 ng/mL, normal <50). Bone marrow biopsy shows adequate iron stores in macrophages. Which mechanism accounts for his anemia despite adequate dietary iron and systemic iron stores?
AImpaired duodenal DMT1 expression from iron deficiency
BElevated hepcidin blocks ferroportin export from enterocytes and macrophages → iron trapping
CLoss of intestinal villi reducing iron absorption surface
DCopper deficiency preventing ferroxidase activity and iron oxidation
EChronic GI blood loss from NSAID use
Correct: B — Hepcidin suppresses ferroportin, trapping iron intracellularly
Hepcidin is the master regulator of iron homeostasis, produced by the liver in response to systemic iron and inflammation. Elevated hepcidin (as in anemia of chronic disease/inflammation from rheumatoid arthritis) causes internalization and degradation of ferroportin, the iron exporter on enterocytes and macrophages. This results in iron sequestration — adequate iron in stores but unable to be mobilized for hemoglobin synthesis, causing anemia despite normal dietary iron and iron availability.

A— DMT1 is actually upregulated when iron is low, but hepcidin effects dominate.
C— Villous architecture is intact.
D— Copper deficiency is not present.
E— No mention of blood loss; stores are high.
Q17 · Calcium Absorption · 1,25-Vitamin D & Calbindin
A 58-year-old woman with hyperthyroidism from Graves' disease develops symptomatic hypocalcemia (Ca 6.8 mg/dL, ionized Ca 2.2 mEq/L) and secondary hyperparathyroidism despite thyroid hormone replacement. Her renal function is normal, and 25-OH vitamin D is adequate (55 ng/mL). However, her 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D levels are inappropriately low (15 pg/mL, normal 20–60). Which mechanism best explains her calcium malabsorption?
ADietary calcium restriction due to lactose intolerance
BIntestinal pH elevation from achlorhydria preventing calcium solubilization
CImpaired renal 1-α-hydroxylase → insufficient 1,25-vitamin D → reduced calbindin induction → poor intestinal calcium absorption
DDefective vitamin D receptor gene mutation
EImpaired basolateral Na/Ca exchanger preventing calcium export
Correct: C — Low 1,25-vitamin D impairs intestinal calbindin induction and calcium absorption
1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (the active hormone form) acts as a ligand for the vitamin D receptor (VDR) in intestinal epithelial cells, inducing expression of calbindin-D9k, a cytoplasmic calcium-binding protein essential for transcellular calcium absorption. Without adequate 1,25-vitamin D, calbindin is not induced, calcium transport is impaired, and hypocalcemia results. In this patient, the low 1,25-vitamin D suggests impaired renal 1-α-hydroxylase activity (possibly from thyroid autoimmunity affecting other endocrine axes, or intrinsic renal disease). With normal kidney function and adequate 25-OH vitamin D substrate, the block is in conversion.

A— Dietary calcium is often adequate in hypocalcemia; malabsorption is the issue.
B— Achlorhydria is unlikely; pH effects are minor compared to 1,25-vitamin D.
D— VDR is intact (secondary hyperparathyroidism indicates responsiveness).
E— Na/Ca exchanger is usually adequate.
Q18 · Vitamin B12 Absorption · IF-Mediated & Cubam Receptors
A 69-year-old woman with history of total gastrectomy (for gastric cancer 15 years ago) now presents with megaloblastic anemia, peripheral neuropathy, and elevated serum methylmalonic acid and homocysteine. She has normal renal function and adequate dietary cobalamin intake. Which statement best explains the mechanism of her B12 deficiency?
ALoss of intrinsic factor from gastric parietal cells; B12 cannot be absorbed
BIntrinsic factor (IF) produced by gastric parietal cells binds B12 → cubam receptors in terminal ileum absorb IF-B12 complex
CR factor (haptocorrin) from saliva is degraded in the stomach, preventing B12 transport
DTerminal ileum villous atrophy from celiac disease reducing absorption surface
ETranscobalamin II deficiency preventing plasma B12 transport
Correct: B — Gastrectomy removes IF-producing parietal cells; IF-B12 absorption at terminal ileum is lost
The vitamin B12 absorption pathway requires: (1) Intrinsic factor (IF) secreted by gastric parietal cells; (2) IF binds B12 (cobalamin) in the stomach; (3) R factor (transcobalamin I/haptocorrin) from saliva initially binds B12 but is cleaved by pancreatic proteases, releasing B12 to IF; (4) IF-B12 complex is absorbed at the terminal ileum via cubam receptors (specialized IF-B12 absorbers); (5) Transcobalamin II (TC2) carries B12 in plasma. Gastrectomy eliminates IF production → B12 cannot be absorbed despite adequate intake.

A— Correct mechanism but not best stated.
C— R factor degradation is normal; IF deficiency is the issue.
D— No villous atrophy mentioned.
E— TC2 transports B12 after absorption; deficiency does not block uptake.
Q19 · Diarrhea Classification · Osmotic vs Secretory
A 31-year-old man recently returned from traveling in Central America develops profuse watery diarrhea (10+ stools/day) with stool osmolality 350 mOsm/kg (normal ~300), stool Na+ 60 mEq/L, and stool K+ 20 mEq/L. Calculated osmotic gap = 350 − 2(60+20) = 190 mOsm/kg. The diarrhea persists during fasting and continues at night, waking him from sleep. Stool culture yields Vibrio cholerae. Which classification and mechanism best describes his diarrhea?
AOsmotic diarrhea from unabsorbed disaccharides; stops with fasting
BSecretory diarrhea from cholera toxin-mediated cAMP increase → Cl− secretion → water follows; continues despite fasting
CInflammatory diarrhea from mucosal invasion and ulceration
DFatty diarrhea (steatorrhea) from pancreatic insufficiency
EMotility disorder causing accelerated transit
Correct: B — Cholera toxin causes secretory diarrhea via cAMP-mediated Cl− secretion
Secretory diarrhea is caused by toxins or hormones that increase intracellular signaling (cAMP, cGMP) in enterocytes, leading to active Cl− secretion into the lumen, with water following osmotically. Cholera toxin (from V. cholerae) ADP-ribosylates Gs protein, locking it in the active state → persistent cAMP elevation → sustained Cl− secretion. Key features: (1) continues during fasting (no substrate needed); (2) continues at night (not dependent on feeding); (3) large osmotic gap (>125 mOsm/kg indicates secretory, not osmotic); (4) stool electrolytes approach plasma.

A— Osmotic diarrhea stops with fasting; this does not.
C— V. cholerae does not invade; it's toxin-mediated.
D— No fat malabsorption here.
E— Motility is not the mechanism.
Q20 · Secondary Disaccharidase Deficiency · Tropical Sprue
A 44-year-old man who has lived in the tropics for 20 years presents with chronic diarrhea, steatorrhea, and weight loss. Serum vitamin B12 is low (150 pg/mL), folate is low (2.5 ng/mL). Duodenal biopsy shows partial villous atrophy with a villous height/crypt depth ratio of 3:1 (normal >5:1). Lactose hydrogen breath test shows a rise in exhaled H2 from baseline 5 ppm to 62 ppm within 90 minutes, confirming lactose malabsorption. Which mechanism accounts for his secondary disaccharidase deficiency?
APrimary lactase deficiency from genetic loss of lactase gene expression
BPancreatic insufficiency reducing brush border disaccharidase synthesis
CVillous atrophy reducing brush border surface area and enzyme-bearing enterocytes
DBacterial overgrowth producing lactase-inhibitory substances
EImmune-mediated destruction of lactase-expressing cells
Correct: C — Villous atrophy reduces brush border surface and disaccharidase-bearing enterocytes
Secondary disaccharidase deficiency occurs when small intestinal disease damages the villous architecture, reducing the total number of brush border-bearing enterocytes and thus enzyme content. Unlike primary lactase deficiency (genetic), this is reversible with mucosal healing. His B12 and folate deficiency reflect mucosal damage and inflammation.
