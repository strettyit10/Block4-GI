LO 1 — Meckel Diverticulum
State the "rule of 2s" for Meckel diverticulum. What is the most common ectopic tissue?
tap to flip
Answer
Rule of 2s: 2% of population, 2 feet long, 2 inches from ileocecal valve, 2 types of ectopic tissue (gastric mucosa most common), 2× more common in males. Ectopic gastric mucosa → gastric acid secretion → ileal ulceration → bleeding (most common complication).
LO 1 — Hirschsprung Disease
What is the embryologic defect in Hirschsprung disease, and which segment is most commonly involved?
tap to flip
Answer
Failure of neural crest cell migration → aganglionic segment. Most common: rectosigmoid (80%). Presents with failure to pass meconium, abdominal distention in infants. Diagnosis: contrast enema (transition zone), rectal biopsy (absence of ganglion cells).
LO 2 — Intestinal Obstruction Overview
List the four cardinal mechanisms of intestinal obstruction and identify the most common cause overall.
tap to flip
Answer
Four mechanisms: 1) Hernia, 2) Adhesions (most common — ~75% of SBO), 3) Intussusception, 4) Volvulus. Small bowel obstruction (SBO) vs. large bowel obstruction (LBO) differ in presentation and urgency; SBO causes abdominal cramping, vomiting earlier. LBO slower onset.
LO 2 — Volvulus
Which type of volvulus is more common in elderly patients, and which in younger patients?
tap to flip
Answer
Sigmoid volvulus → elderly (most common volvulus in older age). Cecal volvulus → younger patients, longer mesentery. Both cause "bird's beak" on contrast imaging (twisted mesentery). High mortality if ischemia/necrosis develops.
LO 3 — Ischemic Bowel
Name the two major types of ischemic bowel disease and explain the difference between thrombotic and embolic causes.
tap to flip
Answer
Acute mesenteric ischemia: Embolic (50%, sudden, cardiac source) vs. Thrombotic (25%, gradual, atherosclerosis). Watershed zones vulnerable: splenic flexure (between SMA & IMA) and rectosigmoid junction (between IMA & hypogastric). Presents with severe pain out of proportion to exam.
LO 4 — Celiac Disease
What HLA alleles are associated with celiac disease, and what is the primary antigen targeted?
tap to flip
Answer
HLA-DQ2 (95%) or HLA-DQ8 (5%) required for celiac. Target antigen: tissue transglutaminase (tTG) — cross-reacts with gliadin. Pathology: villous atrophy + crypt hyperplasia. Complications: dermatitis herpetiformis, iron-deficiency anemia, osteoporosis.
LO 5 — Crohn's Disease
What are the pathologic hallmarks of Crohn disease, and which portion of the GI tract is classically involved?
tap to flip
Answer
Terminal ileum (90% involvement). Pathologic hallmarks: skip lesions, transmural inflammation, cobblestone appearance, fissuring ulcers, fistulas, granulomas (non-caseating). Complications: strictures, fistulas, abscesses. Perianal disease common (skin tags, fistulas).
LO 5 — Ulcerative Colitis
How does ulcerative colitis differ from Crohn disease in terms of extent, depth, and pathologic features?
tap to flip
Answer
UC: continuous inflammation (no skip lesions), mucosal/submucosal only (not transmural), crypt abscesses, pseudopolyps. Always involves rectum, extends proximally in continuous pattern. NO granulomas, NO fistulas. Higher colorectal cancer risk (~1% per year after 10 years).
LO 6 — Diverticular Disease
Why does diverticular disease preferentially affect the sigmoid colon, and what is the anatomic vulnerability?
tap to flip
Answer
Sigmoid colon: smallest lumen (Laplace's law) → highest intramural pressure. Vasa recta penetrate muscular wall at sites of increased pressure. Low-fiber diet → increased pressures. Diverticulitis (inflammation) → fever, LLQ pain, elevated WBC. Complications: perforation, abscess, fistula.
LO 7 — Colonic Polyp Classification
Name the three main categories of colonic polyps and give an example of each.
tap to flip
Answer
1) Non-neoplastic: hyperplastic (most common, no cancer risk). 2) Hamartomatous: Peutz-Jeghers (hereditary, increased cancer risk), juvenile polyps. 3) Neoplastic (adenomatous): tubular (75%, low villous), villous (highest cancer risk), tubulovillous. Serrated polyps (alternative pathway to cancer).
LO 8 — Adenoma-Carcinoma Sequence
Outline the adenoma-carcinoma sequence for colorectal cancer, naming the key genetic mutations.
tap to flip
Answer
Normal epithelium → APC loss → adenoma → KRAS mutation → advanced adenoma → TP53 mutation → carcinoma. Takes ~10–15 years. APC inactivation (adenomatous polyposis coli) is early/initiating event. TP53 (p53 tumor suppressor) critical for malignant progression.
LO 8 — Familial Adenomatous Polyposis (FAP)
What is the genetic basis of FAP, what is its penetrance, and what is the standard screening/treatment approach?
tap to flip
Answer
Germline APC mutation. 100% penetrance, develops hundreds to thousands of adenomas. Nearly 100% cancer risk by age 50 if untreated. Treatment: prophylactic total colectomy. Screening: colonoscopy starting age 10–12 years. Extraintestinal manifestations: osteomas, desmoid tumors, congenital hypertrophy of retinal pigment epithelium (CHRPE).
LO 8 — Lynch Syndrome (HNPCC)
What genetic abnormality defines Lynch syndrome, and how does it predispose to cancer?
tap to flip
Answer
Mismatch repair (MMR) gene mutations (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2). Defective DNA repair → microsatellite instability (MSI-high). ~70% lifetime risk of CRC (often right-sided, earlier onset). Screening: colonoscopy every 1–2 years starting age 25. Increased risk of endometrial, ovarian, gastric cancers.
LO 8 — Anal Canal Squamous Cell Carcinoma
What is the primary risk factor for anal canal SCC, and how does HPV infection contribute?
tap to flip
Answer
HPV infection (mostly HPV-16) → >80% of cases. Additional risk factors: smoking, immunosuppression, anal receptive intercourse. Presents with anal pain, bleeding, mass. Treatment: Nigro regimen (chemotherapy + radiation) preferred over surgery to preserve continence. Better prognosis than rectal SCC.
LO 2 — Intussusception
What is intussusception, what is the most common age of presentation, and what is the typical radiologic finding?
tap to flip
Answer
Invagination of intestine into adjacent bowel. Most common in children ages 6 months–3 years. Most cases idiopathic (hypertrophied Peyer's patches); can follow viral infection. Classic presentation: intermittent abdominal pain, "currant jelly" stools. Imaging: "target sign" (ultrasound/CT). Treatment: hydrostatic or pneumatic reduction; surgery if ischemia.
LO 2 — Gallstone Ileus
How does a gallstone cause intestinal obstruction, and what is the classic imaging triad?
tap to flip
Answer
Cholecystoenteric fistula (from large stone eroding gallbladder wall) → gallstone impacted in terminal ileum (narrowest part of small bowel). Classic imaging triad: pneumobilia, intestinal obstruction, ectopic gallstone (Rigler's triad). More common in elderly. Treatment: enterotomy + stone extraction; manage gallbladder/fistula if feasible.
LO 4 — Dermatitis Herpetiformis
What is the relationship between dermatitis herpetiformis and celiac disease?
tap to flip
Answer
Dermatitis herpetiformis is a cutaneous manifestation of celiac disease (same pathogenic HLA-DQ2/DQ8 and tTG antibodies). Presents with intensely pruritic papulovesicular rash, typically on extensor surfaces. ~15–25% of celiac patients develop DH. Responds to gluten-free diet; also responds to dapsone (faster symptom relief).
LO 5 — IBD Cancer Risk
Compare colorectal cancer risk in Crohn disease vs. ulcerative colitis. How does duration and extent affect risk?
tap to flip
Answer
Both have increased cancer risk, higher in UC (~1% per year after 10 years disease duration). Crohn cancer risk lower but increased. Risk factors: disease duration >8–10 years, extent of colonic involvement, primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC), family history. Screening colonoscopy/surveillance biopsies recommended.
LO 3 — Chronic Mesenteric Ischemia
How does chronic mesenteric ischemia present clinically, and what diagnostic approach is recommended?
tap to flip
Answer
Postprandial abdominal pain ("intestinal angina"), food fear → weight loss. Caused by progressive atherosclerosis of mesenteric vessels (SMA most common). Diagnosis: CT/MR angiography or doppler ultrasound. Treatment: revascularization (endovascular or surgical) to prevent acute-on-chronic crisis.

Test Traps: Read each statement and decide if it's True or False. Click to reveal the explanation.
1
Ulcerative colitis involves transmural inflammation and commonly presents with fistulas and cobblestone lesions.
FALSE. This describes Crohn disease. Ulcerative colitis is limited to the mucosa/submucosa, involves the rectum and spreads proximally in a continuous pattern, and does NOT cause fistulas or granulomas. UC features crypt abscesses and pseudopolyps instead.
2
Meckel diverticulum is located in the proximal small bowel (duodenum/jejunum) and rarely causes symptoms.
FALSE. Meckel diverticulum is located in the ileum (2 feet from ileocecal valve). It commonly contains ectopic gastric mucosa which secretes acid → ileal ulceration → GI bleeding. This is the most common complication.
3
Intussusception is a common cause of small bowel obstruction in adults and typically presents with sudden onset of abdominal pain.
FALSE. Intussusception is most common in children ages 6 months–3 years, not adults. Presents with intermittent crampy pain and "currant jelly" stools. In adults, intussusception usually indicates an underlying pathology (mass, diverticulum) and is less common than adhesions or hernias.
4
Celiac disease requires lifelong gluten avoidance and is caused by IgE-mediated allergy to gliadin.
PARTIALLY FALSE. Lifelong gluten avoidance is correct, but celiac is NOT an IgE-mediated allergy—it's an autoimmune condition. Pathophysiology: HLA-DQ2/DQ8 molecules present gliadin peptides → T-cell activation → production of anti-tTG antibodies → intestinal inflammation.
5
FAP (familial adenomatous polyposis) has low penetrance and patients require surveillance colonoscopy only after age 40.
FALSE. FAP has 100% penetrance (not low). Patients develop hundreds to thousands of adenomas and nearly 100% cancer risk by age 50 if untreated. Screening colonoscopy should start at ages 10–12 years (not 40), and prophylactic total colectomy is standard treatment.
6
Lynch syndrome is caused by mutations in adenomatous polyposis coli (APC) and presents with thousands of polyps.
FALSE. That describes FAP, not Lynch syndrome. Lynch syndrome is caused by mismatch repair (MMR) gene mutations (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2) → microsatellite instability → high CRC risk (~70% lifetime). Lynch patients typically have fewer polyps than FAP patients and often develop right-sided cancers.
7
Acute mesenteric ischemia from arterial emboli typically develops gradually and presents with chronic postprandial abdominal pain.
FALSE. Acute embolic mesenteric ischemia develops SUDDENLY (not gradually) and presents with severe pain out of proportion to exam. Gradual onset with postprandial pain describes CHRONIC mesenteric ischemia from atherosclerotic disease ("intestinal angina").
8
Anal canal squamous cell carcinoma is primarily caused by smoking and is more common in heterosexual men than in men who have sex with men.
FALSE. The primary cause is HPV infection (>80% of cases), not smoking. While smoking is a risk factor, anal receptive intercourse is a major risk factor (high HPV transmission), making the condition more common in men who have sex with men. Immunosuppression also increases risk.
Meckel Diverticulum — Rule of 2s
Parameter Value
Prevalence 2% of population
Length 2 inches (technically feet, but commonly cited as 2 feet)
Location 2 feet proximal to ileocecal valve
Gender ratio 2× more common in males
Ectopic tissues 2 types: gastric mucosa (most common) & pancreatic tissue
Most common complication GI bleeding (from gastric acid-induced ulcer)
Clinical Pearl: Rule of 2s helps clinicians remember Meckel diverticulum criteria. Ectopic gastric mucosa secretes acid → ileal ulceration → bleeding is the most common presentation. Diagnosis: Technetium-99m scan (picks up ectopic gastric mucosa).
Causes of Small Bowel Obstruction (SBO)
Cause Frequency (%) Key Features
Adhesions ~75% Post-surgical most common; can occur after abdominal trauma or surgery
Hernia ~15% Inguinal, ventral, incisional, or internal hernia
Intussusception ~5% Most common in children 6 months–3 years; "target sign" on imaging
Volvulus ~3% Sigmoid (elderly), cecal (younger); "bird's beak" sign
Gallstone ileus <1% Cholecystoenteric fistula; Rigler's triad (pneumobilia, obstruction, stone)
Malignancy <1% Less common in SBO; more common in LBO
SBO vs. LBO: SBO presents earlier with frequent vomiting and crampy pain. LBO slower onset with progressive constipation; more likely to perforate. Adhesions account for majority of SBO; obstruction requires imaging (CT) and sometimes surgical evaluation.
Celiac Disease — Pathophysiology & Manifestations
Parameter Details
HLA associations HLA-DQ2 (~95% of patients), HLA-DQ8 (~5%)
Antigen targeted Tissue transglutaminase (tTG); cross-reactive with gliadin
Pathology Villous atrophy + crypt hyperplasia
Dermatitis herpetiformis Cutaneous manifestation; intensely pruritic papulovesicles on extensor surfaces; same pathogenic mechanism as CD
Complications Iron-deficiency anemia (most common), osteoporosis, increased malignancy risk (lymphoma), infertility
Treatment Lifelong gluten-free diet; supplementation for iron, B12, calcium
Screening: Anti-tTG IgA antibodies (high sensitivity/specificity). Confirm with small bowel biopsy showing villous atrophy. Celiac affects ~1% of population; underdiagnosed due to variable presentations.
IBD Comparison: Crohn vs. Ulcerative Colitis
Feature Crohn Disease Ulcerative Colitis
Distribution Skip lesions; any GI location (terminal ileum ~90%) Continuous; rectum always involved, extends proximally
Depth Transmural (full thickness) Mucosal & submucosal only
Pathology Granulomas (non-caseating), cobblestone lesions, fissuring ulcers Crypt abscesses, pseudopolyps, continuous ulceration
Complications Fistulas, strictures, abscesses; perianal disease common Toxic megacolon (rare); massive hemorrhage (rare)
Cancer risk Increased (~2–8% risk); lower than UC Higher (~1% per year after 10 years disease)

Diagnosis: Colonoscopy with biopsy is gold standard for both. CD biopsy shows granulomas & transmural inflammation; UC shows crypt abscesses in mucosal biopsies. Both increase CRC risk; surveillance colonoscopy recommended.
