Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome
Tumor type, location, fasting gastrin level, secretin test response?
Answer
Tumor: Gastrinoma (G-cell tumor) — 60-70% in duodenum, 30% pancreas head
Mechanism: ↑↑Gastrin → ↑↑HCl secretion → multiple refractory peptic ulcers (duodenal > gastric) + chronic diarrhea (HCl inactivates pancreatic lipase)
Lab: Fasting serum gastrin >1000 pg/mL diagnostic; gastric pH <2
Secretin stimulation: Paradoxical ↑gastrin (most tumors; suppression in normal stomach)
Verner-Morrison Syndrome (VIPoma)
WDHA triad components, mechanism, GI pH?
Answer
Tumor: VIP-secreting pancreatic islet cell tumor
WDHA Triad: Watery Diarrhea (>10 L/day), Hypokalemia (↓K⁺), Achlorhydria (VIP inhibits HCl)
Mechanism: VIP → activates Gs → ↑cAMP in crypt epithelium → maximal Cl⁻ secretion via CFTR → osmotic water loss → "pancreatic cholera"
GI pH: High (achlorhydria) — opposite of ZES
Pernicious Anemia
Antibodies involved, pathophysiology, B12 deficiency consequences?
Answer
Autoimmune: Anti-parietal cell + anti-intrinsic factor (IF) antibodies → destroy gastric parietal cells
Result: ↓IF → ↓B12 absorption in terminal ileum → B12 deficiency
Manifestations: Megaloblastic anemia (↑MCV) + subacute combined degeneration (dorsal/lateral columns) → paresthesias, ataxia, dementia
Extra: ↑fasting gastrin (loss of acid feedback); ↑risk gastric cancer/parietal cell carcinoma
Sjögren's Syndrome
Primary antibodies, affected glands, systemic GI complications?
Answer
Antibodies: Anti-SSA (Ro), anti-SSB (La) — more specific
Affected: Lacrimal + salivary glands (exocrine) → xerophthalmia (dry eyes) + xerostomia (dry mouth)
Complications: ↑MALT lymphoma risk (mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue); also affects esophagus → dysphagia, odynophagia
Carcinoid Syndrome
Cell of origin, secretory product, classic features, when syndrome appears?
Answer
Origin: Enterochromaffin (EC) cells secrete 5-HT (serotonin) + other amines (substance P, tachykinins)
Features: Flushing (face/neck), diarrhea (watery), bronchospasm, right-sided valvular disease (tricuspid/pulmonary valve fibrosis)
Diagnosis: ↑urine 5-HIAA (serotonin metabolite)
Key: Syndrome appears only with LIVER METASTASES (local serotonin → first-pass metabolism by MAO; mets bypass liver)
Somatostatinoma
Classic triad of inhibitory effects?
Answer
Tumor: Somatostatin-secreting pancreatic islet cell (rare)
Triad: Steatorrhea (↓pancreatic lipase), Achlorhydria (↓HCl), Hyperglycemia (↓insulin)
Mechanism: Somatostatin inhibits ALL secretions + motility
Note: Very rare; high mortality at diagnosis (usually advanced)
H. pylori Antral Infection
Antral location, D cell damage consequence, ulcer location, acid level?
Answer
Location: H. pylori antrum → damages D cells (δ cells) that secrete somatostatin
Result: ↓Somatostatin → disinhibition of G cells → ↑↑Gastrin secretion
Consequence: ↑↑HCl → DUODENAL ulcer (90% of H. pylori-associated ulcers)
Stomach pH: ACIDIC (↑HCl)
Note: DU more common because duodenal mucosa less resistant to acid
H. pylori Corpus Infection
Corpus location, parietal cell damage, ulcer location, acid level?
Answer
Location: H. pylori corpus → damages parietal cells (acid-secreting cells)
Result: ↓Acid secretion → achlorhydria (if severe) + atrophic gastritis
Consequence: GASTRIC ulcer or no ulcer; more predisposes to gastric cancer
Stomach pH: HIGH (achlorhydria)
Pathophysiology: Corpus damage → atrophy → intestinal metaplasia → dysplasia → cancer (Correa cascade)
H. pylori Virulence Factors
Urease function, VacA, CagA significance?
Answer
Urease: Cleaves urea → NH3 + CO2 (ammonia neutralizes stomach acid → allows survival)
Diagnostic test: 13C-urea breath test (gold standard for eradication confirmation)
VacA: Vacuolating cytotoxin → induces apoptosis, mucosal damage
CagA: Type IV secretion toxin → WHO Class I carcinogen → ↑gastric cancer/MALT lymphoma risk
Note: CagA+ strains more aggressive
Cholera Toxin Mechanism
Toxin mechanism, target, massive diarrhea pathophysiology?
Answer
Mechanism: AB5 toxin — B binds to GM1 ganglioside on intestinal epithelium; A subunit ADP-ribosylates Gs protein
Result: Constitutive Gs activation → ↑↑cAMP → massive CFTR-mediated Cl⁻ secretion
Consequence: Water follows osmotically → "rice-water" diarrhea up to 20 L/day
Treatment: ORS (bypasses toxin) + supportive care (fluid/electrolyte replacement)
ETEC Heat-Labile Toxin (LT)
Mechanism, similarity to cholera?
Answer
Mechanism: Similar to cholera toxin — ADP-ribosylates Gs → ↑cAMP → CFTR-mediated Cl⁻ secretion
Result: Watery diarrhea (though usually less severe than cholera)
Key difference: Heat-STABLE toxin (ST) instead acts via guanylate cyclase → ↑cGMP → same CFTR activation
Treatment: ORS, fluoroquinolones if needed
3 Phases of Gastric Acid Secretion
Cephalic, gastric, intestinal phases: stimuli, percentage contribution, vagotomy effect?
Answer
Cephalic (30-40%): Sight/smell/thought → vagus (CN X) → ACh (direct parietal cells) + GRP (→G cells→gastrin)
Gastric (50-60%): Distension + amino acids/peptides → local/vagal reflexes + gastrin
Intestinal (5-10%): Chyme in duodenum → brief gastrin spike; DOMINATED by inhibition (secretin, CCK, GIP, enterogastric reflex)
Vagotomy: Eliminates cephalic phase; gastric phase partially preserved; intestinal phase preserved
PPI Pharmacology
Class, mechanism, prodrug activation, target step, timing?
Answer
Class: Proton pump inhibitor (irreversible)
Mechanism: Prodrug → activated by acid in parietal cell canaliculus → binds H⁺/K⁺ ATPase → irreversible inhibition
Target: Final step of HCl secretion (rate-limiting)
Timing: Must take BEFORE meal (requires active pumps)
Efficacy: ↓acid output by ~95% (most potent acid suppressant)
Long-term risks: C. difficile, ↑pneumonia, hypomagnesemia, osteoporosis, B12 deficiency
H2 Blocker Pharmacology
Mechanism, receptor, examples, efficacy vs. PPIs?
Answer
Mechanism: Competitive histamine H2 receptor antagonist on parietal cells → ↓cAMP → ↓HCl secretion
Examples: Cimetidine (↓CYP450 → drug interactions; anti-androgen → gynecomastia), famotidine (fewer side effects), ranitidine
Efficacy: ↓acid by 50-70% (less than PPIs)
Use: GERD, PUD (not ZES — need PPI)
Advantage: Simpler dosing; fewer long-term risks than PPIs
Octreotide (Somatostatin Analog)
Mechanism, clinical indications, GI effects?
Answer
Mechanism: Synthetic somatostatin analog → binds somatostatin receptors (Gi-coupled) → ↓cAMP → inhibits secretion
Indications: Gastrinoma (ZES), VIPoma, carcinoid syndrome, variceal bleeding
GI effects: ↓HCl, ↓pancreatic enzymes, ↓motility, ↓diarrhea
Bonus: Splanchnic vasoconstriction → ↓portal pressure (useful in variceal bleed)
Side effect: Steatorrhea (inhibits pancreatic enzymes)
Pancreatic Insufficiency
Common causes, steatorrhea mechanism, ADEK deficiency consequences?
Answer
Causes: Chronic pancreatitis (alcohol, CF), pancreatic cancer, Shwachman-Diamond syndrome
Pathophysiology: ↓pancreatic lipase/proteases → fat/protein malabsorption
Result: Steatorrhea (oily stools), ↓fat-soluble vitamin absorption (ADEK)
Deficiency consequences: A (blindness), D (osteoporosis), E (neuropathy), K (coagulopathy)
Diagnosis: Fecal elastase-1 <200 μg/g (positive)
Treatment: Pancreatic enzyme replacement (with meals)
Cystic Fibrosis: GI Manifestations
CFTR mutation location, pancreatic pathophysiology, intestinal complications?
Answer
Gene: CFTR mutation (chromosome 7) → ↓/absent CFTR channel in ducts
Pancreas: Viscous secretions → ductal obstruction → acinar autodigestion → ↓pancreatic enzymes → steatorrhea + ADEK deficiency
GI complications: Meconium ileus (neonates), DIOS (distal intestinal obstruction), chronic pancreatitis
Other: Nasal polyps, sinusitis, bronchiectasis, male infertility (CBAVD = congenital bilateral absence vas deferens)
Diagnosis: Sweat chloride test >60 mEq/L
Secretory vs. Osmotic Diarrhea
Mechanisms, stool characteristics, fasting response, diagnostic osmotic gap?
Answer
Secretory: Active Cl⁻ secretion (→H2O follows) — e.g., cholera, VIPoma, carcinoid, fatty acids
Osmotic: Non-absorbed solute (↓water absorption) — e.g., lactose intolerance, Mg2+ salts, mannitol
Stool characteristics: Secretory = watery, large volume; Osmotic = less volume, stops with fasting
Stool osmotic gap: [Na+K] vs. measured osmolality
• Gap <50 → Secretory (ions, not osmoles)
• Gap >100 → Osmotic (unmeasured solutes)
Fasting: Secretory diarrhea PERSISTS (>10 L/day for cholera); osmotic stops
GI Hormone Tumors Overview
Four main functional GI neuroendocrine tumors, what each secretes?
Answer
1. Gastrinoma (ZES): Gastrin → ↑HCl → DU/PUD
2. VIPoma (Verner-Morrison): VIP → ↑cAMP → watery diarrhea, hypokalemia, achlorhydria
3. Somatostatinoma: Somatostatin → ↓all secretions → steatorrhea, achlorhydria, hyperglycemia
4. Insulinoma: Insulin → hypoglycemia (NOT secretion-focused but important)
Key: Gastrinomas 60-90% associated with MEN1 syndrome (3 P's: Pancreas, Pituitary, Parathyroid)

Question 1 of 10 — Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome
A 45-year-old man presents with recurrent, severe peptic ulcers refractory to standard-dose H2 blockers. Lab studies show fasting serum gastrin 1,200 pg/mL (normal <100) and gastric pH 1.2. A secretin stimulation test is performed, which shows a paradoxical increase in serum gastrin. Which of the following BEST explains the pathophysiology?
AHyperplasia of gastric parietal cells with increased acid secretion
BGastrin-secreting tumor (gastrinoma) resistant to normal negative feedback from acid
CIncreased sensitivity of antral G cells to dietary peptides
DSevere decrease in somatostatin-secreting D cells in the gastric antrum
ELoss of H+ feedback inhibition on parietal cell acid secretion
B — Gastrinoma resistant to feedback. Zollinger-Ellison syndrome is caused by an autonomous gastrin-secreting tumor (usually in the "gastrinoma triangle" — duodenum/pancreatic head). Normal G cells suppress gastrin secretion when stomach pH drops (acid negative feedback). Gastrinomas IGNORE this feedback → paradoxical ↑gastrin with secretin stimulation (secretin normally suppresses antral G cells). Fasting gastrin >1000 pg/mL + low gastric pH is diagnostic. Treatment: high-dose PPIs ± surgical resection.
Question 2 of 10 — H. pylori Antral vs. Corpus Infection
Two patients both have H. pylori infection, but at different gastric locations. Patient A has antral predominant infection and develops duodenal ulcer. Patient B has corpus predominant infection and develops gastric atrophy with intestinal metaplasia. Which statement BEST explains the difference in pathophysiology?
AAntral infection damages parietal cells → ↑acid → DU; corpus infection damages parietal cells → ↓acid → atrophy
BAntral infection damages D cells (↓somatostatin) → ↑G cells → ↑gastrin → ↑acid → DU; corpus damage → ↓parietal cells → achlorhydria → atrophy
CH. pylori is only pathogenic in the antrum; corpus involvement is asymptomatic
DBoth cause similar acid secretion changes but gastric vs. duodenal location depends on bacterial virulence
ECorpus infection produces somatostatin-secreting metaplastic tissue that causes hypersecretion
B — Site-specific D cell vs. parietal damage. Antral infection: Damages D cells → ↓somatostatin → disinhibition of G cells → ↑↑gastrin → ↑↑HCl → duodenal ulcer (duodenal mucosa less resistant). Corpus infection: Damages parietal cells → ↓acid secretion → achlorhydria + atrophic gastritis → intestinal metaplasia (Correa sequence) → gastric cancer risk. The location of H. pylori infection determines the clinical outcome.
Question 3 of 10 — Verner-Morrison Syndrome (VIPoma)
A 52-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of massive watery diarrhea (15 L/day), hypokalemia (K+ 2.8), and achlorhydria (gastric pH 6.8). Imaging reveals a 3 cm pancreatic tumor. Serum VIP is markedly elevated. Which of the following BEST explains why this patient has achlorhydria (in contrast to Zollinger-Ellison syndrome)?
AVIPoma secretes somatostatin, which inhibits gastrin release
BVIP directly inhibits parietal cell HCl secretion by activating inhibitory G proteins
CVIP activates gastric D cells to increase somatostatin production
DThe massive diarrhea causes secondary parietal cell damage and atrophy
EVIP converts gastrin-secreting cells into somatostatin-secreting cells
B — VIP directly inhibits parietal cell HCl. VIP activates Gs-coupled receptors on parietal cells → ↑cAMP → paradoxically ↓HCl secretion (VIP is primarily inhibitory to acid cells but stimulatory to intestinal crypts). The massive diarrhea is from VIP-stimulated CFTR-mediated Cl⁻ secretion in intestinal crypt cells. This is opposite to ZES (↑↑acid). The WDHA triad is pathognomonic: Watery diarrhea + Hypokalemia + AChlorhydria.
Question 4 of 10 — Cholera Pathophysiology
A patient with severe Vibrio cholerae infection presents with voluminous "rice-water" diarrhea (>20 L/day) and severe hypokalemia. Despite high stool osmolarity and massive fluid loss, the patient is treated successfully with oral rehydration solution (ORS) containing glucose and sodium. Which of the following BEST explains why ORS is effective despite the massive secretory diarrhea?
AGlucose directly inhibits cholera toxin and prevents further cAMP generation
BSodium and glucose increase osmotic pressure in the intestinal lumen, driving water absorption
CSGLT1-mediated Na+ and glucose cotransport is independent of cAMP and CFTR, allowing water absorption despite active Cl− secretion
DGlucose binds cholera toxin and neutralizes it in the intestinal lumen
ESodium activates alternative water channels that bypass CFTR
C — SGLT1 bypass mechanism. Cholera toxin ADP-ribosylates Gs → constitutive ↑cAMP → permanent CFTR-mediated Cl⁻ secretion. However, SGLT1 (Na⁺-glucose cotransporter) on the apical membrane is NOT cAMP-dependent → co-transporting Na⁺ + glucose still drives water absorption via osmosis. ORS (with 1:1 molar ratio Na⁺:glucose) maximizes this intact pathway → compensates for secretory losses. This is why ORS is the mainstay of cholera treatment (prevents hypovolemic shock) rather than trying to stop the diarrhea.
Question 5 of 10 — Pernicious Anemia Pathophysiology
A 68-year-old woman presents with megaloblastic anemia, paresthesias, and ataxia. Serum B12 is severely reduced. Intrinsic factor antibodies are positive. Which of the following statements BEST explains why her B12 is low but she also has ELEVATED fasting serum gastrin?
AB12 deficiency directly stimulates G cells to increase gastrin production
BAutoimmune destruction of parietal cells → ↓IF → ↓B12 absorption AND ↓acid secretion → loss of acid feedback inhibition on G cells → ↑gastrin
CGastrin directly inhibits intrinsic factor production, causing B12 deficiency
DElevated gastrin causes atrophic gastritis, which reduces IF production
EB12 supplementation will normalize gastrin levels but not reverse neurologic damage
B — Loss of acid feedback → ↑gastrin. Pernicious anemia is autoimmune destruction of parietal cells → ↓IF (B12 absorption failure) + ↓HCl (acid secretion). Normally, low pH (acid) provides negative feedback on G cells. Without acid → loss of feedback → ↑↑fasting gastrin. This is a classic trap: high gastrin in pernicious anemia can mimic ZES, but the gastric pH is HIGH (achlorhydria), not low. B12 replacement does NOT reverse neurologic damage (subacute combined degeneration) if chronic → urgent treatment needed.
Question 6 of 10 — Carcinoid Syndrome + Liver Metabolism
A patient with a small bowel carcinoid tumor presents with flushing, diarrhea, and bronchospasm. Serum chromogranin A and urine 5-HIAA are both elevated. However, at the time of diagnosis, the patient has PRIMARY carcinoid (in small bowel) with NO liver metastases. Which of the following BEST explains why this patient is symptomatic despite the absence of liver metastases?
ASerotonin is produced directly in the bloodstream, bypassing portal circulation
BThe tumor produces substance P and tachykinins in addition to serotonin
CSmall bowel carcinoid can invade the mesenteric veins, draining directly to systemic circulation, bypassing the liver filter
DSerotonin is completely resistant to hepatic monoamine oxidase (MAO) metabolism
EThe liver is not a primary filter for serotonin; the kidneys metabolize it instead
C — Mesenteric vein invasion bypasses hepatic metabolism. Carcinoid syndrome classically requires LIVER METASTASES because: normal primary tumors → serotonin into portal blood → liver first-pass metabolism by MAO → inactivation. HOWEVER, direct mesenteric vein invasion or hepatic metastases allow serotonin to reach systemic circulation unopposed. The fact that this patient is symptomatic WITHOUT documented liver mets suggests mesenteric invasion. Elevated urine 5-HIAA confirms serotonin production/metabolism. Treatment: octreotide (somatostatin analog) blocks serotonin release.
Question 7 of 10 — PPI Mechanism + Timing
A patient is prescribed omeprazole for PUD. The patient takes the medication at various times of day (sometimes with meals, sometimes 8 hours after eating). Which statement BEST explains why timing of PPI administration matters for efficacy?
APPIs are absorbed better in the acidic environment of a full stomach
BPPIs are prodrugs activated by acid in the parietal cell canaliculus; they require active H⁺/K⁺ ATPase pumps, which are maximally active during the postprandial period
CPPIs must be taken with food to prevent gastric irritation
DTiming does not matter; PPI efficacy is independent of meal timing
EPPIs should be taken in the fasting state to maximize absorption and liver metabolism
B — Require active pumps at mealtimes. PPIs are PRODRUGS → converted to active form only in the acidic canaliculus of activated parietal cells. Taking PPI BEFORE MEALS ensures absorption into the parietal cell when pumps are maximally active (during mealtime acid secretion). This allows maximum irreversible inhibition of H⁺/K⁺ ATPase. Taking PPI hours after eating (when pumps are inactive/downregulated) reduces efficacy. Optimal dosing: 30 min before breakfast (most potent acid suppression).
Question 8 of 10 — Gastric Secretion Phases
A patient undergoes truncal vagotomy for peptic ulcer disease. Prior to surgery, the patient's acid secretion is divided into three phases: cephalic (30-40%), gastric (50-60%), and intestinal (5-10%). After truncal vagotomy, which phase of acid secretion is MOST severely impaired?
ACephalic phase — vagus (CN X) is the sole mediator of cephalic gastrin and direct ACh secretion
BGastric phase — gastrin production requires vagal stimulation to be initiated
CIntestinal phase — secretin and CCK require vagal potentiation to be effective
DAll three phases are equally impaired because the vagus controls all GI secretions
ENone of the phases are significantly affected because local reflexes compensate
A — Cephalic phase most impaired. Cephalic phase (sight/smell/thought → vagus) depends ENTIRELY on the vagus nerve (both direct ACh on parietal cells AND ACh/GRP on G cells → gastrin). Truncal vagotomy eliminates cephalic phase almost completely. Gastric phase (local/vagal reflexes) is partially preserved by local mechanisms. Intestinal phase (secretin, CCK) is actually PRESERVED or enhanced post-vagotomy (intestinal inhibition is unopposed). Complication: vagotomy causes gastric stasis → dumping syndrome → requires pyloroplasty drainage procedure.
Question 9 of 10 — Pancreatic Insufficiency + CFTR
A 6-year-old child with cystic fibrosis (CFTR mutation) presents with chronic steatorrhea, ADEK vitamin deficiencies, and failure to thrive. Which of the following BEST explains why pancreatic enzyme supplementation (with meals) is the primary treatment?
ACFTR mutation directly prevents enzyme synthesis by pancreatic acinar cells
BCFTR mutation in pancreatic DUCTS → viscous secretions → ductal obstruction → acinar autodigestion → loss of functional enzymes; supplementation bypasses the need for endogenous enzymes
CPancreatic enzymes are not actually deficient; steatorrhea is from intestinal CFTR dysfunction
DCFTR mutation causes autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells
ESteatorrhea in CF is from excessive fat intake, not pancreatic insufficiency
B — Viscous ducts → obstruction → acinar atrophy. In CF, CFTR on ductal epithelium is nonfunctional → thick, viscous pancreatic secretions → ductal obstruction → progressive acinar autodigestion/atrophy → ↓pancreatic lipase/amylase/proteases → malabsorption. Enzyme replacement supplements what the damaged pancreas cannot produce. Additionally, fat-soluble vitamin (ADEK) absorption requires adequate lipase, so deficiencies are inevitable without supplementation. Diagnosis: fecal elastase-1 <200 μg/g. Prognosis has improved dramatically with modern CFTR modulators.
Question 10 of 10 — H. pylori Virulence: CagA + Cancer Risk
Two patients are both infected with H. pylori. Patient A is infected with a CagA-positive strain; Patient B is infected with a CagA-negative strain. Both patients have chronic antral inflammation. Which of the following BEST explains why Patient A has a significantly higher risk of gastric cancer?
ACagA directly mutates tumor suppressor genes like TP53
BCagA is a WHO Class I carcinogen that translocates into epithelial cells via type IV secretion, activates intracellular signaling pathways (Wnt/β-catenin), and promotes chronic inflammation → dysplasia
CCagA produces more serotonin than other H. pylori strains, leading to carcinoid transformation
DCagA is only virulent in patients with MEN1 syndrome
ECagA causes MALT lymphoma but is protective against gastric adenocarcinoma
B — Type IV secretion + oncogenic signaling. CagA (cytotoxin-associated gene A) is a type IV secretion toxin that directly translocates into gastric epithelial cells → activates Wnt/β-catenin signaling and other oncogenic pathways → chronic proliferation + apoptosis resistance. It is classified as WHO IARC Class I carcinogen. CagA+ strains are associated with markedly ↑gastric cancer risk, MALT lymphoma, and severe atrophic gastritis. Eradication of H. pylori (especially CagA+ strains) is associated with ↓gastric cancer rates in populations with high prevalence.

Vignette 1 — Zollinger-Ellison
A 48-year-old man with severe, refractory peptic ulcer disease (duodenal + gastric ulcers) and chronic watery diarrhea presents to your clinic. He has been on standard-dose omeprazole for 3 months without improvement. Serum studies reveal gastrin 2,000 pg/mL (N <100), gastric pH 1.1. Secretin stimulation causes paradoxical gastrin increase to 2,400. CT shows a 2 cm mass in the pancreatic head. What is the BEST treatment approach?
AH2 blocker (famotidine) monotherapy + antacids
BHigh-dose PPI (omeprazole 40 mg twice daily) + surgical exploration for gastrinoma resection
CAntacid + dietary modification + watchful waiting
DOctreotide monotherapy
ETruncal vagotomy
B — High-dose PPI + surgical resection. This is classic ZES: fasting gastrin >1000, acid pH <2, paradoxical secretin response, refractory to standard-dose meds. High-dose PPIs (40-80 mg) are now first-line (replace old vagotomy). Octreotide is adjunctive for symptom control. Surgical resection of the gastrinoma is curative but depends on localization and metastasis status. MEN1 screening is mandatory (60-90% of gastrinomas are MEN1-associated).
Vignette 2 — Verner-Morrison (VIPoma)
A 54-year-old woman with a 1-year history of intractable watery diarrhea (>10 L/day), severe hypokalemia (K+ 2.1), and achlorhydria (gastric pH 6.5) is found on imaging to have a 4 cm pancreatic tumor. Her gastrin level is normal (48 pg/mL). Serum VIP is markedly elevated at 850 pg/mL (normal <75). What mechanism explains her ACHLORHYDRIA, in contrast to Zollinger-Ellison syndrome?
AThe massive diarrhea has damaged the parietal cells over time
BVIP directly inhibits parietal cell acid secretion while stimulating crypt cell Cl⁻ secretion via Gs coupling
CTumor secretes somatostatin, which suppresses both acid and intestinal fluid
DThe VIP tumor is in the corpus of the pancreas, causing local gastric ischemia
EShe has concurrent H. pylori corpus gastritis
B — VIP is dual-action: parietal inhibitor + crypt stimulator. VIP activates Gs-coupled receptors. In parietal cells, Gs → ↑cAMP → paradoxically ↓HCl (VIP has inhibitory signaling on acid cells). In crypts, Gs → ↑cAMP → CFTR activation → maximal Cl⁻ secretion. Result: achlorhydria + massive secretory diarrhea (WDHA triad). Treatment: octreotide (somatostatin analog) blocks VIP release + splanchnic vasoconstriction. Prognosis: often advanced at diagnosis due to late presentation.
Vignette 3 — H. pylori Antral Infection
A 42-year-old man with H. pylori infection (antral predominant per biopsy) develops a duodenal ulcer. On endoscopy, you note inflammation primarily in the antrum with sparing of the gastric body/fundus. Gastric pH is very low (1.2). His fasting serum gastrin is elevated at 180 pg/mL. Which finding BEST explains his elevated gastrin in the setting of low gastric pH?
AGastrin-secreting cells have proliferated due to chronic infection
BH. pylori damaged antral D cells → ↓somatostatin → loss of G-cell inhibition → ↑gastrin (normal feedback is overwhelmed by local inflammation)
CAcid normally suppresses gastrin, but in this patient acid feedback is preserved (so gastrin should be low)
DThe duodenal ulcer itself stimulates gastrin secretion
EH. pylori antral infection causes achlorhydria, not increased acid
B — Local D-cell damage overrides acid feedback. Antral H. pylori preferentially damages D cells (somatostatin-secreting) → loss of tonic G-cell inhibition → ↑↑gastrin despite low pH. This is a "local" mechanism: the inflammatory environment overrides global acid feedback. The massive gastrin → massive HCl → duodenal ulcer (90% of H. pylori ulcers are duodenal, not gastric). Eradication therapy (triple or quadruple regimens) restores D-cell function and ulcer healing.
Vignette 4 — Cholera & ORS Paradox
A 35-year-old traveler returns from Bangladesh with severe cholera (confirmed by Vibrio cholerae culture). He develops massive secretory diarrhea (>20 L/day), severe dehydration, and hypokalemia. IV fluids are given, but the volume is hard to keep up with. Despite the MASSIVE secretory losses, oral rehydration solution (ORS) with glucose and sodium is the cornerstone of treatment. How can ORS be effective against secretory diarrhea?
AORS directly neutralizes cholera toxin in the lumen
BSGLT1 (Na⁺-glucose cotransport) is cAMP-independent; ORS maximizes intact absorption pathway despite CFTR-mediated Cl⁻ secretion
CGlucose competitively inhibits adenylyl cyclase
DORS hyperosmolarity pulls fluid out of the epithelium
ESodium alone would be sufficient; glucose is just a taste enhancer
B — SGLT1 bypass: the brilliant clinical insight. Cholera toxin ADP-ribosylates Gs → constitutive ↑cAMP → permanent CFTR opening → Cl⁻ loss. BUT SGLT1 (the apical Na⁺-glucose cotransporter on intestinal epithelium) is NOT coupled to cAMP or CFTR. Taking 1:1 molar Na⁺:glucose allows osmotic water reabsorption via SGLT1 even while toxin-driven Cl⁻ is secreted. This "trick" saves millions of lives (prevents hypovolemic shock) without stopping the diarrhea. IV fluids are still needed for severe cases, but ORS buys time and is much cheaper.
Vignette 5 — Pernicious Anemia Trap
A 72-year-old woman with pernicious anemia (anti-IF antibodies positive) has megaloblastic anemia, paresthesias, and ataxia. Her serum gastrin is 420 pg/mL (elevated). Her gastric pH is 5.8 (high). Which diagnosis would be a MAJOR TRAP if you confused her with Zollinger-Ellison syndrome based only on elevated gastrin?
AYou would miss that she needs B12 replacement, not acid suppression
BBoth A and the critical insight: ZES has LOW gastric pH, pernicious anemia has HIGH pH (achlorhydria). The elevated gastrin in PA is from LOSS of acid feedback (no acid → no G-cell suppression). Treating with PPIs would be wrong.
CPernicious anemia doesn't cause elevated gastrin at all; this is a lab error
DAtorvastatin should be given to lower gastrin
EHer paresthesias prove she has diabetic neuropathy, not pernicious anemia
B — Gastric pH is the KEY differentiator! Pernicious anemia: Autoimmune parietal cell destruction → ↓IF (↓B12 abs) + ↓HCl (achlorhydria) → LOSS of acid negative feedback → ↑↑fasting gastrin. pH HIGH (5-7), not low. This is a classic Step 1 TRAP. Zollinger-Ellison: Gastrinoma → ↑↑HCl → multiple ulcers + diarrhea. pH LOW (<2), gastrin >1000. The pH difference is diagnostic! PA requires B12 + IF injections + monitoring for gastric cancer (↑risk from chronic achlorhydria).
Vignette 6 — Carcinoid Syndrome
A 58-year-old man with a small bowel carcinoid tumor presents with flushing, diarrhea, and right-sided heart murmur (tricuspid regurgitation). Serum chromogranin A is elevated; urine 5-HIAA is markedly elevated at 180 nmol/24h (normal <8). Interestingly, he has NO documented liver metastases on CT/MRI. How is he symptomatic without liver mets?
AHis primary tumor is actually in the stomach, not small bowel, so serotonin enters systemic blood directly
BThe small bowel tumor has invaded mesenteric veins, allowing serotonin to bypass hepatic first-pass metabolism (normally inactivated by MAO)
CHis elevated 5-HIAA proves there IS occult hepatic metastasis that was missed on imaging
DThe tricuspid valve disease is not from serotonin but from endocarditis
ECarcinoid syndrome can present with primary tumors (liver mets are a myth)
B — Mesenteric vein invasion is the KEY. Classic carcinoid syndrome requires liver metastases because normal primary tumors secrete serotonin into portal blood → hepatic MAO metabolism → inactivation → NO systemic effects. HOWEVER, if the tumor invades mesenteric veins or has hepatic mets, serotonin reaches systemic circulation unopposed → flushing, diarrhea, bronchospasm, right-heart fibrosis. This patient's high 5-HIAA (serotonin metabolite) + symptoms WITHOUT documented liver mets suggests mesenteric invasion. Imaging should look for tumor invasion of vessels. Treatment: octreotide (somatostatin analog) blocks serotonin release; consider surgical resection if resectable.
Vignette 7 — H. pylori CagA Virulence
Two patients with H. pylori infection: Patient A is CagA-positive strain; Patient B is CagA-negative strain. Both have chronic antral inflammation. Which patient has significantly higher gastric cancer risk, and why?
APatient B — CagA-negative strains are more aggressive and cause atrophic gastritis
BPatient A — CagA is a WHO Class I carcinogen that translocates into epithelial cells via type IV secretion, activates Wnt/β-catenin signaling, and promotes chronic dysplasia
CBoth have equal cancer risk; strain virulence doesn't matter, only duration of infection
DPatient B — CagA-negative strains produce more VacA toxin (more destructive)
ENeither patient needs treatment; H. pylori eradication doesn't prevent cancer
B — CagA is the oncogenic game-changer. CagA+ strains are classified as WHO IARC Class I carcinogens. CagA protein translocates into gastric epithelial cells via a type IV secretion system → activates Wnt/β-catenin and other oncogenic pathways → chronic cellular proliferation + apoptosis resistance. CagA+ strains carry dramatically higher risk of gastric cancer and MALT lymphoma. Eradication of H. pylori (especially CagA+ strains) reduces gastric cancer incidence in high-prevalence populations (e.g., East Asia). This is why screening and treatment in endemic regions is emphasized.
Vignette 8 — Pancreatic Insufficiency & CFTR Pathophysiology
A 7-year-old boy with cystic fibrosis presents with failure to thrive, chronic steatorrhea (oily stools), and fat-soluble vitamin deficiencies (A, D, E, K). His sweat chloride test is 95 mEq/L (diagnostic >60). Fecal elastase-1 is 85 μg/g (low; normal >200). He is started on pancreatic enzyme replacement with meals. Why does enzyme replacement work despite his pancreatic tissue being structurally intact?
AHis pancreatic acini haven't yet been destroyed; the problem is purely ductal obstruction that will resolve with time
BCFTR mutation in pancreatic duct epithelium → viscous secretions → ductal plugging → acinar autodigestion. Enzyme replacement bypasses the need for endogenous enzyme production by the damaged pancreas
CEnzymes are absorbed systemically and recirculate back to the intestine
DCF patients don't actually have pancreatic insufficiency; steatorrhea is from intestinal CFTR dysfunction alone
EEnzyme replacement is just supportive; the real cure is lung transplantation
B — Duct obstruction → acinar atrophy is the mechanism. In CF, CFTR on pancreatic duct cells is nonfunctional → thick, viscous secretions → chronic ductal obstruction → progressive acinar autodigestion and atrophy → progressive loss of functional pancreatic enzyme capacity. Enzyme replacement (with meals) supplementsthe missing lipase, amylase, proteases → improves fat/protein absorption and fat-soluble vitamin uptake. Diagnosis: fecal elastase-1 <200 μg/g (this patient's 85 is clearly abnormal). Modern CFTR modulators (lumacaftor, elexacaftor) have dramatically improved CF prognosis by partially restoring CFTR function in the pancreas, allowing some patients to normalize pancreatic function.
Classic Step 1 Traps: These are frequently missed concepts that seem intuitive but have a critical twist. Test your true understanding.
Trap 1 — Elevated Gastrin ≠ Zollinger-Ellison
Student sees elevated fasting gastrin (>150) and jumps to ZES diagnosis. TRAP: Pernicious anemia ALSO causes elevated gastrin!
True — But the KEY is gastric pH! In ZES: gastrin is ↑↑↑ AND pH is LOW (<2) because gastrinoma drives massive HCl. In pernicious anemia: gastrin is elevated but pH is HIGH (4-6, achlorhydria) because autoimmune destruction of parietal cells → loss of acid feedback inhibition on G cells. The pH difference is diagnostic. Students who only remember "high gastrin = ZES" will miss pernicious anemia (and won't give B12!).
Trap 2 — PPI Timing Doesn't Matter (FALSE TRAP)
Students often think PPI timing is arbitrary: "Just take it whenever." TRAP: Timing DOES matter significantly for efficacy!
True — PPIs are PRODRUGS that require ACTIVE pumps. PPIs must be activated by acid in the parietal cell canaliculus. The H⁺/K⁺ ATPase pumps are maximally active DURING MEALS (stimulated acid secretion). Taking PPI 30 min before breakfast = maximum pump activity when PPI arrives → maximum irreversible inhibition. Taking PPI 8 hours after eating = pumps downregulated → reduced efficacy. This is WHY optimal dosing is "before breakfast" for GERD prophylaxis.
Trap 3 — H. pylori Always Causes Duodenal Ulcer (FALSE)
Many students: "H. pylori → duodenal ulcer always." TRAP: The location of H. pylori infection determines ulcer location!
True — Site of infection matters! Antral H. pylori (90%) → damages D cells → ↑gastrin → duodenal ulcer. But corpus H. pylori → damages parietal cells → ↓acid → gastric ulcer or no ulcer, BUT atrophic gastritis + gastric cancer risk. One patient with H. pylori can have EITHER duodenal or gastric ulcer depending on where the infection predominantly lives. This is a critical pathophysiologic distinction that changes management.
Trap 4 — Cholera Diarrhea = Stop Hydration (FALSE TRAP)
Student sees massive diarrhea and thinks: "More water intake will make it worse." TRAP: The diarrhea is SECRETORY, not osmotic. ORS (glucose + Na⁺) actually helps!
True — SGLT1 is the lifesaver. Cholera toxin causes SECRETORY diarrhea via CFTR (can't be stopped by fasting). ORS works because SGLT1 (the glucose-Na⁺ cotransporter) is cAMP-independent → still absorbs Na⁺ + glucose + water even while CFTR bleeds Cl⁻. This is why ORS is the global standard (prevents death from shock) and why we DON'T restrict fluids in cholera (we encourage oral intake!). Students who confuse secretory with osmotic diarrhea will give the wrong advice.
Trap 5 — Carcinoid Syndrome = Always Liver Mets (FALSE TRAP)
Classic teaching: "Carcinoid syndrome only with liver mets." TRAP: Mesenteric vein invasion can cause symptoms WITHOUT hepatic mets!
True — The rule is "liver mets OR mesenteric invasion." Normal small bowel carcinoid → serotonin into portal blood → hepatic MAO inactivation → no symptoms. But if tumor invades mesenteric veins OR has hepatic mets, serotonin bypasses the liver filter → systemic effects. This patient could have flushing + diarrhea WITHOUT documented liver mets if mesenteric veins are invaded. Elevated urine 5-HIAA proves serotonin production. The Step 1 trap is assuming liver mets are required.
Trap 6 — WDHA Triad = Cholera (FALSE)
Student sees "watery diarrhea + hypokalemia + ?" and thinks cholera. TRAP: VIPoma ALSO causes WDHA (with A = achlorhydria, not something else)!
True — Both cause "A" but with opposite mechanisms! WDHA (Watery Diarrhea, Hypokalemia, AChlorhydria) is VIPoma's classic triad. VIP inhibits parietal cells (→ achlorhydria) while stimulating crypt cells (→ massive diarrhea via CFTR). Cholera diarrhea is also secretory but cholera does NOT suppress acid (acid is NORMAL in cholera). The presence of achlorhydria is the KEY discriminator: VIPoma = achlorhydria; cholera = normal/↑acid. Step 1 often tests whether you know that "A" specifically means achlorhydria, not just any "diarrhea complication."
Trap 7 — Cystic Fibrosis Pancreas Is "Destroyed" (Misleading)
Student thinks CF = pancreas just fails to make enzymes." TRAP: The acini can MAKE enzymes, but can't DELIVER them due to ductal obstruction!
True — The problem is TRANSPORT, not SYNTHESIS (early on). In CF, CFTR on duct epithelium is broken → viscous secretions → ductal obstruction → backup of enzymes → chronic activation inside the ducts → autodigestion + atrophy. Eventually the acini ARE destroyed, but early CF has functional enzyme-producing cells that just can't get enzymes OUT. This is why enzyme replacement works: you're bypassing the ductal bottleneck. This distinction matters for understanding WHY enzymes are required even when acini seem "intact," and why CFTR modulators (that restore some CFTR function) can partially restore pancreatic function in newer therapies.
Trap 8 — Vagotomy Eliminates ALL Acid Secretion (FALSE)
Student thinks: "Vagotomy = no more acid." TRAP: Cephalic phase is lost, but gastric + intestinal phases PARTIALLY persist!
True — Vagotomy is NOT a complete acid-blocking surgery. Cephalic phase (30-40% of baseline) is vagus-dependent → completely lost post-vagotomy. Gastric phase (50-60%) is partially preserved by local gastrin + distension reflexes. Intestinal phase (5-10%) is actually ENHANCED post-vagotomy (inhibitory vagal tone is gone, so secretin/CCK inhibition is unopposed). Total acid output post-vagotomy is reduced ~50-70%, which is enough for ulcer healing but not the "complete achlorhydria" students expect. This is why modern treatment uses high-dose PPIs instead of vagotomy.
Trap 9 — High Gastrin in PA = Treat with PPIs (WRONG!)
Student sees elevated gastrin in pernicious anemia and prescribes more acid suppression. TRAP: She needs B12, not less acid!
True — PA treatment is B12 + IF replacement, NOT acid suppression. Giving PPIs to a PA patient with high gastrin would be HARMFUL: it would further reduce already-low acid → worsen achlorhydria → worsen absorption (even with B12) and increase gastric cancer risk. The high gastrin in PA is a SYMPTOM of lost acid feedback, not the problem to treat. Correct management: B12 injections (IF antibodies prevent oral absorption), monitor for gastric cancer/parietal cell carcinoma, monitor gastric pH trends. This is a deadly trap if you treat the lab value instead of the disease.
Trap 10 — H. pylori CagA = More VacA (FALSE LOGIC)
Student confuses H. pylori virulence factors: "If strain has CagA, it also has more VacA." TRAP: CagA and VacA are independent virulence determinants!
True — CagA is the MOST critical carcinogenic factor (WHO Class I carcinogen). CagA and VacA are separate toxins. A strain can be CagA+ VacA+ (most aggressive), CagA+ VacA- (still very aggressive), or CagA- VacA+ (less aggressive). The PRESENCE of CagA is what drives the ↑↑ cancer risk and MALT lymphoma via type IV secretion and Wnt/β-catenin signaling. This is why CagA+ strains in Japan/Korea drive government-level screening/eradication programs. VacA is apoptosis-inducing but secondary to CagA's oncogenic effects. Step 1 focuses on CagA as the key carcinogen.

High-Yield Testing Notes: Key patterns to recognize on exams. Badges indicate trap concepts, buzzword clusters, and testing-focused insights.
TESTING Hirschsprung vs. Achalasia — The Neural Crest Distinction
Both test ENS dysfunction but from different angles. Hirschsprung = failure of neural crest MIGRATION (embryology focus). Achalasia = loss of inhibitory NEURONS at LES (neurotransmitter focus). Step 1 loves pairing these because the underlying pathology (absent inhibitory innervation) is conceptually similar, but the location, age of onset, and management differ completely.
TRAP The Dilated vs. Narrow Segment Trap
The #1 exam trap for Hirschsprung: students assume the dilated segment is abnormal. Wrong — the NARROW segment lacks ganglia (aganglionic, abnormal). The dilated segment is NORMAL bowel that has expanded from backed-up stool. Biopsy of the narrow segment shows absent ganglion cells. This is tested repeatedly in different formats.
BUZZWORD Carcinoid Syndrome Buzzword Cluster
When a vignette includes ANY combination of: flushing + diarrhea + wheezing + right-sided heart murmur + hepatic masses → carcinoid with liver mets. Confirmatory test = elevated urine 5-HIAA. The liver mets detail is key — without it, serotonin is degraded by first-pass hepatic metabolism and the syndrome does not manifest.
TESTING Secretory Tumor Differentiation
Step 1 differentiates gastrinoma, VIPoma, and carcinoid by their clinical fingerprint. Gastrinoma: refractory ulcers + paradoxical secretin test. VIPoma: WDHA (watery diarrhea, hypokalemia, achlorhydria). Carcinoid: flushing + right-sided heart disease + liver mets. Each has a unique lab marker (gastrin, VIP, 5-HIAA). They often appear as "which tumor" questions with a clinical vignette.
TRAP Naloxone vs. Methylnaltrexone — BBB Trap
Exam trap: a patient on morphine with constipation — which drug treats it without reversing analgesia? Naloxone crosses the BBB → reverses everything (pain control lost). Methylnaltrexone does NOT cross the BBB → blocks peripheral μ-receptors only → restores GI motility while preserving CNS analgesia. This is tested as a pharmacology application question, not pure recall.
BUZZWORD Pellagra from Carcinoid — Tryptophan Diversion
Carcinoid tumors divert tryptophan toward serotonin synthesis, depleting the pool available for niacin (vitamin B3) production. Result: pellagra (the 3 Ds — diarrhea, dermatitis, dementia). Buzzword cluster on Step 1: carcinoid + pellagra-like skin changes + diarrhea + psychiatric symptoms. The connection is that serotonin overproduction starves the niacin pathway. Serum marker: chromogranin A (neuroendocrine tumors, APUD cells). Right-sided heart valvular disease occurs because lungs contain MAO that metabolizes serotonin before it reaches the left heart.
TESTING Parietal Cell Three Pathways — Why PPIs Beat H2 Blockers
Three independent pathways stimulate acid secretion: histamine (H2 → cAMP), ACh (M3 → IP3/Ca²⁺), and gastrin (CCK-B → IP3/Ca²⁺). H2 blockers only block one arm, which is why they mainly suppress nocturnal acid (histamine-dominant). PPIs block the H⁺/K⁺ ATPase — the final common pathway where all three signals converge — making them effective regardless of the stimulus. Step 1 tests this as: "Why is a PPI more effective than an H2 blocker?" or presents a patient failing H2 blocker therapy and asks for the next pharmacologic step.

Pharmacology
GI Drug Targets — Step 1 Pharmacology Summary
The GI pharmacology tested on Step 1 maps directly to receptor physiology:

Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs): Block H⁺/K⁺ ATPase on parietal cells. Require activation at low pH. Used for GERD, PUD, ZES, H. pylori eradication.

H2 blockers: Block histamine H2 receptors on parietal cells → ↓cAMP → ↓HCl. Less potent than PPIs.

Ondansetron: 5-HT3 antagonist → blocks serotonin-mediated vagal afferents → antiemetic. No GI motility effect.

Metoclopramide: D2 + 5-HT4 receptor modulator → prokinetic + antiemetic. Crosses BBB → risk of tardive dyskinesia with prolonged use.

Atropine: Muscarinic (M3) antagonist → ↓GI motility, ↓secretion. Used in organophosphate poisoning and bradycardia.

Methylnaltrexone: Peripheral μ-opioid antagonist → reverses opioid-induced constipation/ileus without reversing analgesia.
GI Physiology + Boards
Fluid Balance — Clinical Consequences
The scale of GI fluid handling creates predictable clinical scenarios:

Vomiting → Metabolic alkalosis + hypokalemia: Loss of HCl; volume depletion → aldosterone → K⁺ wasting + "paradoxical aciduria" (kidneys retain Na⁺ + HCO₃⁻, excrete K⁺ + H⁺).

Diarrhea → Metabolic acidosis + hypokalemia: Loss of HCO₃⁻-rich intestinal/pancreatic fluid; colon secretes K⁺ normally → hypokalemia amplified with high stool output.

Cholera → Isotonic volume depletion: Vibrio cholerae toxin → ↑cAMP → massive Cl⁻/water secretion. ORS works via SGLT1 (Na⁺-glucose cotransport is cAMP-independent). Rice-water stools, up to 20 L/day.
Acid-Base from GI losses:

• Fistula (high-output, pancreatic/SI) → lose HCO₃⁻ → metabolic acidosis
• Both cause hypokalemia (different mechanisms)
