Question 1 · LO 1
A 34-year-old woman is being evaluated for fat malabsorption after a small bowel resection. Her physician explains that dietary lipids have difficulty crossing a specific zone adjacent to enterocytes before they can be absorbed. Which of the following best describes the primary reason lipids cannot cross this zone without assistance?
Which of the following best explains why lipids cannot cross the unstirred water layer without bile?
✓ B — Lipids are hydrophobic and cannot diffuse through the aqueous UWL. The UWL is an aqueous zone created by slow laminar flow near the gut wall and mucus/HCO₃⁻ secretion. Lipids aggregate into hydrophobic fat droplets in the aqueous lumen — they cannot diffuse across this water barrier. Bile (amphipathic) forms micelles that encapsulate the lipids in a hydrophobic core, presenting a hydrophilic exterior that can travel through the UWL. Lipid absorption does not require ATP-dependent transporters (passive diffusion at the enterocyte) and there is no charge repulsion involved.
Question 2 · LO 1
A gastroenterologist explains the role of bile in lipid absorption to a medical student. She states that bile does not chemically break down fat, but it performs essential steps that allow fat to be digested and absorbed. Which of the following best describes the two roles of bile in lipid absorption?
Which of the following best describes the two roles of bile?
✓ C — Emulsification + micelle formation. Bile acts as a detergent: (1) it emulsifies large fat globules into smaller droplets in the duodenum, increasing the surface area for lipase to act on, and (2) it forms amphipathic micelles that package lipid fragments (FA, MG, fat-soluble vitamins) and carry them through the aqueous UWL to the enterocyte brush border. Bile does NOT hydrolyze triglycerides (that is pancreatic lipase — A is wrong), does not activate lipase by phosphorylation, and does not transport fatty acids via endocytosis (E is wrong). Alkaline pH comes from HCO₃⁻, but that is not a primary role of bile in lipid absorption.
Question 3 · LO 1
A researcher studies the fate of bile molecules during lipid absorption. After a micelle delivers fatty acids to an enterocyte, what happens to the bile molecules?
What happens to bile molecules after lipid delivery to the enterocyte?
✓ C — Bile remains in the lumen and is recycled at the terminal ileum. After delivering lipids to enterocytes, bile stays in the intestinal lumen (it does not enter the cell). It continues to the terminal ileum, where it is actively reabsorbed via the ASBT transporter into portal circulation, returning to the liver — this is enterohepatic circulation. Only ~10% of bile is lost in feces per cycle; the body recycles the same bile pool 6–10×/day. Secondary bile acid conversion occurs in the colon by bacterial action, not the jejunum (E is wrong).
Question 4 · LO 2
A 52-year-old man is started on atorvastatin for hypercholesterolemia. His physician explains that the drug blocks the rate-limiting enzyme in cholesterol synthesis. Which of the following is this enzyme, and which bile component does its inhibition most directly reduce?
Which enzyme is blocked by statins and what bile component is most directly affected?
✓ B — HMG-CoA reductase; cholesterol backbone of primary bile acids. HMG-CoA reductase is the rate-limiting enzyme in cholesterol synthesis. Statins inhibit this → ↓ cholesterol → hepatocytes upregulate LDL receptors → ↓ serum LDL. Since primary bile acids are synthesized from a cholesterol backbone, statin therapy also reduces primary bile acid synthesis. The rate-limiting step for bile acid synthesis FROM cholesterol is cholesterol 7α-hydroxylase, but HMG-CoA reductase controls the upstream cholesterol supply.
Question 5 · LO 2
A medical student learns that primary bile acids are amphipathic because they are conjugated with an amino acid. A patient with a genetic defect in this conjugation step would most likely present with which of the following?
What is the consequence of defective bile acid amino acid conjugation?
✓ B — Fat malabsorption and steatorrhea. Conjugation with glycine (→ glycocholic acid) or taurine (→ taurocholic acid) creates the hydrophilic head of the amphipathic bile molecule — essential for micelle formation. Without conjugation, bile cannot form proper micelles → lipids cannot cross the UWL → fat malabsorption → steatorrhea + fat-soluble vitamin (A, D, E, K) deficiency. Bilirubin excretion uses UDP-glucuronosyltransferase for conjugation with glucuronic acid, not amino acid conjugation — so jaundice would NOT be the primary problem (A is wrong).
Question 6 · LO 2
A 45-year-old woman presents with progressive fatigue, pruritus, and jaundice. Labs show elevated alkaline phosphatase, anti-mitochondrial antibodies (AMA), and a liver biopsy reveals granulomatous destruction of intrahepatic bile ducts. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis, and what is the expected effect on fat absorption?
What is the diagnosis and how does it affect fat absorption?
✓ B — Primary biliary cholangitis; steatorrhea + fat-soluble vitamin deficiency. PBC = middle-aged woman + pruritus + AMA (highly specific) + granulomatous destruction of intrahepatic bile ducts. Bile duct destruction → cholestasis → ↓ bile in duodenum → impaired micelle formation → steatorrhea + fat-soluble vitamin (A, D, E, K) deficiency. PSC (A) = young man + UC + p-ANCA + "beads on string" on ERCP + extrahepatic involvement. PBC treatment = ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA).
Question 7 · LO 3
A 28-year-old woman eats a high-fat meal. Thirty minutes later, she develops episodic cramping right upper quadrant pain. Imaging reveals multiple echogenic foci in the gallbladder with posterior acoustic shadowing. Which hormone triggered her symptoms, and what are its three main effects on biliary physiology?
Which hormone caused her symptoms and what are its 3 biliary actions?
✓ C — CCK; ↑ bile production, gallbladder contraction, sphincter of Oddi relaxation. Fat + protein in duodenum → I-cells release CCK. CCK's 3 biliary effects: (1) ↑ bile production at hepatocytes, (2) rhythmic gallbladder contraction (releasing concentrated stored bile), (3) relaxation of sphincter of Oddi (bile flows into duodenum). This patient has gallstones (echogenic foci on ultrasound) — the gallbladder contracting against a stone in the cystic duct → biliary colic (episodic RUQ pain after fatty meals). CCK also stimulates pancreatic enzyme secretion (lipase, proteases, amylase).
Question 8 · LO 3
A patient undergoes resection of 120 cm of terminal ileum for Crohn's disease. Postoperatively, she develops persistent watery diarrhea and steatorrhea. Stool analysis shows elevated fat. Which of the following best explains her diarrhea?
What is the mechanism of diarrhea after terminal ileum resection?
✓ B — Impaired bile acid reabsorption → bile acids irritate colon → diarrhea. The terminal ileum is the exclusive site for active bile acid reabsorption (ASBT transporter). After resection, bile acids enter the colon → act as osmotic and secretory agents → watery diarrhea (bile acid/cholerheic diarrhea). Additionally, the depleted bile acid pool impairs micelle formation → steatorrhea. Also at risk: B₁₂ deficiency (terminal ileum = IF-B₁₂ absorption) and fat-soluble vitamin deficiency. Treat bile acid diarrhea with cholestyramine (bile acid sequestrant).
Question 9 · LO 3 + 4
A 55-year-old man with a history of peptic ulcer disease presents with recurrent diarrhea, significant weight loss, and steatorrhea. Serum gastrin level is 1,200 pg/mL (normal <100). Upper endoscopy shows multiple duodenal ulcers. Which of the following best explains his steatorrhea?
What mechanism explains steatorrhea in Zollinger-Ellison syndrome?
✓ B — Excess acid inactivates bile salts and pancreatic lipase (ZES). ZES: gastrinoma → ↑↑ gastrin → massive HCl secretion → duodenal pH drops so low that it inactivates both bile salts (optimal pH ~6) and pancreatic lipase (optimal pH ~6–7). Without functional bile and lipase, fat cannot be digested or absorbed → steatorrhea. Classic vignette: ZES = multiple duodenal ulcers + markedly high serum gastrin + steatorrhea. Treatment: high-dose PPIs + surgical resection of gastrinoma if localized.
Question 10 · LO 4
A researcher discovers that pancreatic lipase cannot access dietary triglycerides within micelles because the hydrophilic ends of bile molecules form a shell that blocks enzyme entry. Which of the following correctly describes the mechanism that overcomes this barrier?
What is the mechanism by which lipase gains access to the lipid core of a micelle?
✓ B — Trypsin cleaves pro-colipase → colipase binds hydrophilic bile ends → access for pancreatic lipase. Pro-colipase is secreted by the pancreas in an inactive form. Trypsin (activated from trypsinogen by enterokinase at the brush border) cleaves pro-colipase → active colipase. Colipase then binds the hydrophilic ends of bile acid molecules on the micelle surface, creating a "door" for pancreatic lipase to access the lipid core. Pancreatic lipase then hydrolyzes TG → 2 FA + 1 MG. Without colipase, fat digestion is severely impaired despite bile and lipase both being present.

SAQ 11 · LO 1 + 4
A 30-year-old woman has been on a strict fat-free diet for 8 months. She then eats a large high-fat meal and develops greasy, foul-smelling floating stools the next day. Explain the physiological mechanism behind this acute steatorrhea. What would you advise her to do differently?
Mechanism:
  • Chronic low-fat intake → ↓ stimulus for bile production and storage → small bile pool (bile production is upregulated by dietary fat demand)
  • Sudden large fat load arrives in duodenum → overwhelms available bile → insufficient micelle formation
  • Unpackaged lipids cannot cross the aqueous UWL → fat passes unabsorbed → steatorrhea (greasy, floating, foul-smelling stool)
  • The liver can compensate over time, but cannot respond acutely to the mismatch

Advice: Gradually reintroduce dietary fat rather than sudden large fat loads. A moderate daily fat intake is healthier for gut function than complete restriction followed by excess — and allows the bile pool to maintain normal capacity.
SAQ 12 · LO 2 + 4
Compare primary and secondary bile acids: where is each made, what determines amphipathicity, and which one forms micelles? Explain why secondary bile cannot perform micelle formation.
Primary bile acids:
  • Synthesized by hepatocytes from a cholesterol backbone
  • Conjugated with glycine (→ glycocholic acid) or taurine (→ taurocholic acid) → adds a hydrophilic head
  • Result: amphipathic — hydrophilic head + lipophilic tail (cholesterol backbone)
  • Located in small intestine (duodenum → ileum); form micelles

Secondary bile acids:
  • Formed in the colon when gut bacteria deconjugate primary bile → stripping the hydrophilic amino acid
  • Result: predominantly lipophilic — no longer amphipathic
  • Cannot form micelles — without a hydrophilic exterior, they cannot arrange around a lipid core and present a water-compatible surface
  • Examples: deoxycholic acid, lithocholic acid
SAQ 13 · LO 3
A 32-year-old man with Crohn's disease requires resection of 150 cm of terminal ileum. List four specific consequences of this surgery and explain the mechanism behind each one.
  • 1. Bile acid diarrhea (watery diarrhea): Terminal ileum = site of active bile acid reabsorption (ASBT transporter). Resection → bile acids enter the colon → osmotic and secretory diarrhea. Treat with cholestyramine.
  • 2. Steatorrhea + fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (A, D, E, K): Depleted bile acid pool → less micelle formation → fat malabsorption. Fat-soluble vitamins require micelles for absorption.
  • 3. B₁₂ deficiency → megaloblastic anemia: Terminal ileum is the only site that absorbs intrinsic factor–B₁₂ complex. Resection → B₁₂ malabsorption → megaloblastic anemia (↑ MCV, hypersegmented neutrophils).
  • 4. Potential for gallstone formation: Depleted bile acid pool → relative ↑ in cholesterol concentration in bile → ↑ risk of cholesterol gallstones (bile supersaturated with cholesterol).
SAQ 14 · LO 4
Trace the full sequence of lipid absorption from fat entering the duodenum to a triglyceride entering the circulation. Include the role of each key player: CCK, bile, colipase, pancreatic lipase, and chylomicrons.
  • 1. CCK release: Fat + protein in duodenum → I-cells → CCK → gallbladder contraction + sphincter of Oddi relaxation → bile enters duodenum. CCK also triggers pancreatic enzyme secretion (lipase, pro-colipase, trypsin).
  • 2. Emulsification: Bile emulsifies large fat globules into smaller droplets → ↑ surface area for lipase.
  • 3. Micelle formation: Bile amphipathic molecules arrange around lipid fragments → hydrophilic exterior, lipophilic core → micelle = "lipid taxi" through the aqueous UWL.
  • 4. Colipase action: Trypsin (from pancreas) cleaves pro-colipase → active colipase binds hydrophilic ends of bile on micelle → opens door for pancreatic lipase to access lipid core.
  • 5. Pancreatic lipase digests: Triglycerides → 2 free fatty acids + 1 monoglyceride (inside micelle).
  • 6. Enterocyte uptake: Micelle reaches brush border → FA + MG passively diffuse off into enterocyte (lipophilic → cross lipid bilayer freely). Bile stays in lumen.
  • 7. Chylomicron formation: Inside enterocyte: FA + MG re-esterified → TG → packaged with apolipoprotein B-48, cholesterol, fat-soluble vitamins into chylomicrons → secreted into lacteals (lymph) → thoracic duct → enters systemic circulation at left subclavian vein.
SAQ 15 · Integration
A 6-year-old presents with failure to thrive, steatorrhea, acanthocytes on peripheral smear, and neurological symptoms. Contrast this presentation with cystic fibrosis-related fat malabsorption: what is the specific molecular defect in each, what is the level of the absorption defect, and how would you confirm each diagnosis?
Abetalipoproteinemia:
  • Defect: Absent MTP (microsomal triglyceride transfer protein) — AR
  • Level: Chylomicron export step — enterocytes absorb FA + MG normally from micelles but CANNOT package them into chylomicrons
  • Result: Fat accumulates inside enterocytes; absent serum VLDL, LDL, chylomicrons; acanthocytes (abnormal RBC membrane lipids); neurological symptoms from Vitamin E deficiency (ataxia, retinitis pigmentosa)
  • Diagnosis: Absent apolipoprotein B on serum protein electrophoresis; biopsy shows fat-laden enterocytes; acanthocytes on smear

Cystic Fibrosis:
  • Defect: CFTR mutation → defective Cl⁻ channel → thick mucus → pancreatic duct obstruction → pancreatic exocrine insufficiency
  • Level: Digestion step — absent pancreatic lipase (and protease, amylase) → triglycerides undigested → cannot enter micelles → steatorrhea
  • Result: Steatorrhea + fat-soluble vitamin deficiency, but bile is intact and enterocytes are normal — chylomicron export works if lipids are provided
  • Diagnosis: Sweat chloride test (>60 mEq/L); CFTR genetic testing; fecal elastase-1 (↓ in pancreatic insufficiency)
