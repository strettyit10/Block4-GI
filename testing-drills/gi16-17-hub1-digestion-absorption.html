LO 1 — Small Intestine Anatomy
What three anatomical features of the small intestine dramatically increase absorptive surface area?
tap to flip
Answer
Plicae circulares (~3× increase), villi (~10× increase), microvilli/brush border (~20× increase). Combined: ~600-fold amplification vs. smooth tube.
LO 2 — Villi & Lacteals
What is the functional significance of lacteals in intestinal villi?
tap to flip
Answer
Lacteals are lymphatic capillaries that absorb chylomicrons (too large for blood capillaries). Chylomicrons bypass portal blood and enter lymph → thoracic duct → systemic circulation.
LO 2 — Unstirred Water Layer
How do bile salt micelles overcome the rate-limiting step of the unstirred water layer?
tap to flip
Answer
Micelles are amphipathic (hydrophobic core, hydrophilic exterior) → ferry lipid digestion products across the unstirred water layer. Without micelles, fat-soluble compounds cannot traverse the UWL efficiently → steatorrhea.
LO 3 — Principal Enzymes
Which enzyme is the "master activator" of the pancreatic protease cascade, and where is it produced?
tap to flip
Answer
Enterokinase (enteropeptidase) — produced by duodenal brush border. Cleaves trypsinogen → trypsin, which then activates all other zymogens (chymotrypsinogen, proelastase, procarboxypeptidases).
LO 4 — Transport Mechanisms
Name the four main mechanisms of nutrient absorption across the enterocyte membrane.
tap to flip
Answer
1) Primary active transport (Na+/K+-ATPase, ATP-driven). 2) Secondary active transport (co-transport via Na+ gradient: SGLT1, PepT1). 3) Facilitated diffusion (GLUT5, GLUT2). 4) Passive diffusion (lipids, water).
LO 5 — Fiber
Why is cellulose (dietary fiber) not digested by human enzymes?
tap to flip
Answer
Cellulose is a glucose polymer with β-1,4 glycosidic bonds. Human digestive enzymes (amylase, maltase) only cleave α-glycosidic bonds. No cellulase (β-glucosidase) is produced → cellulose passes through undigested.
LO 6 — Carbohydrate Digestion
What is the two-phase process of starch digestion, and which enzyme dominates in each phase?
tap to flip
Answer
Phase 1 (luminal): Salivary amylase (~5%, inactivated by gastric acid) + pancreatic amylase (dominant, cleaves α-1,4 bonds → maltose, maltotriose, α-limit dextrins). Phase 2 (brush border): Saccharidases (maltase, sucrase-isomaltase, lactase) complete digestion.
LO 7 — Monosaccharide Transporters
Which monosaccharide transporters are apical, and which are basolateral? What are their substrates?
tap to flip
Answer
Apical: SGLT1 (glucose + galactose, Na+-coupled), GLUT5 (fructose, facilitated). Basolateral: GLUT2 (all three monosaccharides exit into portal blood, facilitated). SGLT1 failure → glucose-galactose malabsorption; GLUT5 saturation (high fructose) → osmotic diarrhea.
LO 8 — Lactose Intolerance
Explain the two mechanisms by which undigested lactose causes symptoms in lactase-deficient individuals.
tap to flip
Answer
1) Osmotic diarrhea: Undigested lactose draws water into lumen → watery stools. 2) Gas + bloating: Colonic bacteria ferment lactose → H₂, CO₂, short-chain fatty acids → bloating, cramping, flatulence. Symptoms begin 30–60 min post-lactose ingestion.
LO 9 — Protein Digestion Cascade
Outline the three-stage protein digestion cascade: gastric, pancreatic, and brush border/cytoplasmic.
tap to flip
Answer
Stage 1 (Gastric): HCl denatures proteins; pepsin cleaves peptide bonds (~10% digestion). Stage 2 (Pancreatic): Enterokinase activates trypsinogen → trypsin → chymotrypsin, elastase, carboxypeptidases (70% digestion). Stage 3 (Brush border + cytoplasm): Aminopeptidases, dipeptidases → free amino acids + dipeptides/tripeptides.
LO 10 — Peptide Absorption
What is PepT1, and why is di/tripeptide absorption more efficient than free amino acid absorption?
tap to flip
Answer
PepT1 = H+-peptide cotransporter (secondary active, H+-gradient from Na+/H+ antiporter). Di/tripeptides are absorbed intact via PepT1, then hydrolyzed by cytoplasmic peptidases. PepT1 pathway is more efficient than multiple free amino acid transporters.
LO 11 — Lipid Digestion
Describe the role of pancreatic lipase and co-lipase in triglyceride digestion.
tap to flip
Answer
Pancreatic lipase cleaves sn-1 & sn-3 ester bonds → 2-monoglyceride + 2 free fatty acids. Co-lipase (activated by trypsin) anchors lipase at the lipid-water interface, preventing bile salt inhibition. Without co-lipase: bile salts displace lipase → steatorrhea.
LO 12 — Micelle Formation
What is a mixed micelle, and what role do bile salts play in its formation and function?
tap to flip
Answer
Mixed micelle: 4–8 nm cylindrical structure with hydrophobic core (2-MAG, FA, cholesterol, vitamins) + hydrophilic exterior. Bile salts (amphipathic) are essential: they emulsify large TG droplets → increase lipase substrate area AND form micelles to ferry lipids across the unstirred water layer.
LO 13 — Chylomicron Assembly
What happens to lipid digestion products inside the enterocyte, and how is chylomicron formed?
tap to flip
Answer
Lipids diffuse across enterocyte membrane → carried to SER by FABP → re-esterification (2-MAG + 2 FA → TG). Assembled with cholesterol esters, phospholipids, apoB-48 (unique intestinal form) → chylomicrons → Golgi → basolateral exocytosis → lacteals (not portal blood, too large).
LO 14 — Abetalipoproteinemia
What is the genetic defect in abetalipoproteinemia, and why does it cause steatorrhea and fat-soluble vitamin deficiency?
tap to flip
Answer
Defect: Microsomal triglyceride transfer protein (MTP) mutation → cannot assemble apoB-48-containing chylomicrons. Fat accumulates in enterocytes (cannot be exported) → steatorrhea. Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) malabsorbed → vitamin E deficiency causes spinocerebellar degeneration (ataxia).
LO 15 — Bile Acid Circulation
Where and how are conjugated bile acids reabsorbed, and what percentage are recycled daily?
tap to flip
Answer
Conjugated bile acids are actively reabsorbed in the terminal ileum via ASBT (apical sodium-dependent bile acid transporter) — Na+-bile acid co-transport. Recycled 6–10 times daily. ~95% of bile acids conserved; only ~5% lost daily (replenished by hepatic synthesis).
LO 16 — Ileal Resection
What are the three major consequences of terminal ileum resection exceeding 100 cm?
tap to flip
Answer
1) Bile acid diarrhea: Loss of active reabsorption → ↑ colonic bile acids stimulate water secretion. 2) Fat malabsorption: Depleted bile acid pool → ↓ micelle formation → steatorrhea. 3) Vitamin B12 deficiency: Terminal ileum required for B12-IF absorption → megaloblastic anemia.
LO 17 — Water & Electrolyte Absorption
What is the net effect of Na+ absorption on water absorption in the small intestine?
tap to flip
Answer
Na+ absorption (via Na+/K+-ATPase, SGLT1, NaCl co-transporters) creates an osmotic gradient → water follows osmotically into portal blood. Most water absorption is secondary to electrolyte absorption. This is why cholera (CFTR overactivation) causes massive watery diarrhea despite normal Na+ gradient.
LO 18 — Iron Absorption
Trace the pathway of dietary iron from the intestinal lumen to incorporation into transferrin in portal blood.
tap to flip
Answer
Fe³⁺ → ferrireductase (apical) → Fe²⁺ → DMT1 (divalent metal transporter, H+-coupled) → iron enters enterocyte. Stored as ferritin; basolateral exit via ferroportin → hephaestin oxidizes Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺ → binds transferrin in portal blood.
LO 19 — Calcium Absorption
What is the role of vitamin D, PTH, and calbindin in active calcium absorption?
tap to flip
Answer
PTH stimulates 1,25-(OH)₂-vitamin D₃ synthesis (renal). Vitamin D induces calbindin (CaBP) synthesis in enterocytes. Calbindin binds apical Ca²⁺ (via TRPV6) → intracellular buffering → basolateral Ca²⁺-ATPase extrudes Ca²⁺ into portal blood. Vitamin D also ↑ TRPV6 and Ca²⁺-ATPase expression.
LO 20 — Fat-Soluble Vitamins
What is the main prerequisite for absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K)?
tap to flip
Answer
Micelle formation. Fat-soluble vitamins must be incorporated into mixed micelles (with bile salts) to cross the unstirred water layer. Any condition reducing bile salt delivery (cholestasis, ileal disease, CF) → fat-soluble vitamin malabsorption → deficiency diseases (night blindness, osteomalacia, bleeding tendency, neurologic symptoms).
LO 20 — Vitamin B12 Pathway
Describe the B12 absorption pathway from dietary source to terminal ileum.
tap to flip
Answer
B12 released from food proteins by gastric acid/pepsin → bound by transcobalamin I (TC1) in saliva → intrinsic factor (IF) (gastric parietal cells) binds B12 → B12-IF complex → terminal ileum receptor (cubilin-amnionless) → absorbed → bound by transcobalamin II (TC2) in blood.
LO 21 — Osmotic vs. Secretory Diarrhea
Differentiate osmotic and secretory diarrhea by their response to fasting and stool osmolar gap.
tap to flip
Answer
Osmotic diarrhea: Stops with fasting; stool osmolar gap > 50 mOsm/kg (caused by unabsorbed solutes). Examples: lactose intolerance, magnesium supplement. Secretory diarrhea: Continues despite fasting; normal osmolar gap (active water secretion). Examples: cholera, VIPoma, celiac disease.

Trap 1
Micelles are absorbed intact into enterocytes and contribute to chylomicron formation.
CORRECT ANSWER: False. Micelles ferry lipid digestion products across the unstirred water layer, but the products (2-MAG, FA, cholesterol) diffuse OUT of the micelle at the brush border and passively enter the enterocyte. Bile salts remain in the lumen and are recycled in the terminal ileum. Only the absorbed lipids are re-esterified and packaged into chylomicrons.
Trap 2
Lactose intolerance is caused by bacterial overgrowth in the small intestine due to undigested lactose.
CORRECT ANSWER: False (partially). Undigested lactose does reach the colon (not overgrowth in SI), where normal colonic bacteria ferment it → osmotic diarrhea (main mechanism) + gas production (bloating, flatulence). The osmotic effect is the primary cause of symptoms.
Trap 3
Pancreatic lipase requires no accessory factor to cleave triglycerides in the presence of bile salts.
CORRECT ANSWER: False. Pancreatic lipase REQUIRES co-lipase because bile salts competitively inhibit lipase by displacing it from the lipid-water interface. Co-lipase (activated by trypsin) anchors lipase at the interface, restoring activity. Without co-lipase, steatorrhea ensues.
Trap 4
Free amino acids are absorbed more efficiently than dipeptides and tripeptides because of their higher concentration.
CORRECT ANSWER: False. Di/tripeptides are absorbed MORE efficiently than free amino acids despite lower concentration, because PepT1 (H+-peptide cotransporter) is high-affinity and utilizes the proton gradient from the Na+/H+ antiporter. Free AAs use multiple lower-efficiency transporters.
Trap 5
Terminal ileum resection only affects fat absorption and has no impact on carbohydrate or protein absorption.
CORRECT ANSWER: False. Terminal ileum resection has TWO major consequences: (1) Loss of bile acid reabsorption → cholerheic diarrhea + fat malabsorption. (2) Loss of B12-IF absorption site → vitamin B12 deficiency → megaloblastic anemia. The ileum is also the site of some mineral absorption.
Trap 6
Abetalipoproteinemia is caused by mutations in the APOB gene encoding apoB-100.
CORRECT ANSWER: False. Abetalipoproteinemia is caused by mutations in the MTP gene (microsomal triglyceride transfer protein), not APOB. Familial hypobetalipoproteinemia (milder) is caused by APOB mutations. MTP mutations prevent assembly of ALL apoB-containing lipoproteins → severe fat malabsorption.
Trap 7
Osmotic diarrhea from lactose intolerance continues despite fasting, whereas secretory diarrhea stops with fasting.
CORRECT ANSWER: False (reversed). Osmotic diarrhea STOPS with fasting (lactose removed from lumen). Secretory diarrhea CONTINUES despite fasting (active water secretion). Stool osmolar gap differentiates: osmotic > 50 mOsm/kg; secretory normal.
Trap 8
Congenital enterokinase deficiency would present similarly to severe pancreatic exocrine insufficiency with low amylase and lipase levels.
CORRECT ANSWER: False. Enterokinase deficiency presents with protein malabsorption (failure to thrive, hypoproteinemia) BUT amylase and lipase are NORMAL because they are produced by the pancreas—the problem is activation. Pancreatic insufficiency has ↓ amylase/lipase. This distinction is key on exams.
