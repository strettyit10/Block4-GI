Question 1 · LO 1
A 42-year-old woman presents with sudden severe left flank pain radiating to the left groin after blunt abdominal trauma during a motor vehicle collision. On examination, she has ecchymosis over the left flank and left costovertebral angle tenderness. CT shows a small retroperitoneal hematoma but no active bleeding. Which of the following retroperitoneal organs is most likely injured based on the trauma mechanism and clinical presentation?
A Pancreas
B Kidney
C Duodenum
D Colon
E Small intestine
Correct Answer: B — Kidney

The kidney is the most commonly injured retroperitoneal organ in blunt trauma, particularly with flank trauma. The left flank bruising and CVA tenderness strongly suggest renal injury. Kidneys are retroperitoneal and are protected laterally by the ribs and musculature, but blunt force penetrating this protection causes hematuria and flank pain. Small retroperitoneal hematomas (hemostatic without active bleeding) typically resolve conservatively.

Distractors:
A (Pancreas): Pancreatic injury presents with epigastric pain and elevated amylase; less likely with isolated flank trauma.
C (Duodenum): Duodenal injury is from direct epigastric trauma and causes peritonitis; retroperitoneal duodenum bleeds into the retroperitoneum with abdominal pain, not flank pain.
D (Colon): Colonic injury is less common with blunt flank trauma and presents with peritoneal signs.
E (Small intestine): Small intestine is mainly peritoneal; intraperitoneal bleeding causes peritonitis.
Question 2 · LO 1
A 38-year-old male with a history of intra-abdominal sepsis from diverticulitis develops a psoas abscess (confirmed on CT). Which of the following organs is NOT part of the SAD PUCKER mnemonic for secondary retroperitoneal organs?
A Liver
B Ascending colon
C Duodenum — entire second portion
D Descending colon
E Pancreas
Correct Answer: C — Duodenum — entire second portion

The SAD PUCKER mnemonic lists secondary retroperitoneal organs: Stomach, Ascending colon, Duodenum (2nd and 3rd portions only, not 1st), Pancreas, Uters, Colon (descending), Kidney, Esophagus, Rectum. The duodenum is partially retroperitoneal—only the 2nd, 3rd, and 4th portions; the 1st (superior) portion is intraperitoneal. Therefore, the statement that the "entire second portion" is part of SAD PUCKER is misleading; SAD PUCKER specifies portions 2 and 3.

Distractors:
A (Liver): Not retroperitoneal; intraperitoneal organ (covered by peritoneum anteriorly and laterally).
B, D, E: All are correctly listed in SAD PUCKER as secondary retroperitoneal organs.
Question 3 · LO 2
A 19-year-old college student presents with left flank pain, gross hematuria, and imaging shows left renal vein compression between the SMA and aorta. He also has a left varicocele. Which of the following best describes the underlying vascular abnormality?
A Left renal artery stenosis causing renovascular hypertension
B Aortic bifurcation aneurysm compressing the left ureter
C Nutcracker syndrome — left renal vein entrapment between SMA and aorta
D Left gonadal vein thrombosis
E Renal cell carcinoma with venous invasion
Correct Answer: C — Nutcracker syndrome

Nutcracker syndrome is compression of the left renal vein between the superior mesenteric artery (anteriorly) and the abdominal aorta (posteriorly). This compression impedes venous outflow from the left kidney, causing increased renal vein pressure and hematuria. The elevated pressure is transmitted into the left gonadal vein (which drains into the left renal vein), causing a left varicocele (dilation of the pampiniform plexus). Patients often present with flank pain, hematuria, and flank mass (varicocele) in young adults. This is a key clinical pearl: left varicocele in an adult is pathologic until proven otherwise—suspect renal vein obstruction.

Distractors:
A: Renal artery stenosis causes hypertension, not hematuria or varicocele.
B: Would compress the ureter, not the renal vein.
D: Gonadal vein thrombosis would not explain the renal vein compression finding.
E: RCC is possible but would show a tumor mass on imaging, not just vascular compression.
Question 4 · LO 2
A 67-year-old hypertensive male undergoes abdominal imaging for flank pain. The radiologist describes an aortic branch at the L1 vertebral level. Which of the following is most consistent with this anatomical level?
A Celiac artery
B Superior mesenteric artery (SMA) and renal arteries
C Inferior mesenteric artery (IMA)
D Aortic bifurcation
E Lumbar arteries
Correct Answer: B — SMA and renal arteries

Classic aortic branch vertebral levels:
T12: Celiac artery
L1: Superior mesenteric artery (SMA) and renal arteries
L3: Inferior mesenteric artery (IMA)
L4: Aortic bifurcation into iliac arteries

The renal arteries arise at L1-L2, slightly lower than the SMA, but both are typically at the L1 level. This is a high-yield fact for both radiology reports and clinical correlation.

Distractors:
A: Celiac artery is at T12, one level higher.
C: IMA is at L3, two levels lower.
D: Aortic bifurcation is at L4.
E: Lumbar arteries are segmental branches throughout the lumbar region.
Question 5 · LO 3
A 55-year-old male presents with chest pain and dysphagia. During esophageal manometry, the esophagus is noted to pass through the diaphragm at the level of T10. Which of the following is the most likely associated complication if there is a tear in this region?
A Tension pneumothorax from aortic rupture
B Mediastinitis from esophageal perforation
C Hemorrhagic shock from IVC rupture
D Peritonitis from stomach perforation
E Acute renal failure from aortic thrombosis
Correct Answer: B — Mediastinitis from esophageal perforation

The esophagus passes through the esophageal hiatus at T10, accompanied by the vagal trunks. Tears or perforations in this region lead to esophageal perforation and subsequent mediastinitis (infection of the mediastinal tissues). This is a surgical emergency with high mortality if not recognized quickly.

Distractors:
A: The aorta passes through the aortic hiatus at T12, not T10.
C: The IVC passes through the caval opening at T8, not T10.
D: The stomach is below the diaphragm (abdominal organ); perforation would cause peritonitis, not mediastinitis.
E: Unrelated to esophageal diaphragmatic pathology.
Question 6 · LO 3
A trauma surgeon is reviewing CT images of a patient with a penetrating chest wound. The imaging shows a laceration involving the structure that pierces the diaphragm at T8 through the central tendon. Which of the following structures is NOT a component of this diaphragmatic opening?
A Inferior vena cava
B Left phrenic nerve
C Right phrenic nerve
D Hepatic veins
E Right atrium (post-caval portion)
Correct Answer: B — Left phrenic nerve

The IVC passes through the caval opening (T8) in the central tendon, accompanied by the right phrenic nerve only. The left phrenic nerve pierces the diaphragm separately, passing through the left hemidiaphragm (muscular portion) and overlying the left ventricle. Both the right and left phrenic nerves originate from C3-C5 and provide motor innervation to the diaphragm; the right phrenic follows the IVC through T8, while the left phrenic is separate.

Distractors:
A, D, E: All are correct components of the T8 caval opening.
C: Right phrenic nerve correctly accompanies the IVC through T8.
Question 7 · LO 4
A 42-year-old male with a history of nephrolithiasis presents with acute left flank pain radiating to the groin. Imaging confirms a 6 mm stone impacted at the ureteropelvic junction (UPJ). Based on anatomical knowledge, at which vertebral level would you expect this stone to be lodged?
A T11-L2 (loin to groin referred pain)
B L3-L4 (pelvic brim level)
C L5-S1 (sacroiliac region)
D T12-L1 (renal artery level)
E L2-L3 (gonadal vein level)
Correct Answer: A — T11-L2

The ureter has three anatomical constriction sites where stones commonly lodge:
1. Ureteropelvic junction (UPJ) — most common site (40-60% of stones); most proximal constriction
2. Pelvic brim — where ureter crosses iliac vessels
3. Ureterovesical junction (UVJ) — distal to bladder

The UPJ is at T11-L2 level, which corresponds to the kidney's location. Ureteric colic causes "loin to groin" referred pain (T11-L2 distribution) because the ureter is innervated by branches from these spinal levels. The pain radiates from the flank (loin) down to the groin and external genitalia.

Distractors:
B: Pelvic brim constriction is at L3-L4, not the UPJ.
C: Too caudal for UPJ.
D: Renal artery level, but UPJ is more proximal.
E: Gonadal vessels are at L2-L3, not the UPJ.
Question 8 · LO 4
A 58-year-old woman undergoes a total hysterectomy for fibroids. Two hours post-op, her urine output drops and she develops flank pain. On re-exploration, the surgeon discovers the left ureter has been ligated. The surgeon recalls that the ureter passes in a specific anatomical relationship to pelvic structures. Which of the following best describes the classic relationship of the ureter to the uterine artery?
A Ureter runs medial to the uterine artery
B Ureter runs under (posterior to) the uterine artery ("water under the bridge")
C Ureter runs lateral to the uterine artery
D Ureter and uterine artery run parallel with no crossing
E Ureter is located within the broad ligament with the uterine artery
Correct Answer: B — Ureter runs under (posterior to) the uterine artery

The classic surgical pearl is "water under the bridge": the ureter passes under the uterine artery at the level of the internal cervical os in females. More specifically, the ureter passes posterior/deep to the uterine artery as it approaches the bladder. In males, the vas deferens similarly crosses over the ureter. This relationship is critical during gynecologic surgery (hysterectomy, oophorectomy, cystectomy) because inadvertent ligation of the ureter during uterine artery ligation is a common complication if the surgeon is not careful to identify and preserve the ureter.

Distractors:
A, C, D, E: All describe incorrect anatomical relationships or miss the key crossing point.
Question 9 · LO 5
A 51-year-old male with palpitations and intermittent severe headaches undergoes evaluation for a suspected pheochromocytoma. Physical exam reveals hypertension and the patient admits to episodes of profuse sweating. Anatomically, the adrenal medulla is innervated by preganglionic sympathetic fibers originating from which spinal levels?
A T1-T4
B T3-T8
C T5-T11
D T8-L2
E T12-L5
Correct Answer: C — T5-T11

The adrenal medulla is innervated by preganglionic sympathetic fibers from T5-T11 (sometimes quoted as T5-T10 or T5-L1, but T5-T11 is the most common range taught). Unlike most postganglionic sympathetic neurons, the chromaffin cells of the adrenal medulla are actually modified postganglionic neurons that receive direct preganglionic innervation through the splanchnic nerves (greater, lesser, and least splanchnic nerves). When stimulated, they release catecholamines (epinephrine and norepinephrine) directly into the bloodstream.

In pheochromocytoma (a catecholamine-secreting tumor of the adrenal medulla), patients experience the classic triad of hypertension, severe headache, and profuse diaphoresis due to massive catecholamine release. The innervation from T5-T11 is clinically relevant in cases of adrenal crisis or when considering splanchnic nerve blocks for pain management.

Distractors:
A, B, D, E: All represent incorrect ranges for adrenal medullary innervation.
Question 10 · LO 5
A surgical team is preparing for an open adrenalectomy in a patient with a left adrenocortical carcinoma. The surgeon must be careful to identify and ligate the adrenal venous drainage before removing the gland. Which of the following correctly describes the venous anatomy of BOTH adrenal glands?
A Both drain directly into the inferior vena cava
B Both drain into the renal veins
C Right adrenal → IVC directly; left adrenal → left renal vein
D Right adrenal → renal vein; left adrenal → IVC directly
E Both drain into the hepatic veins
Correct Answer: C — Right adrenal → IVC directly; left adrenal → left renal vein

This is a classic surgical pearl that is tested frequently. The adrenal venous drainage is asymmetric:
Right adrenal vein: Short vein draining directly into the IVC
Left adrenal vein: Longer vein draining into the left renal vein

During adrenalectomy, the surgeon must be aware that the right adrenal vein is short and directly posterolateral to the IVC; injury to the IVC during dissection of the right adrenal can be catastrophic. The left adrenal vein, being longer and draining into the renal vein, is more forgiving but must still be carefully controlled during left adrenalectomy.

Distractors:
A: Left adrenal does NOT drain directly into the IVC.
B: Right adrenal does NOT drain into the renal vein; it goes directly to the IVC.
D: Reversed; this is incorrect.
E: Neither gland drains into hepatic veins.
Question 11 · LO 1
A 65-year-old male with diverticulitis develops a retroperitoneal abscess. On CT, the abscess is localized to the left paracolic gutter. Which of the following organs is most commonly involved in the infectious process given its retroperitoneal location?
A Descending and sigmoid colon
B Ascending colon and liver
C Duodenum and right kidney
D Pancreas and small intestine
E Stomach and spleen
Correct Answer: A — Descending and sigmoid colon

The descending and sigmoid colon are secondary retroperitoneal organs (part of SAD PUCKER). When diverticulitis occurs in the sigmoid colon, perforation allows bacterial seeding into the retroperitoneal space, forming abscesses in the left paracolic gutter (the potential space between the descending colon and the left posterior parietal peritoneum). The retroperitoneal location of these colonic segments makes them prone to forming peritonic collections that communicate with the retroperitoneum.

Distractors:
B: Ascending colon is retroperitoneal but on the right; sigmoid diverticulitis affects the left paracolic gutter.
C: Right-sided organs; left paracolic abscess is left-sided.
D: Small intestine is intraperitoneal; pancreatic infection would present differently.
E: Stomach and spleen are intraperitoneal.
Question 12 · LO 3
A 44-year-old woman with acute left shoulder pain and left flank pain presents to the ED. Imaging reveals a splenic laceration with a small sub-capsular hematoma. The shoulder pain is NOT directly from splenic injury but rather from irritation of a specific nerve. Which nerve is responsible for this referred pain?
A Left vagus nerve
B Left phrenic nerve (Kehr sign)
C Left intercostal nerves
D Left suprascapular nerve
E Left accessory nerve
Correct Answer: B — Left phrenic nerve (Kehr sign)

Kehr sign is pain in the left shoulder (or right shoulder if splenic rupture) referred from splenic injury. The phrenic nerve (C3-C5) innervates the diaphragm and the diaphragmatic pleura. Irritation of the left hemidiaphragm from bleeding or inflammation under the left costal margin transmits pain via the phrenic nerve, which has referred sensation to the left shoulder (C3-C5 dermatome = shoulder region). This is a classic clinical pearl for splenic injury and is part of the diagnostic criteria for evaluating hemodynamically stable splenic trauma.

Distractors:
A: Vagus nerve does not provide sensation to the shoulder.
C: Intercostal nerves provide somatic pain, not referred shoulder pain from splenic injury.
D, E: Unrelated to diaphragmatic or splenic innervation.
Question 13 · LO 2
A 58-year-old male with cirrhosis and portal hypertension develops esophageal varices. Which of the following venous pathways best explains the shunting of blood away from the portal system in portal hypertension?
A Superior mesenteric vein → left colic vein → systemic circulation
B Portal vein → left gastric vein → esophageal veins → azygos vein → SVC
C Superior rectal vein → internal pudendal vein → femoral vein
D Colic veins → lumbar veins → IVC directly
E Portal vein → hepatic veins → right atrium
Correct Answer: B — Portal vein → left gastric vein → esophageal veins → azygos vein → SVC

In portal hypertension, blood backs up in the portal system and seeks alternative routes to bypass the liver. One of the classic portocaval anastomoses is between the left gastric (coronary) vein (draining into the portal vein) and the esophageal veins (which drain into the azygos system and ultimately the superior vena cava). This dilatation causes esophageal varices, which are prone to bleeding in cirrhotic patients.

Other portocaval anastomoses include:
1. Esophageal varices (left gastric ↔ azygos)
2. Internal hemorrhoids (superior rectal ↔ middle/inferior rectal)
3. Caput medusae (paraumbilical ↔ epigastric/superficial epigastric)
4. Retroperitoneal collaterals (colic ↔ lumbar)

Distractors:
A, C, D: Are valid portocaval anastomoses but do NOT explain esophageal varices.
E: Incorrect; in cirrhosis, the hepatic veins are also congested; this pathway is compromised.
Question 14 · LO 5
A 43-year-old female with uncontrolled hypertension and episodes of diaphoresis and palpitations is diagnosed with a pheochromocytoma. The tumor is located in the left adrenal medulla. Which of the following statements best describes the functional relationship between adrenal medullary innervation and catecholamine release in this patient?
A ACTH stimulates the adrenal medulla directly to release catecholamines
B Preganglionic sympathetic fibers (T5-T11) directly stimulate chromaffin cells to release catecholamines
C Postganglionic sympathetic neurons release acetylcholine to stimulate the medulla
D Parasympathetic fibers (vagus nerve) inhibit medullary catecholamine release
E Direct hormonal feedback from the hypothalamus controls medullary secretion
Correct Answer: B — Preganglionic sympathetic fibers directly stimulate chromaffin cells to release catecholamines

The adrenal medulla is unique in that it receives direct preganglionic sympathetic innervation from T5-T11 spinal segments. The chromaffin cells are modified postganglionic neurons that respond to acetylcholine (released from preganglionic fibers) by exocytosing catecholamines (epinephrine 80%, norepinephrine 20%) directly into the bloodstream. In a pheochromocytoma, the tumor cells uncontrollably release catecholamines, causing the classic triad of hypertension, headache, and diaphoresis.

Distractors:
A: ACTH stimulates the adrenal cortex (to release cortisol), not the medulla.
C: This describes typical postganglionic sympathetic neurons, but the adrenal medulla is different—it receives preganglionic fibers directly.
D: Parasympathetic/vagal input does not directly control adrenal medullary secretion.
E: Endocrine feedback is important for the cortex, but sympathetic innervation is primary for the medulla.
Question 15 · LO 1
A 34-year-old male is thrown from a motorcycle and strikes a tree with his epigastrium. He presents with severe epigastric pain and elevated serum amylase. Imaging suggests a pancreatic contusion in the retroperitoneal space. Why is pancreatic injury particularly concerning when it occurs in the retroperitoneal location?
A Pancreatic injury causes immediate peritonitis
B Retroperitoneal pancreatic injury can lead to retroperitoneal hematoma and pancreatitis without early peritoneal signs
C Pancreatic enzymes cannot harm retroperitoneal tissues
D The pancreas is always completely intraperitoneal
E Pancreatic injury requires immediate surgery in all cases
Correct Answer: B — Retroperitoneal pancreatic injury can lead to retroperitoneal hematoma and pancreatitis without early peritoneal signs

The pancreas is a secondary retroperitoneal organ (part of SAD PUCKER). When injured, the pancreas can bleed into the retroperitoneal space, forming a retroperitoneal hematoma. Pancreatic juice (amylase, lipase) leaks into the retroperitonium, causing chemical pancreatitis. Importantly, because the pancreas is retroperitoneal, these inflammatory changes do NOT immediately cause peritoneal signs (like rebound tenderness or guarding). This delay in peritoneal presentation can make diagnosis challenging; CT imaging is essential. Elevated amylase is the key laboratory clue.

Distractors:
A: Peritonitis is NOT immediate with retroperitoneal pancreatic injury; that's the key point.
C: Pancreatic enzymes absolutely harm tissues—that's pancreatitis.
D: The pancreas is retroperitoneal, not intraperitoneal.
E: Many pancreatic contusions can be managed conservatively; surgery is reserved for severe injuries or complications.
Question 16 · LO 2
A 72-year-old male with a history of abdominal aortic aneurysm is found to have an infrarenal AAA measuring 6.5 cm. The imaging report notes the location as being below the renal arteries. At which vertebral level do the renal arteries originate?
A T12
B L1
C L1-L2 (variable, slightly below SMA)
D L3
E L4
Correct Answer: C — L1-L2

The renal arteries originate at L1-L2, slightly lower than the SMA (which is at L1). This is important clinically because:
1. Infrarenal AAA: Below the renal arteries (L2 or lower)
2. Suprarenal AAA: Above the renal arteries (between celiac and renal arteries)
3. Juxtarenal AAA: At the renal artery level

Infrarenal AAAs are more common and have better surgical outcomes. The distinction is critical for surgical planning because suprarenal AAA repair requires cross-clamping above the renal arteries, risking renal ischemia.

Distractors:
A: T12 is the celiac artery level.
B: L1 is the SMA level; renal arteries are slightly lower.
D, E: Below the renal arteries.
Question 17 · LO 4
A 29-year-old female with a history of calcium oxalate kidney stones develops acute left flank pain and nausea. Ultrasound shows hydronephrosis with an echogenic focus at the ureterovesical junction. Which of the following is the most common reason stones lodge at the ureterovesical junction?
A The ureterovesical junction is the widest part of the ureter
B The ureterovesical junction is the most distal constriction of the ureter
C The ureterovesical junction is where the ureter crosses the iliac vessels
D The ureterovesical junction is at the level of the renal hilum
E The ureterovesical junction is where the ureter becomes retroperitoneal
Correct Answer: B — The ureterovesical junction is the most distal constriction of the ureter

The ureter has three main constriction sites where stones lodge:
1. Ureteropelvic junction (UPJ) — most common (40-60%)
2. Pelvic brim — second most common (where ureter crosses iliac vessels)
3. Ureterovesical junction (UVJ) — third most common (25-30%)

The UVJ is a natural constriction as the ureter enters the bladder at an oblique angle through the intramural portion (within the bladder wall). Stones lodged here cause hydronephrosis and flank pain. The "loin to groin" referral pattern still applies, but pain may be localized more to the suprapubic region if the stone is at the UVJ.

Distractors:
A: The UVJ is a constriction, not the widest part.
C: That's the pelvic brim constriction, not the UVJ.
D: The UVJ is distal (near the bladder), not at the renal level.
E: The ureter becomes retroperitoneal at the pelvic brim, not at the UVJ.
Question 18 · LO 5
An 8-year-old child with acute meningococcemia develops acute adrenal crisis with hypotension, petechial rash, and fulminant septic shock. Autopsy findings reveal bilateral hemorrhagic necrosis of the adrenal glands. This rare but life-threatening condition is known as which syndrome?
A Addison disease
B Cushing syndrome
C Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome
D Pheochromocytoma crisis
E Congenital adrenal hyperplasia
Correct Answer: C — Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome

Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome is a rare but fulminant presentation of acute adrenal failure caused by bilateral adrenal hemorrhage, classically associated with meningococcal septicemia (Neisseria meningitidis). The organism causes disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), leading to hemorrhagic necrosis of both adrenal glands. Patients develop sudden shock, hypotension, and petechial/purpuric rash. It is a medical emergency requiring immediate treatment with antibiotics, vasopressors, and high-dose corticosteroids.

Other organisms rarely associated with W-F syndrome include:
- Streptococcus pneumoniae
- Staphylococcus aureus
- Gram-negative bacilli

Distractors:
A: Addison disease is chronic primary adrenal insufficiency (autoimmune or infectious), not acute hemorrhagic necrosis.
B: Cushing syndrome is cortisol excess, not adrenal hemorrhage.
D: Pheochromocytoma crisis is catecholamine excess, not adrenal hemorrhage.
E: CAH is a genetic enzyme deficiency, not hemorrhagic necrosis.
Question 19 · LO 5
A 3-day-old female neonate presents with virilization (clitoromegaly) and salt wasting. Laboratory tests show elevated 17-hydroxyprogesterone. The adrenal cortex is hyperplastic due to a deficiency in which enzyme?
A 21-hydroxylase
B 17-alpha-hydroxylase
C 11-beta-hydroxylase
D 3-beta-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase
E Cholesterol desmolase
Correct Answer: A — 21-hydroxylase

21-hydroxylase deficiency is the most common form of congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH), accounting for ~90% of cases. The enzyme deficiency blocks the conversion of:
1. 17-hydroxyprogesterone → 11-deoxycortisol (cortisol pathway blocked)
2. Progesterone → deoxycorticosterone (aldosterone pathway blocked)

This leads to:
- Elevated 17-hydroxyprogesterone (diagnostic marker)
- Cortisol deficiency → ACTH rise → adrenal hyperplasia
- Androgen excess → virilization in females
- Aldosterone deficiency → salt wasting (classic presentation)

The adrenal cortex is hyperplastic because high ACTH drives continual adrenal stimulation (attempting to overcome the enzymatic block).

Distractors:
B: 17-alpha-hydroxylase deficiency causes hypertension (from DOC excess) and hypogonadism; low 17-OHP.
C: 11-beta-hydroxylase deficiency causes hypertension and virilization but normal/low 17-OHP.
D: 3-beta-HSD deficiency causes mild virilization; elevated DHEA (not 17-OHP).
E: Cholesterol desmolase (P450scc) deficiency causes lipoid CAH; no steroid synthesis at all.
Question 20 · LO 2
A 45-year-old male undergoing a laparoscopic left nephrectomy for renal cell carcinoma requires ligation of the left renal vein. The surgeon notes the gonadal vein and left adrenal vein as tributaries that must be carefully preserved or ligated during dissection. Which of the following statements best describes the venous anatomy relevant to this case?
A The left gonadal vein drains into the left adrenal vein, then into the renal vein
B The left gonadal and left adrenal veins are separate tributaries that drain independently into the left renal vein
C The left gonadal vein drains directly into the IVC, bypassing the renal vein
D Both the gonadal and adrenal veins drain into the splenic vein
E The left gonadal vein drains into the superior mesenteric vein
Correct Answer: B — The left gonadal and left adrenal veins are separate tributaries that drain independently into the left renal vein

In the left-sided venous anatomy:
- Left testicular/ovarian (gonadal) vein → drains into the left renal vein (not the IVC like the right gonadal vein)
- Left adrenal vein → drains into the left renal vein

These are separate tributaries that drain independently into the left renal vein. The asymmetry is important: the right testicular/ovarian vein drains directly into the IVC, while the left drains into the renal vein. During nephrectomy, careful ligation of the renal vein must account for these tributaries to prevent unwanted bleeding from these small vessels or venous congestion of the gonad or adrenal gland.

Distractors:
A: They don't form a cascade; both drain independently into the renal vein.
C: The left gonadal vein does NOT drain directly into the IVC; that's the right gonadal vein.
D: Neither drains into the splenic vein.
E: The gonadal vein does not drain into the SMV.
