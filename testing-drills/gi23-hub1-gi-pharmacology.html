LO 1 — Acid Production
Describe the H⁺/K⁺ ATPase on parietal cells. What three stimuli activate it? What class of drugs inhibits it?
tap to flip
Answer
H⁺/K⁺ ATPase: "proton pump" on apical membrane of parietal cell → actively secretes H⁺ into lumen in exchange for K⁺

Stimulated by: Gastrin (CCK-B receptor) + Histamine (H2 receptor) + ACh (M3 receptor)

Drug classes that inhibit: PPIs (irreversible), H2 blockers (reversible), P-CABs (vonoprazan), anticholinergics (pirenzepine)
LO 1 — Antacids
Name four classes of antacids. For each: mechanism, onset, side effects, and one key contraindication or clinical pearl.
tap to flip
Answer
CaCO₃: rapid (<5 min), acid rebound (↑acid 2–3 hrs after), constipation, hypercalcemia, milk-alkali syndrome
Mg(OH)₂: fast (~15 min), diarrhea risk, hypermagnesemia in renal disease
Al(OH)₃: slow (~30 min), constipation, phosphate binding (hypophosphatemia), accumulation in renal failure
NaHCO₃: very rapid, systemically absorbed → ↑pH (risk in CHF/HTN; "systemic" antacid)
LO 1 — H₂ Blockers
Name four H₂-receptor blockers and their key clinical differences. Which one has metabolic concerns?
tap to flip
Answer
Cimetidine: CYP450 INHIBITOR (↑drug interactions) + antiandrogen (gynecomastia, impotence)
Ranitidine, Famotidine, Nizatidine: no CYP inhibition, safer drug profiles

All are reversible H2 antagonists — less potent than PPIs, onset ~30–60 min
LO 1 — PPIs
List five PPI drugs. How do they work? What are the three major adverse effect categories?
tap to flip
Answer
PPIs: omeprazole, lansoprazole, pantoprazole, esomeprazole, rabeprazole

Mechanism: Irreversibly bind H⁺/K⁺ ATPase (proton pump blockade) → profound acid suppression

Adverse effects:
• C. difficile infection (loss of acid barrier)
• Hypomagnesiemia, B12 deficiency, hypocalcemia (malabsorption)
• ↑hip fracture risk (chronic use; calcium malabsorption)
LO 1 — P-CABs
What is vonoprazan? How does it differ from PPIs? What is the clinical advantage?
tap to flip
Answer
Vonoprazan (P-CAB): Potassium-competitive acid blocker

vs. PPIs: Reversible (not irreversible) binding to H⁺/K⁺ ATPase

Advantage: Faster onset, more consistent acid suppression, shorter half-life → greater flexibility in dosing. Newer agent (less long-term safety data)
LO 2 — H. pylori Therapy
What is the gold-standard first-line regimen for H. pylori eradication? What are the four drugs?
tap to flip
Answer
Quadruple therapy (preferred): PPI + Bismuth + Metronidazole + Tetracycline (PBMT) × 10–14 days

Alternative triple therapy: PPI + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin (or Metronidazole if penicillin allergy)

Key: Test-and-treat approach with urea breath test or stool antigen. Confirm eradication 4+ weeks post-treatment.
LO 3 — GERD Pathophysiology
What causes GERD? Name five triggers that ↓LES tone or cause transient LES relaxations.
tap to flip
Answer
Pathophysiology: Transient LES relaxations (TLESRs) + decreased basal LES tone → reflux of gastric contents into esophagus

Common triggers: Fatty meals, chocolate, caffeine, nicotine, alcohol. Also: CNS depressants (benzodiazepines, opioids) → ↓LES pressure
LO 3 — GERD Stepwise Treatment
Outline the stepwise pharmacologic approach to GERD management. When does each tier apply?
tap to flip
Answer
Step 1 (Lifestyle): Weight loss, elevation, avoid triggers, small meals
Step 2 (H₂ blockers): Mild-to-moderate, intermittent symptoms
Step 3 (PPIs): Moderate-to-severe, daily symptoms, erosive esophagitis
Step 4 (Surgery): Refractory disease, medication intolerance (laparoscopic Nissen fundoplication)
LO 4 — Sucralfate
How does sucralfate work? Why must it be given on an empty stomach? When should it NOT be co-dosed with PPIs?
tap to flip
Answer
Sucralfate: sucrose sulfate-aluminum compound
Mechanism: Requires acidic environment to polymerize → binds directly to ulcer base, forms protective gel, ↑mucus & bicarbonate
Dosing: On empty stomach (1 hr before/2 hrs after meals) — needs acid to activate
Key: Do NOT give simultaneously with PPIs or H₂ blockers (acid suppression inhibits activation)
LO 4 — Misoprostol
What is misoprostol? How does it prevent NSAID-induced ulcers? What is the critical absolute contraindication?
tap to flip
Answer
Misoprostol: PGE₁ analog
Mechanism: ↑cytoprotective mucus & HCO₃⁻ secretion, ↓acid, ↑mucosal blood flow → prevents NSAID-induced ulcers

ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATION: Pregnancy (teratogenic, abortifacient — use only in post-menopausal or on reliable contraception)

Side effect: Diarrhea (most common reason for discontinuation)
LO 5 — Vomiting Reflex Centers
Name the three key neural structures in the vomiting reflex and what stimulus each receives.
tap to flip
Answer
Chemoreceptor trigger zone (CTZ/area postrema): OUTSIDE blood-brain barrier; senses blood-borne toxins (D₂, 5-HT₃ receptors)
Vestibular system: Detects motion (H₁, M₁ receptors)
Vomiting center (nucleus tractus solitarius/NTS): Integrates all inputs, coordinates actual vomiting reflex

Bonus: GI tract sends signals via 5-HT₃ vagal afferents (chemo/radiation)
LO 5 — Motion Sickness Drugs
Why do motion sickness drugs (antihistamines, anticholinergics) work differently from chemotherapy-induced nausea drugs (ondansetron)?
tap to flip
Answer
Motion sickness: Vestibular input uses H₁ & M₁ receptors
→ Treat with dimenhydrinate (H₁ antagonist) + scopolamine (M₁ antagonist)

Chemo-induced nausea: CTZ/GI sources use D₂ & 5-HT₃ receptors
→ Treat with ondansetron (5-HT₃ blocker)

Key principle: Match drug receptor to emetic pathway
LO 6 — Ondansetron
What type of drug is ondansetron? What are its three main adverse effects?
tap to flip
Answer
Ondansetron: 5-HT₃ receptor antagonist (serotonin blocker)
Uses: Chemotherapy-induced nausea/vomiting (CINV), postoperative nausea/vomiting (PONV)

Adverse effects:
• QT prolongation (monitor ECG in high-dose/IV use)
• Headache
• Constipation (frequent)
LO 6 — Metoclopramide
What is metoclopramide's dual mechanism? List four adverse effects that limit its use.
tap to flip
Answer
Metoclopramide: D₂ antagonist (dopamine blocker) + 5-HT₄ agonist (prokinetic)

Uses: Nausea + gastroparesis (↑gastric contractility)

Adverse effects:
• Tardive dyskinesia (irreversible involuntary movements)
• Extrapyramidal symptoms (parkinsonism, dystonia)
• Hyperprolactinemia (gynecomastia, amenorrhea)
• CONTRAINDICATED in bowel obstruction (can perforate)
LO 6 — Scopolamine & Dramamine
For motion sickness: mechanism of scopolamine (patch) vs. dimenhydrinate. Which is better for long trips?
tap to flip
Answer
Scopolamine (hyoscine): M₁ muscarinic antagonist → transdermal patch (sustained release, 72 hrs)
• Ideal for long trips; ↓central effects
• Side effect: dry mouth, blurred vision

Dimenhydrinate (Dramamine): H₁ antihistamine → oral; faster onset but shorter duration
• Side effect: sedation (useful, also undesired)

Choice: Scopolamine for prophylaxis; Dramamine for acute use
LO 7 — ORS & SGLT1
Why is ORS (oral rehydration solution) so effective? What is the SGLT1 co-transporter mechanism?
tap to flip
Answer
SGLT1 co-transport: Glucose + Na⁺ compete for same transporter on enterocyte apical membrane → active transport into cell → water follows osmotically

ORS formula: ~75 mmol/L Na⁺, ~65 mmol/L Cl⁻, ~20 mmol/L K⁺, ~25 mmol/L glucose (1:1 glucose:Na⁺ ratio is key)

Impact: Saved millions from cholera/infectious diarrhea. Simple, cheap, effective.
LO 7 — Diphenoxylate vs. Loperamide
Both are μ-opioid antidiarrheals. Why is diphenoxylate combined with atropine? How does loperamide differ?
tap to flip
Answer
Diphenoxylate + atropine (Lomotil): μ-opioid agonist; atropine added to discourage abuse (anticholinergic side effects at high doses)

Loperamide (Imodium): μ-opioid agonist; peripheral only — does NOT cross BBB at therapeutic doses → no euphoria, minimal abuse potential, available OTC

CONTRAINDICATION for both: Infectious diarrhea (C. diff, shigella, Salmonella) — risk of toxic megacolon
LO 8 — Laxatives by Mechanism
Name four laxative classes, one drug per class, onset time, and mechanism.
tap to flip
Answer
Bulk-forming: Psyllium, methylcellulose → water retention; 1–3 days
Osmotic/saline: Magnesium citrate, lactulose, PEG → osmotic water draw; hours to overnight
Stimulant/irritant: Senna, bisacodyl → myenteric nerve stimulation; hours (abuse potential)
Stool softener: Docusate → ↑water penetration of stool; days
LO 8 — Lactulose & Hepatic Encephalopathy
Why is lactulose used in hepatic encephalopathy, not just as a laxative? What is the mechanism?
tap to flip
Answer
Lactulose mechanism in HE: Non-absorbable disaccharide → bacterial fermentation → ↓colonic pH

Low pH traps ammonia (NH₃ ⇌ NH₄⁺) → ammonia becomes non-absorbable → ↓CNS ammonia toxicity

Dual benefit: ↓ammonia levels + osmotic diarrhea (flushes toxins)

Note: Lactulose also effective for constipation, but this HE mechanism is clinically crucial
LO 8 — Opioid-Induced Constipation
What is methylnaltrexone? Why is it unique for opioid-induced constipation?
tap to flip
Answer
Methylnaltrexone: μ-opioid receptor antagonist (peripheral only)

Why unique: Peripherally active (does NOT cross BBB) → blocks μ receptors in GI tract (reversing opioid-induced constipation) WITHOUT antagonizing opioid analgesia in CNS

Use: Palliative care patients on opioids; allows adequate pain control without severe constipation
LO 9 — 5-ASA Drugs
What is mesalamine (5-ASA)? What role in IBD? How does sulfasalazine differ?
tap to flip
Answer
Mesalamine (5-aminosalicylic acid): Anti-inflammatory; ↓prostaglandins & leukotrienes
Uses: UC first-line induction & maintenance (topical enema form ↑efficacy); less effective in Crohn's

Sulfasalazine: 5-ASA + sulfapyridine (carrier); requires colonic bacteria for activation
• Older agent; GI side effects common
• Sulfa allergy? Use mesalamine instead
LO 9 — Steroids in IBD
Why are corticosteroids used in IBD? Which steroid (budesonide vs. prednisone) is preferred for Crohn's and why?
tap to flip
Answer
Role: INDUCTION only (not maintenance) — potent anti-inflammatory

Budesonide: Preferred for ileocecal Crohn's
• High first-pass hepatic metabolism (↓systemic side effects)
• Topical action in terminal ileum/colon

Prednisone: Systemic steroid for severe UC/Crohn's; more systemic side effects

Key: Taper after remission achieved; long-term steroids → immunosuppressants (azathioprine, 6-MP, TNF inhibitors)
LO 9 — Anti-TNF Biologics
Name two anti-TNF-α drugs. Compare chimeric (infliximab) vs. fully human (adalimumab) forms.
tap to flip
Answer
Infliximab: Chimeric (mouse-human) IgG → IV infusion; older
• Requires premedication (diphenhydramine, acetaminophen)
• TB screening MANDATORY (↑reactivation risk)

Adalimumab: Fully human IgG → subcutaneous; newer
• Self-administered; less immunogenic
• Still requires TB screening

Both: Preferred for Crohn's (transmural disease) + moderate-severe UC; ↑infection risk overall
LO 9 — Other IBD Biologics
What are vedolizumab and ustekinumab? How do their mechanisms differ from anti-TNF drugs?
tap to flip
Answer
Vedolizumab: Anti-integrin α4β7 (gut-selective) → blocks lymphocyte homing to GI mucosa; effective in UC + Crohn's

Ustekinumab: Anti-IL-12/23 (blocks cytokine signaling) → effective in moderate-severe Crohn's

Advantage vs. anti-TNF: Gut-selective (vedolizumab) or different immune pathway (ustekinumab) → potentially fewer systemic infections

Test Trap Mode: Each statement is either TRUE or FALSE as written. Many are designed to catch common exam errors. Click T or F — then read the explanation carefully.
Trap 01 — LO 1
"PPIs are reversible inhibitors of the H⁺/K⁺ ATPase, while H₂ blockers are irreversible."
FALSE. This is REVERSED! PPIs bind IRREVERSIBLY to the proton pump — accounts for their sustained effect and why they're tapered. H₂ blockers bind REVERSIBLY to the H₂ receptor. Once you learn which is which, remember: PPI = Persistent (irreversible).
Trap 02 — LO 1
"Cimetidine is preferred over other H₂ blockers because it has no drug interactions or hormonal side effects."
FALSE. Cimetidine is actually the WORST choice for routine use! It inhibits CYP450 (↑drug interactions) AND causes antiandrogen effects (gynecomastia, impotence). Ranitidine, famotidine, and nizatidine are safer alternatives with minimal CYP450 interactions.
Trap 03 — LO 1
"Sucralfate should be taken simultaneously with PPIs for maximal acid suppression and mucosal protection."
FALSE. Sucralfate requires an acidic environment to polymerize and bind the ulcer base. If you co-dose with PPIs/H₂ blockers, the acid is suppressed → sucralfate CANNOT activate → no benefit! Give sucralfate 1 hr before or 2 hrs after acid suppressants.
Trap 04 — LO 4
"Misoprostol is an excellent choice for NSAID-induced ulcer prevention in pregnant patients."
FALSE. Misoprostol is ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED in pregnancy — it is teratogenic AND abortifacient. Use only in post-menopausal women or those on reliable contraception. Best choice for NSAID ulcer prevention is switching to a COX-2 inhibitor or adding a PPI instead.
Trap 05 — LO 5–6
"Ondansetron (5-HT₃ antagonist) is the best first-line antiemetic for motion sickness."
FALSE. Ondansetron works for CHEMO & POSTOP nausea (D₂/5-HT₃ sources). Motion sickness involves VESTIBULAR pathways (H₁ & M₁ receptors), so use dimenhydrinate (H₁ antagonist) or scopolamine (M₁ antagonist) instead. Wrong drug, wrong pathway.
Trap 06 — LO 6
"Metoclopramide has no risk of neurologic side effects when used long-term, making it ideal for chronic gastric dysmotility."
FALSE. Metoclopramide causes TARDIVE DYSKINESIA (irreversible involuntary movements), extrapyramidal symptoms, and parkinsonism — especially with long-term use. Black box warning for neurologic toxicity. Avoid chronic use; reserve for acute, short-term nausea only.
Trap 07 — LO 7
"Loperamide (Imodium) readily crosses the blood-brain barrier and has significant abuse potential at standard doses."
FALSE. Loperamide is a PERIPHERAL μ-opioid — does NOT cross the BBB at therapeutic doses. No euphoria, minimal abuse potential, safe for OTC. (Diphenoxylate has atropine added to deter abuse because it CAN cause CNS effects at high doses.) This is the key difference.
Trap 08 — LO 9
"5-ASA drugs are most effective for treating moderate-to-severe Crohn's disease and should be first-line induction therapy."
FALSE. 5-ASA is FIRST-LINE for UC (mild-moderate induction & maintenance), but LESS effective in Crohn's. For moderate-severe Crohn's → use corticosteroids (induction) + biologics (TNF inhibitors preferred). 5-ASA plays a role in UC, not Crohn's first-line therapy.
