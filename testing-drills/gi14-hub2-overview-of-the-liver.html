LO 1 — Dual Blood Supply
What % of liver's oxygen does the hepatic artery vs portal vein contribute?
tap to flip
Answer
Hepatic artery: 50% of oxygen (high O2, low volume). Portal vein: 50% of oxygen (low O2, high nutrient-rich blood). Dual supply = explains why liver tolerates ischemia poorly if artery alone is compromised.
LO 1 — Acinus Zones
Zone 3 hepatocytes are most vulnerable to damage. Why?
tap to flip
Answer
Zone 3 = perivenous (around central vein); lowest O2 & nutrient levels. Zone 1 = periportal (highest O2). Zone 3 is first to fail in ischemia, acetaminophen toxicity, and cirrhosis.
LO 2 — Cirrhosis Definition
What pathologic change defines cirrhosis and why does portal hypertension develop?
tap to flip
Answer
Cirrhosis = diffuse hepatic necrosis → fibrosis → loss of normal architecture. Portal HTN results from: fibrous bands compress intrahepatic vessels → increased resistance → blood backs up in portal system → varices, ascites, splenomegaly.
LO 3 — Carbohydrate Metabolism
Liver controls blood glucose via which 2 pathways? What happens when both fail?
tap to flip
Answer
Glycogenolysis (breakdown) + gluconeogenesis (synthesis). Liver failure → hypoglycemia (can't make/break down glucose) + hyperammonemia (urea cycle fails) + hepatic encephalopathy.
LO 3 — Lipid Metabolism
Liver synthesizes which lipoproteins? What does low production signal?
tap to flip
Answer
Liver makes VLDL, HDL, albumin. Low albumin + ↓HDL + ↑VLDL = liver failure sign. Albumin particularly = synthetic function marker (half-life 20 days, so detects recent decline).
LO 4 — HMG-CoA Reductase
What is the rate-limiting enzyme in cholesterol synthesis? Where do statins work?
tap to flip
Answer
HMG-CoA reductase catalyzes: acetyl-CoA → HMG-CoA → mevalonate (rate-limiting step). Statins competitively inhibit this enzyme → ↓LDL production → ↓cardiovascular risk.
LO 4 — Statin Side Effect
Why do statins cause rhabdomyolysis? What's the mechanism?
tap to flip
Answer
Statins inhibit HMG-CoA reductase → ↓mevalonate → depletes CoQ10 (ubiquinone). CoQ10 needed for mitochondrial ATP production in muscle. Muscle can't generate ATP → myolysis. Risk ↑↑ with high-dose statins + fibrates + renal failure.
LO 5 — Bilirubin Pathway
Heme → Bilirubin. What's the sequence: free → liver → bile?
tap to flip
Answer
Heme (from RBC lysis) → unconjugated bilirubin (UCB) in blood (not water-soluble) → liver uptake → conjugation with glucuronic acid → conjugated bilirubin (CB) (water-soluble) → bile → excreted into intestine.
LO 5 — Jaundice Types Overview
4 types of jaundice. Which raises UCB vs CB?
tap to flip
Answer
Prehepatic: ↑↑UCB, ↓CB (hemolysis). Hepatic: ↑↑UCB + ↑↑CB (liver can't conjugate or excrete). Posthepatic: ↓UCB, ↑↑CB (bile duct obstruction). Gilbert/Crigler-Najjar/Dubin-Johnson/Rotor: congenital conjugation/excretion defects.
LO 5 — Gilbert Syndrome
How does Gilbert syndrome differ from Crigler-Najjar Type I? Why is one benign?
tap to flip
Answer
Gilbert: 30% bilirubin UDP-glucuronosyltransferase activity → mild ↑UCB (jaundice with stress/illness). Crigler-Najjar Type I: 0% activity → severe ↑↑UCB → kernicterus if untreated. Gilbert benign because residual enzyme suffices at baseline.
LO 6 — Bile Constituents
What are the 3 main components of bile and their functions?
tap to flip
Answer
Bile acids: emulsify fats (primary = cholic, chenodeoxycholic; secondary = deoxycholic, lithocholic). Cholesterol: structural component. Phospholipids + bilirubin: cholesterol solubilization, waste elimination.
LO 7 — Enterohepatic Circulation
Order the EHC steps: ileum reabsorption, portal return, liver excretion.
tap to flip
Answer
1) Liver synthesizes/excretes bile acids into bile. 2) Bile enters intestine, emulsifies fats. 3) Terminal ileum reabsorbs 95% of bile acids. 4) Portal vein returns them to liver. Cycle repeats 6–8×/day. Loss = ↑new synthesis.
LO 7 — Bile Acid Regulation
How does the liver sense bile acid shortage and respond?
tap to flip
Answer
FXR (farnesoid X receptor) on hepatocytes senses bile acid levels. ↓bile acids → ↑FGF19 (ileal signal) → ↓CYP7A1 (cholesterol → bile acid synthesis). Negative feedback loop maintains bile acid pool homeostasis (~600 mg).
LO 8 — Cholecystectomy Consequences
Post-cholecystectomy: why do patients have loose stools but bile still works?
tap to flip
Answer
Gallbladder = storage/concentration organ. Without it, bile drips continuously (not bolus) → inadequate fat emulsification with meals → steatorrhea (fatty stools), mild malabsorption. EHC still functions; just less efficient meal-time delivery. 90% adapt over months.
LO 10 — Ileal Resection
Ileal resection removes the main reabsorption site. What's the consequence?
tap to flip
Answer
Loss of ileal reabsorption → bile acids enter colon (↑colonic fluid) → diarrhea. Liver ↑synthesis BUT can't replace lost bile acids fast enough (limited cholesterol → bile acid conversion). Bile acid pool shrinks → ↓fat emulsification → steatorrhea + fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (A,D,E,K).
LO 1 — Hepatic Acinus Zones
Zone 1, 2, 3: which does what metabolically?
tap to flip
Answer
Zone 1 (periportal): oxidative metabolism, gluconeogenesis. Zone 2: transitional. Zone 3 (perivenous): glycolysis, detoxification (CYP450, acetaminophen metabolism). Zone 3 is metabolically distinct → most drug toxicity occurs here.
LO 2 — Ascites vs Edema
Portal hypertension → ascites. Peripheral edema from what?
tap to flip
Answer
Ascites: portal HTN → splanchnic capillary leakage + lymphatic overload. Peripheral edema: hypoalbuminemia (liver failure) → ↓plasma oncotic pressure → fluid shifts into interstitium. Combined: both mechanisms in advanced cirrhosis.
LO 5 — Dubin-Johnson vs Rotor
Both have conjugated hyperbilirubinemia. What's the key difference?
tap to flip
Answer
Dubin-Johnson: defect in bile acid excretion (MRP2 mutation) → dark urine (conjugated bilirubi excreted kidneys) + dark liver on biopsy. Rotor: organic anion uptake defect → conjugated bilirubin backs up but no dark liver. Both benign.
LO 6 — Bile Acid Functions
What's the main function of bile acids and where are primary vs secondary ones produced?
tap to flip
Answer
Main function: emulsify dietary fats (micellar formation → ↑absorption). Primary bile acids synthesized in liver from cholesterol (cholic, chenodeoxycholic). Secondary bile acids formed by bacterial metabolism in colon (deoxycholic, lithocholic). ~95% reabsorbed in terminal ileum.
LO 9 — CCK vs Secretin
Which hormone triggers gallbladder contraction and which stimulates pancreatic/biliary secretion?
tap to flip
Answer
CCK: triggers gallbladder contraction (releases concentrated bile into duodenum) + pancreatic enzyme secretion. Secretin: stimulates pancreatic bicarbonate secretion (neutralizes acid) + hepatic bile secretion (watery, bicarbonate-rich). Both released by duodenal I-cells in response to fats and acid.

Ordering Drills — Sequence each process in correct order.

Bilirubin Metabolism Pathway
Order the steps from hemolysis to bile excretion.
Heme degradation in macrophages
Unconjugated bilirubin binds albumin
Hepatic uptake via carrier-mediated transport
Conjugation with glucuronic acid (UDP-GT)
Excretion into bile canaliculi
Transport to gallbladder for storage
Correct sequence:
  1. Heme degraded to unconjugated bilirubin (spleen/macrophages)
  2. UCB binds albumin for transport (not water-soluble)
  3. Liver takes up UCB via carrier
  4. Conjugation with glucuronic acid via UDP-glucuronosyltransferase
  5. CB excreted into bile canaliculi (active transport)
  6. Stored in gallbladder; released with meal (CCK stimulus)
Enterohepatic Circulation Steps
Order the cycle of bile acid movement.
Liver synthesizes primary bile acids from cholesterol
Bile acids stored in gallbladder between meals
CCK released → gallbladder contracts → bile into duodenum
Bile acids emulsify dietary fats in small intestine
Terminal ileum reabsorbs 95% of bile acids
Portal vein returns bile acids to liver; cycle repeats
Correct sequence:
  1. Liver makes primary bile acids (cholesterol → 7α-hydroxylase pathway)
  2. Stored in gallbladder (concentrated 10–20×)
  3. Meal → CCK → GB contracts; bile flows into duodenum
  4. Bile acids emulsify fat droplets (micellar formation)
  5. Terminal ileum absorbs ~95%; lost 5% excreted in stool
  6. Reabsorbed bile acids return via portal vein; cycle 6–8×/day
HMG-CoA Reductase Pathway (Statin MOA)
Order the steps of cholesterol synthesis and statin inhibition.
Acetyl-CoA enters cholesterol synthesis pathway
HMG-CoA reductase catalyzes rate-limiting conversion
Statin competitively inhibits HMG-CoA reductase
↓ Mevalonate production blocks downstream synthesis
Liver upregulates LDL receptors (compensatory)
↑ LDL clearance from blood; ↓ plasma cholesterol
Correct sequence:
  1. Acetyl-CoA substrate fed into mevalonate pathway
  2. HMG-CoA reductase = rate-limiting enzyme (committed step)
  3. Statin (lovastatin, atorvastatin, etc.) competitively binds active site
  4. Mevalonate production ↓ → downstream products (cholesterol, CoQ10, sterols) ↓
  5. Liver senses low cholesterol → ↑LDL receptor expression (SREBP activation)
  6. ↑ Receptor-mediated endocytosis of LDL; plasma LDL ↓ ~30–50%
Portal Hypertension Cascade
Order the progression from cirrhosis to decompensation.
Chronic liver injury (viral hepatitis, alcohol, autoimmune)
Hepatic necrosis → fibrosis → cirrhosis (collagen deposition)
Intrahepatic vascular compression → portal resistance ↑
Portal pressure >12 mmHg → portal hypertension defined
Splanchnic vasodilation + collateral vessel formation (esophageal varices)
Ascites, hepatic encephalopathy, spontaneous bacterial peritonitis develop
Correct sequence:
  1. Chronic liver injury from multiple etiologies
  2. Parenchymal necrosis → fibrotic replacement → cirrhosis
  3. Nodules + fibrous septa compress sinusoidal vessels mechanically
  4. Portal pressure rises above normal ~12 mmHg threshold
  5. Splanchnic circulation dilates (vasodilators: NO, prostaglandins); varices form as portal shunts
  6. Complications: ascites (splanchnic leak), encephalopathy (NH3 shunts systemic), SBP, variceal bleeding

Single Best Answer — Pick ONE correct answer per question.

Q1
A 45-year-old man with alcoholic cirrhosis is found to have a plasma albumin of 2.8 g/dL (normal ~3.5–5.0). Which of the following mechanisms best explains his hypoalbuminemia?
A Increased albumin catabolism by splenic macrophages
B Decreased hepatic synthetic function due to cirrhosis
C Enhanced renal filtration of serum albumin
D Increased plasma volume from portal hypertension dilution
E Sequestration of albumin in ascitic fluid
Correct answer: B Cirrhosis destroys hepatic parenchyma → hepatocytes cannot synthesize albumin. Albumin has a 20-day half-life, so low levels reflect recent synthetic failure. A, C, D, E don't explain isolated hypoalbuminemia in liver disease. This is a hallmark of decompensated cirrhosis.
Q2
A 3-month-old infant presents with severe hyperbilirubinemia (total ~20 mg/dL, predominantly unconjugated). Phototherapy has been ineffective. The infant's urine is normal color. Which inherited condition is most likely?
A Dubin-Johnson syndrome
B Crigler-Najjar syndrome type I
C Rotor syndrome
D Gilbert syndrome
E Biliary atresia
Correct answer: B Crigler-Najjar I = absent UDP-glucuronosyltransferase activity → severe UCB accumulation (kernicterus risk). Phototherapy often fails; needs plasma exchange or gene therapy. Gilbert is mild (30% enzyme activity). Dubin-Johnson/Rotor = conjugated hyperbilirubinemia. Biliary atresia = posthepatic (high CB).
Q3
A 52-year-old woman on atorvastatin 80 mg daily presents with severe myalgias and dark urine. CK = 4500 U/L (normal less than 200). Which metabolite depletion is responsible for her muscle toxicity?
A Reduced acetyl-CoA availability
B Depletion of ubiquinone (CoQ10)
C Inhibition of muscle carnitine synthesis
D Direct statin-induced mitochondrial apoptosis
E Cholesterol depletion in myocyte membranes
Correct answer: B Statins inhibit HMG-CoA reductase → block mevalonate pathway → ↓CoQ10 (ubiquinone) synthesis. CoQ10 = essential cofactor in mitochondrial electron transport chain. Muscle (high ATP demand) can't generate ATP → myolysis, myoglobinuria (dark urine). CoQ10 supplementation may help.
Q4
Which laboratory finding would best differentiate prehepatic jaundice from posthepatic jaundice?
A Elevated conjugated bilirubin
B Unconjugated bilirubin greater than conjugated bilirubin
C Elevated alkaline phosphatase with normal transaminases
D Direct hyperbilirubinemia with acholic (pale) stool
E Reticulocytosis on CBC
Correct answer: B Prehepatic: ↑↑UCB, ↓CB (can conjugate). Posthepatic: ↓UCB, ↑↑CB (obstruction prevents excretion). Unconjugated predominance = prehepatic. Conjugated predominance = hepatic or posthepatic (use imaging to differentiate). Reticulocytosis indicates hemolysis (prehepatic) but isn't lab-based differentiation of bilirubin type.
Q5
A patient with cystic fibrosis undergoes ileal resection for Crohn's disease. Which consequence is MOST likely?
A Increased bile acid reabsorption and conjugation
B Accelerated gastric emptying and diarrhea
C Loss of terminal ileum bile acid absorption leading to bile acid pool shrinkage and steatorrhea plus fat-soluble vitamin deficiency
D Gallstone formation from cholestasis
E Hepatic cirrhosis within 2 years
Correct answer: C Terminal ileum = reabsorption site for 95% of bile acids. Resection → loss of reabsorption → bile acids enter colon (osmotic diarrhea) + lost to stool. Liver can't synthesize replacements fast enough → bile acid pool shrinks → inadequate fat emulsification → steatorrhea, fat-soluble vitamin malabsorption (A, D, E, K).
Q6
During hepatic ischemia, which hepatic acinus zone is most vulnerable to necrosis and why?
A Zone 1 (periportal) — highest oxygen demand
B Zone 2 (transitional) — metabolically intermediate
C Zone 3 (perivenous) — lowest oxygen and nutrient perfusion
D All zones equally vulnerable to ischemia
E Zone 1 due to detoxification enzyme concentration
Correct answer: C Zone 3 = perivenous = "last to be perfused, first to die." Lowest O2 tension; farthest from portal blood supply. Zone 1 has rich blood supply (periportal) → withstands ischemia better. Acetaminophen toxicity, cirrhosis, and shock predominantly affect Zone 3.
Q7
A 60-year-old man with primary biliary cholangitis has persistent cholestasis. Which bile acid derivative is most effective at enhancing hepatic excretion?
A Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA)
B Chenodeoxycholic acid
C Lithocholic acid
D Taurocholic acid
E Deoxycholic acid
Correct answer: A UDCA = ursodeoxycholic acid. Less hydrophobic than primary bile acids → less hepatotoxic. Substitutes for endogenous bile acids, enhances hepatic excretion (FXR/TGR5 agonist), and improves cholestasis in PBC. First-line therapy for cholestatic liver disease.
Q8
A 28-year-old woman notes jaundice and acholic (pale) stools after a meal. Imaging reveals a 1.5 cm stone in the common bile duct. What type of jaundice is present?
A Prehepatic jaundice
B Hepatic jaundice
C Posthepatic (obstructive) jaundice
D Gilbert syndrome
E Hemolytic anemia
Correct answer: C Posthepatic (obstructive) jaundice = bile duct obstruction (stone). Prevents CB excretion into bile → ↑↑CB, ↓UCB. Acholic stool = no bile pigments reaching intestine. ↑ALP/GGT is characteristic of biliary obstruction. Prehepatic would show hemolysis markers + ↑↑UCB.
Q9
Which hepatic metabolic dysfunction is most characteristic of decompensated cirrhosis and best explains asterixis (flapping tremor)?
A Failure of glycogenolysis → hypoglycemia
B Inability to metabolize ammonia (urea cycle) → hyperammonemia
C Loss of bile acid synthesis → malabsorption
D Decreased albumin synthesis → hypoproteinemia
E Portal hypertension → splenic infarction
Correct answer: B Hepatic encephalopathy = hyperammonemia (urea cycle impaired) + elevated false neurotransmitters. Asterixis (flapping tremor) = signature neurologic sign of hepatic encephalopathy. Ammonia is CNS toxin. A is relevant to hypoglycemia but doesn't cause asterixis specifically.
Q10
A 35-year-old with recently diagnosed Gilbert syndrome reports jaundice during a viral illness. Why does his hyperbilirubinemia worsen with stress/illness?
A Increased hemolysis from immune activation
B UDP-glucuronosyltransferase activity is reduced (30% normal) and stress/illness further decreases enzyme function
C Viral inhibition of hepatic uptake of unconjugated bilirubin
D Acute cholestasis from viral hepatitis
E Splenic sequestration of RBCs → reduced liver bilirubin clearance
Correct answer: B Gilbert = mildly reduced UDP-GT activity (30% normal). At baseline, residual enzyme is sufficient. Stress/illness/fasting → further ↓enzyme activity + ↑bilirubin production (hemolysis) = clinical jaundice becomes visible. Benign; no treatment needed. Crigler-Najjar I would have 0% activity (severe jaundice always).

Clinical Vignettes — Read the case, select answer, learn the mechanism.

Vignette 1
23-year-old college student presents with jaundice during final exams. Labs: total bilirubin 3.2 mg/dL (mostly unconjugated), normal ALT/AST, normal ALP. Hemoglobin 14.5 g/dL, reticulocyte count 2% (normal). Viral serologies negative. The patient notes jaundice episodes during previous illnesses. What's the diagnosis?
A Acute viral hepatitis
B Gilbert syndrome
C Crigler-Najjar type II
D Hemolytic anemia
E Dubin-Johnson syndrome
Correct answer: B — Gilbert Syndrome
Key findings: ↑UCB, normal transaminases/ALP (no hepatocellular damage), normal hemoglobin/retics (no hemolysis), recurrent jaundice with stress/illness. Mechanism: 30% UDP-GT activity. Normally sufficient at baseline. Stress/fasting/illness → ↓enzyme + ↑bilirubin → clinical jaundice. Benign; no treatment needed. Crigler-Najjar would have much higher bilirubin (greater than 10 mg/dL) and lifelong jaundice. Dubin-Johnson has conjugated hyperbilirubinemia + dark liver.
Vignette 2
8-day-old newborn presents with severe jaundice (total bilirubin 18 mg/dL, mostly unconjugated), lethargy, high-pitched cry, and poor feeding. Mothers are O+ and baby is O+. Direct antiglobulin test (Coombs) is negative. The baby was previously jaundiced at day 2 of life but phototherapy was stopped. What's the underlying enzyme defect?
A Hemoglobin S (sickle cell disease)
B Bilirubin UDP-glucuronosyltransferase (absent)
C Glucose-6-phosphatase (glycogenolysis defect)
D Heme oxygenase (overproduction of bilirubin)
E Glucokinase (hyperglycemia)
Correct answer: B — Crigler-Najjar Type I
Red flags: Severe unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia, neurologic signs (lethargy, cry, feeding difficulty = early kernicterus), phototherapy failed/inadequate. Negative Coombs = no hemolysis. Mechanism: Complete absence of bilirubin UDP-glucuronosyltransferase → cannot conjugate bilirubin → severe ↑UCB → bilirubin crosses BBB (lipid-soluble) → deposits in basal ganglia/hippocampus → kernicterus (irreversible brain damage). Requires plasma exchange ± gene therapy. Phototherapy alone is insufficient. Gilbert would never be this severe.
Vignette 3
52-year-old woman with HCV cirrhosis presents with abdominal distension and weight gain despite poor appetite. Exam: distended abdomen, fluid wave, hyperactive bowel sounds, but legs are non-edematous. Labs: albumin 2.1 g/dL (low), total bilirubin 4.8 mg/dL (mostly conjugated), INR 2.3 (prolonged). What's the PRIMARY mechanism of ascites in cirrhosis?
A Nephrotic syndrome (urinary protein loss)
B Cardiac heart failure (backward flow)
C Portal hypertension (splanchnic capillary leakage) + hypoalbuminemia (decreased oncotic pressure)
D Lymphatic obstruction from tumor
E Bacterial peritonitis (increased peritoneal inflammation)
Correct answer: C — Portal Hypertension + Hypoalbuminemia
Clinical clue: Ascites WITHOUT peripheral edema (classic cirrhosis pattern). Mechanisms: (1) Portal HTN (cirrhotic nodules compress vessels) → splanchnic capillary hydrostatic pressure ↑ → fluid transudation into peritoneal cavity. (2) Low albumin (2.1 = severe, from failed synthetic function) → ↓plasma oncotic pressure → more fluid leakage. (3) Splanchnic vasodilation (NO production) → renal hypoperfusion → secondary hyperaldosteronism → sodium/fluid retention. Non-edematous legs = albumin low but not low ENOUGH for peripheral edema yet (ascites forms first because peritoneum has lower hydrostatic threshold).
Vignette 4
34-year-old man on simvastatin for familial hypercholesterolemia takes azithromycin for a respiratory infection. Within 1 week, he develops severe myalgias, dark urine, and is found to have CK 8000 U/L. What's the mechanism of this interaction?
A Azithromycin induces statin metabolism → higher statin levels
B Azithromycin inhibits CYP3A4, prolonging statin half-life leading to increased statin concentration and increased CoQ10 depletion
C Synergistic activation of myofibril apoptosis
D Azithromycin causes direct myositis
E Rhabdomyolysis from hyperkalemia
Correct answer: B — CYP3A4 Inhibition + Statin Accumulation
Key interaction: Simvastatin is metabolized by CYP3A4 (hepatic). Azithromycin (a macrolide) INHIBITS CYP3A4 → simvastatin clearance ↓ → plasma concentration ↑ → excessive HMG-CoA reductase inhibition → massive CoQ10 depletion → mitochondrial ATP crisis in muscle → rhabdomyolysis (dark urine = myoglobinuria). Other CYP3A4 inhibitors: grapefruit juice, ketoconazole, diltiazem. Avoid statin + strong CYP3A4 inhibitor combinations or reduce statin dose.
Vignette 5
40-year-old woman with a history of recurrent jaundice since childhood now presents for evaluation. Labs show conjugated hyperbilirubinemia (1.8 mg/dL direct) with normal transaminases. Abdominal imaging is normal; ultrasound shows no biliary dilation or stones. Patient reports dark urine. Liver biopsy shows dark/black liver on gross inspection. What's the diagnosis?
A Primary sclerosing cholangitis
B Dubin-Johnson syndrome
C Rotor syndrome
D Primary biliary cholangitis
E Hemolytic anemia (chronic)
Correct answer: B — Dubin-Johnson Syndrome
Pathognomonic finding: Dark/black liver on biopsy = golden-brown melanin-like pigment (not actual melanin) deposits in hepatocytes. Mechanism: Defective MRP2 transporter → impaired conjugated bilirubin excretion into bile canaliculi → CB backs up → some enters blood (conjugated hyperbilirubinemia) + some is re-excreted into urine (dark urine). Benign. Rotor = conjugated hyperbilirubinemia BUT no dark liver pigment. Both are autosomal recessive. Prognosis excellent for both.

Step 1 Traps — Avoid these classic confusions.

Trap 1
Unconjugated bilirubin is water-soluble and is excreted by the kidneys, causing dark urine in prehepatic jaundice.
INCORRECT — This is the trap!
Unconjugated bilirubin is NOT water-soluble. It binds to albumin for blood transport and is not filtered by kidneys → urine stays normal color even in prehepatic jaundice. Only conjugated bilirubin is water-soluble → filtered by kidneys → dark urine in hepatic/posthepatic jaundice. Dark urine = conjugated hyperbilirubinemia.
Trap 2
Zone 1 hepatocytes are most vulnerable to ischemic injury because they perform the most metabolic work.
INCORRECT — This is the trap!
Zone 3 (perivenous) is most vulnerable to ischemia, NOT Zone 1. Zone 3 is farthest from the portal blood supply → lowest O2 tension. Zone 1 (periportal) has the HIGHEST oxygen because it's first to receive portal and arterial blood. Zone 1 is most resistant to ischemia; Zone 3 is the "last to be perfused, first to die." Acetaminophen toxicity, cirrhosis, and ischemic hepatitis all preferentially affect Zone 3.
Trap 3
Gilbert syndrome and Crigler-Najjar type I are indistinguishable and both require treatment to prevent kernicterus.
INCORRECT — This is the trap!
Gilbert: 30% bilirubin UDP-GT activity → mild ↑UCB (~2–3 mg/dL) → jaundice only with stress/illness. Benign; no treatment.
Crigler-Najjar I: 0% bilirubin UDP-GT activity → severe ↑↑UCB (~20+ mg/dL) → lifelong jaundice → kernicterus risk → requires treatment (plasma exchange, gene therapy). Severity is dramatically different. Gilbert is often diagnosed incidentally and is NOT a contraindication to normal living.
Trap 4
The hepatic artery contributes most of the blood volume to the liver, so hepatic artery occlusion is less tolerated than portal vein occlusion.
INCORRECT — This is the trap!
Portal vein: 75% of blood volume (rich in nutrients), 50% of oxygen. Hepatic artery: 25% of blood volume, 50% of oxygen. Hepatic artery occlusion is WORSE tolerated because losing the high-O2 blood → ischemia. Portal vein occlusion spares the arterial blood supply (partially compensates). Classic exam scenario: Accidentally clip hepatic artery during surgery → hepatic necrosis. Clip portal vein → liver may survive.
Trap 5
Statins cause rhabdomyolysis by directly activating muscle apoptosis via a caspase-independent pathway.
INCORRECT — This is the trap!
Statins cause rhabdo by depleting CoQ10, NOT by caspase activation. Mechanism: HMG-CoA reductase inhibition → ↓mevalonate → ↓CoQ10 (ubiquinone) → mitochondrial ATP production fails → muscle can't maintain ATP-dependent Na-K pump → myolysis. Treatment: stop statin, consider CoQ10 supplementation. Direct apoptosis is not the mechanism.
Trap 6
Ileal resection causes diarrhea solely because of bile acids remaining in the intestine, unrelated to fat absorption.
PARTIALLY WRONG — This is the trap!
Bile acids in the colon DO cause diarrhea (osmotic), BUT the main problem is bile acid pool depletion. Lost bile acids enter stool; liver can't replace them fast enough → bile acid pool shrinks → inadequate fat emulsification → steatorrhea. BOTH mechanisms matter: diarrhea from excess colonic bile acids + fat malabsorption from depleted bile acid pool. Fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (A, D, E, K) is a major consequence.
Trap 7
Cholecystectomy results in permanent fat malabsorption and steatorrhea because the liver can no longer concentrate bile.
INCORRECT — This is the trap!
Cholecystectomy removes the storage/concentration function, but the liver still secretes bile (just continuously, not as a bolus). ~90% of patients adapt over months → steatorrhea resolves. Fat emulsification is less efficient immediately post-op but usually adequate. Not permanent. Contrast this to ileal resection, where bile acid pool is permanently lost.
Trap 8
In posthepatic jaundice, unconjugated bilirubin is elevated while conjugated bilirubin remains normal.
INCORRECT — This is the trap!
Posthepatic jaundice: ↓↓UCB, ↑↑CB (mostly conjugated). Bile duct obstruction prevents CB excretion → backs up into liver → leaks into blood. Prehepatic jaundice: ↑↑UCB, ↓CB. The conjugated/unconjugated ratio is the KEY differentiator: UCB predominance = prehepatic; CB predominance = hepatic or posthepatic (use imaging to distinguish).

Bile Acid Pool: Synthesis, Circulation & Loss
Process Location Key Regulators Effect on Bile Acid Pool
Synthesis Liver CYP7A1 (7α-hydroxylase; rate-limiting), cholesterol substrate ↑ New bile acid production when pool depleted
Enterohepatic Circulation Liver → GI tract → terminal ileum (reabsorption) → portal vein → liver Bile acid synthesis, FXR/TGR5 (farnesoid X receptor) Cycle 6–8×/day; ~95% reabsorbed
Loss Stool (5% of pool daily normally) — Lost bile acids must be replaced by new synthesis; normal rate ~0.5 g/day
Feedback inhibition Ileal epithelium (FXR activation by reabsorbed bile acids) FXR → FGF19 → hepatic CYP7A1 inhibition ↓ Synthesis when pool is adequate
