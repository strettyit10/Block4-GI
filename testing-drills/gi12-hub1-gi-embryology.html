LO 1 — Folding
Describe cranial-caudal folding of the embryo and what endoderm tissue it creates.
tap to flip
Answer
Cranial-caudal folding: Head and tail ends of embryo fold ventrally (toward ventral plate)
Creates: Primitive foregut (from splanchnic mesoderm-lined endoderm)

Forms the tube-within-a-tube architecture — endoderm becomes the gut epithelium; splanchnic mesoderm → gut smooth muscle, mesentery
LO 1 — Folding
Explain lateral (left-right) folding and how it completes the tube-within-a-tube.
tap to flip
Answer
Lateral folding: Right and left lateral body folds converge at the ventral midline

Creates the closed GI tube — endoderm meets at midline → completes the epithelial tube
Splanchnic mesoderm from both sides → dorsal & ventral mesentery + smooth muscle sleeve

Result: continuous tube from pharynx → anus, lined by endoderm, surrounded by splanchnic mesoderm
LO 2 — Ventral Mesentery
Where does ventral mesentery exist, and what does it form in the foregut?
tap to flip
Answer
Exists ONLY in foregut — nowhere else in midgut or hindgut

Divided by the developing liver:
• Cranial (superior): Falciform ligament (attaches liver to anterior body wall)
• Caudal (inferior): Lesser omentum (between liver & stomach)

Hepatoduodenal ligament (part of lesser omentum) contains the portal triad: hepatic artery, portal vein, common bile duct
LO 2 — Dorsal Mesentery
What is the dorsal mesentery, where does it span, and which organs derive from it? List the SAD PUCKER derivatives.
tap to flip
Answer
Dorsal mesentery: continuous sheet of splanchnic mesoderm stretching from esophagus → rectum (all three gut regions)

SAD PUCKER (retroperitoneal structures):
Spleen · Adrenals · Duodenum (2nd, 3rd, 4th parts)
Pancreas (except uncinate + head) · Ureters · Colon (ascending, descending) · Kidneys · Esophagus · Rectum

White Line of Toldt = peritoneal reflection where ascending & descending colon transition from intraperitoneal to retroperitoneal
LO 3 — Foregut Definition
Define the foregut, name its structures, and state its arterial supply and distal boundary.
tap to flip
Answer
Foregut: Pharynx, esophagus, stomach, duodenum (1st & 2nd parts), liver, pancreas, spleen

Arterial supply: Celiac trunk (from T12 level)

Distal boundary: Joins midgut at the junction of D2/D3 (just beyond ampulla of Vater)

Key: Vagal trunks (parasympathetic) — Left vagus → anterior trunk; Right vagus → posterior trunk
LO 3 — Stomach Rotation
Describe the 90° clockwise rotation of the stomach during development and its anatomical consequence.
tap to flip
Answer
Rotation: Stomach rotates 90° clockwise around the anteroposterior axis

Consequence:
• Originally: left side dorsal, right side ventral
• After rotation: left side becomes anterior (greater curvature), right side becomes posterior (lesser curvature)
• Creates: Lesser sac (omental bursa) — potential space behind stomach

Left vagus → anterior vagal trunk (supplies anterior lesser curvature); Right vagus → posterior vagal trunk (supplies posterior lesser curvature)
LO 3 — Midgut Definition
Define the midgut, name its structures, arterial supply, and name the embryonic artery that serves as its reference.
tap to flip
Answer
Midgut: Duodenum (3rd & 4th parts), jejunum, ileum, cecum, appendix, ascending colon, proximal 2/3 transverse colon

Arterial supply: Superior Mesenteric Artery (SMA) (from L1 level)

Embryonic reference artery: Midgut artery — the SMA's embryonic precursor, acts as the reference for 270° CCW rotation

Boundary markers: proximal 2/3 of transverse colon (supplied by SMA), distal 1/3 (supplied by IMA)
LO 3 — Midgut Rotation
Describe the 270° counterclockwise rotation of the midgut around the SMA axis and its normal result.
tap to flip
Answer
Rotation: Midgut rotates 270° CCW around the SMA as a fixed axis

Sequence:
1. Small intestine (jejunum + ileum) initially on RIGHT
2. Rotates to superior mesentery, then crosses midline
3. Ends on LEFT → loops return to abdomen

Normal result: Duodenum crosses midline, becomes C-shaped, duodenojejunal flexure fixed at ligament of Treitz (left side, retroperitoneal)

Abnormal results: Malrotation (incomplete rotation) → midgut volvulus risk — "whirlpool sign" on imaging
LO 3 — Duodenum Development
What is the duodenal C-loop, and what is duodenal atresia/stenosis with the "Double Bubble" sign?
tap to flip
Answer
Duodenal C-loop: Duodenum wraps around the pancreatic head → becomes retroperitoneal after rotation

Duodenal atresia/stenosis: Failure of duodenal lumen recanalization during weeks 8–12

Classic presentation:
• Polyhydramnios (↑ amniotic fluid — no swallowing of meconium)
• "Double Bubble" sign on imaging — gas in dilated stomach + proximal duodenum, NONE distally
• Non-bilious vomiting
• Associated with: Down syndrome (50%), cardiac defects, VACTERL

Treatment: Duodenoduodenostomy (bypass)
LO 3 — Hindgut Definition
Define the hindgut, name its structures, and state its arterial supply. Where does pectinate line originate?
tap to flip
Answer
Hindgut: Distal 1/3 of transverse colon, descending colon, sigmoid colon, rectum, upper anal canal

Arterial supply: Inferior Mesenteric Artery (IMA) (from L3 level)

Pectinate (dentate) line: Marks the junction between hindgut (superior) & proctodeum (inferior)
• Located ~2 cm above anal verge
• Originates from anorectal junction during embryogenesis
• Divides anal canal anatomy: blood supply, innervation, venous drainage, lymphatics

Above pectinate line = hindgut; Below = external ectoderm (proctodeum)
LO 3 — Pancreatic Development
Describe the dorsal and ventral pancreatic buds, their fusion, and which parts each forms.
tap to flip
Answer
Dorsal pancreatic bud: Arises from dorsal duodenal wall → forms body, tail, and proximal head

Ventral pancreatic bud: Arises from hepatic diverticulum (bile duct origin) → forms uncinate process + inferior head

Fusion (week 6): Buds fuse as duodenum rotates → single pancreatic gland

Ducts:
• Dorsal duct: Main pancreatic duct (Wirsung) — drains body & tail
• Ventral duct: Joins common bile duct → enters at major papilla (ampulla of Vater)
• Accessory duct: From dorsal bud → minor papilla

Annular pancreas = ventral bud wraps around duodenum → obstruction risk
LO 4 — ENS Origins
Where do ENS neural crest cells originate, what migration pattern do they follow, and what is the GDNF-RET axis?
tap to flip
Answer
Origins: Vagal neural crest (occipital somites 1–7) + sacral neural crest (S2–S4)

Migration: Vagal cells migrate craniocaudally along the GI tract during weeks 4–12 — populate esophagus → distal colon

GDNF-RET axis:
• GDNF = glial cell-derived neurotrophic factor (target signal in gut mesenchyme)
• RET = receptor tyrosine kinase (on NCC surface)
• GDNF binds RET → signals NCC to migrate, proliferate, survive

Clinical: RET loss-of-function → Hirschsprung (no migration); RET gain-of-function → MEN2 syndrome
LO 4 — Hirschsprung Disease
What is Hirschsprung disease, what causes it, and what is the classic diagnostic finding and clinical presentation?
tap to flip
Answer
Hirschsprung disease: Failure of neural crest cells to migrate into distal colon

Cause: RET mutations (loss-of-function) or other NCC migration defects

Pathology: Aganglionic segment (no ganglion cells) → cannot relax → tonically contracted

Classic presentation: Newborn with failure to pass meconium within 48 hours + abdominal distention

Diagnosis: Rectal biopsy (gold standard) showing absence of ganglion cells & hypertrophied nerve fibers in aganglionic segment

Treatment: Surgical resection of aganglionic segment with pull-through (colostomy initially if severe)

MOANS C mnemonic: association with Down (Trisomy 21)
LO 4 — MOANS C Mnemonic
List the MOANS C mnemonic — syndromes associated with Hirschsprung disease (NCC migration defects).
tap to flip
Answer
MOANS C — Hirschsprung associations:

Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia (MEN2A, MEN2B) — RET mutations
Owens syndrome (no clear definition — sometimes skipped or listed differently)
A — (placeholder, not standard)
Neurocristopathies & Neurofibromatosis (NF1)
Short-segment Hirschsprung (most common — rectosigmoid)

C — Down syndrome (Trisomy 21) — most common chromosomal association

Other key associations: Waardenburg syndrome, Piebaldism, Cardiac defects (VACTERL)
LO 5 — Pyloric Stenosis
What is pyloric stenosis, when does it present, and what are the classic clinical and radiologic findings?
tap to flip
Answer
Pyloric stenosis: Hypertrophy & hyperplasia of pyloric smooth muscle

Presentation: 2–8 weeks of age (typically 4–6 weeks)

Classic symptoms:
• Projectile vomiting (AFTER feeding)
• Non-bilious vomiting (obstruction is proximal to bile duct entry)
• Hungry after vomiting ("hungry vomiter")
• Abdominal distention
• Dehydration, hypokalemic-hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis

Diagnosis: Abdominal ultrasound — thickened pyloric muscle (muscle thickness ≥3 mm)

Risk factors: Male (4:1), 1st-born, family history, maternal erythromycin exposure

Treatment: Pyloromyotomy (Ramstedt procedure)
LO 5 — Omphalocele vs. Gastroschisis
Compare omphalocele and gastroschisis: definition, covering, location, and associated anomalies.
tap to flip
Answer
Omphalocele:
• Incomplete closure of umbilical ring
• Abdominal contents herniate INTO peritoneal sac (covered by amnion + peritoneum)
• Located at umbilicus
• Associations: Beckwith-Wiedemann, Down, cardiac defects, VACTERL
• Treatment: Primary closure or staged repair (mesh if large)

Gastroschisis:
• Full-thickness defect lateral to umbilicus (usually right)
• Abdominal contents herniate OUTSIDE peritoneum (NO sac covering)
• Located 2–4 cm lateral to umbilicus
• Associations: Rare; NOT associated with trisomy or Beckwith-Wiedemann
• Bowel is edematous & indurated
• Treatment: Primary closure or staged repair (high complication risk)

TRAP: Omphalocele is covered (has sac), gastroschisis is naked!
LO 5 — Meckel Diverticulum
State the "Rule of 2s" for Meckel diverticulum and list the key clinical presentations and diagnostic test.
tap to flip
Answer
Meckel diverticulum — Rule of 2s:
• 2% of population
• 2 feet proximal to ileocecal valve
• 2 inches long
• 2 types of ectopic tissue (gastric mucosa → HCl, pancreatic tissue)
• 2× more common in males
• 2 complications: bleeding, obstruction

Clinical presentations:
• Painless GI bleeding (most common presentation) — from ectopic gastric mucosa ulcerating adjacent ileal mucosa
• Intestinal obstruction (volvulus, intussusception, internal herniation)
• Diverticulitis

Diagnosis: Technetium-99m pertechnetate scan (Meckel scan) — isotope concentrates in ectopic gastric mucosa

Treatment: Surgical excision
LO 5 — Annular Pancreas
What is annular pancreas, how does it form, and what are the clinical consequences?
tap to flip
Answer
Annular pancreas: Ventral pancreatic bud wraps AROUND duodenum like a ring

Formation: During rotation, ventral bud's tissue encircles the duodenum instead of fusing normally

Pathology: Duodenal narrowing/obstruction at D2–D3 junction

Clinical presentation:
• Can present in neonates (rare) with duodenal obstruction signs
• More commonly symptomatic in adults with epigastric pain, bloating, early satiety
• May worsen with eating

Diagnosis: Upper endoscopy (narrowing at duodenum) or imaging

Treatment: Laparoscopic duodenoduodenostomy or gastrojejunostomy
KEY POINT: NEVER divide the annular pancreatic ring itself — risk of fistula & injury to pancreatic ducts
LO 5 — Malrotation & Volvulus
What is midgut malrotation, why is volvulus dangerous, and what is the pathognomonic imaging sign?
tap to flip
Answer
Midgut malrotation: Incomplete 270° CCW rotation around the SMA

Consequence: Duodenal-jejunal flexure is NOT fixed at ligament of Treitz → loop can rotate freely around SMA axis

Midgut volvulus: Small intestine twists around the SMA (like a whirlpool)

Clinical emergency: Strangulation of mesentery → ischemia → bowel necrosis → massive mortality if untreated

Pathognomonic imaging sign: "Whirlpool sign" on CT/ultrasound — mesenteric vessels spinning around SMA axis

Presentations: Acute (neonate/infant with bilious vomiting, abdominal pain, shock) or chronic (recurrent symptoms)

Treatment: Emergent ladd procedure (divide ligaments of Treitz, untwist bowel, broaden mesentery)
LO 5 — Imperforate Anus & VACTERL
What is imperforate anus, and what does the VACTERL acronym stand for?
tap to flip
Answer
Imperforate anus (anorectal malformation): Anal opening is absent or covered — most common anorectal anomaly

Results from: Failure of cloacal membrane to perforate (week 7–8)

Presentations:
• Low anomalies: Fistula to perineum (females) or perineal body
• High anomalies: Fistula to urinary system (males) or reproductive system (females)

Diagnosis: Abdominal exam (no anal opening), meconium in urine or external genitalia (fistula sign), imaging

VACTERL association (≥3 features strongly suggestive):
Vertebral defects
Anal atresia (imperforate anus)
Cardiac defects
Tracheal-esophageal fistula (TEF)
Esophageal atresia
Renal/genitourinary anomalies
Limb defects

Treatment: Colostomy initially; definitive repair (anoplasty) after imaging defines anatomy

Test Trap Mode: Each statement is either TRUE or FALSE as written. Many are designed to catch common exam errors. Click T or F — then read the explanation carefully.
Trap 01 — LO 1
"Ventral mesentery exists throughout the entire GI tract, attaching the stomach, small intestine, and colon to the posterior body wall."
FALSE. Ventral mesentery exists ONLY in the FOREGUT. It is divided by the developing liver into the falciform ligament (superior) and lesser omentum (inferior). Midgut and hindgut have ONLY dorsal mesentery.
Trap 02 — LO 2
"The dorsal mesentery derivatives (pancreas, spleen, kidney) are intraperitoneal and freely mobile."
FALSE. SAD PUCKER derivatives are RETROPERITONEAL (secondarily retroperitoneal after peritoneal reflection). The dorsal mesentery extends across all three regions, but the structures listed (excluding spleen) are FIXED behind peritoneum: ascending colon, descending colon, pancreas, duodenum (middle 3 parts), kidneys, ureters, esophagus, rectum.
Trap 03 — LO 3
"The stomach rotates 90° counterclockwise, causing the left side to become the lesser curvature."
FALSE. The stomach rotates 90° CLOCKWISE (not CCW). After rotation, the LEFT side becomes the ANTERIOR (greater curvature); the RIGHT side becomes the POSTERIOR (lesser curvature). This rotation creates the lesser sac (omental bursa).
Trap 04 — LO 3
"Midgut rotation occurs around the IMA (inferior mesenteric artery) as the reference axis."
FALSE. Midgut rotation occurs around the SMA (superior mesenteric artery) axis. The SMA is the embryonic midgut artery and serves as the fixed reference point. The 270° CCW rotation pivots around this SMA axis, not the IMA.
Trap 05 — LO 3
"Omphalocele and gastroschisis are indistinguishable clinically — both are covered by peritoneal sac and both require the same surgical approach."
FALSE. KEY DISTINCTION: Omphalocele IS covered by peritoneal sac (amnion + peritoneum); Gastroschisis is NAKED (uncovered, no sac). Omphalocele is at the umbilicus; gastroschisis is 2–4 cm lateral to umbilicus. Omphalocele associates with Beckwith-Wiedemann; gastroschisis does not. Surgery differs accordingly.
Trap 06 — LO 3
"Meckel diverticulum is located 2 feet distal to the ileocecal valve and contains only gastric mucosa."
FALSE. Meckel location (Rule of 2s) is correct — 2 feet PROXIMAL to ileocecal valve (not distal). Ectopic tissues in Meckel: can be BOTH gastric mucosa AND pancreatic tissue (not just gastric). Gastric mucosa causes ulceration & painless bleeding; pancreatic tissue rare.
Trap 07 — LO 4
"Neural crest cells migrate craniocaudally along the GI tract, but only VAGAL neural crest is essential for ENS formation — sacral neural crest cells do not contribute."
FALSE. BOTH vagal (occipital somites 1–7) AND sacral (S2–S4) neural crest cells contribute to ENS formation. Vagal cells migrate craniocaudally from weeks 4–12; sacral cells also migrate and contribute, especially to distal gut. Loss of either source can impair ENS development.
Trap 08 — LO 4
"In Hirschsprung disease, the aganglionic segment is DILATED because ganglion cells are absent and the bowel relaxes abnormally."
FALSE. CLASSIC TRAP: The AGANGLIONIC segment is NARROW & TONICALLY CONTRACTED (absence of relaxing ganglion cells). The DILATED segment is PROXIMAL — it has normal ganglion cells but becomes distended because stool cannot pass through the contracted aganglionic segment below.
Trap 09 — LO 5
"Annular pancreas should be surgically treated by dividing the annular ring to relieve the duodenal obstruction."
FALSE. NEVER divide the annular pancreatic ring itself. Dividing it risks injury to pancreatic ducts → fistula formation & pancreatitis. Instead, bypass the obstruction with duodenoduodenostomy or gastrojejunostomy. Always preserve the annular tissue.
Trap 10 — LO 5
"Double Bubble sign on abdominal X-ray is pathognomonic for malrotation/midgut volvulus, confirming the diagnosis."
FALSE. "Double Bubble" sign (gas in stomach + proximal duodenum, none distally) is pathognomonic for DUODENAL ATRESIA, not malrotation. Malrotation/volvulus presents with "Whirlpool sign" (mesenteric vessels spiraling on CT/ultrasound). Different anomalies, different signs!

Embryologic Milestones Summary:
Week 4: Gastrulation begins; foregut forms; dorsal pancreatic bud appears
Week 4–5: Cranial-caudal & lateral folding create tube-within-a-tube; gastric rotation begins; ventral pancreatic bud appears
Week 5–6: Gastric rotation completes (90° CW); pancreatic bud fusion; midgut rotation begins (270° CCW)
Week 6–12: Midgut rotation completes; ENS neural crest cells migrate craniocaudally (weeks 4–12); duodenal lumen recanalization (weeks 8–12)
Week 7–8: Cloacal membrane perforates (anorectal development)
