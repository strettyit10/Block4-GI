Couinaud Liver Segments
How many Couinaud segments? Which are supplied by left vs right hepatic artery/vein?
Answer
8 segments (I–VIII) divided by hepatic vein planes (vertical) and portal vein (horizontal)
Left lobe: I (caudate), II, III, IV
Right lobe: V, VI, VII, VIII
Segment I (caudate): Special — drains directly to IVC (SPARED in Budd-Chiari since portal vein thrombosis doesn't affect it)
Porta Hepatis HAP Mnemonic
What structures pass through the porta hepatis from anterior to posterior?
Answer
HAP mnemonic:
H = Hepatic artery (anterior)
A = (Common Bile) Duct (anterior)
P = Portal vein (posterior)
Anterior structures (H, A): Palpable during surgery
Posterior structure (P): Portal vein is deepest; Pringle maneuver clamps hepatic artery + CBD (in porta hepatis) to stop hepatic bleeding while preserving portal flow
Pringle Maneuver & Caudate Sparing
What does Pringle maneuver accomplish? Why is caudate lobe spared in Budd-Chiari?
Answer
Pringle maneuver: Clamp hepatoduodenal ligament → stops bleeding from hepatic artery + CBD during liver surgery
Does NOT directly compress: Portal vein (posterior) or hepatic veins
Budd-Chiari (hepatic vein thrombosis): Caudate (Segment I) is SPARED because it drains directly to IVC via small hepatic veins SEPARATE from main hepatic veins
Result: Caudate remains perfused while rest of liver is congested
Liver Capsule & Ligaments
What is Glisson capsule? What three ligaments attach the liver, and where are they?
Answer
Glisson capsule: Fibrous capsule surrounding liver (extends along Glisson's sheath around portal triads)
Three main ligaments:
1. Falciform: Connects liver to anterior abdominal wall + diaphragm (separates R + L lobes)
2. Coronary: Connects liver to diaphragm posteriorly (superior to bare area)
3. Hepatoduodenal + Hepatogastric: Parts of lesser omentum; hepatoduodenal contains HAP (porta hepatis)
Biliary Tree: Hepatic to Ampulla
Biliary anatomy: CHD + cystic → CBD path through duodenum → ampulla?
Answer
Flow: Hepatocytes → Intrahepatic ducts → Right + Left hepatic ducts → Common hepatic duct (CHD)
CHD + Cystic duct → Common bile duct (CBD)
CBD path: Descends in hepatoduodenal ligament (anterior) → passes BEHIND first part of duodenum → grooves posterior pancreatic head
Enters duodenum: At major duodenal papilla (ampulla of Vater) in D2
Ampulla contains: Sphincter of Oddi (controls bile + pancreatic juice flow)
Murphy's Point & Gallbladder
Where is Murphy's point? What is Hartmann's pouch, and what is its clinical significance?
Answer
Murphy's point: Right 9th costal cartilage at MCL — surface marking of gallbladder fundus
Murphy's sign: RUQ pain + inspiration arrest during deep palpation = cholecystitis
Hartmann's pouch: Outpouch of gallbladder fundus where gallstones commonly lodge
Clinical significance: Stone in Hartmann's pouch can compress CBD externally = Mirizzi syndrome (obstructive jaundice WITHOUT stone in CBD)
Calot's Triangle & Cystic Artery
What are the three boundaries of Calot's triangle? What vessel runs through it?
Answer
Calot's triangle: Surgical space critical for cholecystectomy
Bounded by:
1. Cystic duct (inferomedially)
2. Common hepatic duct (medially)
3. Liver surface (superiorly)
Contains: Cystic artery (from right hepatic artery)
Critical view of safety: Identify 2 structures crossing triangle (cystic artery, cystic vein) before clipping to avoid bile duct injury
Mirizzi Syndrome
What is Mirizzi syndrome? How does it cause jaundice without CBD stone?
Answer
Definition: Stone lodged in Hartmann's pouch (gallbladder fundus) externally compresses the adjacent CBD
Key feature: CBD is PATENT (not obstructed by stone itself) but compressed from OUTSIDE
Result: Obstructive jaundice + biliary colic WITHOUT stone visible in CBD on imaging
Imaging clue: Bile duct dilated proximally, stone visible in gallbladder fundus compressing it
Tx: Cholecystectomy (remove gallstone + gallbladder to relieve external compression)
ERCP vs MRCP
ERCP: invasive or noninvasive? Diagnostic + therapeutic? MRCP: what does it do?
Answer
ERCP (Endoscopic Retrograde CP): INVASIVE endoscopy (duodenoscope via mouth)
Both diagnostic + therapeutic: Visualizes ampulla, cannulates ducts, removes stones, stents, obtains biopsies
Risk: Pancreatitis (3–5%)
MRCP (MR CP): NONINVASIVE imaging (MRI-based)
Purely diagnostic: Visualizes ducts but CANNOT treat
Use: Suspected choledocholithiasis, biliary obstruction (before ERCP if needed)
Splenic Artery Branches
Splenic artery: largest celiac branch. Major branches given off along its tortuous course?
Answer
Splenic artery = largest celiac branch (tortuous along superior pancreatic border)
Major branches:
1. Short gastrics (2-6) → fundus
2. Left gastroepiploic (omental) → greater curvature of stomach
3. Dorsal pancreatic → pancreatic body/tail
4. Pancreatica magna (great pancreatic) → pancreatic body
Terminal branches: Split into 2–3 branches entering splenic hilum
Splenic Vein & Portal Formation
Where does splenic vein run? How is portal vein formed at pancreatic neck?
Answer
Splenic vein: Posterior to pancreatic body + tail (grooves posterior surface)
Portal vein formation: Splenic vein joins superior mesenteric vein (SMV) at the NECK of pancreas → portal vein
Portal vein: Ascends in hepatoduodenal ligament (anterior, posterior to HAD)
Significance: Any mass compressing pancreatic neck (Whipple resection, tumors) can obstruct portal vein formation
Gastrosplenic & Splenorenal Ligaments
What structures are in each ligament? What danger do they present during splenectomy?
Answer
Gastrosplenic ligament (greater omentum): Contains short gastric vessels + left gastroepiploic vessel
Splenorenal ligament: Contains splenic artery + vein + PANCREATIC TAIL
Surgical danger: Must carefully ligate vessels before mobilizing spleen to avoid:
1. Hemorrhage from splenic vessels
2. Pancreatic tail infarction if damaged (tail is intraperitoneal in splenorenal ligament)
Result of tail injury: Pancreatitis, fistula, or necrosis
Sinistral Portal HTN
What causes isolated left-sided portal HTN? Why does it cause ISOLATED gastric varices, not esophageal?
Answer
Cause: Splenic vein thrombosis → blocked drainage from spleen
Result: Pressure backs up into splenic vasculature (short gastrics + left gastroepiploic)
Collateral formation: Short gastric veins dilate → ISOLATED GASTRIC VARICES (fundus)
Key difference: Esophageal varices require PORTAL HTN (left gastric vein → azygos); sinistral HTN bypasses this route
Bonus: Normal portal pressure + normal liver function → OPSI risk minimal; splenomegaly present
Splenic Trauma & Kehr's Sign
Why is spleen the most injured organ in blunt trauma? What is Kehr's sign and its mechanism?
Answer
Most injured organ: Spleen is highly vascular, friable, relatively unprotected (9-11 ribs break easily)
Rib fractures 9-11: Direct lacerations to splenic capsule
Kehr's sign: LEFT shoulder tip pain (referred pain)
Mechanism: Blood/irritant under diaphragm stimulates phrenic nerve (C3-C5 via diaphragmatic plexus) → referred to C4 dermatome (shoulder tip)
Clinical significance: Suggests intra-abdominal hemorrhage; may indicate need for splenectomy vs conservative management
Post-Splenectomy OPSI
What is OPSI? Which organisms? How does post-splenectomy blood smear look?
Answer
OPSI = Overwhelming Post-Splenectomy Infection
SHiN organisms (encapsulated):
- Streptococcus pneumoniae
- Haemophilus influenzae (type b)
- Neisseria meningitidis
Blood smear findings: Howell-Jolly bodies (nuclear remnants), target cells, thrombocytosis
Prevention: Pneumococcal vaccine (before splenectomy if possible), H. flu + meningococcal vaccines, prophylactic antibiotics
Note: Functional asplenia (sickle cell, celiac disease) = same risk
Pancreas Anatomy: Parts
Five parts of pancreas with levels + relationships to duodenum?
Answer
Location: Retroperitoneal L1-L2 (except tail = intraperitoneal in splenorenal ligament)
Head: Nestled in duodenum C-loop; contains uncinate process POSTERIOR to SMA/SMV
Neck: Anterior to portal vein formation (SMA + SMV join here)
Body: Crosses midline at vertebral column; splenic vein grooves posterior surface
Tail: Extends left → splenic hilum (intraperitoneal, at risk during splenectomy)
Uncinate process: Embryologic ventral bud remnant; posterior to SMA = surgical danger zone during Whipple
Pancreatic Ducts & Ampulla
Wirsung + Santorini ducts: which is main, which is accessory? Ampulla anatomy?
Answer
Wirsung duct = main pancreatic duct: Runs through entire pancreas (head to tail)
Santorini duct = accessory pancreatic duct: Embryologic dorsal bud remnant; drains head separately
Ampulla of Vater (major duodenal papilla): Junction where Wirsung + CBD merge → enters D2
Sphincter of Oddi: Smooth muscle surrounding ampulla; controlled by CCK (relaxes) + secretin
Pancreas divisum: Failed fusion of Wirsung + Santorini → MOST COMMON anatomic variant (10% asymptomatic, some develop pancreatitis)
Courvoisier's Law
Painless jaundice + palpable GB + weight loss = ? Why NOT gallstones?
Answer
Courvoisier's Law: Painless progressive jaundice + palpable gallbladder + weight loss = PANCREATIC HEAD CANCER (until proven otherwise)
Rationale: Stone disease causes FIBROTIC gallbladder (cannot distend) → NOT palpable
Cancer: CBD compression causes obstruction GRADUALLY → gallbladder distends (normal wall) → PALPABLE
Classic presentation: Painless obstructive jaundice (no pain = insidious obstruction)
Poor prognosis: Usually presents late (already metastatic)
Acute vs Chronic Pancreatitis
GET SMASHED mnemonic for acute. Chronic: alcoholic predominant, imaging findings?
Answer
Acute pancreatitis — GET SMASHED: Gallstones, Ethanol, Trauma, Steroids, Mumps, Autoimmune, Scorpion venom, Hypercalcemia, ERCP, Drugs (azathioprine)
Presentation: Epigastric pain radiating to back, relieved by leaning forward; elevated amylase + lipase
Chronic pancreatitis (mostly alcoholic): Progressive pancreatic fibrosis
Imaging: Pancreatic calcifications (pathognomonic for chronic)
Sequelae: Diabetes (endocrine insufficiency) + steatorrhea (exocrine insufficiency)
CBD in Pancreatic Groove
Does CBD pierce pancreas or run in groove? Why is this important surgically?
Answer
CBD path: Grooves the POSTERIOR surface of pancreatic head (does NOT pierce parenchyma)
Surgical significance: During Whipple resection, CBD can be carefully dissected from pancreatic tissue
Distinction: If pancreatic head tumors erode posteriorly, can directly involve CBD WITHOUT transecting it
Whipple procedure: Resects pancreatic head + duodenum + distal stomach + gallbladder + distal CBD → Roux-en-Y reconstruction (pancreaticojejunostomy + choledochojejunostomy + gastrojejunostomy)
Bile Salts & Enterohepatic Circulation
How much bile daily? What fraction is recycled? Where reabsorbed? Consequence of ileal disease?
Answer
Bile production: 500-1000 mL/day
Bile salts: Emulsify fats + form micelles for fat-soluble vitamin (ADEK) absorption
Enterohepatic circulation: 95% of bile salts reabsorbed in TERMINAL ILEUM (active transport) per meal
Recycling: Each bile salt molecule recycled ~6 times/day (only ~0.5 g/day lost in stool, replaced by new synthesis)
Ileal disease/resection: Bile salt loss → ↓micelle formation → FAT + ADEK malabsorption
Clinical sequelae: Steatorrhea (fatty stools), night blindness (vitamin A deficiency), osteomalacia (vitamin D deficiency), coagulopathy (vitamin K deficiency)
Pancreatic Enzyme Activation
Enterokinase role? Trypsin as master activator? Key proteases + lipase?
Answer
Enterokinase (enteropeptidase): From duodenal brush border (NOT pancreatic) — activates trypsinogen → trypsin
Trypsin = master activator: Activates all other pancreatic zymogens (chymotrypsinogen → chymotrypsin, proelastase → elastase, procarboxypeptidase → carboxypeptidase, prophospholipase → phospholipase A2)
Key proteases: Trypsin, chymotrypsin, elastase (all proteases)
Lipases: Lipase + colipase (breaks TGs into fatty acids + monoglycerides)
Carbohydrate: Amylase (breaks starch/glycogen)
Phospholipids: Phospholipase A2
CCK & Secretin Hormones
CCK: stimulus + effects? Secretin: stimulus + effects? Both vs each alone?
Answer
CCK (cholecystokinin) — from I-cells of duodenum:
Stimulus: Fat + protein
Effects: GB contraction + pancreatic enzyme secretion + sphincter of Oddi relaxation
Secretin — from S-cells of duodenum:
Stimulus: Acid (H⁺ in duodenum)
Effects: Pancreatic HCO₃⁻ secretion (bicarbonate watery juice from ductal/centroacinar cells) + inhibits gastric acid
Combined effect: CCK provides enzymes; secretin provides bicarbonate-rich liquid to buffer acid + dilute chyme
Key distinction: Acinar cells (CCK) → enzymes; ductal cells (secretin) → HCO₃⁻
Chylomicron Transport & First-Pass
Why do long-chain fatty acids form chylomicrons? Where absorbed? Portal vs lymphatic?
Answer
Long-chain fatty acids (LCFAs, >12 carbons): Reassembled into triglycerides → incorporated into chylomicrons
Chylomicron contents: Dietary TGs + ADEK vitamins (fat-soluble) + cholesterol
Transport: Chylomicrons BYPASS liver via lacteals (lymphatic vessels) → thoracic duct → left subclavian vein
Why bypass?: Large lipid-soluble molecules NOT absorbed directly into portal blood
First-pass metabolism: Applies to most dietary compounds (enter portal → liver) BUT NOT chylomicrons (lymph route)
Clinical relevance: Fat-soluble drugs taken with fat bypass hepatic first-pass (enhanced absorption)

Question 1 of 10 — Hepatobiliary / Calot's Triangle
A 62-year-old woman with acute cholecystitis undergoes open cholecystectomy. During dissection of the hepatocystic triangle, the surgeon identifies a structure crossing the triangle that he must carefully ligate to prevent hemorrhage. Which artery is most likely to be injured in this location if not properly identified?
AHepatic artery proper
BLeft hepatic artery
CCystic artery
DRight gastric artery
ECommon hepatic artery
C — Cystic artery. The cystic artery is the main vascular structure crossing Calot's triangle (bounded by cystic duct, common hepatic duct, and liver edge). It typically arises from the right hepatic artery and crosses the triangle before supplying the gallbladder. The "critical view of safety" identifies two crossing structures (cystic artery + cystic vein) before clipping to prevent hepatic artery injury.
Question 2 of 10 — Pancreas / Courvoisier's Law
A 68-year-old man with no previous history of gallbladder disease presents with 3 months of progressive jaundice (bilirubin 8 mg/dL), dark urine, and a 7 kg weight loss. Physical examination reveals a palpable gallbladder. Abdominal ultrasound shows dilated common bile duct and pancreatic head mass. Which of the following best explains why the gallbladder is palpable in this patient?
AChronic gallstone disease causing mucocele formation
BGallbladder perforation with abscess formation
CInsidious biliary obstruction allowing normal gallbladder wall to distend with bile
DAcute cholecystitis from bile stasis
EMetastatic invasion of gallbladder wall
C — Insidious obstruction from cancer. Courvoisier's law: painless jaundice + palpable GB + weight loss = pancreatic head cancer. Stone disease causes FIBROTIC GB (cannot distend), so NOT palpable. Cancer causes gradual CBD compression, allowing normal GB wall to distend with bile over time. The absence of pain distinguishes this from acute cholecystitis.
Question 3 of 10 — Splenic Trauma / Kehr's Sign
A 45-year-old man is in a motor vehicle accident and sustains rib fractures (9-11 on left). In the ED, he complains of left upper quadrant pain but strikingly also reports sharp pain at the left shoulder tip that worsens with deep inspiration. Vital signs show tachycardia and hypotension. What anatomic structure and nerve pathway explain the referred shoulder pain?
ASplenic rupture → direct nerve irritation of intercostal nerves
BKidney laceration → irritation of lumbar plexus
CSplenic rupture with hemorrhage under diaphragm → phrenic nerve (C3-C5) irritation
DRib fracture with pleurisy → referred pain via thoracic nerves
EPancreatic tail injury → vagal referred pain
C — Kehr's sign. Splenic rupture (most common organ in blunt trauma, especially with rib 9-11 fractures) causes bleeding under the diaphragm. Blood irritates the phrenic nerve (C3-C5), which via its diaphragmatic plexus refers pain to the C4 dermatome (shoulder tip). This is classic Kehr's sign indicating intra-abdominal hemorrhage requiring urgent splenectomy.
Question 4 of 10 — Pancreas / Whipple Resection
A 55-year-old man with pancreatic head cancer undergoes a Whipple procedure (pancreaticoduodenectomy). During dissection, the surgeon must carefully identify and protect a vascular structure that runs posterior to the pancreatic neck and uncinate process. Injury to this vessel during uncinate mobilization would compromise blood supply to the entire jejunum. Which vessel is the surgeon trying to protect?
ASuperior mesenteric artery
BPortal vein
CSplenic vein
DSuperior mesenteric vein
EGastroduodenal artery
D — Superior mesenteric vein. The uncinate process (ventral pancreatic bud embryologically) lies POSTERIOR to the SMA/SMV. These vessels form the portal vein at the pancreatic neck. During Whipple, the uncinate must be carefully dissected away from the SMV without injury. The SMA/SMV are critical surgical landmarks — uncinate mobilization is called "SMA/SMV dissection" because of this danger.
Question 5 of 10 — Biliary / Mirizzi Syndrome
A 58-year-old woman with a history of recurrent right upper quadrant pain presents with progressive jaundice (bilirubin 5 mg/dL). Abdominal ultrasound shows a dilated common bile duct and a large stone in the gallbladder fundus. MRCP is performed and shows the CBD is patent but externally compressed at the point adjacent to the stone. What is this condition called, and why is the CBD patent despite the obstruction?
ACholedocholithiasis; stone partially obstructs the duct lumen
BAcute pancreatitis; inflammation swells the pancreatic head
CMirizzi syndrome; stone in Hartmann's pouch compresses CBD externally
DBiliary dyskinesia; sphincter of Oddi dysfunction
EPrimary sclerosing cholangitis; stricture formation
C — Mirizzi syndrome. The stone lodges in Hartmann's pouch (fundal outpouch) and externally compresses the adjacent CBD. The CBD itself remains patent (no stone inside it) but is obstructed from outside. This explains jaundice without choledocholithiasis, and why treatment is cholecystectomy rather than ERCP.
Question 6 of 10 — Pancreas / Pancreas Divisum
A 34-year-old woman with recurrent pancreatitis of unclear etiology undergoes MRCP imaging. The study reveals that the accessory pancreatic duct (Santorini) drains separately into the minor duodenal papilla, while the main duct (Wirsung) enters at the major papilla. The patient's pancreas is noted to lack normal ductal fusion. Which of the following best describes this anatomic variant?
AAnnular pancreas; ventral bud wraps around duodenum
BPancreas divisum; failed fusion of Wirsung + Santorini ducts
CPancreatic agenesis; congenital absence of pancreatic tissue
DEctopic pancreas; heterotopic pancreatic rest in stomach
ESerous cystadenoma; benign cystic neoplasm
B — Pancreas divisum. This is the MOST COMMON pancreatic anatomic variant (~10% of population), results from failure of embryologic duct fusion. Most people are asymptomatic, but some develop pancreatitis (possibly due to increased resistance at minor papilla). Annular pancreas (A) results from abnormal rotation of the VENTRAL bud, not duct fusion failure.
Question 7 of 10 — Spleen / Post-Splenectomy Infection
A 28-year-old trauma patient undergoes splenectomy after blunt splenic injury. Six months later, he presents with fever (39.2°C), tachycardia, and shock with positive blood cultures growing gram-positive diplococci. His complete blood count shows the presence of Howell-Jolly bodies, target cells, and thrombocytosis on peripheral smear. Which of the following organisms is MOST likely to cause severe infection in this post-splenectomy patient?
AStreptococcus pneumoniae
BEscherichia coli
CStaphylococcus aureus
DListeria monocytogenes
EClostridium difficile
A — Streptococcus pneumoniae. Post-splenectomy patients are vulnerable to OPSI (Overwhelming Post-Splenectomy Infection) from encapsulated organisms: SHiN (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, N. meningitidis). The gram-positive diplococci description fits S. pneumoniae. Howell-Jolly bodies (nuclear remnants) are pathognomonic for asplenia; target cells and thrombocytosis also appear post-splenectomy.
Question 8 of 10 — Bile / Enterohepatic Circulation
A 52-year-old man with Crohn disease undergoes surgical resection of 2 meters of terminal ileum. Post-operatively, he develops steatorrhea (fatty stools) and vitamin K deficiency (INR elevated). Which of the following best explains these findings?
ADecreased pancreatic enzyme secretion from reduced CCK stimulation
BMalabsorption of carbohydrates causing osmotic diarrhea
CLoss of terminal ileum absorption site for bile salts → ↓enterohepatic circulation
DBacterial overgrowth in remaining small bowel
EDisruption of the ileocecal valve function
C — Loss of bile salt reabsorption. Terminal ileum is the site of active bile salt reabsorption (95% recycled per meal). Resection causes bile salt loss → fewer micelles → FAT malabsorption (steatorrhea) + ADEK deficiency. Vitamin K deficiency causes coagulopathy (elevated INR). Night blindness would indicate vitamin A deficiency; osteomalacia indicates vitamin D deficiency.
Question 9 of 10 — Pancreas / Enzyme Activation
A 67-year-old man with pancreatic cancer develops acute pancreatitis after ERCP with accidental perforation of the duodenum. In the duodenum, a critical enzyme is released from the brush border epithelium that normally activates pancreatic trypsinogen to begin the cascade of zymogens activations. Which enzyme is responsible for this initial activation?
APancreatic lipase
BPepsinogen
CEnterokinase (enteropeptidase)
DPancreatic amylase
EGastric lipase
C — Enterokinase. Enterokinase (enteropeptidase) is released from the duodenal brush border and is the ONLY enzyme that activates trypsinogen → trypsin. Trypsin then acts as the master activator for all other zymogens. This is NOT a pancreatic enzyme — duodenal injury can disrupt enterokinase production, potentially impairing protein digestion despite normal pancreatic function.
Question 10 of 10 — Hormones / Bile Flow
A patient consumes a fatty meal. Over the next 30 minutes, the gallbladder contracts vigorously, bile flow into the duodenum increases, the pancreas secretes enzyme-rich juice, and the sphincter of Oddi relaxes. Which hormone is primarily responsible for triggering these coordinated responses?
ACholecystokinin (CCK)
BSecretin
CGastrin
DMotilin
ESomatostatin
A — Cholecystokinin (CCK). CCK is released from duodenal I-cells in response to FAT + PROTEIN. It causes: (1) GB contraction (bile release), (2) pancreatic enzyme secretion, (3) sphincter of Oddi relaxation (allows bile + pancreatic juice into duodenum). Secretin (from S-cells stimulated by acid) triggers pancreatic HCO₃⁻ secretion. Together they optimize fat digestion and neutralization.

    Step 1 Traps: Common false statements that appear plausible but are incorrect. Choose the correct answer to reveal the trap explanation.
    Trap 1 of 10
    Courvoisier's law: A painless, palpable gallbladder + obstructive jaundice = gallstone disease (choledocholithiasis).
    TRAP: Courvoisier's law is a NEGATIVE predictor for stones. Stone disease causes a FIBROTIC, non-distensible gallbladder that CANNOT be palpated. A painless, palpable GB + jaundice + weight loss = CANCER (usually pancreatic head). Cancer causes insidious CBD obstruction, allowing the normal GB wall to distend with bile. This is why it is palpable. Absence of pain indicates slow obstruction, not acute cholecystitis.
    Trap 2 of 10
    Mirizzi syndrome: A stone lodged in the common bile duct externally compresses the sphincter of Oddi.
    TRAP: The stone is NOT in the CBD. In Mirizzi syndrome, a stone lodges in Hartmann's pouch (gallbladder fundus) and compresses the ADJACENT CBD from OUTSIDE. The CBD remains patent (no stone inside), so MRCP shows a patent but externally compressed duct. Treatment is cholecystectomy (remove the gallstone causing external compression), NOT ERCP (CBD is patent). This distinguishes Mirizzi from choledocholithiasis.
    Trap 3 of 10
    In Budd-Chiari syndrome, the caudate lobe (Segment I) is the FIRST segment to be damaged because it receives poor blood supply.
    TRAP: Hepatic vein thrombosis affects ALL of the liver equally EXCEPT the caudate. The caudate is SPARED because it has independent venous drainage directly to the IVC via small hepatic veins separate from the main hepatic veins. So while the rest of the liver is congested and necrotic, the caudate remains perfused and actually hypertrophies. This caudate enlargement is a pathognomonic imaging finding of Budd-Chiari.
    Trap 4 of 10
    The common bile duct PIERCES the pancreatic head tissue during its course to the ampulla of Vater.
    TRAP: The CBD GROOVES the posterior surface of the pancreatic head but does NOT pierce the parenchyma. This is surgically important — during Whipple resection, the CBD can be dissected cleanly from pancreatic tissue. However, if a pancreatic tumor erodes posteriorly, it can directly involve the CBD even if it hasn't "invaded" in a traditional sense. Imaging: CBD runs BEHIND (posterior to) duodenum, not through it.
    Trap 5 of 10
    OPSI (Overwhelming Post-Splenectomy Infection) is most commonly caused by Staphylococcus aureus.
    TRAP: OPSI is caused by ENCAPSULATED organisms: SHiN (Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae, Neisseria meningitidis). The spleen filters encapsulated bacteria. S. aureus is NOT encapsulated and is NOT the cause of OPSI. Remember: post-splenectomy patients need pneumococcal, H. flu, and meningococcal vaccines + prophylactic antibiotics. Howell-Jolly bodies on blood smear are pathognomonic for asplenia.
    Trap 6 of 10
    Howell-Jolly bodies on a peripheral blood smear indicate hypersplenism and splenomegaly.
    TRAP: Howell-Jolly bodies indicate ASPLENIA (absent spleen), not splenomegaly. They are nuclear remnants (DNA fragments) that the spleen normally filters out. In post-splenectomy patients, these appear because there is no spleen to remove them. They are also seen in sickle cell disease (functional asplenia from repeated infarcts), celiac disease, and other conditions. Associated findings: target cells, thrombocytosis.
    Trap 7 of 10
    Enterokinase is a pancreatic enzyme that activates trypsinogen in the duodenum.
    TRAP: Enterokinase is NOT pancreatic — it is produced by the DUODENAL BRUSH BORDER EPITHELIUM. This is the ONLY enzyme that activates trypsinogen → trypsin. Trypsin then acts as the master activator for all other zymogens. This duodenal source is important: duodenal injury can impair enterokinase production and disrupt protein digestion even if the pancreas is functioning normally.
    Trap 8 of 10
    Pancreas divisum is an uncommon anatomic variant that rarely causes clinical problems.
    TRAP: Pancreas divisum is the MOST COMMON pancreatic anatomic variant (~10% of population). It results from failed fusion of the Wirsung (ventral duct) and Santorini (dorsal duct) during embryogenesis. Most people are asymptomatic, but some develop recurrent acute pancreatitis (presumably from increased resistance at the minor duodenal papilla where Santorini drains). It is a key imaging finding on MRCP.
    Trap 9 of 10
    The uncinate process of the pancreas is located ANTERIOR to the superior mesenteric artery and vein.
    TRAP: The uncinate process is POSTERIOR to the SMA/SMV. This is a critical surgical landmark — it is called the "surgical danger zone" during Whipple resection because dissecting the uncinate requires careful SMA/SMV exposure to avoid vascular injury. The uncinate is the embryologic ventral pancreatic bud; it curves AROUND the vessels posteriorly, not in front of them.
    Trap 10 of 10
    Terminal ileum resection causes fat malabsorption because the ileum is the site where dietary lipids are absorbed.
    TRAP: The mechanism is NOT loss of lipid absorption, but loss of BILE SALT reabsorption. The terminal ileum is the sole site of active bile salt reabsorption (95% recycled per meal). Resection causes bile salt loss → ↓micelle formation → ↓fat-soluble vitamin (ADEK) absorption. Steatorrhea results from inadequate micelles, not loss of absorptive epithelium. Management: bile acid binders (cholestyramine) to sequester remaining bile salts, + fat-soluble vitamin supplementation.

    High-Yield Testing Notes: Key patterns to recognize on GI13 exams. Badges indicate anatomy traps, clinical pearls, and buzzword clusters.
    TESTING Courvoisier's Law — Cancer, Not Stones
    Painless jaundice + palpable GB + weight loss = CANCER (pancreatic head). The key: stones cause FIBROTIC, non-distensible GB; cancer causes insidious obstruction, allowing normal GB wall to distend. This reversed logic appears in multiple choice: "Which finding rules OUT gallstone disease?" Answer: palpable GB in jaundice. Step 1 tests this as a clinical reasoning question, not pure recall.
    TRAP Mirizzi Syndrome — External Compression, Not CBD Stone
    Stone in Hartmann's pouch (GB fundus) compresses CBD EXTERNALLY. CBD is patent (no stone inside). ERCP will not find a stone, so it is NOT first-line treatment. Cholecystectomy is treatment (remove GB + stone to relieve external compression). Imaging clue: dilated but patent CBD adjacent to fundal stone. This trap appears as: "MRCP shows patent CBD but dilated. What is the diagnosis?" Answer: Mirizzi, not choledocholithiasis.
    BUZZWORD Pancreas Divisum — Most Common Variant
    Pancreas divisum (failed Wirsung + Santorini fusion) is the most common pancreatic anatomic variant (~10% of population). Most asymptomatic, but some develop recurrent pancreatitis from increased resistance at the minor duodenal papilla. Step 1 buzzword: "pancreatic anatomy variant" + recurrent pancreatitis = pancreas divisum. Imaging finding on MRCP: two separate ducts.
    TESTING Kehr's Sign — Splenic Rupture Pathognomonic
    Left shoulder tip pain (referred via phrenic C3-C5) + blunt left-sided trauma + hemodynamic instability = splenic rupture with bleeding under diaphragm. Kehr's sign is nearly pathognomonic for intra-abdominal hemorrhage. It is tested alongside rib fractures 9-11 (spleen is at highest risk). Step 1 loves this as a clinical correlation: know the anatomy of phrenic nerve referred pain.
    TRAP Howell-Jolly Bodies = ASPLENIA, Not Splenomegaly
    Students confuse Howell-Jolly bodies with splenomegaly. Wrong — they indicate ABSENT spleen (asplenia). They are nuclear remnants normally filtered by the spleen. Found post-splenectomy, in sickle cell disease (functional asplenia), celiac disease. Associated findings: target cells, thrombocytosis. Step 1 tests: "Peripheral smear shows Howell-Jolly bodies. What is the underlying pathology?" Answer: asplenia, OPSI risk.
    TESTING Terminal Ileum Resection → ADEK Deficiency
    Terminal ileum is the ONLY site of active bile salt reabsorption (95% recycled/meal). Resection causes bile salt loss → ↓micelle formation → ADEK malabsorption. NOT loss of absorptive epithelium — the mechanism is lack of micelles. Step 1 tests: Crohn disease with ileal resection + steatorrhea + elevated INR + night blindness = ADEK deficiency. Each vitamin has a signature deficiency: K (coagulopathy), A (night blindness), D (osteomalacia), E (neurologic).
    Clinical Correlates: Review key disease profiles integrating anatomy, physiology, and pathology.
    Hepatobiliary: Calot's Triangle & Cystic Artery Injury
    Anatomy: Cystic artery arises from right hepatic artery, crosses Calot's triangle (bounded by cystic duct, CHD, liver edge). Surgical risk: Failure to identify cystic artery → bleeding during cholecystectomy. Critical view of safety: Identify 2 crossing structures (cystic artery + vein) before clipping. Clinical pearl: If bleeding during cholecystectomy, think cystic artery or right hepatic artery injury.
    Pancreatic Head Cancer: Courvoisier's Law Clinical Context
    Presentation: 65-year-old with 2-month history of painless progressive jaundice, palpable GB, weight loss. Pathophysiology: Gradual CBD obstruction from pancreatic head mass allows normal GB wall to distend with bile (unlike stones which cause fibrosis). Prognosis: Usually diagnosed late (often metastatic); 5-year survival <5%. Imaging: Dilated CBD, pancreatic head mass, possible liver mets. Treatment: Whipple if resectable; often palliative bypass.
    Splenic Rupture: Trauma → OPSI Risk
    Mechanism: Blunt abdominal trauma (rib 9-11 fractures on left) → splenic capsule laceration → intra-abdominal hemorrhage. Clinical finding: Kehr's sign (left shoulder tip pain via phrenic nerve) → bleeding under diaphragm. Management: Massive transfusion protocol, splenic artery angiographic embolization vs splenectomy. Post-splenectomy sequelae: OPSI risk from encapsulated organisms (SHiN); Howell-Jolly bodies on smear. Prevention: Pneumococcal, H. flu, meningococcal vaccines.
    Acute Pancreatitis: Gallstones as Most Common Trigger
    Pathophysiology: Stone obstructs ampulla → ↑pancreatic duct pressure → back-flow of activated zymogens → pancreatic autodigestion. Presentation: Sudden epigastric pain radiating to back, relieved by leaning forward; nausea/vomiting. Labs: Elevated amylase (returns to normal in 48-72 hr) + lipase (more specific, stays elevated longer). Management: NPO, fluids, monitor for complications (necrosis, abscess, cyst formation). Severe pancreatitis: >30% pancreatic necrosis → high mortality; may require necrosectomy.
    Budd-Chiari: Caudate Lobe Sparing from Independent Drainage
    Pathophysiology: Hepatic vein thrombosis (from hypercoagulable state, polycythemia vera, etc.) → liver congestion + necrosis. Key finding: Caudate lobe (Segment I) is SPARED because it drains directly to IVC via small hepatic veins independent of main hepatic veins. Imaging: Caudate hypertrophy relative to atrophied remainder. Clinical presentation: Acute: RUQ pain, jaundice, ascites. Chronic: cirrhosis. Treatment: Anticoagulation, transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt (TIPS), liver transplantation.
