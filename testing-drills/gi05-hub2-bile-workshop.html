Statins & Bile
How do statins reduce bile acid synthesis? What is the rate-limiting enzyme of cholesterol synthesis vs. bile acid synthesis?
Tap to reveal →
Step 1 Answer
Statins inhibit HMG-CoA reductase (rate-limiting for cholesterol synthesis) → ↓ cholesterol → ↓ primary bile acid production (bile acids are made from cholesterol). The rate-limiting step for bile acid synthesis FROM cholesterol is cholesterol 7α-hydroxylase.
Obstructive Jaundice
A patient has a gallstone blocking the common bile duct. What color are their stools and why? What happens to their urine?
Tap to reveal →
Step 1 Answer
Pale/clay-colored stools — bile cannot reach the gut, so no stercobilin forms (stercobilin = brown stool color). Dark urine — conjugated bilirubin backs up into blood → filtered by kidneys → bilirubinuria. Also: pruritus from bile salt deposition in skin.
Hemolytic vs Obstructive Jaundice
Compare unconjugated vs conjugated hyperbilirubinemia — which bilirubin is elevated in each type and why?
Tap to reveal →
Step 1 Answer
Hemolytic (e.g., sickle cell, G6PD): excess RBC destruction → ↑ unconjugated bilirubin (liver can't conjugate fast enough); stools may be darker (more urobilinogen).
Obstructive (e.g., gallstone, pancreatic CA): ↑ conjugated bilirubin; dark urine, pale stools.
Cholestyramine
What is cholestyramine's mechanism and what 2 conditions is it used for?
Tap to reveal →
Step 1 Answer
Mechanism: bile acid sequestrant → binds bile in the gut → ↓ enterohepatic reabsorption → liver converts more cholesterol to bile → ↓ LDL.
Uses: (1) Hypercholesterolemia — ↓ LDL. (2) Pruritus in cholestasis / bile acid diarrhea after terminal ileum resection.
Terminal Ileum Resection
A patient has 120 cm of terminal ileum removed for Crohn's disease. List 4 specific consequences.
Tap to reveal →
Step 1 Answer
1. Bile acid malabsorption → watery diarrhea (bile acids irritate colon)
2. Steatorrhea → fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (A, D, E, K)
3. B₁₂ deficiency → megaloblastic anemia (terminal ileum = site of IF-B₁₂ absorption)
4. Treat bile acid diarrhea with cholestyramine
Zollinger-Ellison & Bile
Explain why Zollinger-Ellison syndrome causes steatorrhea even though bile synthesis is normal.
Tap to reveal →
Step 1 Answer
Gastrinoma → ↑↑ gastrin → massive HCl secretion → duodenal pH drops so low that it inactivates both bile salts and pancreatic lipase (optimal pH for both ~6–7). Fat cannot be digested or absorbed → steatorrhea. Classic triad: multiple duodenal ulcers + high serum gastrin + steatorrhea.
PBC vs PSC
Differentiate PBC from PSC — demographics, antibody, location of duct involvement, and treatment.
Tap to reveal →
Step 1 Answer
PBC: Middle-aged women; anti-mitochondrial antibody (AMA); intrahepatic ducts; granulomatous destruction; treatment = ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA).
PSC: Young men + UC; p-ANCA; intra- AND extrahepatic ducts; "beads on string" on ERCP; ↑ cholangiocarcinoma risk; only cure = liver transplant.
Ampulla of Vater Gallstone
A gallstone lodges at the ampulla of Vater. What two major complications result and why?
Tap to reveal →
Step 1 Answer
The ampulla of Vater is where the common bile duct and pancreatic duct merge. A stone here blocks both:
1. Obstructive jaundice (↑ conjugated bilirubin, pale stools, dark urine)
2. Acute pancreatitis (back-pressure on pancreatic duct → autodigestion)
Ascending Cholangitis
What is Charcot's triad and what does it indicate?
Tap to reveal →
Step 1 Answer
Charcot's Triad: Fever + Jaundice + RUQ Pain = ascending cholangitis (infected bile behind an obstruction — usually a gallstone in the common bile duct). This is a life-threatening emergency requiring urgent biliary decompression (ERCP or surgery).
Abetalipoproteinemia
What is abetalipoproteinemia? What is absent and what are the classic Step 1 findings?
Tap to reveal →
Step 1 Answer
AR disorder; absent MTP (microsomal triglyceride transfer protein) → enterocytes absorb FA + MG normally but CANNOT package them into chylomicrons → fat accumulates in enterocytes.
Classic: steatorrhea + acanthocytes (spiky RBCs) + ataxia + retinitis pigmentosa (↓ Vit E) + fat-soluble vitamin deficiency.
CCK + Pancreas
Besides biliary effects, what else does CCK stimulate? How does CCK relate to secretin?
Tap to reveal →
Step 1 Answer
CCK also stimulates pancreatic enzyme secretion (lipase, proteases, amylase) from acinar cells — same trigger (fat + protein in duodenum).
Secretin (from S-cells, triggered by acid) stimulates pancreatic HCO₃⁻ secretion. CCK + secretin work synergistically to neutralize chyme and digest food.
Cystic Fibrosis & Fat
Why does cystic fibrosis cause steatorrhea? What is the treatment?
Tap to reveal →
Step 1 Answer
CF: thick mucus plugs obstruct pancreatic ducts → pancreatic exocrine insufficiency → absent lipase (and colipase, trypsin) → triglycerides undigested → steatorrhea + fat-soluble vitamin deficiency. Bile production is intact. Treatment: pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy (PERT) — exogenous lipase, protease, amylase.
Vitamin K Deficiency
In steatorrhea, which coagulation factors are affected by Vitamin K deficiency? What test is elevated?
Tap to reveal →
Step 1 Answer
Vitamin K (fat-soluble) is required for γ-carboxylation of factors II (prothrombin), VII, IX, X and Protein C/S. Deficiency → cannot activate these factors → ↑ PT/INR (prolonged prothrombin time). First sign = ↑ PT (factor VII has shortest half-life).
Low-Fat Diet → Steatorrhea
A woman on a fat-free diet for 8 months eats a large high-fat holiday meal and develops steatorrhea. Explain the mechanism.
Tap to reveal →
Step 1 Answer
Chronic low-fat intake → ↓ stimulus for bile production → small bile pool. Sudden large fat load overwhelms the available bile → insufficient micelle formation → fat passes unabsorbed → transient steatorrhea. The liver compensates over time but cannot respond acutely. This is a physiological/adaptive insufficiency, not structural disease.

Question 1 — LO 2
A 52-year-old man is started on atorvastatin for hypercholesterolemia. His physician explains that the drug blocks the rate-limiting enzyme in cholesterol synthesis. Which of the following is this enzyme, and which bile component does its inhibition most directly reduce?
✓ B — HMG-CoA reductase; cholesterol backbone. Statins inhibit HMG-CoA reductase (rate-limiting for cholesterol synthesis) → ↓ cholesterol → ↓ primary bile acid synthesis (bile acids are made from a cholesterol backbone). The immediate rate-limiting step for bile acid synthesis FROM cholesterol is cholesterol 7α-hydroxylase, but HMG-CoA reductase controls the upstream cholesterol supply.
Question 2 — LO 2
A 45-year-old woman presents with fatigue, pruritus, jaundice, and xanthelasma. Labs show elevated alkaline phosphatase and anti-mitochondrial antibodies. Liver biopsy reveals granulomatous destruction of intrahepatic bile ducts. Which of the following best characterizes this condition and its effect on fat absorption?
✓ B — PBC; steatorrhea and fat-soluble vitamin deficiency. PBC = middle-aged women + pruritus + AMA + granulomatous destruction of intrahepatic bile ducts. Bile duct destruction → cholestasis → ↓ bile delivery → impaired micelle formation → steatorrhea + fat-soluble vitamin (A, D, E, K) deficiency. PSC (A/D) is young men + UC + p-ANCA + "beads on string" on ERCP. Treatment = ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA).
Question 3 — LO 3
A 28-year-old woman develops episodic right upper quadrant pain after fatty meals. Ultrasound shows multiple echogenic foci with posterior acoustic shadowing in the gallbladder. Which hormone triggered her gallbladder contraction, and from which cells is it released?
✓ C — CCK from I-cells of the duodenum. Fat + protein in the duodenum → I-cells release CCK → gallbladder contraction + sphincter of Oddi relaxation → bile flows into the duodenum. In this patient with gallstones, gallbladder contraction against a stone in the cystic duct = biliary colic (episodic RUQ pain after fatty meals). Secretin (S-cells) stimulates HCO₃⁻ secretion from the pancreas, not gallbladder contraction.
Question 4 — LO 3
A 32-year-old man with Crohn's disease undergoes resection of 150 cm of terminal ileum. Postoperatively, he develops persistent watery diarrhea despite eating a low-fat diet. A stool Sudan stain is positive. Which of the following best explains BOTH his diarrhea AND his steatorrhea?
✓ B — Bile acid malabsorption → watery diarrhea + ↓ micelle formation → steatorrhea. The terminal ileum is the critical site for active bile acid reabsorption (ASBT transporter). After resection: bile acids enter the colon → osmotic/secretory diarrhea (bile acid diarrhea). The depleted bile acid pool also impairs micelle formation → steatorrhea. Additional risks: B₁₂ deficiency (terminal ileum = IF-B₁₂ absorption site) and fat-soluble vitamin deficiency. Treat bile acid diarrhea with cholestyramine.
Question 5 — LO 4
A 6-year-old boy presents with failure to thrive, severe fat malabsorption, ataxia, and visual problems. A peripheral blood smear shows acanthocytes. Intestinal biopsy reveals enterocytes packed with fat droplets. Which of the following best explains this clinical picture?
✓ C — Abetalipoproteinemia; absent MTP. Abetalipoproteinemia is an AR disorder with absent MTP (microsomal triglyceride transfer protein). Enterocytes absorb FA + MG normally from micelles but CANNOT package them into chylomicrons → fat accumulates in enterocytes (biopsy finding). Result: severe fat malabsorption + fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (especially E → ataxia, spinocerebellar degeneration, retinitis pigmentosa) + acanthocytes (abnormal RBC membrane lipids). Unlike CF (absent lipase) or bile acid deficiency (impaired micelle formation), the absorption step here is intact — the export step fails.
Question 6 — LO 4
A 55-year-old man with a history of peptic ulcer disease presents with recurrent steatorrhea and weight loss. Serum gastrin is 1,400 pg/mL (normal <100) and octreotide scan shows a pancreatic mass. Which mechanism best explains his fat malabsorption?
✓ B — Excess acid inactivates bile salts and pancreatic lipase (Zollinger-Ellison syndrome). ZES: gastrinoma → ↑↑ gastrin → massive HCl → duodenal pH so low that bile salts (optimal pH ~6) and pancreatic lipase (optimal pH ~6–7) are inactivated. Fat cannot be digested or absorbed → steatorrhea. Classic vignette: multiple duodenal/jejunal ulcers + high gastrin + steatorrhea + pancreatic mass. Treatment: high-dose PPIs + surgical resection if localized.
Clinical Correlates — Real-world applications of bile physiology across different disease states.
Clinical Connections — Bile Physiology in Disease
PBC vs PSC — The Discriminator Card
PBC:
  • Middle-aged woman, fatigue, pruritus, jaundice, xanthelasma
  • Anti-mitochondrial antibody (AMA) — highly specific
  • Intrahepatic bile ducts; granulomatous inflammation
  • Treatment: ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA)
  • Complication: cirrhosis → liver failure

PSC:
  • Young man + ulcerative colitis (90% association)
  • p-ANCA antibody
  • Intra- AND extrahepatic ducts; "beads on string" on ERCP
  • Treatment: only liver transplant cures
  • ↑ Risk of cholangiocarcinoma + colon cancer (via UC)

Both: cholestasis → steatorrhea → fat-soluble vitamin deficiency.
Gallstone Complications — Step 1 Cascade
  • Biliary colic: Stone transiently occludes cystic duct → episodic RUQ pain after fatty meals; resolves spontaneously
  • Cholecystitis: Persistent cystic duct obstruction → gallbladder wall inflammation → fever + Murphy's sign (inspiratory arrest on RUQ palpation)
  • Choledocholithiasis: Stone in common bile duct → obstructive jaundice (pale stools, dark urine, ↑ conjugated bilirubin)
  • Ascending cholangitis: Infected bile + obstruction → Charcot's triad (fever, jaundice, RUQ pain) → life-threatening
  • Gallstone pancreatitis: Stone at ampulla of Vater → obstructs pancreatic duct → acute pancreatitis
Fat Malabsorption Framework — Know the Level
Step through the lipid absorption pathway to locate the defect:
  • ↓ Bile (liver/duct): PBC, PSC, gallstone obstruction, liver failure → ↓ micelle formation
  • ↓ Bile reabsorption: Terminal ileum resection (Crohn's) → bile acid diarrhea + ↓ pool
  • ↓ Lipase (pancreas): CF, chronic pancreatitis → triglycerides undigested
  • Acid inactivation: ZES → inactivates bile AND lipase
  • Mucosal damage: Celiac, Whipple → enterocytes can't absorb
  • ↓ Export (chylomicrons): Abetalipoproteinemia → fat stuck in enterocyte
All eventually → fat-soluble vitamin (A, D, E, K) deficiency.
