Question 1 · Vascular Anatomy
A 72-year-old man presents 3 days after open abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) repair with bloody diarrhea and severe left lower quadrant pain. On colonoscopy, you observe mucosal sloughing and hemorrhagic friability limited to the splenic flexure. Laboratory studies show elevated lactate. Which of the following is the most likely vascular cause of this complication?
A Celiac artery stenosis from aortic clamp placement
B Superior mesenteric artery embolism
C Ligation of the inferior mesenteric artery with inadequate collateral circulation at the SMA-IMA watershed
D Portal vein thrombosis from surgical trauma
E Disruption of the mesenteric arterial arcades during dissection
Correct Answer: C

Explanation: This is a classic presentation of watershed area ischemia at the splenic flexure (Griffith's point). The splenic flexure marks the junction between the SMA territory (proximal) and IMA territory (distal). During AAA repair, the IMA is often ligated to prevent back-bleeding. Without adequate collaterals via Riolan's arcade or marginal vessels, the IMA-dependent splenic flexure becomes ischemic. The left colon (supplied by IMA) is more dependent on collateral flow than the right colon, making it vulnerable to ischemia.

Distractors: (A) Celiac stenosis would not specifically affect the splenic flexure. (B) SMA embolism would cause more extensive ischemia from proximal jejunum onward, affecting a much larger segment. (D) Portal thrombosis causes different pathology (ascites, varices) and wouldn't present this acutely with localized mucosal necrosis. (E) Disruption of arterial arcades would cause diffuse ischemia, not focal splenic flexure involvement.
Question 2 · Visceral Innervation
A 19-year-old male presents to the emergency department with a 12-hour history of periumbilical dull pain that has now migrated to the right lower quadrant. He has a fever of 38.5°C, rebound tenderness at McBurney's point, and an elevated WBC. Imaging confirms acute appendicitis. Which nerve pathway explains the initial periumbilical location of his pain?
A Somatic innervation from the abdominal wall via T10 intercostal nerves
B Visceral afferents traveling with the lesser splanchnic nerve (T10–T11) from the midgut
C Referred pain from greater splanchnic afferents (T5–T9)
D Pelvic splanchnic nerve innervation (S2–S4)
E Phrenic nerve (C3–C5) referred pain to the shoulder
Correct Answer: B

Explanation: The appendix, as a midgut structure, receives visceral innervation via the lesser splanchnic nerve (T10–T11). Visceral pain is poorly localized and perceived at the dermatomal level corresponding to the spinal cord segments receiving the visceral afferents. As inflammation progresses and involves the parietal peritoneum, somatic nerves (T12–L1) carrying right lower quadrant sensation are engaged, causing the classic pain migration from periumbilicus to RLQ.

Distractors: (A) Somatic T10 would not be the initial visceral pain pathway. (C) Greater splanchnic (T5–T9) innervates foregut (stomach, biliary system), not midgut. (D) Pelvic splanchnic (S2–S4) supplies the hindgut; appendix is midgut. (E) Phrenic (C3–C5) causes shoulder pain with diaphragmatic irritation, not appendicitis.
Question 3 · Referred Pain & Innervation
A 45-year-old woman with cholelithiasis presents with severe right upper quadrant pain following a fatty meal. On physical examination, Murphy's sign is positive, and she reports pain radiating to her right shoulder and scapular region. Which nerve is responsible for the referred pain to the shoulder?
A Greater splanchnic nerve (T5–T9) from the gallbladder itself
B Phrenic nerve (C3–C5) from diaphragmatic peritoneal irritation
C Intercostal nerves (T9) from chest wall involvement
D Vagus nerve (CN X) referred pain to the neck
E Sympathetic trunk fibers at T1
Correct Answer: B

Explanation: The gallbladder is located against the visceral peritoneum covering the diaphragm. When inflamed (cholecystitis), the visceral peritoneum irritates the adjacent diaphragm, which is innervated by the phrenic nerve (C3–C5). Phrenic afferents reach the dorsal root ganglia of C3–C5, which also supply the shoulder dermatome, causing referred pain to the right shoulder and scapular region.

Distractors: (A) Greater splanchnic nerves do carry the gallbladder's own visceral innervation (T5–T9), causing RUQ pain, but not shoulder pain. (C) Intercostal T9 would cause chest/RUQ pain, not classic shoulder pain. (D) The vagus nerve is parasympathetic and doesn't mediate referred pain in this context. (E) Sympathetic fibers at T1 contribute to the arm innervation, but not the classical phrenic-mediated referred pain.
Question 4 · Vascular Anatomy
A 55-year-old man with a history of peptic ulcer disease presents with sudden massive hematemesis and hemodynamic instability. Emergent upper endoscopy reveals a large posterior wall ulcer in the first portion of the duodenum eroding into a major artery. Which artery is most likely being eroded?
A Left gastric artery
B Splenic artery
C Gastroduodenal artery
D Superior mesenteric artery
E Common hepatic artery
Correct Answer: C

Explanation: The posterior wall of the first part of the duodenum is in direct contact with the gastroduodenal artery (GDA), which descends posterior to the first part of the duodenum before dividing into the superior right colic and right gastroepiploic arteries. Posterior duodenal ulcers classically erode into the GDA, causing massive, life-threatening hemorrhage. This is the most common artery involved in bleeding duodenal ulcers.

Distractors: (A) Left gastric artery is at risk in gastric ulcers (lesser curve), not duodenal ulcers. (B) Splenic artery is retroperitoneal and not exposed to the duodenum. (D) SMA is too proximal and does not supply the duodenum directly at this site. (E) Common hepatic artery is proximal to GDA and would not be eroded by a duodenal ulcer.
Question 5 · Portal-Systemic Collaterals
A 58-year-old male with longstanding alcoholic cirrhosis presents with massive hematemesis. Urgent upper endoscopy confirms variceal bleeding originating from dilated vessels at the gastroesophageal junction. These varices result from portal hypertension causing reversal of flow in a specific portal-systemic anastomosis. Which anastomosis is involved?
A Left gastric (portal) ↔ azygos system (systemic)
B Paraumbilical (portal) ↔ superficial epigastric (systemic)
C Superior rectal (portal) ↔ middle and inferior rectal (systemic)
D Superior mesenteric vein ↔ azygos system directly
E Splenic vein ↔ renal veins
Correct Answer: A

Explanation: Esophageal varices at the gastroesophageal junction arise from dilatation of the left gastric vein (portal tributary) draining into the azygos system (systemic). When portal pressure rises, blood reverses from the left gastric vein through its esophageal branches into the esophageal branches of the azygos system. These varices are life-threatening and the most common cause of variceal hemorrhage in cirrhosis.

Distractors: (B) Paraumbilical anastomosis produces caput medusae (periumbilical dilated veins), not esophageal varices. (C) Superior rectal anastomosis produces internal hemorrhoids and rectal varices, not esophageal. (D) SMV does not connect directly with the azygos system. (E) Splenic-renal anastomosis is not a recognized portal-systemic connection.
Question 6 · Portal-Systemic Collaterals
A 62-year-old man with hepatic cirrhosis is noted on physical examination to have prominent dilated veins radiating outward from the umbilicus (Medusa head appearance). Which portal-systemic anastomosis accounts for this finding?
A Left gastric ↔ azygos
B Paraumbilical (ligamentum venosus remnant) ↔ superficial and inferior epigastric veins
C Superior rectal ↔ middle and inferior rectal
D Colic veins ↔ retroperitoneal veins
E Hepatic veins ↔ diaphragmatic tributaries
Correct Answer: B

Explanation: Caput medusae results from reopening of embryologic umbilical vessels. The paraumbilical veins (remnants of the ligamentum venosus and umbilical vein) reconnect with the portal circulation and drain into the superficial epigastric and inferior epigastric veins (systemic tributaries of the external iliac vein). When portal pressure rises, blood flows outward through these periumbilical veins, creating the characteristic dilated radiating pattern.

Distractors: (A) Left gastric-azygos produces esophageal varices, not caput medusae. (C) Superior rectal produces hemorrhoids and rectal varices. (D) Colic veins do not typically produce visible surface venous dilation. (E) Hepatic veins drain outward; reopening does not explain caput medusae.
Question 7 · Anatomic Pathology
A 24-year-old woman with a body mass index of 14 kg/m² (severe anorexia nervosa) presents with postprandial bilious vomiting and epigastric pain relieved when lying prone. CT angiography shows compression of the third portion of the duodenum between the superior mesenteric artery and the abdominal aorta. Which duodenal segment is compressed?
A D1 (superior)
B D2 (descending)
C D3 (horizontal/inferior)
D D4 (ascending)
E Duodenojejunal junction
Correct Answer: C

Explanation: Superior mesenteric artery syndrome involves compression of the third (horizontal) portion of the duodenum as it crosses anterior to the aorta. The SMA and its branches compress the D3 segment, typically in patients with very low body weight (like anorexia nervosa) who lack protective retroperitoneal fat. Patients present with postprandial vomiting relieved by prone or supine position (which widens the aortomesenteric angle).

Distractors: (A) D1 is retroperitoneal at the hepatic flexure and is compressed by hepatomegaly or ulcers, not SMA. (B) D2 is vertical and would not be compressed by an anterior SMA. (D) D4 ascends and is not compressed. (E) The duodenojejunal junction is proximal to where SMA syndrome occurs.
Question 8 · Lymphatic Drainage
A 62-year-old male presents with progressive anorexia, weight loss, and early satiety. Physical examination reveals a firm, hard left supraclavicular lymph node (Virchow node/Troisier sign). Upper endoscopy confirms gastric adenocarcinoma at the gastric cardia. Which lymphatic pathway explains how gastric cancer can metastasize to the left supraclavicular node?
A Gastric nodes → right lymphatic duct → right venous angle
B Gastric nodes → inguinal lymph nodes → iliac nodes
C Gastric nodes → axillary nodes → supraclavicular nodes
D Gastric nodes → celiac nodes → cisterna chyli → thoracic duct → left venous angle
E Gastric nodes → internal mammary chain → anterior mediastinal nodes
Correct Answer: D

Explanation: Gastric lymphatics drain to celiac lymph nodes, then to the cisterna chyli, then via the thoracic duct to the left venous angle (confluence of left internal jugular and left subclavian veins). When gastric cancer metastasizes to this chain, tumor cells can lodge in left supraclavicular nodes, creating Virchow's node (also called Troisier sign). This is a classical sign of gastric or other upper abdominal malignancy.

Distractors: (A) Right lymphatic duct receives lymph from the right upper extremity and right thorax, not the stomach. (B) Inguinal nodes drain the lower body, not the stomach. (C) Axillary nodes are for upper extremity; the stomach does not drain here. (E) Internal mammary chain is for anterior chest wall and medial breast, not the stomach.
Question 9 · Embryology
A 3-year-old boy presents with painless hematochezia and bright red blood per rectum. Abdominal examination is benign with no pain or masses. A Meckel scan (technetium-99m pertechnetate) shows focal uptake in the right lower quadrant. Which embryologic structure persists in Meckel diverticulum?
A Urachus
B Allantois
C Vitelline (omphalomesenteric) duct
D Ductus venosus
E Notochord
Correct Answer: C

Explanation: Meckel diverticulum is a remnant of the vitelline (omphalomesenteric) duct, which normally obliterates by week 9 of gestation. Failure of obliteration leaves a diverticulum in the ileum (rule of 2s: ~2% of population, ~2 feet from ileocecal valve, ~2 inches long). Ectopic gastric mucosa in the diverticulum secretes acid, causing ulceration and painless bleeding. Technetium pertechnetate is taken up by gastric mucosa, allowing scintigraphic diagnosis.

Distractors: (A) Urachus connects the bladder to the umbilicus; its remnant causes umbilical discharge or cysts, not GI bleeding. (B) Allantois is part of the embryonic disc; it doesn't persist as a gut remnant. (D) Ductus venosus connects umbilical vein to IVC; its remnant is the ligamentum venosus, not a bleeding source. (E) Notochord is a midline embryonic structure; its remnant is the nucleus pulposus.
Question 10 · Embryology & Pathology
A newborn boy fails to pass meconium within 48 hours of birth and presents with abdominal distension, bilious emesis, and enterocolitis. Abdominal X-ray shows a transition zone at the rectosigmoid junction with proximal bowel dilation. Biopsy of the aganglionic segment shows absence of neurons in the myenteric and submucosal plexuses. Which embryologic process is defective in this patient?
A Recanalization of the duodenum
B Rotation and fixation of the midgut
C Cranial-to-caudal migration of neural crest cells to form the enteric nervous system
D Separation of the cloaca by the cloacal septum
E Elongation and herniation of the duodenum
Correct Answer: C

Explanation: Hirschsprung disease results from failure of neural crest cells to migrate caudally and colonize the distal bowel. Neural crest cells migrate from the proximal (cranial) esophagus distally to reach the rectum by week 12 of gestation. If this migration is incomplete or blocked, the distal bowel lacks enteric neurons, creating an aganglionic segment that cannot relax, causing functional obstruction. Most commonly affects the rectosigmoid colon.

Distractors: (A) Duodenal recanalization defects cause duodenal atresia, not Hirschsprung. (B) Midgut malrotation causes volvulus or obstruction, not aganglionosis. (D) Cloacal septal defects cause imperforate anus or urogenital abnormalities, not aganglionosis. (E) Duodenal elongation and herniation are part of normal development, not the defect here.
Question 11 · Esophageal Pathology
A 50-year-old female presents with progressive dysphagia to both solids and liquids over the past 6 months, regurgitation of undigested food, and nocturnal cough. Barium swallow shows a dilated proximal esophagus with a narrowed distal esophagus creating a "bird's beak" appearance. Esophageal manometry shows elevated lower esophageal sphincter pressure with incomplete relaxation. What is the pathophysiologic mechanism of this condition?
A Mechanical hypertrophy and contractility of the lower esophageal sphincter muscle
B Loss of inhibitory myenteric neurons secreting nitric oxide and VIP, causing failure of LES relaxation
C Mechanical obstruction from a Schatzki ring at the gastroesophageal junction
D Squamous cell carcinoma of the distal esophagus
E Eosinophilic inflammation of the esophageal mucosa
Correct Answer: B

Explanation: Achalasia results from degeneration of inhibitory neurons in the myenteric plexus that normally secrete nitric oxide (NO) and vasoactive intestinal peptide (VIP). These neurons mediate the receptive relaxation of the LES. Without them, the LES remains tonically contracted, and the esophagus dilates proximal to the obstruction. The "bird's beak" is a characteristic radiologic sign. Manometry shows elevated LES pressure with incomplete relaxation upon swallowing.

Distractors: (A) Hypertrophy would show increased muscle mass, not neuronal loss. (C) Schatzki rings are thin webs, not achalasia. (D) Cancer would show dysphagia to solids first, with weight loss and ulceration. (E) Eosinophilic esophagitis is associated with dysphagia and rings/strictures, but has eosinophilic infiltration histologically, not neuronal loss.
Question 12 · Tropical Pathology
A 35-year-old Brazilian immigrant presents with a lifelong history of progressive constipation, abdominal distension, and increasing difficulty swallowing. Barium studies show a massively dilated esophagus and colon. Serology is positive for Trypanosoma cruzi. Which vector organism transmits the causative pathogen?
A Helicobacter pylori via fecal-oral route
B Entamoeba histolytica via contaminated water
C Reduviid bug (kissing bug) transmitting Trypanosoma cruzi
D Schistosoma mansoni via cercariae in freshwater
E Ascaris lumbricoides via undercooked meat
Correct Answer: C

Explanation: Chagas disease is caused by Trypanosoma cruzi, transmitted by reduviid bugs (kissing bugs) endemic to Central and South America. The parasite invades tissues and destroys neurons of the myenteric plexus (similar to Hirschsprung disease), causing megaesophagus, megacolon, and megaduodenum. Chronic infection leads to cardiomyopathy, arrhythmias, and GI dysmotility.

Distractors: (A) H. pylori causes gastritis and ulcers, not megaviscera or neural destruction. (B) E. histolytica causes dysentery and liver abscesses, not megaviscera. (D) Schistosoma causes granuloma formation and chronic inflammation, not selective myenteric neuron destruction. (E) Ascaris causes mechanical obstruction in children, not selective neural destruction.
Question 13 · Anatomic & Pathologic Pain
A 48-year-old man with a long history of heavy alcohol use presents to the emergency department with severe, sharp epigastric pain radiating to the back. Laboratory studies show markedly elevated serum lipase and amylase. Abdominal CT confirms acute pancreatitis. Which anatomic feature explains the characteristic radiation of pain to the back?
A Inflammation of the greater omentum overlying the pancreas
B Compression of the superior mesenteric artery by the pancreatic head
C Pancreatic inflammation in the retroperitoneal space irritating the posterior peritoneum and lesser sac
D Irritation of the phrenic nerve at the diaphragm
E Peritoneal carcinomatosis from pancreatic adenocarcinoma
Correct Answer: C

Explanation: The pancreas is a retroperitoneal organ lying posterior to the peritoneum. When inflamed, pancreatic tissue and the inflammatory exudate irritate the retroperitoneal space and the posterior peritoneum of the lesser sac, as well as nearby structures (duodenum, posterior gastric wall). This accounts for the characteristic epigastric pain with radiation to the back, which is a hallmark of pancreatitis. The pain is worse when lying supine and improves when leaning forward (fetal position).

Distractors: (A) The greater omentum is anterior and would not cause back pain. (B) SMA compression occurs in chronic pancreatitis and causes vascular complications, not classic back pain. (D) Phrenic irritation causes shoulder pain, not back pain. (E) Carcinoma may present similarly, but acute pancreatitis is the scenario here, and the retroperitoneal location is the anatomic feature.
Question 14 · Hepatic Anatomy & Surgery
During a trauma resuscitation in the operating room, a surgeon temporarily applies the Pringle maneuver to control hepatic bleeding by clamping the hepatoduodenal ligament. Which three structures are compressed by this maneuver?
A Portal vein, hepatic artery proper, and common bile duct
B Inferior vena cava, hepatic veins, and portal vein
C Abdominal aorta, celiac trunk, and superior mesenteric artery
D Common hepatic artery, left gastric artery, and splenic artery
E Hepatic artery proper, cystic artery, and gallbladder
Correct Answer: A

Explanation: The hepatoduodenal ligament is the free edge of the lesser omentum and contains three main structures arranged left-to-right: portal vein (posterior), hepatic artery proper (anterior-left), and common bile duct (anterior-right). The Pringle maneuver compresses all three, stopping all inflow to the liver (both arterial and venous) and halting cholestasis, thereby reducing hepatic bleeding. It can be safely applied for up to 20 minutes in normal liver, longer in cirrhotic liver.

Distractors: (B) IVC and hepatic veins are posterior to the hepatoduodenal ligament, not part of it. (C) Aorta and celiac trunk are in the retroperitoneum, not in the ligament. (D) Left gastric and splenic arteries are branches of celiac, not in the hepatoduodenal ligament. (E) Cystic artery branches from hepatic artery proper but is not as large; gallbladder is separate.
Question 15 · Chronic Intestinal Pathology
A 25-year-old female presents with chronic right lower quadrant pain, diarrhea, weight loss, and oral ulcers. Colonoscopy reveals discontinuous areas of inflammation (skip lesions) with cobblestone mucosa and fissuring ulcers. Biopsy shows transmural inflammation with noncaseating granulomas. Which segment of bowel is most commonly involved in this disease?
A Jejunum
B Terminal ileum
C Cecum
D Ascending colon
E Sigmoid colon
Correct Answer: B

Explanation: This patient has Crohn disease, a transmural inflammatory bowel disease characterized by skip lesions (discontinuous inflammation), cobblestone mucosa, fissuring ulcers, and noncaseating granulomas. The terminal ileum is the most commonly affected site (present in ~40% of cases). The ileocecal region is also frequently involved. Crohn disease can affect any part of the GI tract from mouth to anus, but the terminal ileum is a classic presentation site.

Distractors: (A) Jejunum is less commonly affected and is not the most common site. (C) Cecum can be involved but is not the primary site. (D) Ascending colon involvement is less common than terminal ileum. (E) Sigmoid colitis is more typical of ulcerative colitis, which is continuous and limited to the colon/rectum.
Question 16 · Surgical Anatomy
A 40-year-old man undergoes splenectomy for post-traumatic splenic rupture. Three days postoperatively, imaging reveals a loculated fluid collection in the left upper quadrant with markedly elevated amylase content. Which anatomic relationship explains this complication?
A The pancreatic body is retroperitoneal and lies medial to the spleen
B The pancreatic tail is intraperitoneal and lies within the splenorenal ligament
C The pancreatic head is retroperitoneal and extends into the left upper quadrant
D The accessory pancreatic duct (duct of Santorini) opens into the duodenum
E The main pancreatic duct (duct of Wirsung) courses through the splenic hilum
Correct Answer: B

Explanation: The pancreatic tail is the only intraperitoneal part of the pancreas, lying within the splenorenal ligament between the spleen and left kidney. During splenectomy, the pancreatic tail can be injured or the splenorenal ligament torn, causing a pancreatic leak. The high amylase content in the fluid collection confirms pancreatic injury. The pancreatic body and head are retroperitoneal and at lower risk during routine splenectomy.

Distractors: (A) Pancreatic body is retroperitoneal, not intraperitoneal. (C) Pancreatic head is in the C-curve of the duodenum and does not extend to the left upper quadrant. (D) Duct of Santorini is the accessory duct and doesn't explain the complication. (E) Duct of Wirsung (main duct) runs longitudinally through the pancreas but injury alone wouldn't cause such a large collection.
Question 17 · Volvulus Pathology
An elderly, chronically bedbound patient presents with acute abdominal distension, constipation, and left lower quadrant pain. Abdominal X-ray shows a "coffee-bean" sign with massive colonic dilation. CT confirms sigmoid volvulus (twisted bowel loop). Which anatomic feature predisposes the sigmoid colon to volvulus more than other colonic segments?
A Retroperitoneal fixation of the ascending colon
B Long mesentery of the sigmoid colon (intraperitoneal on mesosigmoid)
C Length of the transverse mesocolon allowing excessive mobility
D Teniae coli creating points of mechanical weakness
E Appendices epiploicae increasing friction during peristalsis
Correct Answer: B

Explanation: The sigmoid colon has a long, mobile mesentery (mesosigmoid), making it an intraperitoneal structure. This mobility allows the sigmoid to twist on its mesentery, causing volvulus (a full 180–360° rotation that strangulates the blood supply). The long mesosigmoid is the key anatomic risk factor. Volvulus is common in patients with functional constipation, megacolon, or prolonged immobility. The "coffee-bean" sign is the characteristic radiographic appearance of the twisted, dilated loop.

Distractors: (A) Retroperitoneal ascending colon is fixed and cannot volvulus. (C) Transverse mesocolon is long but the transverse colon is less mobile due to its position. (D) Teniae coli are muscular bands involved in haustral formation, not mechanical weakness. (E) Appendices epiploicae are fat-filled pouches that don't directly cause volvulus.
Question 18 · Parasympathetic Innervation
During an anesthesia lecture, a faculty member reviews parasympathetic innervation of the gastrointestinal tract and asks which nerve pathway innervates the descending colon. A student correctly responds that the hindgut has a distinct parasympathetic supply different from the vagus-innervated foregut and midgut. Which nerve fibers provide parasympathetic innervation to the descending colon?
A Vagus nerve (CN X)
B Greater splanchnic nerves (T5–T9)
C Pelvic splanchnic nerves (S2–S4)
D Lumbar splanchnic nerves (L1–L2)
E Lesser splanchnic nerves (T10–T11)
Correct Answer: C

Explanation: The hindgut (distal third of transverse colon, descending colon, sigmoid, rectum) receives parasympathetic innervation from pelvic splanchnic nerves (S2–S4), which arise from the sacral spinal cord. This is distinct from the vagus nerve (CN X) supply to the foregut and proximal midgut, and the lesser splanchnic (T10–T11) supply to the distal midgut. The descending colon is part of the hindgut, so pelvic splanchnic S2–S4 innervation applies.

Distractors: (A) Vagus innervates foregut (esophagus, stomach, biliary) and proximal midgut (up to proximal two-thirds transverse colon). (B) Greater splanchnic (T5–T9) carries sympathetic fibers to foregut, not parasympathetic. (D) Lumbar splanchnic (L1–L2) are sympathetic, not parasympathetic. (E) Lesser splanchnic (T10–T11) are sympathetic fibers to distal midgut, not parasympathetic supply to hindgut.
