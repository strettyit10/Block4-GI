LO 1 — Foregut Vasculature
What artery supplies the foregut (distal esophagus to D2), at what level does it arise, and name the three branches of the celiac trunk.
tap to flip
Answer
Celiac trunk at T12/L1

Three branches (LSC): Left gastric, Splenic, Common hepatic

Left gastric → lesser curvature stomach; Splenic → spleen + short gastrics; Common hepatic → right gastric, GDA, hepatic artery proper
LO 1 — Midgut Vasculature
What artery supplies the midgut (D3 to proximal 2/3 transverse colon), at what level, and name three of its key branches?
tap to flip
Answer
Superior mesenteric artery (SMA) at L1

Key branches: ileocolic, right colic, middle colic, jejunal arteries, ileal arteries

Jejunal arteries have long vasa recta, few arcades; ileal arteries have short vasa recta, many arcades
LO 1 — Hindgut Vasculature
What artery supplies the hindgut (distal 1/3 transverse colon to upper rectum), at what level, and what are its three branches?
tap to flip
Answer
Inferior mesenteric artery (IMA) at L3

Three branches: left colic, sigmoid arteries, superior rectal

Note: pelvic splanchnics are parasympathetic (S2-S4), unlike lumbar splanchnics (sympathetic L1-L2)
LO 1 — Portal Triad
In the hepatoduodenal ligament, name the three structures of the portal triad in order from POSTERIOR to ANTERIOR and list a clinical mnemonic.
tap to flip
Answer
Posterior to anterior: Portal vein (posterior), Hepatic artery proper (left), Common bile duct (right)

Mnemonic: VHD — Vein, Hepatic artery, Duct

Clinical: Pringle maneuver compresses hepatoduodenal ligament to control hepatic hemorrhage
LO 1 — Gastroduodenal Artery
Describe the course of the gastroduodenal (GDA) artery relative to the duodenum, and what happens clinically if there is a posterior duodenal ulcer?
tap to flip
Answer
GDA runs posterior to D1

Posterior duodenal ulcer → erodes into GDA → massive upper GI hemorrhage (classic Step 1 trap)

Anterior ulcer → peritonitis (perforates into peritoneal cavity)
LO 1 — Marginal Artery
What is the marginal artery of Drummond, why is it clinically important, and where is the watershed zone that is most vulnerable to ischemia?
tap to flip
Answer
Marginal artery of Drummond = continuous colonic arcade formed by anastomoses of colic arteries

Watershed zone at splenic flexure (SMA/IMA junction)

Vulnerable after AAA repair → ischemic colitis at splenic flexure and rectosigmoid junction
LO 1 — Duodenum Four-Part
Describe the peritoneal classification and key features of each duodenal segment (D1 through D4).
tap to flip
Answer
D1 (superior): intraperitoneal, posterior to stomach
D2 (descending): retroperitoneal, ampulla of Vater on medial wall
D3 (horizontal): retroperitoneal, crossed by SMA (SMA syndrome risk)
D4 (ascending): retroperitoneal, suspensory ligament of Treitz attaches at D3/D4 junction

Mnemonic: Only D1 intraperitoneal; D2-D4 retroperitoneal
LO 1 — Jejunum vs Ileum
Compare jejunum and ileum: wall thickness, mesenteric fat, plicae, vasa recta, arcades, and Peyer's patches.
tap to flip
Answer
Jejunum: larger diameter, thicker wall, LESS fat, tall close-packed plicae, LONG vasa recta, FEW simple arcades, NO Peyer's

Ileum: smaller diameter, thinner wall, MORE fat, low sparse plicae, SHORT vasa recta, MANY complex arcades, abundant Peyer's patches
LO 1 — Peritoneal SAM
State the peritoneal classification rule "SAD PUCKER" and list what each intraperitoneal structure requires for mobility.
tap to flip
Answer
SAD PUCKER = intraperitoneal: Stomach, Appendix, Duodenum (D1 only), Pancreatic TAIL, Uterus, Colon (transverse & sigmoid), Kidneys (NO — retroperitoneal!), Esophagus (NO — retroperitoneal!), Rectum (upper only)

All others D2-D4, pancreas (head/body/neck), colon (ascending/descending), lower rectum = retroperitoneal
LO 1 — Innervation Summary
For foregut, midgut, and hindgut: list parasympathetic nerve, sympathetic splanchnic nerve, spinal level, and referred pain location.
tap to flip
Answer
Foregut: vagus, greater splanchnic (T5-T9), celiac ganglia, epigastric pain
Midgut: vagus, lesser splanchnic (T10-T11), SMA ganglia, periumbilical pain
Hindgut: pelvic splanchnics (S2-S4) NOT vagus, lumbar splanchnics (L1-L2), IMA ganglia, hypogastric/suprapubic pain
LO 2 — Lymphatic Drainage
What is the pathway of GI lymphatics from the gut wall to the thorax? Name key stations and the final vessel.
tap to flip
Answer
Gut wall → regional lymph nodes (celiac, SMA, IMA nodes) → cisterna chyli (at L1) → thoracic duct → left venous angle (confluence of left internal jugular + left subclavian vein)

Foregut → celiac; midgut → SMA; hindgut → IMA nodes
LO 2 — Virchow's Node
What is Virchow's node (Troisier's sign), where is it, and what malignancy is classically associated?
tap to flip
Answer
Virchow's node = enlarged left supraclavicular lymph node from metastatic intra-abdominal malignancy

Pathway: intra-abdominal cancer → thoracic duct → left venous angle → left supraclavicular nodes

Classic: gastric cancer (gastric CA)
LO 2 — Portosystemic Anastomoses
In portal hypertension, name three portosystemic anastomoses and the vessels involved in each, plus the clinical sign for one.
tap to flip
Answer
1. Esophageal varices: left gastric vein ↔ azygos vein
2. Caput medusae: paraumbilical veins ↔ superficial epigastric vein
3. Anorectal varices: superior rectal vein ↔ middle/inferior rectal veins

Note: Also portopulmonary shunts
LO 2 — Stomach Anatomy
Describe the anatomical regions of the stomach (cardia, fundus, body, pylorus) and its two curvatures with their arterial supply.
tap to flip
Answer
Regions: cardia → fundus → body → pylorus/antrum

Lesser curvature: left gastric + right gastric arteries
Greater curvature: left gastroomental + right gastroomental arteries

Greater omentum = "policeman of the abdomen" (migrates to inflammation)
LO 2 — Colon Features
Name three distinctive gross anatomical features of the colon (as opposed to small intestine) and explain why they are present.
tap to flip
Answer
1. Taeniae coli: three longitudinal muscle bands (incomplete circular coat)
2. Haustra: outpouchings between taeniae (characteristic shape)
3. Omental appendages: small fat-filled pouches on visceral peritoneum

Result of incomplete muscularization and colon's water-absorption role
LO 3 — Referred Pain Mechanism
Explain why visceral abdominal pain is often referred to the dermatome, and give three classic examples of referred pain patterns.
tap to flip
Answer
Mechanism: visceral + somatic afferents converge on same dorsal horn neurons → brain mislocalizes to dermatome

Examples:
• Cholecystitis: epigastric → right shoulder/scapula (phrenic C3-C5)
• Pancreatitis: epigastric → back (retroperitoneal)
• Appendicitis: periumbilical first (visceral midgut) then RLQ (somatic)
LO 3 — Appendicitis vs Meckel
State the Meckel's 2's rule and describe how appendicitis pain migration differs from a symptomatic Meckel's diverticulum.
tap to flip
Answer
Meckel's 2's rule: 2% population, 2 feet from ileocecal valve, 2 inches long, 2:1 male, 2% have complications (bleeding, obstruction)

Appendicitis pain: periumbilical (midgut visceral) → RLQ (McBurney's point, somatic)
Meckel's pain: typically RLQ or periumbilical (same midgut supply as ileum)
LO 3 — Omental Foramen
Describe the omental foramen (foramen of Winslow): what it connects, and its four borders.
tap to flip
Answer
Connects greater peritoneal sac ↔ lesser sac

Four borders:
Anterior: hepatoduodenal ligament (portal triad)
Posterior: IVC
Superior: caudate lobe of liver
Inferior: D1

Clinical: allows fingers to enter lesser sac during surgical exploration
LO 1 — Ligament of Treitz
What is the ligament of Treitz, where does it attach, and what is its clinical significance?
tap to flip
Answer
Suspensory ligament of Treitz = musculofascial band from D4/D3 junction to right crus of diaphragm

Attaches at junction of retroperitoneal D4 and intraperitoneal jejunum

Significance: anatomical boundary between upper and lower GI tract; upper endoscopy stops here; location of ligament of Treitz fixates the duodenojejunal flex
LO 2 — Lymph Pathway
Trace the pathway of lymph from a GI structure to the venous angle, naming key stations and the final vessel.
tap to flip
Answer
GI mucosa → regional nodes (celiac, SMA, IMA nodes depending on foregut/midgut/hindgut) → cisterna chyli at L1 → thoracic duct → left venous angle (confluence of left internal jugular + left subclavian veins)

Clinical: Virchow's node (left supraclavicular lymph node enlargement) can indicate metastatic gastric cancer via retrograde thoracic duct spread

Arrange these embryologic divisions from most proximal to most distal, with key structures in each.

  2. Midgut (SMA, L1): D3-D4, jejunum, ileum, cecum, appendix, ascending colon, proximal 2/3 transverse colon

Drill 2 — Celiac Trunk Branches: LSC Order
Arrange the three celiac branches in the standard teaching sequence (L-S-C).
⠿Splenic artery → spleen, short gastrics, left gastroomental
⠿Common hepatic artery → right gastric, GDA, hepatic artery proper
⠿Left gastric artery → lesser curvature, esophageal branches
  1. Left gastric — small, supplies lesser curvature + esophagus
  2. Splenic — largest celiac branch, tortuous course, supplies spleen + short gastrics + left gastroomental
  3. Common hepatic → divides into right gastric (to greater curvature), GDA (posterior to D1, risk of ulcer hemorrhage), and hepatic artery proper (to liver)
Drill 3 — SMA Colonic Branches: Proximal → Distal
Arrange the named SMA branches that supply the colon from proximal to distal.
⠿Middle colic → transverse colon (midgut/SMA)
⠿Ileocolic → cecum + appendix + terminal ileum
⠿Right colic → ascending colon

  2. Right colic — supplies ascending colon
  3. Middle colic — supplies transverse colon (proximal 2/3 is SMA/midgut, but runs across abdomen)
  4. Jejunal and ileal branches also come from SMA trunk

⠿SMA at L1 (midgut) — middle

Test Trap Mode: Each statement is either TRUE or FALSE as written. Many are designed to catch common exam errors. Click T or F — then read the explanation carefully.
Trap 01 — LO 1
"The pelvic splanchnics provide sympathetic innervation to the hindgut."
FALSE. This is a CLASSIC trap! Pelvic splanchnics (S2–S4) are PARASYMPATHETIC, not sympathetic. All other splanchnics (greater, lesser, lumbar) are sympathetic. The hindgut parasympathetics come from pelvic splanchnics (NOT vagus). Sympathetic = lumbar splanchnics (L1–L2).
Trap 02 — LO 1
"A posterior duodenal ulcer that erodes into the gastroduodenal artery will cause peritonitis because it perforates into the peritoneal cavity."
FALSE. Posterior duodenal ulcer → GDA erosion → massive upper GI hemorrhage (not peritonitis). An ANTERIOR ulcer that perforates causes peritonitis. The key: posterior ulcer bleeds into the vessel; anterior ulcer perforates into the peritoneal cavity.
Trap 03 — LO 1
"The pancreatic head and body are intraperitoneal structures, while the pancreatic tail is retroperitoneal."
FALSE. This is reversed. The pancreatic tail is INTRAPERITONEAL (it hangs freely in the greater omentum). The pancreatic head, neck, and body are RETROPERITONEAL. Remember: only the TAIL is mobile (intraperitoneal); the rest is fixed (retroperitoneal).
Trap 04 — LO 1
"Both D1 and D2 are intraperitoneal segments of the duodenum."
FALSE. Only D1 is intraperitoneal. D2, D3, and D4 are all retroperitoneal. This is often tested: "Which segment is intraperitoneal?" Answer = D1 only. Mnemonic: "Only D1 intraperitoneal; D2–D4 retro."
Trap 05 — LO 2
"After a spinal cord injury at T10 that severs the spinal cord, the patient will lose sympathetic innervation to the entire GI tract including the hindgut."
FALSE. Spinal cord injury at T10 severs the greater and lesser splanchnics (foregut & midgut), BUT the lumbar splanchnics (L1–L2) exit below T10, so hindgut sympathetics remain intact. Additionally, hindgut parasympathetics (pelvic splanchnics S2–S4) also exit below T10 and remain intact.
Trap 06 — LO 3
"The splenic flexure is a common site of ischemic colitis BECAUSE it has rich, redundant blood supply and excellent collateralization."
FALSE. The splenic flexure is a watershed zone (SMA/IMA junction) with POOR collateralization — that's why it's vulnerable to ischemia, especially after AAA repair. Similarly, the rectosigmoid junction is another watershed area. "Watershed" = end-artery junction = vulnerable.
Trap 07 — LO 3
"Appendicitis pain begins in the RLQ at McBurney's point because the appendix is supplied by the ileocolic artery (midgut)."
FALSE. Yes, the appendix is midgut (ileocolic artery) BUT appendicitis pain classically begins PERIUMBILICAL (visceral midgut afferent via T10), then localizes to RLQ (parietal somatic pain at McBurney's point). Periumbilical pain then RLQ is pathognomonic for appendicitis.
Trap 08 — LO 1
"The ligament of Treitz marks the junction between the intraperitoneal jejunum and the retroperitoneal duodenum (D3/D4)."
TRUE. The suspensory ligament of Treitz attaches the duodenojejunal flexion (D4/D3 junction in the retroperitoneum) to the right crus of the diaphragm, marking the boundary between UPPER GI tract (duodenum) and LOWER GI tract (jejunum). It's where upper endoscopy stops.
