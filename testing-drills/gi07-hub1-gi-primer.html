LO 1 — Endoderm Origin
What is the embryonic source of the definitive endoderm?
tap to flip
Answer
Epiblast cells that ingress through the primitive streak (~day 14–16) and displace the hypoblast. The hypoblast is NOT the gut endoderm — it becomes the extraembryonic yolk sac.
LO 1 — Ingression Depth
How does ingression depth through the primitive streak determine germ layer fate?
tap to flip
Answer
Deepest ingression → displaces hypoblast → definitive endoderm
Intermediate → spreads laterally → mesoderm
No ingression → stays on surface → ectoderm
Primitive node specifically → notochord
LO 1 — Master TFs
Name the key transcription factors required for definitive endoderm specification.
tap to flip
Answer
Sox17 = master TF for definitive endoderm specification (required for gut tube).
Foxa2 (HNF3β) = pioneer TF for anterior foregut + liver induction.
Driven by high Nodal signaling (TGF-β family).
LO 1 — Membrane Landmarks
What are the buccopharyngeal and cloacal membranes, and what do they become?
tap to flip
Answer
Buccopharyngeal membrane = cranial end of primitive streak → future mouth (breaks down ~week 4).
Cloacal membrane = caudal end → future anus / urogenital openings. These define the cranial and caudal ends of the gut tube.
LO 2 — 3 Regions
What are the three gut tube regions and their arterial supplies?
tap to flip
Answer
Foregut → Celiac artery (T12)
Midgut → Superior Mesenteric Artery / SMA (L1)
Hindgut → Inferior Mesenteric Artery / IMA (L3)
Three unpaired ventral aortic branches map perfectly to the three gut regions.
LO 2 — Foregut/Midgut Boundary
Where exactly is the foregut-midgut boundary within the duodenum?
tap to flip
Answer
At the ampulla of Vater (entry of the common bile duct).
D1 + proximal D2 (above ampulla) = foregut → celiac.
Distal D2, D3, D4 (below ampulla) = midgut → SMA.
Duodenum is the only organ that spans two regions.
LO 2 — Foregut Accessories
What are the 4 non-GI-tube derivatives of the foregut?
tap to flip
Answer
Mnemonic: "LLGP"
Liver · Lungs · Gallbladder · Pancreas
All arise as outgrowths/diverticula from foregut endoderm. The respiratory diverticulum buds ventrally from the foregut → entire respiratory tree.
LO 2 — Watershed
Why is the splenic flexure especially vulnerable to ischemia?
tap to flip
Answer
It sits at the SMA ↔ IMA watershed (embryologic midgut-hindgut boundary). The connecting marginal artery of Drummond is often tenuous here. During low-flow states or IMA occlusion → ischemic colitis most commonly at splenic flexure. Second watershed: rectosigmoid junction.
LO 2 — Anal Canal Zones
How do the two zones of the anal canal differ in origin, epithelium, and innervation?
tap to flip
Answer
Above dentate line = hindgut endoderm → columnar epithelium → IMA / visceral innervation → painless (internal hemorrhoids).
Below dentate line = ectoderm (proctodeum) → stratified squamous → internal pudendal / somatic → painful (external hemorrhoids).
LO 3 — Tube-in-Tube
What drives embryonic folding, and what does the tube-within-a-tube body plan produce?
tap to flip
Answer
The embryo and amnion grow faster than the yolk sac → cranio-caudal and lateral folding roll the flat endodermal sheet into a tube. Result: inner gut tube (endoderm) inside outer body wall tube (ectoderm), separated by the intraembryonic coelom (→ peritoneal cavity).
LO 3 — Mesoderm Layers
Name the 3 mesoderm layers around the gut and what each becomes.
tap to flip
Answer
Splanchnic mesoderm (lines gut) → smooth muscle, serosa of gut wall, mesenteries.
Somatic mesoderm (lines body wall) → parietal peritoneum, body-wall muscle/CT.
Intermediate mesoderm → kidneys, gonads, adrenal cortex.
LO 3 — Dorsal Mesentery
Name the major adult derivatives of the dorsal mesentery.
tap to flip
Answer
Dorsal mesogastrium → greater omentum (+ gastrosplenic, splenorenal ligaments).
Mesoduodenum → largely disappears (duodenum becomes 2° retroperitoneal).
Mesentery proper → suspends jejunum/ileum.
Transverse & sigmoid mesocolons → suspend respective colons. Present along entire gut tube.
LO 3 — Ventral Mesentery
Where is the ventral mesentery, and what 2 adult ligaments does it form?
tap to flip
Answer
Present only in the foregut region (cranial abdomen; absent caudal to umbilicus). Forms:
(1) Falciform ligament (liver → anterior abdominal wall; contains ligamentum teres).
(2) Lesser omentum = gastrohepatic + hepatoduodenal (portal triad inside) ligaments.
LO 3 — Retroperitoneal
List the secondarily retroperitoneal organs and contrast with primary retroperitoneal.
tap to flip
Answer
Secondarily retroperitoneal (started with mesentery, fused to post wall): duodenum D2–D4, pancreas head/body, ascending colon, descending colon.
Primarily retroperitoneal (never had mesentery): kidneys, ureters, adrenals, aorta, IVC, rectum (lower 2/3).
LO 4 — Stomach Rotation
Describe stomach rotation and its effect on the vagal trunks.
tap to flip
Answer
Stomach rotates 90° clockwise (ventral view) at ~day 35.
Left vagus → anterior vagal trunk.
Right vagus → posterior vagal trunk.
Posterior wall (faster-growing) → greater curvature; anterior → lesser curvature.
LO 4 — Greater Omentum
How does the greater omentum and lesser sac form?
tap to flip
Answer
As the stomach rotates 90° CW, the dorsal mesogastrium is dragged left and elongates → greater omentum (apron hanging from greater curvature). The space created behind the stomach = lesser sac (omental bursa), accessed via the epiploic foramen of Winslow.
LO 4 — Liver Bud
Where does the liver bud form and what two tissues contribute to the adult liver?
tap to flip
Answer
The hepatic diverticulum grows from ventral foregut (caudal) endoderm into the septum transversum through the ventral mesentery.
Endoderm → hepatocytes + bile duct epithelium.
Septum transversum mesoderm → Kupffer cells, stellate cells, connective tissue, sinusoids.
LO 4 — Midgut Rotation
Describe the total midgut rotation around the SMA.
tap to flip
Answer
270° total CCW rotation around the SMA:
• Week 6: physiologic herniation into umbilical cord + 90° CCW.
• Week 10–11: gut returns to abdomen + additional 180° CCW.
Cecum begins RUQ → descends to RLQ by week 12.
LO 4 — Malrotation
What is the pathophysiology and danger of intestinal malrotation?
tap to flip
Answer
Incomplete 270° CCW rotation → cecum stays in RUQ; fibrous Ladd's bands compress duodenum. Critical danger: the entire midgut hangs on a narrow SMA pedicle → midgut volvulus → SMA occlusion → infarction (surgical emergency). Rx: Ladd's procedure.
LO 4 — Meckel's
State the rule of 2's for Meckel's diverticulum.
tap to flip
Answer
Persistence of the vitelline (omphalomesenteric) duct.
2% of population · 2 ft from ileocecal valve · 2 inches long · 2% symptomatic · 2 types of ectopic tissue (gastric, pancreatic) · presents in first 2 years. Painless rectal bleeding; Dx = ⁹⁹ᵐTc pertechnetate scan. True diverticulum (all 3 layers).

Classic exam traps. Each statement sounds plausible — decide True or False. These are the exact misconceptions that Step 1 loves to test.
Trap 1 · LO 1
The definitive endoderm of the gut tube is derived from the hypoblast layer of the bilaminar embryonic disc.
FALSE. Definitive endoderm comes from epiblast cells that ingress through the primitive streak and displace the hypoblast. The hypoblast becomes the extraembryonic yolk sac — NOT the gut.
Trap 2 · LO 1
Sox17-knockout embryos can still form a functional gut tube from hypoblast-derived endoderm.
FALSE. Sox17 is the master TF for definitive endoderm — without it, epiblast cells ingressing through the streak cannot adopt an endoderm fate. The hypoblast does not contribute to the gut tube.
Trap 3 · LO 2
The entire duodenum is a foregut derivative supplied exclusively by branches of the celiac artery.
FALSE. The duodenum is the only organ that spans two gut regions. D1 + proximal D2 (above the ampulla of Vater) = foregut / celiac. Distal D2, D3, D4 = midgut / SMA. That's why pancreaticoduodenal arteries are both superior (celiac) and inferior (SMA).
Trap 4 · LO 2
The upper and lower halves of the anal canal share the same epithelium, blood supply, and pain sensation.
FALSE. The dentate line divides two distinct zones: above = hindgut endoderm / columnar / IMA / visceral / painless; below = ectoderm / squamous / pudendal / somatic / painful. This is why internal hemorrhoids are painless and external are painful.
Trap 5 · LO 3
The ventral mesentery is present along the entire length of the gut tube, providing anterior suspension for the small and large bowel.
FALSE. The ventral mesentery exists only in the foregut (cranial abdomen). The small and large bowel are suspended only by dorsal mesentery. This absence of ventral attachment is why physiologic umbilical herniation is possible.
Trap 6 · LO 3
All parts of the duodenum are intraperitoneal and suspended by mesentery.
FALSE. Only D1 (the duodenal cap) is intraperitoneal. D2–D4 are secondarily retroperitoneal (their dorsal mesentery fused to the posterior abdominal wall). The pancreas head/body, ascending colon, and descending colon are similarly secondarily retroperitoneal.
Trap 7 · LO 4
The stomach rotates 90° counterclockwise during embryogenesis, so the right vagus becomes the anterior vagal trunk.
FALSE. Stomach rotates 90° clockwise (ventral view). Because the LEFT side rotates anteriorly, the left vagus → anterior vagal trunk, and the right vagus → posterior vagal trunk. (Midgut goes CCW; stomach goes CW — don't mix them up.)
Trap 8 · LO 4
Omphalocele and gastroschisis both present with membrane-covered herniation of bowel through the umbilicus and are strongly associated with chromosomal abnormalities.
FALSE. Omphalocele: herniation THROUGH umbilicus, membrane present, may contain liver, associated with Trisomy 13/18 / Beckwith-Wiedemann. Gastroschisis: defect to the RIGHT of umbilicus, no membrane, bowel inflamed from amniotic fluid, usually isolated (no chromosomal association).
