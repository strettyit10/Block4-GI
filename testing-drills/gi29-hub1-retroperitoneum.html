Recite the SAD PUCKER mnemonic for primary retroperitoneal organs.
tap to flip
Answer

LO 1 — Primary vs Secondary
What is the difference between primary and secondary retroperitoneal organs?
tap to flip
Answer
Primary: developed retroperitoneally from the start; always retroperitoneal (kidneys, adrenals, aorta, IVC, pancreas head/body, duodenum 2-4, ascending/descending colon)

Secondary: developed intraperitoneal, then fused to posterior wall during development (pancreas tail, duodenum 1st part, colon transverse/sigmoid)
LO 1 — Peritoneal Reflections
What are peritoneal reflections? Name two examples and their clinical relevance.
tap to flip
Answer
Folds of peritoneum anchoring organs to abdominal wall. Examples: mesentery of SI, mesocolon (transverse, sigmoid).

Clinical: Determine spread of infection/abscess. Intraperitoneal bleeding follows peritoneal planes. Mesenteric blood vessels run in peritoneal folds (vulnerable during surgery).
LO 2 — Abdominal Aorta
At what spinal levels do celiac trunk, SMA, and IMA originate from the aorta?
tap to flip
Answer
Celiac trunk: T12
SMA (Superior Mesenteric A): L1
IMA (Inferior Mesenteric A): L3

Mnemonic: "Celiac at T12, SMA at L1, IMA at L3" (think descending levels)
LO 2 — Renal Vessels
At what level do renal arteries originate? Why are left and right renal veins asymmetric?
tap to flip
Answer
Origin: L1-L2 level (just below SMA)

Asymmetry: Left renal vein is LONGER; passes anterior to aorta (between SMA & aorta); receives L gonadal + L adrenal veins.

Right renal vein: short; enters IVC directly; does NOT receive gonadal/adrenal veins.
LO 2 — Nutcracker Syndrome
What is Nutcracker Syndrome and why does it occur?
tap to flip
Answer
Compression of left renal vein between SMA & aorta → hematuria, flank pain, potential thrombosis.

Why: Left renal vein's anomalous anterior course passes through SMA-aorta angle. Narrowing of this space (abdominal mass, SMA kinking) pinches the vein.
LO 2 — Portal Venous System
Name three venous anastomoses between portal and systemic (caval) circulation.
tap to flip
Answer
1. Esophagus: esophageal veins (portal) ↔ azygos (caval)
2. Rectum: superior rectal (portal) ↔ middle/inferior rectal (caval)
3. Paraumblical: paraumblical (portal) ↔ superficial epigastric (caval)

→ Enlarge with portal HTN (esophageal varices, hemorrhoids, caput medusa)
LO 3 — Diaphragm: T8 Caval Hiatus
What passes through the caval hiatus at T8? Includes venous, arterial, and nerve structures.
tap to flip
Answer
T8 Caval Hiatus: IVC + Right phrenic nerve

NOTE: Left phrenic nerve passes through esophageal hiatus (T10), not caval hiatus. Right phrenic only.
LO 3 — Diaphragm: T10 Esophageal Hiatus
What passes through the esophageal hiatus at T10?
tap to flip
Answer
T10 Esophageal Hiatus: Esophagus + Left vagal trunk + Right vagal trunk

(Both left & right vagal trunks pass through T10, not T8)
LO 3 — Diaphragm: T12 Aortic Hiatus
What passes through the aortic hiatus at T12?
tap to flip
Answer
T12 Aortic Hiatus: Thoracic aorta + Thoracic duct + Azygos vein

Mnemonic: "I 8 10 eggs at the table" (IVC-T8, Esophagus-T10, Aorta-T12)
LO 3 — Phrenic Nerve
What spinal nerve roots form the phrenic nerve? Where does it originate embryologically?
tap to flip
Answer
Spinal roots: C3, C4, C5

Mnemonic: "C3, 4, 5 keeps the diaphragm alive"

Embryo: Originates from cervical plexus (C3-5), descends through neck & thorax. Explains referred shoulder pain (C4 dermatome) in diaphragmatic pathology.
LO 4 — Ureter: 3 Constrictions
Name the three anatomic sites where ureters narrow, where kidney stones commonly lodge.
tap to flip
Answer
1. Ureteropelvic junction (UPJ): where ureter leaves renal pelvis
2. Pelvic brim: where ureter crosses iliac vessels (iliopectineal ligament)
3. Ureterovesical junction (UVJ): where ureter enters bladder

Stones lodge at these sites due to narrowing.
LO 4 — Urolithiasis Referred Pain
What is the dermatome distribution for referred kidney stone pain? Why "loin to groin"?
tap to flip
Answer
Dermatomes: T11-L2 (flank pain → groin/labia/scrotum)

"Loin to groin": Flank (loin) pain radiates toward inguinal region/genitalia following T11-L2 nerve distribution

Kidney @ T12 level; ureter @ T11-L2 innervation
LO 4 — "Water Under the Bridge"
In females and males, what passes ABOVE (and BELOW) the ureter at the pelvis?
tap to flip
Answer
Females: Uterine artery passes ABOVE ureter ("water under the bridge" = ureter under artery)

Males: Vas deferens passes ABOVE ureter (similarly, ureter under the vas)

Clinical: Avoid ureteric injury during hysterectomy or vasectomy — recognize ureter's inferior position.
LO 5 — Adrenal Cortex Innervation
Does the adrenal cortex have direct nerve innervation? What is the primary control mechanism?
tap to flip
Answer
NO direct innervation. Cortex is controlled entirely by endocrine signals:
• Zona glomerulosa: RAAS (angiotensin II)
• Zona fasciculata: ACTH (from anterior pituitary)
• Zona reticularis: ACTH + androgens

Unique: Only endocrine organ without direct autonomic control.
LO 5 — Adrenal Medulla Innervation
What is the embryologic origin of adrenal medulla cells? What nerves innervate them?
tap to flip
Answer
Origin: Neuroectoderm (neural crest) → chromaffin cells = modified postganglionic neurons

Innervation: Direct preganglionic sympathetic fibers (T5-T11) synapse on chromaffin cells

Function: Release epinephrine/norepinephrine upon sympathetic stimulation (unique: somatic motor control of endocrine gland)
LO 5 — Adrenal Arteries
Name the three sources of arterial supply to the adrenal glands (superior, middle, inferior).
tap to flip
Answer
Superior suprarenal artery: from inferior phrenic artery
Middle suprarenal artery: from abdominal aorta
Inferior suprarenal artery: from renal artery

All three = rich vascular supply to small glands
LO 5 — Adrenal Veins
What is the asymmetry between left and right adrenal venous drainage? Why is this surgically important?
tap to flip
Answer
Left adrenal vein: Long vein → drains into LEFT renal vein
Right adrenal vein: Short vein → drains directly into IVC

Surgical concern: L adrenal vein vulnerable during left nephrectomy/renal vessel surgery. R adrenal vein can be difficult to access due to IVC proximity.
LO 2 — End-Arterial Renal Segments
What are renal end-arteries and why do they matter clinically?
tap to flip
Answer
Renal artery branches that do NOT anastomose with adjacent branches. Segments:
• Superior
• Anterosuperior
• Anteroinferior
• Inferior
• Posterior

Clinical: Occlusion → segmental infarction with no collateral flow. Important during renal surgery/partial nephrectomy planning.
LO 6 — Blood Supply
Which artery supplies each GI division: foregut, midgut, hindgut? Give a structure served by each.
tap to flip
Answer
Celiac trunk: foregut (stomach, duodenum 1st/2nd, liver, spleen, pancreas)
SMA: midgut (duodenum 3rd/4th → proximal 2/3 transverse colon)
IMA: hindgut (distal 1/3 transverse colon → upper rectum)
LO 7 — Autonomics
Compare parasympathetic vs. sympathetic effects on GI motility, secretion, and sphincter tone.
tap to flip
Answer
Parasympathetic (vagus/pelvic nerve): ↑motility, ↑secretion, ↓sphincter tone ("rest and digest")

Sympathetic (splanchnic nerves): ↓motility, ↓secretion, ↑sphincter tone, vasoconstriction ("fight or flight")
LO 7 — Cephalic Phase
What is the cephalic phase of digestion, what nerve mediates it, and what does it prepare the stomach to do?
tap to flip
Answer
Cephalic phase: sight, smell, taste, or thought of food triggers anticipatory GI activation BEFORE food arrives

Mediated by: vagus nerve (CN X) → ACh → stimulates parietal cells (HCl), chief cells (pepsinogen), gastric motility

Prepares the stomach to receive and begin digesting food

Test Trap Mode: Each statement is either TRUE or FALSE as written. Many are designed to catch common exam errors. Click T or F — then read the explanation carefully.
Trap 01 — LO 1
"The pancreas and duodenum are both secondary retroperitoneal organs because they fused to the posterior abdominal wall during development."
FALSE — PARTIALLY. The pancreas HEAD & BODY are PRIMARY retroperitoneal (never moved). The pancreas TAIL is secondary (fused). The duodenum PARTS 2-4 are PRIMARY; only the 1st part is secondary.
Trap 02 — LO 2
"The left renal vein is shorter than the right renal vein because the left kidney is positioned higher in the retroperitoneum."
FALSE. The left renal vein is LONGER because it passes anterior to the aorta (between SMA & aorta) to reach the IVC. The right renal vein is short — it goes directly into the IVC. Kidney position is similar; the difference is the left vein's anterior course. Nutcracker syndrome occurs when the left renal vein is compressed in the SMA-aorta angle.
Trap 03 — LO 3
"The phrenic nerve originates from the thoracic spine (T3-T5) and thus is called an 'abdominal' nerve."
FALSE. The phrenic nerve originates from CERVICAL spinal levels C3, C4, C5 (not thoracic). This is why the mnemonic is "C3, 4, 5 keeps the diaphragm alive." The phrenic nerve descends through the neck and chest to innervate the diaphragm — explaining referred pain to the shoulder (C4 dermatome) in diaphragmatic pathology.
Trap 04 — LO 4
"A patient with a kidney stone at the UPJ will experience referred pain in the lower back, but NOT in the groin because the pain only follows the dermatome of the kidney (T12)."
FALSE. Ureteric pain follows the ENTIRE path of ureter innervation (T11-L2), so it radiates from the LOIN (flank, T11-12) to the GROIN (L1-2 dermatomes). This "loin to groin" radiation is classic for urolithiasis at any level, not just the UPJ.
Trap 05 — LO 4
"Because the ureter passes above the uterine artery in females, surgeons can safely ligate the uterine artery during hysterectomy without risking ureteric injury."
FALSE. This is a classic surgical trap! The uterine artery passes ABOVE the ureter ("water under the bridge") — meaning the ureter is BELOW. During hysterectomy, if the surgeon doesn't carefully identify the ureter, they risk injuring it while ligating the uterine artery. "Water under the bridge" = ureter passes UNDER (inferior to) the artery.
Trap 06 — LO 5
"The adrenal cortex is highly innervated by sympathetic nerve fibers, allowing rapid hormonal responses during the 'fight or flight' response."
FALSE. The adrenal CORTEX has NO direct nerve innervation. It is controlled solely by endocrine signals (ACTH from the pituitary, angiotensin II from RAAS). The MEDULLA is directly innervated by preganglionic sympathetic fibers. This is a common point of confusion between cortex and medulla.
Trap 07 — LO 5
"The adrenal medulla is a true endocrine gland like the adrenal cortex, producing hormones that are regulated by the anterior pituitary."
FALSE. The medulla is embryologically a modified POSTGANGLIONIC NEURON (neuroendocrine), not a true endocrine gland. It receives direct preganglionic sympathetic innervation (T5-T11) and releases catecholamines (epinephrine/norepinephrine) upon nerve stimulation — it's basically a neuron that secretes hormones. The anterior pituitary does NOT regulate the medulla.
Trap 08 — LO 2
"Because the celiac trunk, SMA, and IMA all branch from the abdominal aorta, they have extensive collateral circulation with each other, so occlusion of one rarely causes intestinal ischemia."
FALSE. While some marginal collateral vessels exist (e.g., arc of Riolan between SMA & IMA), these are often inadequate. Occlusion of the SMA (acute mesenteric ischemia) causes severe malperfusion despite collaterals. The renal arteries are END-ARTERIES with no collaterals — occlusion = segmental infarction. Always suspect ischemia when major vessels are occluded.

Adrenal Cortex Control — NO DIRECT NERVE INNERVATION: Zona glomerulosa = RAAS (angiotensin II); Zona fasciculata = ACTH (anterior pituitary); Zona reticularis = ACTH + androgen-regulating hormones. This is unique among endocrine organs — purely hormonal control.
Adrenal Medulla: Embryologically modified postganglionic sympathetic neurons. Upon stimulation by preganglionic sympathetic fibers (T5-T11), releases epinephrine (~80%) & norepinephrine (~20%). This is the ONLY endocrine tissue with direct somatic motor control.
