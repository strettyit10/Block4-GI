Question 1 · LO 3 — Pancreas
A 62-year-old man presents to the clinic with a 3-month history of painless, progressive jaundice and unintentional weight loss of 15 lb. On examination, his abdomen is soft but you palpate a smooth, non-tender, and markedly distended gallbladder (Courvoisier sign). Laboratories show conjugated hyperbilirubinemia and mildly elevated transaminases. Abdominal ultrasound shows dilation of the common bile duct with no evidence of gallstones. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?
A
Acute pancreatitis from gallstone migration
B
Benign common bile duct stricture
C
Pancreatic head adenocarcinoma
D
Primary biliary cholangitis
E
Ampullary carcinoma with tumor necrosis
Correct Answer: C — Pancreatic Head Adenocarcinoma

Courvoisier sign (palpable, non-tender gallbladder with painless jaundice and weight loss) is a classic presentation of pancreatic head cancer. The expanding tumor obstructs the distal common bile duct, causing the gallbladder to dilate gradually. The key phrase is "painless" jaundice—acute pancreatitis and cholangitis are typically painful. Absence of gallstones on imaging excludes choledocholithiasis.

Distractor dismissals:
A — Acute pancreatitis causes sudden epigastric pain, elevated lipase, and would show a smaller, contracted gallbladder (not dilated).
B — BCD strictures are rare and do not produce systemic weight loss or palpable gallbladder.
D — PBC causes progressive cholestatic liver injury; it is not obstructive and does not present with abdominal mass.
E — Ampullary tumors obstruct at the ampulla; while possible, pancreatic head cancer is far more common with this constellation.
Question 2 · LO 1 — Hepatobiliary
A 57-year-old woman with known gallstones presents with acute onset right upper quadrant pain, fever (101.5°F), and progressive jaundice over the past 12 hours. Laboratory studies show conjugated hyperbilirubinemia (total bili 6.2, direct 5.1), elevated alkaline phosphatase, and mildly elevated transaminases. Abdominal ultrasound reveals a dilated common bile duct with an echogenic focus and no bile ductal pneumatosis. Which of the following is the most appropriate next step in management?
A
Stat laparoscopic cholecystectomy and intraoperative choledochotomy
B
Oral ursodeoxycholic acid and supportive care
C
ERCP with sphincterotomy and stone extraction
D
MRCP for confirmation, then delayed ERCP in 1 week
E
Percutaneous transhepatic cholangiography only
Correct Answer: C — ERCP with Sphincterotomy

Charcot triad (fever, jaundice, RUQ pain) indicates ascending cholangitis, a medical emergency. ERCP is both diagnostic AND therapeutic—it removes the obstructing stone and decompresses the biliary tree. Prompt ERCP within 12–24 hours improves outcomes and reduces mortality in acute cholangitis.

Distractor dismissals:
A — Acute cholangitis requires decompression first; surgical intervention is delayed until infection resolves.
B — Medical therapy alone is insufficient for an acutely infected, obstructed biliary system.
D — While MRCP can confirm diagnosis, ERCP should not be delayed in active infection; perform ERCP immediately if clinically suspected.
E — PTC is reserved for cases where ERCP fails or anatomy precludes endoscopic access.
Question 3 · LO 1 — Hepatobiliary
A 64-year-old woman with a history of multiple prior cholangitis episodes presents with fever, jaundice, RUQ pain, hypotension (78/42), and altered mental status. Blood cultures are pending. She is already on broad-spectrum antibiotics. What is the most appropriate next step?
A
Initiate vasopressors and aggressive fluid resuscitation only
B
Urgent ERCP with biliary decompression; continue antibiotics and vasopressor support
C
Obtain MRCP and schedule elective ERCP in 48 hours
D
Supportive care with hydration and antibiotics; surgery consult only if deterioration
E
Percutaneous transhepatic cholangiography and external drainage only
Correct Answer: B — Urgent ERCP with Biliary Decompression

Reynold pentad (Charcot triad + hypotension + altered mental status) represents suppurative cholangitis with septic shock. This is a surgical emergency requiring immediate biliary decompression to source control the infection. ERCP is the gold standard; if it fails or anatomy is unfavorable, PTC or surgery may be needed. Supportive care alone is insufficient.

Distractor dismissals:
A — Hemodynamic support is necessary but NOT sufficient; the infected, obstructed biliary tree must be decompressed.
C — Delayed management in septic shock is unacceptable; requires urgent intervention.
D — Conservative care risks progression to irreversible septic shock and multi-organ failure.
E — PTC may be needed if ERCP fails, but ERCP is first-line and faster.
Question 4 · LO 3 — Pancreas
A 45-year-old woman with a history of cholelithiasis and obesity presents with acute onset epigastric pain radiating to the back, worse with food intake. Vital signs show tachycardia (HR 102). Laboratory findings include lipase 580 U/L (normal <160) and amylase 420 U/L. Abdominal ultrasound shows multiple gallstones within the gallbladder and no evidence of gallbladder wall thickening or pericholecystic fluid. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?
A
Acute cholecystitis with biliary colic
B
Gallstone-induced acute pancreatitis
C
Peptic ulcer disease with gastric perforation
D
Alcoholic pancreatitis with incidental gallstones
E
Sphincter of Oddi dysfunction
Correct Answer: B — Gallstone-Induced Acute Pancreatitis

The combination of known cholelithiasis, epigastric pain radiating to the back, and marked elevation of lipase are classic for gallstone pancreatitis. Gallstones (usually <6 mm) migrate into the common bile duct, obstruct the ampulla, and trigger enzymatic inflammation of the pancreatic parenchyma. Risk factors: female, fat, forty, fertile.

Distractor dismissals:
A — Acute cholecystitis presents with RUQ pain and imaging signs (wall thickening, pericholecystic fluid); lipase would not be this elevated.
C — PUD causes epigastric pain but lipase and amylase would be normal; typically associated with H. pylori or NSAIDs.
D — Alcoholic pancreatitis is possible, but no alcohol history is provided; gallstones are the more likely trigger given the imaging.
E — SOD causes biliary dyskinesia; it does not cause acute pancreatitis with such marked enzyme elevation.
Question 5 · LO 3 — Pancreas
A 55-year-old man with a 20-year history of heavy alcohol use presents with chronic epigastric pain, steatorrhea, and newly diagnosed diabetes mellitus. CT imaging of the abdomen shows pancreatic calcifications and atrophy. Which of the following best explains his steatorrhea?
A
Increased intestinal motility due to chronic inflammation
B
Loss of pancreatic exocrine function and reduced pancreatic enzyme secretion
C
Bile salt malabsorption from liver cirrhosis
D
Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth from reduced gastric acid
E
Increased fecal loss of lipoproteins
Correct Answer: B — Loss of Pancreatic Exocrine Function

Chronic pancreatitis (from prolonged alcohol use) destroys pancreatic acini, reducing lipase and other digestive enzyme secretion. Without sufficient pancreatic lipase, dietary fat cannot be emulsified and absorbed—resulting in steatorrhea. Concurrent islet cell loss causes diabetes ("pancreatogenic diabetes").

Distractor dismissals:
A — Increased motility would cause diarrhea, not specifically steatorrhea; pancreatic insufficiency is the primary problem.
C — Liver cirrhosis can cause malabsorption, but the imaging shows pancreatic pathology, not liver disease.
D — SIBO could worsen malabsorption but does not directly cause loss of fat-soluble nutrient absorption.
E — Lipoprotein loss is not a mechanism of steatorrhea in pancreatic insufficiency.
Question 6 · LO 2 — Splenic Blood Supply & Trauma
A 28-year-old male is brought to the ED after a motor vehicle collision. On secondary survey, he has left-sided abdominal pain and left shoulder pain (referred pain). Blood pressure is 95/60 mmHg, heart rate 118 bpm. Focused abdominal ultrasound confirms free fluid in the peritoneal cavity. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?
A
Liver laceration with retroperitoneal hemorrhage
B
Splenic rupture with intraperitoneal hemorrhage
C
Renal contusion with perirenal hematoma
D
Pancreatic ductal injury with fluid collection
E
Small bowel perforation with peritonitis
Correct Answer: B — Splenic Rupture

Kehr sign (left shoulder pain from diaphragmatic irritation) in the context of LUQ trauma and hemodynamic instability is pathognomonic for splenic rupture. Free intraperitoneal fluid on ultrasound confirms hemorrhage. The splenic vessels are large and bleed rapidly; hemodynamic instability indicates urgent need for transfusion and definitive intervention (operative or angiographic embolization).

Distractor dismissals:
A — Liver injury would cause RUQ tenderness, not LUQ with shoulder pain.
C — Renal injury presents with CVA tenderness and hematuria; does not cause shoulder pain.
D — Pancreatic injury is rare in blunt trauma and does not cause acute hemorrhage or Kehr sign.
E — Bowel perforation causes peritonitis with rigid abdomen; hemodynamic changes are from sepsis, not hemorrhage.
Question 7 · LO 2 — Splenic Blood Supply & Trauma
A 7-year-old child was splenectomized 6 months ago after a splenic rupture from blunt trauma. He now presents to the ED with fulminant sepsis, fever (40.2°C), and mental status changes. Rapid bloodwork shows a WBC of 28,000 with a left shift and a blood smear revealing many immature neutrophils. The patient expires despite aggressive antimicrobial therapy. Which organism was most likely responsible for this overwhelming post-splenic infection?
A
Streptococcus pneumoniae
B
Haemophilus influenzae
C
Neisseria meningitidis
D
Mycoplasma pneumoniae
E
Chlamydia trachomatis
Correct Answer: A — Streptococcus pneumoniae

OPSI (Overwhelming Post-Splenic Infection) is a catastrophic complication of splenectomy, most commonly caused by encapsulated bacteria: S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae type B (Hib), N. meningitidis. The mnemonic is SHiN. S. pneumoniae accounts for ~50% of cases. The spleen is critical for opsonization and clearance of polysaccharide-coated organisms; asplenic patients lose this protection and develop fulminant bacteremia.

Distractor dismissals:
B, C — Also possible encapsulated organisms but less common than pneumococcus.
D, E — Atypical organisms; do not cause classic OPSI in asplenic patients.
Question 8 · LO 2 — Splenic Blood Supply & Trauma
A 62-year-old woman with a history of splenic infarction (from sickle cell disease in childhood) has compensated asplenia. Her CBC shows mild thrombocytosis (550,000/μL). A peripheral blood smear reveals small, round, basophilic inclusions in red blood cells. What are these inclusions?
A
Howell-Jolly bodies (nuclear remnants)
B
Heinz bodies (denatured hemoglobin)
C
Pappenheimer bodies (iron deposits)
D
Cabot rings (remnant microfilaments)
E
Target cells from hemolysis
Correct Answer: A — Howell-Jolly Bodies

Howell-Jolly bodies are nuclear remnants (fragments of DNA) in RBCs. The spleen normally removes them ("culling"). In asplenia or functional asplenia, these inclusions persist in circulating RBCs. Their presence on blood smear is a marker of asplenia. Associated findings include target cells, spherocytes, and thrombocytosis.

Distractor dismissals:
B — Heinz bodies are denatured hemoglobin (seen in G6PD deficiency); they are more diffuse and stain differently.
C — Pappenheimer bodies are siderotic granules; seen in sideroblastic anemia.
D — Cabot rings are microfilament remnants; rare and not specific to asplenia.
E — Target cells are due to low hemoglobin-to-surface ratio; not pathognomonic for asplenia.
Question 9 · LO 2 — Splenic Blood Supply & Trauma
A 51-year-old man with pancreatitis presents with hematemesis. Upper endoscopy reveals isolated gastric varices at the fundus, with no esophageal varices. Portal vein pressure gradient is normal. CT angiography shows thrombosis of the splenic vein. Which of the following best explains the varices?
A
Portal vein thrombosis with portosystemic collateralization
B
Sinistral portal hypertension due to diverted splenic flow via short gastric vessels
C
Hepatic vein thrombosis causing centroacinar hypoxia
D
Acute alcoholic hepatitis with elevated sinusoidal pressure
E
Primary biliary cholangitis causing cirrhosis
Correct Answer: B — Sinistral Portal Hypertension

Splenic vein thrombosis (often from pancreatitis) causes blood to back up into the spleen and then preferentially drain via short gastric and left gastroepiploic vessels into the fundus of the stomach. This creates localized (sinistral) hypertension in the gastric venous system—hence isolated gastric fundal varices WITHOUT esophageal varices. Portal pressure gradient remains normal because the main portal vein is unaffected.

Distractor dismissals:
A — PVT would cause esophageal varices; sinistral HTN causes fundal varices only.
C — HVT (Budd-Chiari) causes hepatomegaly and ascites; not typically isolated gastric varices.
D, E — Would cause systemic portal HTN (esophageal + gastric varices) and liver dysfunction.
Question 10 · LO 1 — Hepatobiliary
During a laparoscopic cholecystectomy, the surgeon identifies a small artery within Calot's triangle that is inadvertently ligated. The patient develops minimal bleeding but later experiences right upper quadrant pain and a small collection of bile in the operative bed. Which structure was most likely injured?
A
Cystic artery
B
Right hepatic artery
C
Right gastric artery
D
Cystic duct
E
Hepatocystic triangle (hepatic artery proper)
Correct Answer: A — Cystic Artery

Calot's triangle is bounded by the cystic artery (arises from right hepatic artery), the common hepatic duct, and the cystic duct. The cystic artery is the smallest vessel in this space and is the safest structure to ligate during cholecystectomy. Ligation causes minimal bleeding (small caliber) but can lead to ischemic bile duct stricture or bile leaks if the duct is also inadvertently injured.

Distractor dismissals:
B — RHA injury would cause massive hemorrhage and hepatic ischemia; would not be a "small" bleeding event.
C — Not a normal anatomical structure in Calot's triangle.
D — Cystic duct injury causes bile leak but not the described artery injury.
E — Proper hepatic artery ligation would result in massive hepatic necrosis; not compatible with survival.
Question 11 · LO 1 — Hepatobiliary
A 68-year-old woman with longstanding cholelithiasis presents with progressive jaundice and elevated alkaline phosphatase. Ultrasound shows a stone impacted in the cystic duct with dilation of the intrahepatic bile ducts, but the common bile duct is normal in caliber. MRCP shows no filling defect in the CBD. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?
A
Choledocholithiasis with impacted stone in the ampulla
B
Mirizzi syndrome (extrinsic CBD compression)
C
Malignancy of the pancreatic head
D
Intrahepatic cholestasis of pregnancy
E
Primary sclerosing cholangitis
Correct Answer: B — Mirizzi Syndrome

Mirizzi syndrome occurs when a stone in the cystic duct or gallbladder neck causes external compression of the common hepatic duct. This results in jaundice and cholestasis WITHOUT an obstructing stone in the CBD itself (hence normal CBD caliber on imaging and no stone on MRCP). Intrahepatic ducts dilate due to proximal obstruction. Treatment is cholecystectomy (which also removes the compressing stone).

Distractor dismissals:
A — Choledocholithiasis would show a filling defect in the CBD on MRCP.
C — Pancreatic cancer would present with painless jaundice but not a cystic duct stone; would show CBD dilation.
D, E — Do not have a stone impacted in the cystic duct; different etiologies of hyperbilirubinemia.
Question 12 · LO 4 — Anatomy-Physiology Match
A patient eats a high-fat meal. Which hormone, released from which cell type, is primarily responsible for contraction of the gallbladder and increased secretion of pancreatic digestive enzymes?
A
Gastrin from G-cells of the antrum
B
Cholecystokinin (CCK) from I-cells of the duodenum
C
Secretin from S-cells of the jejunum
D
Motilin from M-cells of the duodenum
E
Enteroglucagon from L-cells of the ileum
Correct Answer: B — Cholecystokinin (CCK)

CCK is released from I-cells in response to intraluminal fat and amino acids. It has dual actions: (1) stimulates gallbladder contraction (muscle contraction), and (2) stimulates pancreatic acinar cells to secrete digestive enzymes (lipase, amylase, protease). Together, these coordinate the delivery of digestive juices with nutrients for optimal digestion.

Distractor dismissals:
A — Gastrin stimulates acid secretion; minimal gallbladder effect.
C — Secretin stimulates bicarbonate secretion from the pancreas (not enzymes); no gallbladder effect.
D — Motilin controls inter-digestive motility (MMC); not a postprandial hormone.
E — Enteroglucagon does not regulate gallbladder or pancreatic enzyme secretion.
Question 13 · LO 4 — Anatomy-Physiology Match
In the duodenum, which enzyme is activated first and then catalyzes the activation of all other pancreatic zymogens?
A
Lipase (activated by colipase)
B
Amylase (activated by enterokinase)
C
Trypsinogen → trypsin (via enterokinase)
D
Phospholipase A2 (spontaneously activated)
E
Carboxypeptidase (activated by gastrin)
Correct Answer: C — Trypsinogen → Trypsin (Master Activator)

Enterokinase (a serine protease on duodenal brush border) cleaves trypsinogen to trypsin. Once activated, trypsin becomes the master activator—it activates all other pancreatic zymogens (chymotrypsinogen, proelastase, procarboxypeptidases, and additional trypsinogen molecules through positive feedback). This is why pancreatic insufficiency (loss of trypsin) causes severe maldigestion of all macronutrients.

Distractor dismissals:
A — Lipase is activated by colipase (a cofactor), not enterokinase.
B — Amylase is secreted in active form; enterokinase does not activate amylase.
D — Phospholipase A2 activation requires trypsin.
E — Carboxypeptidase activation requires trypsin; not activated by gastrin.
Question 14 · LO 4 — Anatomy-Physiology Match
A patient with Crohn's disease requiring resection of the terminal ileum develops fat-soluble vitamin malabsorption and night blindness (dark adaptation impairment). Which of the following best explains the mechanism?
A
Loss of gastric intrinsic factor leading to vitamin B12 deficiency
B
Reduced pancreatic lipase production from chronic inflammation
C
Loss of bile salt reabsorption site; reduced enterohepatic circulation of bile salts
D
Overgrowth of gram-negative bacteria causing lipid peroxidation
E
Surgical ischemia of the liver causing reduced bile production
Correct Answer: C — Loss of Enterohepatic Circulation

The terminal ileum is the primary site of bile salt reabsorption. Upon resection (in Crohn's), bile salts are not recycled back to the liver via the enterohepatic circulation. This results in: (1) reduced bile salt pool (fewer micelles for fat emulsification), and (2) increased hepatic synthesis to compensate, drawing down cholesterol and lipid-soluble vitamin absorption. Night blindness reflects vitamin A deficiency. Fatty stools (steatorrhea) result from impaired fat emulsification and absorption.

Distractor dismissals:
A — Terminal ileum is not a site of intrinsic factor production; that occurs in the stomach.
B — Crohn's primarily affects the small bowel mucosa, not pancreatic parenchyma.
D — While bacterial overgrowth can occur, it is not the primary mechanism of vitamin malabsorption post-ileal resection.
E — Surgical resection does not cause liver ischemia or reduce bile production.
Question 15 · LO 1 — Hepatobiliary
A 40-year-old patient with a hypercoagulable disorder develops hepatic vein thrombosis causing ischemic hepatitis and congestion. On imaging, the liver shows a mottled pattern (nutmeg appearance). Interestingly, the caudate lobe appears to be spared. Which of the following best explains this sparing of the caudate lobe?
A
Caudate lobe is supplied by the left hepatic artery exclusively
B
Caudate lobe has direct venous drainage into the IVC; bypasses hepatic vein thrombosis
C
Caudate lobe develops collateral circulation around the occluded hepatic vein
D
Caudate lobe is innervated by the vagus nerve; maintains metabolic activity
E
Portal vein perfusion of the caudate lobe is preserved
Correct Answer: B — Direct Venous Drainage to IVC

The caudate lobe is embryologically distinct (from the dorsal hepatic mesentery). Its hepatic venules drain directly into the retrohepatic IVC, bypassing the major hepatic veins. When hepatic vein thrombosis occurs (in Budd-Chiari syndrome), the caudate lobe is spared because its venous drainage is unaffected. This allows the caudate to hypertrophy and take on compensatory function. Meanwhile, the rest of the liver becomes congested and atrophic, creating the "nutmeg" appearance of mixed hemorrhage and necrosis.

Distractor dismissals:
A — Caudate lobe is supplied by both hepatic arteries; not exclusively left.
C — Collateral circulation is slow to develop; acute Budd-Chiari does not rely on collaterals for the caudate.
D — Neural supply does not explain preservation of hepatic architecture.
E — Portal perfusion alone does not protect against hepatic congestion from venous obstruction.
Question 16 · LO 1 — Hepatobiliary
During hepatic surgery, the surgeon performs a Pringle maneuver by cross-clamping the hepatoduodenal ligament. Which of the following structures are compressed, and which one is NOT part of the contents of this ligament?
A
Hepatic artery (all), Portal vein, Common bile duct
B
Hepatic artery (right only), Splenic vein, Cystic artery
C
Bile duct (hepatic portion), Hepatic artery (proper), Portal vein
D
Common hepatic duct, Proper hepatic artery, Portal vein
E
All of the above contain correct structures
Correct Answer: C — Contains All Correct Structures

The hepatoduodenal ligament is part of the hepatogastric ligament and contains BD HAP: Bile duct, hepatic artery (proper), Portal vein. The Pringle maneuver compresses this entire structure, temporarily stopping blood flow to the liver to control hepatic hemorrhage. Wait—let me re-examine the options: Option C lists bile duct (hepatic portion), hepatic artery (proper), and portal vein—these are the correct contents of the hepatoduodenal ligament. This is the correct answer. The mnemonic is BD HAP (standing for Bile Duct, Hepatic Artery, Portal vein) from anterior to posterior in the ligament.

Distractor dismissals:
A — Splenic vein is NOT in hepatoduodenal ligament; it drains into portal vein elsewhere.
B — Cystic artery is not a major structure of this ligament.
D — Common hepatic duct (rather than proper) is technically part of the system but the answer C is more precise.
Question 17 · LO 3 — Pancreas
A 58-year-old man undergoes a pancreaticoduodenectomy (Whipple procedure) for pancreatic head adenocarcinoma. Which of the following organs is NOT resected during this operation?
A
Pancreatic head and neck
B
Duodenum (entire) and proximal jejunum
C
Distal body and tail of pancreas
D
Distal common bile duct and gallbladder
E
Antrum of the stomach (optional)
Correct Answer: C — Distal Body & Tail of Pancreas

The Whipple procedure (pancreaticoduodenectomy) resects the pancreatic head (which lies in the C-loop of the duodenum), the entire duodenum, the proximal jejunum, the distal CBD and gallbladder, and optionally the gastric antrum. The distal pancreas (body and tail) is NOT removed—it is preserved to maintain endocrine and remaining exocrine function. The pancreatic body and tail blood supply is separate (from splenic vessels) and can be preserved.

Distractor dismissals:
A, B, D, E — All are correctly included in the Whipple procedure; only the distal pancreas is preserved.
Question 18 · LO 3 — Pancreas
A 3-day-old newborn presents with projectile vomiting shortly after feeding. Abdominal imaging shows the classic "double bubble" sign (air in the stomach and proximal duodenum, but absent distal duodenal and small bowel gas). Upper GI series shows compression of the second portion of the duodenum. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?
A
Pyloric stenosis from hypertrophic muscle
B
Duodenal atresia (complete web)
C
Annular pancreas compressing the duodenum
D
Malrotation with midgut volvulus
E
Meconium ileus from cystic fibrosis
Correct Answer: C — Annular Pancreas

Annular pancreas is a congenital anomaly where pancreatic tissue wraps around and compresses the second portion of the duodenum, causing obstruction. Presents in the neonatal period with projectile vomiting and the classic "double bubble" sign. Diagnosis is by imaging; treatment is surgical duodenoduodenostomy to bypass the obstruction.

Distractor dismissals:
A — Pyloric stenosis presents with obstruction of the pylorus, not the duodenum; different mechanism and demographics.
B — Duodenal atresia would show complete absence of distal intestinal gas; annular pancreas is a stricture (not complete occlusion).
D — Malrotation + volvulus presents with acute bilious vomiting and peritoneal signs; more acute than annular pancreas.
E — Meconium ileus presents later (after days of age) with abdominal distention and meconium-filled distal ileum.
Question 19 · LO 3 — Pancreas
A 32-year-old man with recurrent acute pancreatitis (no gallstones, no significant alcohol use) undergoes MRCP for evaluation. The study reveals that the majority of the pancreatic parenchyma drains via the accessory duct into the duodenum at the minor papilla, with only a small portion draining via the main duct into the major papilla. Which developmental anomaly explains these findings?
A
Annular pancreas restricting flow
B
Pancreas divisum (failed fusion of ventral and dorsal ducts)
C
Pancreatic hypoplasia causing duct narrowing
D
Sphincter of Oddi dysfunction causing reflux
E
Duodenal diverticulum compressing the main duct
Correct Answer: B — Pancreas Divisum

Pancreas divisum results from failure of fusion of the ventral (from ventral pancreatic bud) and dorsal (from dorsal pancreatic bud) pancreatic ducts during embryogenesis. In this anomaly, the majority of pancreatic tissue (dorsal pancreas) drains via the accessory (minor) duct, which has a small opening and is prone to obstruction. This can lead to recurrent pancreatitis. The main (ventral) duct drains a small portion. Treatment is ERCP with minor papilla sphincterotomy to improve accessory duct drainage.

Distractor dismissals:
A — Annular pancreas restricts blood flow and duodenal passage, not ductal anatomy.
C — Pancreatic hypoplasia does not cause duct fusion failure.
D — SOD causes pain but not anatomical ductal separation.
E — Duodenal diverticulum is rare and would not explain bilateral duct anatomy.
Question 20 · LO 1 — Hepatobiliary
A patient with suspected common bile duct stone presents for diagnostic imaging. Which of the following is the diagnostic gold standard for detecting CBD stones, and which modality is both diagnostic AND therapeutic?
A
Abdominal ultrasound (most sensitive for biliary pathology)
B
MRCP only (diagnostic, non-invasive)
C
ERCP (both diagnostic and therapeutic via sphincterotomy and stone extraction)
D
CT with IV contrast (best sensitivity and specificity)
E
PTC (percutaneous access)
Correct Answer: C — ERCP (Diagnostic + Therapeutic)

ERCP (Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography) is the gold standard diagnostic AND therapeutic modality for CBD stone disease. It allows direct visualization of the biliary tree via endoscope, confirms the presence and location of stones, and enables immediate treatment (sphincterotomy, stone extraction, stent placement). While MRCP is non-invasive and excellent for diagnosis, it cannot be used for intervention. ERCP carries small risks (pancreatitis, perforation) but is justified when therapeutic intervention is anticipated.

Distractor dismissals:
A — Ultrasound is excellent for gallbladder stones and ductal dilation but has limited sensitivity for CBD stones (30–50%).
B — MRCP is highly accurate (>90%) for diagnosis but cannot remove stones or perform sphincterotomy.
D — CT is good for pancreatic and hepatic evaluation but not the gold standard for CBD stones.
E — PTC is reserved for cases where ERCP fails (altered anatomy, duodenal obstruction).
