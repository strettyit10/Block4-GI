LO 1 — Saliva Sources
What are the three major salivary glands, and which produces serous vs. mucous vs. mixed secretion?
tap to flip
Answer
Parotid glands: serous (α-amylase for starch digestion)
Submandibular & sublingual: mixed (mucous + serous components)

Parotid = 25% of saliva; submandibular = 70%; sublingual = 5%
LO 1 — Saliva Secretion Mechanism
Describe the two-stage model of salivary secretion and how it produces hypotonic saliva at low flow rates.
tap to flip
Answer
Stage 1: Acinar cells secrete isotonic NaCl + water
Stage 2: Ductal cells reabsorb Na+ & Cl− (electroneutral transporter) while secreting K+ & HCO₃−

Result: Hypotonic at low flow (more time for reabsorption); approaches isotonic at high flow
LO 2 — Unique Autonomic Control of Saliva
Why is salivary secretion unique in its autonomic control? How do SNS and PSNS differ in their effects?
tap to flip
Answer
UNIQUE: BOTH SNS & PSNS stimulate salivation (not opposing as in most organs)
PSNS dominant: M3 receptors → IP3/Ca2+ → watery, enzyme-rich saliva
SNS brief: α1/β receptors → thick, mucous saliva

Test trap: Don't assume SNS inhibits!
LO 2 — Saliva Functions
List the four major functions of saliva and one protein component for each.
tap to flip
Answer
1) Digestion: α-amylase (starch), lingual lipase (fat)
2) Antimicrobial: lysozyme, lactoferrin, sIgA
3) Buffering: HCO₃− neutralizes reflux acid
4) Lubrication: mucins (glycoproteins)

Also: EGF, kallikrein, R-proteins (haptocorrin for B12)
LO 4 — Satiety & Ghrelin
What is ghrelin, where is it produced, and what is its relationship to appetite?
tap to flip
Answer
Ghrelin: peptide hormone from stomach fundus (X/A cells)
Function: "hunger signal" — ↑appetite, triggers meal initiation
Timing: peaks BEFORE meals (anticipatory)

Mnemonic: "Growing" = ghrelin stimulates growth of appetite
LO 4 — Leptin & Obesity
What is leptin, how does obesity affect leptin levels, and what is leptin resistance?
tap to flip
Answer
Leptin: satiety hormone from adipocytes; long-term appetite suppression
In obesity: leptin levels are HIGH (↑fat = ↑leptin)
Leptin resistance: cells unresponsive to elevated leptin despite high serum levels

Test trap: Leptin is HIGH in obesity, not low
LO 4 — Post-Prandial Satiety Hormones
Name three hormones that signal satiety after a meal and their primary stimuli.
tap to flip
Answer
CCK (I cells): ↑ after fat & protein; short-term satiety
PYY 3-36 (L cells, ileum): post-prandial; ileal brake effect
GLP-1 (L cells): incretin effect + satiety; agonists = semaglutide, liraglutide

Also: insulin (acute satiety)
LO 5 — Parietal Cell Secretion
What two products do parietal cells secrete, and what is the clinical significance of each?
tap to flip
Answer
1) HCl: denatures proteins, activates pepsinogen → pepsin
2) Intrinsic factor (IF): required for B12 (cobalamin) absorption in ILEUM

Location: fundus & body of stomach
Loss of IF → pernicious anemia (autoimmune) or achlorhydria
LO 5 — Parietal Cell Acid Mechanism
Explain the mechanism of HCl secretion by parietal cells. What is the rate-limiting step?
tap to flip
Answer
Mechanism: CO₂ + H₂O → (carbonic anhydrase) → H₂CO₃ → H+ + HCO₃−
Rate-limiting: H+/K+ ATPase (apical membrane) pumps H+ into canaliculus
Cl− via apical channel; HCO₃− exits via basolateral Cl−/HCO₃− antiporter

Test trap: Rate-limiting = ATPase, NOT carbonic anhydrase
LO 5 — Acid Stimulation: "HAG"
Name three stimuli for parietal cell acid secretion and how they are synergistic.
tap to flip
Answer
Mnemonic "HAG":
H = Histamine (from ECL cells) via H2 receptors
A = ACh (vagus, M3) from vagal neurons
G = Gastrin (from G cells) via CCK-B receptors

Synergistic: all three amplify acid secretion; H2 blockers & PPIs interrupt this
LO 6 — Three Phases of Gastric Secretion
Name the three phases of gastric secretion, the mechanism of each, and approximate % contribution to total HCl.
tap to flip
Answer
1) Cephalic: 30–40%; vagus (CNX) → ACh → stimulates before food arrival
2) Gastric: 50–60%; gastric distension + gastrin from G cells
3) Intestinal: 5–10%; MOSTLY INHIBITORY (secretin, PYY, GLP-1)

Key: intestinal phase suppresses further gastric acid
LO 6 — Somatostatin Inhibition
What is somatostatin? Where are D cells located, and what does somatostatin inhibit?
tap to flip
Answer
Somatostatin: hormone from D cells (stomach antrum & duodenum)
Inhibits EVERYTHING: ↓HCl, ↓pepsinogen, ↓gastrin, ↓pancreatic enzymes, ↓pancreatic HCO₃−, ↓small intestine motility

Mechanism: paracrine (direct local effect) & endocrine
Test trap: Somatostatin = "brake on digestion"
LO 7 — H. pylori Infection Patterns
Compare antral vs. corpus H. pylori infection: location, effect on D cells, and resulting ulcer type.
tap to flip
Answer
Antral infection: damages D cells → ↓somatostatin → ↑gastrin → ↑↑HCl → DUODENAL ulcer
Corpus infection: damages parietal cells → ↓HCl → achlorhydria → GASTRIC ulcer + atrophy

Test trap: Antral H. pylori = counterintuitively leads to duodenal (not gastric) ulcer
LO 7 — H. pylori Virulence
Describe three virulence factors of H. pylori and how they promote infection.
tap to flip
Answer
1) Urease: neutralizes gastric acid locally + damages epithelium
2) VacA toxin: creates vacuoles, causes cell damage
3) CagA protein: injected via type IV secretion; triggers inflammatory cascade

Result: inflammation, epithelial erosion, ulcer formation
LO 8 — Pancreatic Enzyme Secretion
List the major pancreatic digestive enzymes and classify each by substrate (protein, carb, or fat).
tap to flip
Answer
Protein: trypsinogen, chymotrypsinogen, elastase, carboxypeptidase (all zymogens)
Carbs: amylase (active)
Fat: lipase, phospholipase A2

Key: zymogens are inactive → activated by enterokinase (trypsinogen → trypsin) in duodenum
LO 8 — Pancreatic HCO₃⁻ Secretion
What triggers pancreatic HCO₃⁻ secretion, which hormone mediates it, and what is its purpose?
tap to flip
Answer
Stimulus: acidic chyme from stomach
Hormone: Secretin (S cells, duodenum)
Source: ductal cells (via CFTR + Cl−/HCO₃− exchange)
Purpose: neutralize acid, create optimal pH for pancreatic enzymes (pH 7–8)

Contrast: CCK stimulates enzyme secretion
LO 9 — Small Intestine Secretion Overview
What structures in the duodenum and crypts of Lieberkühn secrete, and what do they secrete?
tap to flip
Answer
Brunner's glands (duodenum, submucosal): HCO₃−-rich mucus (protects from acid chyme)
Crypts of Lieberkühn: NaCl + water (via CFTR)
Paneth cells (crypt base): lysozyme + defensins (antimicrobial)

Stimulated by: secretin, VIP, cAMP; heat-stable toxins cause cGMP-mediated diarrhea
LO 10 — Enterokinase Cascade
What is enterokinase? Which enzyme does it activate, and what happens if it's deficient?
tap to flip
Answer
Enterokinase: brush border enzyme in duodenum
Activates: trypsinogen → trypsin (first zymogen to activate)
Trypsin then: autocatalytically activates remaining zymogens

Deficiency → protein malabsorption (zymogens remain inactive)
Test trap: Enterokinase activates trypsinogen (not vice versa)
LO 13 — Colon Secretion & Diarrhea
What do colonic goblet cells and epithelial cells secrete, and how is diarrhea electrolyte loss related?
tap to flip
Answer
Goblet cells: mucus (inner layer sterile, outer layer with commensals) + sIgA
Epithelial cells: HCO₃− (neutralizes SCFA) + K+ (via aldosterone)

In diarrhea: loss of HCO₃− → non-gap metabolic acidosis; loss of K+ → hypokalemia
LO 14 — Secretory vs. Osmotic Diarrhea
Distinguish between secretory and osmotic diarrhea by stool electrolytes and mechanisms.
tap to flip
Answer
Secretory: intestinal epithelium secretes Cl− (water follows); stool osmotic gap = normal; persists during fasting
Osmotic: non-absorbable solute (lactose, MgSO4) draws water into lumen; stool osmotic gap >50; stops with fasting

Example secretory: cholera toxin (↑cAMP), heat-stable toxin (↑cGMP)
LO 11 — Acid-Suppressive Drugs
Compare H2 blockers vs. PPIs: mechanism, potency, and clinical use.
tap to flip
Answer
H2 blockers (cimetidine, famotidine): block H2 receptor on parietal cell; reversible; modest ↓acid
PPIs (omeprazole, pantoprazole): prodrug; activated in acidic canaliculus; IRREVERSIBLY binds H+/K+ ATPase; potent (↓acid 90%)

PPIs superior for GERD, ulcers, Zollinger-Ellison

Test Trap Mode: Each statement is either TRUE or FALSE as written. Many are designed to catch common exam errors. Click T or F — then read the explanation carefully.
Trap 01 — LO 2
"Both sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems inhibit salivation (as in most organs)."
FALSE. Salivation is UNIQUE: BOTH SNS & PSNS STIMULATE it (not antagonistic as in other organs). PSNS dominant (M3 → watery, enzyme-rich); SNS brief (α1/β → thick, mucous). This is a classic test trap.
Trap 02 — LO 3
"Saliva is ISOTONIC at both low and high flow rates because acinar cells produce isotonic secretion."
FALSE. Saliva is HYPOTONIC at low flow (ductal cells have more time to reabsorb Na⁺/Cl⁻) and approaches ISOTONIC at high flow (less time for modification). The ducts are the key to understanding saliva osmolarity.
Trap 03 — LO 4
"Obese patients have HIGH ghrelin levels that drive their continued overeating."
FALSE. This is partially true for leptin resistance but NOT for ghrelin. In obesity: leptin is HIGH (but cells are unresponsive = resistance). Ghrelin is often NORMAL or even LOW. The trap is confusing leptin and ghrelin.
Trap 04 — LO 5
"Carbonic anhydrase is the rate-limiting step in parietal cell HCl production."
FALSE. The H⁺/K⁺ ATPase is the rate-limiting step, not carbonic anhydrase. PPIs target this pump. This is a high-yield trap on standardized exams.
Trap 05 — LO 6
"Antral H. pylori infection damages parietal cells, leading to DUODENALulcer."
FALSE (partially). Antral H. pylori damages D CELLS (not parietal) → ↓somatostatin → ↑gastrin → ↑↑↑ HCl from parietal cells → DUODENAL ulcer. Corpus infection damages parietal cells → GASTRIC ulcer. Antral infection counterintuitively causes duodenal ulcers.
Trap 06 — LO 8
"Trypsin activates trypsinogen in the duodenum, starting the zymogen activation cascade."
FALSE. Enterokinase (brush border) activates trypsinogen → trypsin (first step). Trypsin then autocatalytically activates the other zymogens. Backwards activation is a classic trap.
Trap 07 — LO 9
"At high gastric pH (e.g., during PPI use), pepsinogen is maximally activated."
FALSE. Pepsinogen requires ACIDIC pH <3.5 to be converted to pepsin. High pH (from PPIs) → pepsinogen remains INACTIVE. Over-suppression of acid can impair protein digestion.
Trap 08 — LO 10
"Cystic fibrosis presents with thick pancreatic secretions because the pancreatic acinar cells produce abnormally viscous enzymes."
FALSE. Enzymes are normal; the problem is with DUCTAL CFTR (cystic fibrosis transmembrane regulator). CFTR defect → ↓HCO₃⁻ secretion → thick, acidic secretions (not enough water & alkali to dilute) → duct obstruction → autodigestion → steatorrhea.
Trap 09 — LO 13
"Loss of K⁺ in diarrhea is primarily due to decreased K⁺ intake."
FALSE. Hypokalemia in diarrhea is due to LOSS of K⁺ in stool (colonic epithelial cells secrete K⁺ via aldosterone-driven channels), not decreased intake. The colon normally reabsorbs K⁺; in diarrhea, transit is too rapid.
Trap 10 — LO 14
"Osmotic diarrhea persists even when the patient fasts overnight."
FALSE. Osmotic diarrhea STOPS with fasting because the osmotically active solute is no longer entering the gut. Secretory diarrhea PERSISTS despite fasting (active epithelial Cl⁻ secretion continues). This is the key clinical distinction.
