LO 1 — Blood Supply
State the dual blood supply of the liver: which vessel provides 25%/75% of blood, which provides 75%/50%, and what is the total hepatic blood flow?
tap to flip
Answer
Hepatic artery: 25% of blood volume, 75% of O₂ supply (oxygenated)
Portal vein: 75% of blood volume, 50% of O₂ supply (partially deoxygenated, nutrient-rich)
Total hepatic blood flow: ~1500 mL/min

Unique: liver receives blood from BOTH arterial AND venous sources
LO 1 — Hepatic Acinus
Name the three zones of the hepatic acinus from periportal (Zone 1) to centrilobular (Zone 3). Which zone is most vulnerable to ischemia and which to toxins?
tap to flip
Answer
Zone 1 (periportal): receives blood first (highest O₂) → first-hit toxins (viral hepatitis, yellow fever)
Zone 2 (middle): intermediate oxygenation
Zone 3 (centrilobular/pericentral): receives blood last (lowest O₂) → ischemia-sensitive + CYP-rich (drug metabolism toxicity)

Mnemonic: "Z3 is three ways screwed" — lowest O₂, highest drug toxicity, site of centrilobular necrosis
LO 1 — Kupffer Cells
What are Kupffer cells? Where are they located, and what is their primary function?
tap to flip
Answer
Kupffer cells: resident macrophages of the liver; specialized hepatic sinusoidal endothelial cells
Location: line the hepatic sinusoids (blood-liver interface)
Function: phagocytose bacteria, old RBCs, and cellular debris → immune defense; part of reticuloendothelial system
LO 2 — Clinical Definitions
Define hepatomegaly, jaundice, and cirrhosis.
tap to flip
Answer
Hepatomegaly: enlarged liver (>12 cm at midclavicular line or palpable below costal margin)
Jaundice: yellow discoloration of skin/sclera from hyperbilirubinemia (>2–3 mg/dL)
Cirrhosis: end-stage liver disease characterized by extensive fibrosis + nodular regeneration → loss of normal architecture & function
LO 2 — Ascites vs. Edema
What is ascites? How does it differ pathophysiologically from peripheral edema in liver disease?
tap to flip
Answer
Ascites: accumulation of fluid in peritoneal cavity (TRANSUDATE — low protein)
Mechanism in liver disease: ↓hepatic albumin synthesis → ↓plasma oncotic pressure + portal hypertension (↑hydrostatic pressure)
Peripheral edema (RV failure): ↓oncotic pressure + venous congestion → accumulation in dependent areas

Key difference: ascites is "pulled" by decreased oncotic pressure, not just gravity
LO 3 — Liver Functions
Name the five main metabolic/synthetic functions of the liver.
tap to flip
Answer
1. Metabolic: carbohydrate (glycogenesis, glycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis), lipid (β-oxidation, ketogenesis, lipoprotein synthesis VLDL), protein (transamination, urea cycle)
2. Detoxification: Phase I (CYP450), Phase II (conjugation)
3. Synthetic: albumin, clotting factors (II, V, VII, IX, X), other plasma proteins
4. Storage: glycogen, fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K), B12, iron
5. Immune: Kupffer cells, complement synthesis
LO 3 — Carbohydrate Metabolism
Describe hepatic carbohydrate metabolism: glycogenesis, glycogenolysis, and gluconeogenesis. When does each occur?
tap to flip
Answer
Glycogenesis: glucose → glycogen storage (fed state, insulin-driven)
Glycogenolysis: glycogen breakdown → glucose (fasting, exercise; glucagon/epinephrine)
Gluconeogenesis: amino acids, lactate, glycerol → glucose (fasting, stress; glucagon/cortisol)

Key: liver is primary organ for glucose homeostasis — maintains 70–100 mg/dL
LO 3 — Lipid & Cholesterol Metabolism
Describe hepatic lipid metabolism: β-oxidation, ketogenesis, lipoprotein synthesis, and cholesterol handling.
tap to flip
Answer
β-oxidation: fatty acids → acetyl-CoA → ATP (energy)
Ketogenesis: excess acetyl-CoA → ketone bodies (ketones, acetoacetate, β-hydroxybutyrate) — fuel for brain/muscle in fasting
VLDL synthesis: liver packages triglycerides + apoB-100 → export to tissues
Cholesterol: de novo synthesis via HMG-CoA reductase; used for bile acids, steroid hormones, vitamin D, membrane synthesis
LO 4 — Cholesterol Metabolism
What is the regulatory mechanism controlling cholesterol synthesis? How do statins work?
tap to flip
Answer
Feedback regulation: dietary cholesterol ↓ de novo synthesis via SREBP (sterol regulatory element-binding protein) → downregulates HMG-CoA reductase
Statins: competitively inhibit HMG-CoA reductase (rate-limiting enzyme) → ↓LDL-C by ~30–50%

Key: liver is primary site of cholesterol synthesis & regulation
LO 5 — Bilirubin Metabolism
Trace the pathway of bilirubin metabolism from heme to urobilinogen, naming the enzymes and intermediate compounds.
tap to flip
Answer
Heme (from RBC breakdown in spleen/reticuloendothelial system)
→ Biliverdin (via heme oxygenase)
→ Unconjugated bilirubin (via bilirubin reductase)
→ albumin-bound transport to liver
→ Conjugated bilirubin (via UGT1A1 glucuronidation in hepatocytes)
→ secretion into bile
→ gut bacteria → Urobilinogen (some reabsorbed, some → stercobilin/urobilin in stool/urine)
LO 5 — Four Jaundice Types
Name and differentiate the four types of jaundice by pattern of bilirubin elevation and lab findings.
tap to flip
Answer
1. Prehepatic (hemolytic): ↑↑ unconjugated, ↑ urine urobilinogen, NO urine bili
2. Intrahepatic (hepatocellular): ↑ both unconj & conj, ↑↑ AST/ALT, mild ↑ ALP/GGT
3. Posthepatic (obstructive): ↑↑ conjugated, dark urine (conjugated spillage), PALE stool, ↑↑ ALP/GGT
4. Congenital: Gilbert (mild unconj, stress-triggered), Crigler-Najjar (severe unconj), Dubin-Johnson (conj + black liver), Rotor (conj, no black liver)
LO 6 — Bile Composition
Name the major constituents of bile and their approximate percentages by weight.
tap to flip
Answer
Bile salts/acids: ~50% (primary roles: emulsification, absorption)
Phospholipids (lecithin): ~40%
Cholesterol: ~4%
Bilirubin: ~0.3%
Electrolytes + water: remainder

Bile is produced by hepatocytes → secreted into canaliculi → concentrated in gallbladder 5–20×
LO 7 — Bile Acids & Enterohepatic Circulation
Name the primary bile acids and describe the enterohepatic circulation (EHC): synthesis, conjugation, reabsorption, recycling rate.
tap to flip
Answer
Primary bile acids: cholic acid & chenodeoxycholic acid (made from cholesterol via 7α-hydroxylase)
Conjugation: hepatocytes conjugate to glycine/taurine → more soluble, lower pKa, ionized at gut pH
EHC: secreted in bile → ileum absorption via ASBT transporter → portal vein return to liver → re-secreted
Recycling: 6–10 cycles/day; ~95% reabsorbed in terminal ileum

Efficient system — only ~0.5 g/day of new bile acid synthesis needed
LO 8 — Bile Secretion & Storage
Describe bile secretion from hepatocytes and storage/concentration in the gallbladder. What happens to bile salts?
tap to flip
Answer
Hepatocytes: secrete canalicular bile (hepatic bile) into bile canaliculi
→ Ductules → bile ducts → CBD
→ Gallbladder (between meals): stores & concentrates via active Na⁺/H₂O reabsorption (5–20× concentration)
→ CCK stimulation: gallbladder contracts + Sphincter of Oddi relaxes → bile enters duodenum

Key: bile salts NOT reabsorbed in gallbladder — they drive water reabsorption osmotically
LO 9 — Bile Regulation
Describe the hormonal and neural regulation of bile secretion. Compare CCK vs. secretin effects.
tap to flip
Answer
CCK (cholecystokinin): fat/protein in duodenum → I cells → gallbladder CONTRACTION + Oddi RELAXATION + pancreatic enzyme secretion
Secretin: acid in duodenum → S cells → ↑ ductal (watery, HCO₃⁻-rich) bile secretion
Vagal stimulation: enhances bile secretion
Bile acid feedback: bile acids inhibit 7α-hydroxylase → ↓ new bile acid synthesis (negative feedback)

Net: dietary fat/protein → ↑bile flow; acid → ↑HCO₃⁻ in bile
LO 10 — Cholecystectomy
What are the consequences of cholecystectomy? How does loss of gallbladder storage affect fat absorption?
tap to flip
Answer
Loss of bile storage → continuous low-volume bile flow instead of bolus after meals
Mild fat malabsorption: especially with large fatty meals (reduced bile micellar concentration)
Bile acid diarrhea (post-cholecystectomy diarrhea): excess bile acids reach colon → ↑secretion + motility
No jaundice, no clotting defect (EHC still intact)

Most patients tolerate well; fatty food intolerance improves over time
LO 10 — Ileal Resection
What are the GI and nutritional consequences of ileal resection or disease (Crohn's, etc.)?
tap to flip
Answer
Loss of bile acid reabsorption (ASBT site) →
• Bile acid diarrhea: ↑fecal loss → ↓EHC cycling → hepatic demand ↑ synthesis → ↓bile acid pool
• Fat malabsorption: insufficient bile salts → steatorrhea
• Gallstones: ↓bile acid pool → ↑cholesterol saturation
• Vitamin B12 deficiency: if terminal ileum involved (intrinsic factor-B12 complex absorbed there)
• Fat-soluble vitamin deficiency: A, D, E, K from malabsorption

Management: bile acid sequestrants (cholestyramine) or low-fat diet
LO 3 — Protein Metabolism
What is the urea cycle? Name it as the major hepatic protein metabolism pathway and explain its clinical significance.
tap to flip
Answer
Urea cycle: Amino acids (NH₃-containing) → converted to urea in liver mitochondria → excreted in urine
Rate-limiting enzyme: Carbamoyl phosphate synthetase I (CPSI)
Clinical significance: Liver failure → ↓urea synthesis → ↑ammonia (NH₃) → hyperammonemia → hepatic encephalopathy (altered consciousness, asterixis)
Treatment: Lactulose (↓NH₃ absorption), rifaxomicin, L-ornithine aspartate
LO 3 — Hepatic Synthetic Function
Which liver-synthesized clotting factors are Vitamin K-dependent? Why is PT/INR elevated in liver disease?
tap to flip
Answer
Vitamin K-dependent factors (liver-synthesized): II (prothrombin), VII, IX, X (mnemonic: 2,7,9,10)
Also synthesized in liver (NOT Vit K-dependent): V, fibrinogen, XIII
In liver failure:
• ↓ALL clotting factor synthesis → prolonged PT/INR
• Factor V particularly sensitive to liver damage
• Factor V/VIII ratio useful marker: normal ~1:1, in liver disease VIII relatively spared → ratio <1
• PT/INR NOT corrected by vitamin K (unlike biliary obstruction where K-malabsorption is primary)
LO 1 — Liver Detoxification
Describe Phase I and Phase II detoxification pathways in the liver. Which drugs are metabolized primarily in Zone 3?
tap to flip
Answer
Phase I (Oxidation): CYP450 monooxygenase system (esp. CYP3A4) → oxidative metabolism of drugs (often creates reactive metabolites)
Phase II (Conjugation): UDP-glucuronosyltransferase, sulfotransferase, glutathione S-transferase → attach water-soluble groups → more excretable
Zone 3 (centrilobular) enriched in CYP450 →
• Acetaminophen toxicity (NAPQI metabolite) → centrilobular necrosis
• Isoniazid, barbiturates, carbon tetrachloride (CCl₄) also cause Zone 3 damage
• Risk ↑ with enzyme induction (phenobarbital, rifampicin)

Test Trap Mode: Each statement is either TRUE or FALSE as written. Many are designed to catch common exam errors. Click T or F — then read the explanation carefully.
Trap 01 — LO 1
"The hepatic artery provides the majority of blood volume to the liver, while the portal vein provides most of the O₂."
FALSE. This is REVERSED! Portal vein = 75% blood volume (but O₂-poor from GI tract). Hepatic artery = 25% blood volume, 75% of O₂ (oxygenated). Students often mix this up because the artery is arterial (expect more volume), but the portal vein actually delivers MORE volume due to its dual inputs (superior & inferior mesenteric veins, splenic vein).
Trap 02 — LO 1
"Zone 3 (centrilobular) hepatocytes are protected from drug toxicity because they receive blood from the hepatic artery (well-oxygenated) first."
FALSE. Zone 3 is VULNERABLE to both ischemia AND drug toxicity because: (1) lowest O₂ (receives blood LAST) → ischemia-sensitive, and (2) HIGHEST CYP450 enzyme content → maximum drug metabolism → max toxic metabolites. This is why acetaminophen toxicity causes centrilobular necrosis.
Trap 03 — LO 2
"Ascites in cirrhosis is caused primarily by impaired hepatocyte clearance of aldosterone, leading to sodium retention and edema."
FALSE. While secondary hyperaldosteronism DOES occur in cirrhosis, the PRIMARY driver of ascites is: (1) ↓plasma oncotic pressure (↓albumin synthesis), and (2) portal hypertension (↑hydrostatic pressure). It's a transudate, not retention from aldosterone alone. Aldosterone excess contributes but is secondary.
Trap 04 — LO 5
"In Gilbert syndrome, jaundice occurs because the liver cannot produce bilirubin-binding proteins."
FALSE. Gilbert syndrome is caused by ↓UGT1A1 enzyme activity (mild, ~20% normal), NOT a problem with binding proteins or production. Bilirubin is produced normally but conjugated slowly → accumulates as unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia (mild, 1–3 mg/dL). Stress, fasting, or illness worsens it. Albumin binding is completely normal.
Trap 05 — LO 5
"Conjugated (direct) hyperbilirubinemia always means cholestasis because bilirubin is being forced backward into the blood."
FALSE. Conjugated hyperbilirubinemia occurs in TWO contexts: (1) Cholestasis — bile duct obstruction (posthepatic), AND (2) Hepatocellular injury (intrahepatic) where hepatocytes are damaged but still leak conjugated bili back into blood. Both cause ↑conjugated bilirubin, but for different reasons. Check AST/ALT and ALP to differentiate.
Trap 06 — LO 7
"Bile acids are reabsorbed in the colon, so >95% are recycled back to the liver daily."
FALSE — PARTIALLY. While >95% ARE recycled, this happens in the TERMINAL ILEUM, not the colon. The ileum has ASBT transporters for active absorption of bile acids. The colon plays no role in bile acid reabsorption. This is why terminal ileum disease/resection causes bile acid diarrhea and steatorrhea — the acids reach the colon where they increase fluid secretion.
Trap 07 — LO 8
"The gallbladder concentrates bile by actively reabsorbing bile salts and cholesterol."
FALSE. The gallbladder concentrates bile by actively reabsorbing Na⁺ and H₂O via the mucosal epithelium (isosmotic reabsorption). Bile salts and cholesterol are NOT reabsorbed in the gallbladder — they remain behind, becoming increasingly concentrated (5–20×). Bile salts are reserved for the ileum (terminal, ASBT-mediated reabsorption).
Trap 08 — LO 9
"Secretin is released in response to dietary fat, and it stimulates the pancreas to release digestive enzymes."
FALSE. Secretin is released by S cells in response to ACID (not fat), and it stimulates watery, HCO₃⁻-rich ductal bile secretion (from hepatocytes/ducts) — NOT pancreatic enzymes. CCK (released by fat/protein) stimulates gallbladder contraction AND pancreatic enzyme secretion. Don't mix them up!
