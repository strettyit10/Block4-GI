Question 1 · Embryonic Duodenal Development
A 2-day-old full-term newborn presents with projectile vomiting 4 hours after initial feeding with formula. The infant appears jaundiced, and on examination has mild abdominal distention. Abdominal X-ray shows the classic "double bubble" sign with gas in the stomach and proximal duodenum but absence of distal bowel gas. The infant has trisomy 21 with features of Down syndrome noted on karyotype.

Which of the following embryologic events is most likely disrupted in this patient?
A Failure of the ventral pancreatic bud to migrate
B Incomplete rotation of the midgut loop around the SMA
C Failure of recanalization of the duodenum (duodenal atresia)
D Incomplete mesentery formation from dorsal foregut mesoderm
E Failure of the omphalomesenteric duct to obliterate
Answer: C — Failure of recanalization of the duodenum (duodenal atresia)

Duodenal atresia results from failure of the duodenum to recanalize during week 8-10 of development. The duodenum arises as a solid cord that normally recanalizes; failure of this process leads to a complete web or membrane (most common at the junction with the bile duct opening). The "double bubble" sign reflects gastric and proximal duodenal dilation proximal to the obstruction, with absence of distal air. Duodenal atresia is strongly associated with Down syndrome (trisomy 21). Why other options fail: Failure of ventral pancreatic migration causes annular pancreas; incomplete midgut rotation causes malrotation; incomplete mesentery formation is not the primary mechanism; omphalomesenteric duct patency causes Meckel diverticulum.
Question 2 · Gastric Muscularization Defect
A 3-week-old male infant (former term infant) presents with a 1-week history of projectile vomiting immediately after feeding. The vomit is non-bilious, and the infant appears hungry and feeds again after vomiting. On physical exam, visible peristaltic waves are noted across the epigastrium from left to right, and palpation reveals a firm, olive-sized mass in the epigastrium just to the right of midline. The infant has a 2-day history of jaundice. Serum electrolytes show hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis.

What is the mechanism of gastric outlet obstruction in this condition?
A Pyloric mucosa herniation into the gastric lumen
B Excessive smooth muscle hypertrophy of the pyloric sphincter
C Incomplete muscularization of the antral wall
D Web formation from aberrant tissue in the pylorus
E Innervation defect of pyloric myenteric plexus (similar to Hirschsprung)
Answer: B — Excessive smooth muscle hypertrophy of the pyloric sphincter

Infantile hypertrophic pyloric stenosis results from unexplained smooth muscle hypertrophy and hyperplasia of the pyloric sphincter, creating a "olive-like" mass that obstructs the gastric outlet. The etiology involves failure of normal relaxation/innervation balance; abnormal levels of neuronal nitric oxide synthase (nNOS) and decreased activation of muscle relaxant pathways may contribute. Unlike pyloric atresia (embryologic malformation), pyloric stenosis presents 3-5 weeks postnatally, is more common in males (4:1 ratio), and has no specific embryologic developmental defect—rather, it is a functional disorder related to abnormal myogenic/neurogenic control. The non-bilious vomiting, hypochloremic hypokalemic alkalosis, and peristaltic waves distinguish it from other etiologies. Why other options fail: Mucosa herniation is not the primary mechanism; antral wall muscularization defects are not involved; web formation is not typical; innervation defects more closely resemble Hirschsprung disease.
Question 3 · Neural Crest Cell Migration Defect
A 2-day-old male neonate fails to pass meconium within 48 hours of birth despite apparent normal abdominal exam. When the infant finally passes stool after rectal digital examination, explosive diarrhea ensues. The infant develops fever, abdominal distention, and lethargy over the next 24 hours. Genetic testing reveals a heterozygous loss-of-function mutation in the RET proto-oncogene. Rectal biopsy confirms the diagnosis by demonstrating absence of ganglion cells.

What is the embryologic basis of ganglion cell aganglionosis in this patient?
A Failure of vagal neural crest cell migration distally along the intestine (GDNF-RET signaling defect)
B Abnormal splanchnic mesoderm differentiation preventing ganglion cell proliferation
C Failure of the endoderm to signal ENS progenitors for proper differentiation
D Apoptotic death of ganglion cells due to excessive nNOS expression
E Premature smooth muscle differentiation blocking ganglion cell migration
Answer: A — Failure of vagal neural crest cell migration distally along the intestine (GDNF-RET signaling defect)

Hirschsprung disease (HSCR) results from failed migration of neural crest cells derived from the vagal plexus into the distal hindgut during weeks 5-12. The enteric nervous system (ENS) is populated by neural crest-derived precursor cells that migrate cranial to caudal along the intestine, guided by GDNF (glial cell-derived neurotrophic factor) signaling via the RET receptor tyrosine kinase. Loss-of-function RET mutations impair this chemotactic gradient, preventing cells from reaching distal segments → aganglionosis (most commonly rectosigmoid). Patients present with failure to pass meconium and Hirschsprung-associated enterocolitis if untreated. Why other options fail: Splanchnic mesoderm provides smooth muscle, not ganglion cells; endoderm provides epithelium; nNOS expression is actually protective; smooth muscle differentiation follows ganglion cell arrival.
Question 4 · Omphalomesenteric Duct Patency
A 7-year-old boy presents with painless melena and iron-deficiency anemia (Hgb 7.2 g/dL). Colonoscopy and upper endoscopy are both negative. A Tc-99m pertechnetate scan ("Meckel scan") shows focal radionuclide uptake in the right lower quadrant not corresponding to the stomach or other normal tissue. At surgery, a 2 cm ileal diverticulum is found 2 feet proximal to the ileocecal valve, with a fibrous cord extending to the umbilicus. Histology reveals heterotopic gastric mucosa within the diverticulum.

What is the embryologic origin of this heterotopic mucosa?
A Ectopic foregut endoderm invaginating into the midgut
B Abnormal proliferation of pyloric mucosa along the intestine
C Incomplete obliteration of the vitelline duct with retained foregut endoderm
D Aberrant splanchnic mesoderm migration during foregut rotation
E Heterotopic pancreatic tissue from accessory ventral bud
Answer: C — Incomplete obliteration of the vitelline duct with retained foregut endoderm

Meckel diverticulum is the most common congenital anomaly of the GI tract, occurring in ~2% of the population and following the "rule of 2s": 2% incidence, 2 feet from ileocecal valve, 2 inches long, 2 types of heterotopic mucosa (gastric and pancreatic), 2x more common in males, 2 main complications (bleeding and obstruction). The vitelline (omphalomesenteric) duct normally completely obliterates by week 9; incomplete obliteration leaves a patent or partially patent remnant containing midgut endoderm. Heterotopic gastric mucosa is present in ~50% of Meckels and secretes HCl → ileal ulceration and painless bleeding (detected on Meckel scan due to pertechnetate uptake by gastric mucosa). Why other options fail: Ectopic foregut endoderm would not explain midgut location; pyloric mucosa proliferation is not the mechanism; splanchnic mesoderm produces smooth muscle, not mucosa; pancreatic tissue is a separate heterotopic finding.
Question 5 · Midgut Rotation Defect
A 2-week-old female infant presents with acute onset of bilious vomiting and abdominal distention. Abdominal exam shows moderate distention with diminished bowel sounds. Upper GI series and abdominal ultrasound show a "whirlpool sign"—spiral orientation of mesenteric vessels and twisted small bowel loops around the superior mesenteric artery. The infant is rushed to the OR, where intraoperative findings confirm mid-gut volvulus with compromised blood supply to the small bowel and proximal colon.

What embryologic event predisposed this infant to volvulus?
A Complete 90° clockwise rotation of the duodenum and jejunum only
B Failure of the midgut to complete its 270° counterclockwise rotation around the SMA
C Persistence of the ventral mesentery preventing dorsal mesentery formation
D Failure of the mesentery to fuse to the posterior peritoneum
E Agenesis of the SMA preventing normal visceral attachments
Answer: B — Failure of the midgut to complete its 270° counterclockwise rotation around the SMA

Normal midgut rotation occurs in two phases: (1) herniation (week 6) where the elongating midgut-loop herniates out of the abdomen and rotates 90° counterclockwise around the SMA; (2) retraction (weeks 10-12) where the loop returns to the abdomen and completes an additional 180° rotation for a total of 270° counterclockwise. Incomplete rotation (malrotation) leaves the midgut in an abnormal position with a narrow mesenteric base, predisposing to volvulus (twisting around the SMA). The "whirlpool sign" is pathognomonic for midgut volvulus. Why other options fail: 90° rotation alone is incomplete; ventral/dorsal mesentery issues don't cause the twisting pattern; failure of mesentery fusion causes different malrotation patterns; SMA agenesis is incompatible with life.
Question 6 · Abdominal Wall Closure Defect
A full-term newborn male is noted at birth to have a small abdominal wall defect to the right of the umbilicus with exposed bowel (small intestine, colon) and liver herniating outside the abdomen. The defect is approximately 3 cm in diameter and edges appear to be uncovered by peritoneum or skin. The exposed viscera are bright red and edematous, appear inflamed (likely from amniotic fluid exposure in utero), and there is no umbilical hernial sac. The infant was born to a 19-year-old primigravida with a history of maternal cocaine use during the first trimester.

What is the embryologic mechanism of this defect?
A Failure of umbilical fold fusion during weeks 3-4, with incomplete closure of all 4 folds
B Rupture of the amnion causing breakdown of developing somatic mesoderm lateral to the umbilicus
C Failure of coelom partitioning by the diaphragm and body wall
D Agenesis of the rectus abdominis and external oblique muscles
E Failure of the ventral mesentery to form, leaving exposed viscera
Answer: B — Rupture of the amnion causing breakdown of developing somatic mesoderm lateral to the umbilicus

Gastroschisis (Greek for "abdominal cleft") is a defect in the anterior abdominal wall typically to the right of midline, with herniated viscera NOT covered by peritoneum or skin. The defect is thought to result from breakdown of somatic mesoderm, possibly from amniotic membrane rupture with resultant chemical/mechanical injury to the developing body wall. Unlike omphalocele, there is no sac. Gastroschisis is associated with maternal risk factors (young maternal age, cocaine use in first trimester) and carries high morbidity/mortality if untreated. The exposed viscera show inflammatory changes from amniotic fluid contact. Why other options fail: Umbilical fold fusion failure causes a smaller centered defect (omphalocele) with sac; diaphragm/coelom issues cause congenital diaphragmatic hernia; rectus agenesis is rare and causes diastasis, not gastroschisis; ventral mesentery is in the abdomen, not the body wall.
Question 7 · Ventral Abdominal Fold Failure
A term female newborn presents with a midline abdominal defect at the umbilicus, approximately 5 cm in diameter. The defect contains liver, bowel, and other abdominal organs covered by a thin, translucent peritoneal/fascial sac (the omphalocele sac). The umbilical cord inserts directly into the sac. On genetic testing, the infant is found to have trisomy 18 (Edwards syndrome) with features including cardiac defects (ventricular septal defect), craniofacial abnormalities, and clenched fists. The obstetric history reveals polyhydramnios during pregnancy.

What is the embryologic defect underlying omphalocele?
A Amniotic membrane rupture with inflammatory damage to mesoderm
B Incomplete obliteration of the vitelline duct
C Failure of the ventral abdominal folds (cranial, caudal, and lateral folds) to fuse around the umbilicus
D Insufficient splanchnic mesoderm migration to form the abdominal wall layers
E Failure of the dorsal mesentery to separate from the parietal peritoneum
Answer: C — Failure of the ventral abdominal folds (cranial, caudal, and lateral folds) to fuse around the umbilicus

Omphalocele results from failure of the four ventral abdominal folds (cranial/cephalic fold from the septum transversum, caudal/cephalic fold, and bilateral lateral folds) to fuse during weeks 3-10. This leaves a midline defect at the umbilicus covered by peritoneum and fascia (the omphalocele sac), distinguishing it from gastroschisis. Omphalocele is strongly associated with chromosomal abnormalities (trisomy 18, trisomy 13, trisomy 21), the Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome (macroglossia, macrosomia, hypoglycemia), and cardiac defects. The size depends on the degree of fold fusion failure. Why other options fail: Amniotic rupture causes gastroschisis (uncovered defect); vitelline duct patency causes Meckel diverticulum; splanchnic mesoderm contributes to muscle, not fold formation; dorsal mesentery issues don't cause ventral body wall defects.
Question 8 · Diaphragmatic Muscularization Defect
A term male newborn is noted immediately after delivery to have severe respiratory distress with tachypnea and cyanosis. Initial chest X-ray shows bowel loops within the left chest cavity with mediastinal shift to the right and compression atelectasis of the left lung. The infant's abdomen appears scaphoid (sunken). On auscultation, breath sounds are decreased on the left with audible bowel sounds in the left chest. The infant requires intubation and mechanical ventilation. An esophageal atresia has been ruled out.

What is the embryologic basis of this defect?
A Failure of the pleuroperitoneal membrane to form during week 3
B Rupture of the pericardioperitoneal canals in utero
C Failure of the pleuroperitoneal folds to fuse and separate the pleural and peritoneal cavities (Bochdalek foramen)
D Incomplete muscularization of the septum transversum
E Agenesis of splanchnic mesoderm derivatives in the thorax
Answer: C — Failure of the pleuroperitoneal folds to fuse and separate the pleural and peritoneal cavities (Bochdalek foramen)

Congenital diaphragmatic hernia (CDH) results from incomplete diaphragmatic development, most commonly due to failure of the pleuroperitoneal folds (Bochdalek folds, also called pleuroperitonal membranes) to fuse by week 8-9, leaving a posterolateral diaphragmatic defect (Bochdalek hernia, 90% of cases occur on the left). This allows abdominal contents (bowel, liver, spleen) to herniate into the thoracic cavity, compressing lungs and heart. Presentation is with neonatal respiratory distress, cyanosis, and scaphoid abdomen. Associated conditions include VACTERL sequence, cardiac defects, and chromosomal abnormalities. Why other options fail: Pericardioperitoneal canals are related to pericardial formation; incomplete septum transversum contributes to diaphragm but the lateral defect is from pleuroperitoneal fold failure; splanchnic mesoderm provides smooth muscle, not diaphragmatic muscle.
Question 9 · Tracheoesophageal Fistula Embryology
A full-term male newborn presents at 4 hours of age with excessive drooling, coughing, and respiratory distress. The infant was noted to have polyhydramnios in utero. A nasogastric tube cannot be passed beyond ~10 cm (blind pouch). Chest X-ray shows air in the stomach and bowel loops, but there is also air in the left mainstem bronchus. Prone abdominal X-ray (cross-table) confirms a fistulous tract from the distal esophagus to the lower trachea. The infant is taken to the OR for repair of esophageal atresia with distal tracheoesophageal fistula.

What is the embryologic event that predisposed to this defect?
A Incomplete separation of the dorsal foregut into esophagus and respiratory diverticulum
B Abnormal division of the tracheoesophageal septum by the foregut mesoderm, leaving a fistula between distal esophagus and ventral trachea
C Failure of the laryngotracheal groove to fully separate from the foregut endoderm
D Incomplete muscularization of the cricopharyngeal sphincter
E Abnormal peristalsis due to myenteric plexus agenesis (similar to Hirschsprung)
Answer: B — Abnormal division of the tracheoesophageal septum by the foregut mesoderm, leaving a fistula between distal esophagus and ventral trachea

Tracheoesophageal fistula (TEF) results from incomplete or abnormal separation of the foregut into the respiratory diverticulum (ventral) and esophagus (dorsal) via the tracheoesophageal septum during weeks 4-6. The most common type (85%) is esophageal atresia with a distal TEF, where the proximal esophagus ends in a blind pouch and the distal esophagus connects to the lower trachea. Infants present with excessive drooling, choking/coughing with feeds, and respiratory distress (air enters the stomach via the fistula). Other types include isolated atresia, isolated H-type fistula (no atresia), and proximal fistula. TEF is associated with VACTERL sequence. Why other options fail: Dorsal foregut issues affect the entire foregut, not specifically the trachea-esophageal separation; laryngotracheal groove failure is part of the same developmental process; cricopharyngeal issues cause swallowing dysfunction; myenteric agenesis causes motility defects, not structural fistula.
Question 10 · Pancreatic Bud Migration Defect
A 3-week-old male infant presents with projectile vomiting and poor feeding. Unlike the case of infantile hypertrophic pyloric stenosis seen earlier, this infant's vomit is bile-stained (bilious), suggesting a more distal obstruction. Upper GI series shows a "double bubble" sign but the bubbles are stomach and duodenum with no pyloric abnormality on ultrasound. Abdominal imaging reveals a ring-like constriction of the duodenum at the ampulla of Vater, with duodenal dilation proximal to the obstruction. At surgery, pancreatic tissue is found encircling the second portion of the duodenum.

What is the embryologic basis of this finding?
A Excessive proliferation of the dorsal pancreatic bud around the duodenum
B Failure of the ventral pancreatic bud to migrate and fuse with the dorsal bud, leaving pancreatic tissue encircling the duodenum
C Incomplete muscularization of the duodenal wall allowing pancreatic infiltration
D Persistent ventral mesentery with pancreatic tissue attachment
E Abnormal embryonic rotation of the duodenum trapping pancreatic tissue
Answer: B — Failure of the ventral pancreatic bud to migrate and fuse with the dorsal bud, leaving pancreatic tissue encircling the duodenum

Annular pancreas results from embryologic maldevelopment of the pancreas during weeks 6-8. The pancreas develops from two buds: the dorsal bud (arises from the dorsal duodenal wall) and the ventral bud (arises from the bile duct). Normally, during duodenal rotation, the ventral bud swings around the duodenum and fuses with the dorsal bud. If the ventral bud fails to properly migrate or form, or if it forms an incomplete ring, pancreatic tissue can encircle the duodenum → annular pancreas. This causes duodenal constriction and proximal obstruction with bilious vomiting (distal to pylorus). Why other options fail: Excessive dorsal bud proliferation doesn't create a ring; duodenal muscularization defects don't involve pancreatic tissue; ventral mesentery is in the forgut, not the pancreas; duodenal rotation itself is normal in annular pancreas.
Question 11 · SMA-Dependent Bowel Necrosis
A 2-day-old full-term newborn develops bilious vomiting and abdominal distention. Abdominal X-ray shows multiple dilated loops of small bowel with air-fluid levels. Upper GI series and follow-through imaging reveal absent jejunum with the distal ileum appearing to spiral directly from proximal ileum in a "Christmas tree" or "apple-peel" configuration around a single mesenteric vessel (the SMA). At surgery, jejunal atresia is found with absent proximal mesentery; the ileum spirals around a single SMA tributary. The bowel appears to have healed from a previous intrauterine perforation.

What is the embryologic mechanism of this intestinal anomaly?
A Failure of the mesentery to form around the jejunum during herniation
B Incomplete recanalization of the jejunum (similar to duodenal atresia)
C Intrauterine SMA vascular insufficiency or mesenteric vascular accident (torsion, thrombosis) causing jejunal ischemic necrosis and atresia
D Abnormal midgut rotation causing jejunal compression
E Maturation defect of jejunal smooth muscle during weeks 8-10
Answer: C — Intrauterine SMA vascular insufficiency or mesenteric vascular accident causing jejunal ischemic necrosis and atresia

Apple-peel atresia (or "Christmas tree" atresia) is a rare form of jejunal/ileal atresia with a distinctive morphology: absent mesentery and jejunum with the ileum spiraling around a single mesenteric vessel (usually the SMA or a branch). The pathophysiology is thought to involve intrauterine mesenteric vascular insufficiency—a vascular accident (torsion, thrombosis, or vascular occlusion) during fetal life causes intestinal ischemia and necrosis, leading to atresia. The healed appearance of the bowel and absence of mucosa suggest prior perforation and resorption. Unlike simple atresia (recanalization failure), this is an acquired vascular lesion. Why other options fail: Mesentery formation is usually normal; recanalization failure affects duodenum/jejunum uniformly, not specifically the proximal jejunum; midgut rotation is usually normal; smooth muscle maturation defects don't cause atresia.
Question 12 · Hindgut Embryology and Vascular Supply
A 45-year-old male presents with painless rectal bleeding during bowel movements (blood on toilet paper, not mixed with stool). Physical exam reveals internal hemorrhoids just above the dentate (pectinate) line of the rectum. Anoscopy confirms small, non-thrombosed internal hemorrhoids draining blood with bright red venous bleeding. The patient asks why the bleeding is bright red and why these hemorrhoids are specifically located just above a particular anatomical landmark of the rectum.

What is the embryologic basis for the clinical features of internal hemorrhoids above the pectinate line?
A Arterial rather than venous blood supply results in bright red bleeding (but hemorrhoids are venous lesions)
B The pectinate line marks the junction between midgut/hindgut (proctodeum above) and foregut/midgut derivatives (dentate line below), with superior rectal veins (from midgut) draining to portal vein → portal system has lower pressure and bright red blood
C The pectinate line separates endoderm (above) from mesodermal tissues (below)
D Internal hemorrhoids are supplied by branches of the inferior mesenteric vein, not portal vein
E The pectinate line represents a failure of embryologic mesentery fusion
Answer: B — The pectinate line marks the junction between midgut/hindgut with superior rectal veins (from midgut) draining to portal vein → portal system has lower pressure and bright red blood

The pectinate (dentate) line is the junction between endoderm-derived tissue above (rectum, supplied by superior rectal vessels from IMA and superior rectal veins) and ectoderm-derived tissue below (anal canal, supplied by inferior rectal vessels from internal pudendal artery and veins). The pectinate line also marks transitions in: venous drainage (above → portal system via superior rectal vein; below → systemic via inferior rectal vein), lymphatic drainage (above → internal iliac; below → superficial inguinal), and innervation (above → visceral [hypogastric]; below → somatic [pudendal]). Internal hemorrhoids above the pectinate line bleed bright red because the superior rectal veins drain into the portal vein, which has lower pressure than systemic veins. Why other options fail: Hemorrhoids are venous, not arterial lesions; pectinate line does separate endoderm from ectoderm (but the vascular supply difference is the clinical key); inferior rectal vein is below the pectinate line; mesentery fusion is not the embryologic basis.
Question 13 · Hepatic Duct Development
A 4-week-old female infant presents with jaundice persisting beyond 2 weeks (pathologic neonatal jaundice), acholic (pale/clay-colored) stools, and dark urine. Serum labs show predominantly direct (conjugated) hyperbilirubinemia with elevated alkaline phosphatase and GGT. Abdominal ultrasound shows a small liver, absent or hyoplastic gallbladder, and no dilated intrahepatic bile ducts. Hepatobiliary scintigraphy shows no excretion of tracer into the intestine. Liver biopsy demonstrates bile duct proliferation and fibrosis consistent with neonatal cholestasis. The infant is referred for hepatic portoenterostomy (Kasai procedure).

What is the embryologic basis of biliary atresia?
A Failure of the hepatic diverticulum to separate from the duodenum
B Agenesis of the cystic duct and common bile duct
C Failure of bile duct recanalization and/or progressive obliteration of extrahepatic bile ducts (etiology unclear: possibly viral infection, immune-mediated destruction, or developmental arrest)
D Incomplete hepatic parenchymal development from the endoderm
E Absence of the ventral mesentery preventing bile duct formation
Answer: C — Failure of bile duct recanalization and/or progressive obliteration of extrahepatic bile ducts

Biliary atresia is a progressive fibro-obliterative disorder of the extrahepatic bile ducts, not a true congenital agenesis. During weeks 5-7, the hepatic diverticulum (part of the foregut endoderm) separates from the duodenum and differentiates into liver parenchyma and bile ducts. Biliary atresia likely results from either failure of proper recanalization of the initially solid bile duct epithelium or from progressive postnatal obliteration due to viral infection (rotavirus, cytomegalovirus), autoimmune mechanisms, or developmental factors. Infants present with direct hyperbilirubinemia, acholic stools, hepatomegaly, and progressive cirrhosis if untreated. The Kasai hepatoportoenterostomy attempts to restore bile drainage. Why other options fail: Hepatic diverticulum separation is usually normal; cystic duct agenesis is a different condition; hepatic parenchymal development is usually intact; ventral mesentery carries the bile duct but its presence doesn't determine patency.
Question 14 · Hindgut and Cloacal Development
A term female newborn fails to pass meconium in the first 24 hours and on perineal exam has no visible anal opening (imperforate anus/anal agenesis). Spinal imaging reveals lumbar vertebral anomalies (hemivertebra, fused vertebrae). Echocardiography shows a secundum atrial septal defect. Renal ultrasound demonstrates bilateral renal hypoplasia. The infant is also found to have a tracheoesophageal fistula (H-type), discovered incidentally during feeding attempts. Chromosomal analysis is normal.

What embryologic insult likely affected multiple organ systems in this infant?
A Selective failure of cloacal septation affecting only the anorectal region
B Mutation in the sonic hedgehog (Shh) gene affecting notochord signaling
C VACTERL association (Vertebral, Anal, Cardiac, Tracheoesophageal, Renal, Limb anomalies) — likely multifactorial etiology affecting notochord/midline mesodermal tissues
D Failure of neural crest migration (similar to Hirschsprung disease) affecting multiple systems
E Selective ectoderm-derived defect affecting all tissues derived from caudal ectoderm
Answer: C — VACTERL association

VACTERL is an acronym for a non-random cluster of congenital anomalies affecting multiple organ systems: Vertebral (hemivertebra, fused/missing vertebrae), Anal (imperforate anus, anal stenosis, anorectal malformations), Cardiac (ASD, VSD, TOF), TracheoEsophageal fistula, Renal (hypoplasia, dysplasia, hydronephrosis), and Limb abnormalities (polydactyly, syndactyly). VACTERL is not a single genetic disorder but rather a recognizable pattern of anomalies suggesting disruption of midline mesodermal tissue development, possibly from abnormalities in notochord/midline signaling or a shared embryologic insult during weeks 4-8. The association is defined by the occurrence of ≥3 of these features. It is not associated with specific chromosomal abnormalities. Why other options fail: Selective cloacal septation failure would not explain vertebral/cardiac/renal anomalies; Shh mutations cause specific holoprosencephaly, not VACTERL; neural crest defects primarily affect ENS (Hirschsprung), not multiple organ systems this way; ectoderm-derived tissue defects don't explain notochord-related vertebral or midline anomalies.
Question 15 · RET Gain-of-Function in Endocrine Neoplasia
A 32-year-old male with a family history of thyroid cancer is found on screening to have a thyroid nodule. Fine needle aspiration reveals medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC), and serum calcitonin is markedly elevated. Subsequent genetic testing reveals a germline heterozygous activating (gain-of-function) mutation in the RET proto-oncogene (exon 11, M918T). Adrenal imaging shows a 2 cm pheochromocytoma, and serum metanephrines are elevated. Physical exam reveals mucosal neuromas and marfanoid habitus. A diagnosis of MEN2B syndrome is confirmed.

How does this RET gain-of-function mutation differ embryologically from RET loss-of-function mutations seen in Hirschsprung disease?
A RET GOF increases neural crest cell migration distally, causing ganglion cell hyperplasia in the entire GI tract
B RET GOF promotes excessive proliferation and survival of neural crest-derived C cells (calcitonin-secreting cells) and chromaffin cells, leading to medullary thyroid carcinoma and pheochromocytoma
C Both RET GOF and LOF cause Hirschsprung disease, but GOF results in earlier-onset, more aggressive phenotype
D RET GOF prevents differentiation of neural crest cells along the ENS lineage
E RET GOF increases apoptosis of EntANTeric neurons, paradoxically resulting in aganglionosis like Hirschsprung
Answer: B — RET GOF promotes excessive proliferation and survival of neural crest-derived C cells and chromaffin cells

RET (Rearranged during Transfection) is a receptor tyrosine kinase critical for neural crest cell development and function. RET mutations have opposite phenotypes depending on whether they cause loss-of-function (LOF) or gain-of-function (GOF): (1) RET LOF (Hirschsprung disease): impaired GDNF signaling → failed neural crest migration → aganglionosis. (2) RET GOF (MEN2 syndromes): constitutive activation of RET → excessive proliferation and transformation of RET-expressing cells, including medullary thyroid carcinoma (from C cells) and pheochromocytomas (from chromaffin cells). MEN2A (exon 10, 11) and MEN2B (exon 16, M918T) differ in phenotypic severity. The paradox: both mutations affect RET-dependent lineages, but opposite signaling effects cause opposite clinical outcomes. Why other options fail: RET GOF doesn't cause hyperplasia of ENS ganglion cells (those are parasympathetic, not affected by RET similarly); GOF does not prevent differentiation; GOF does not increase apoptosis of ENS neurons.
Question 16 · Endoderm and Splanchnic Mesoderm Differentiation
An anatomy student is asked to identify which germ layers give rise to specific components of the small intestine. The question specifies: "Identify the embryonic origin of the mucosal epithelium, the smooth muscle layers, and the mesentery of the jejunum."

Which of the following correctly identifies the germ layer origins?
A Epithelium from ectoderm; smooth muscle from mesoderm; mesentery from mesenchyme
B Epithelium from endoderm (foregut, midgut, hindgut); smooth muscle from splanchnic mesoderm; mesentery (dorsal) from splanchnic mesoderm
C Epithelium from endoderm; smooth muscle from neural crest; mesentery from paraxial mesoderm
D Epithelium from visceral peritoneum; smooth muscle from parietal peritoneum; mesentery from endoderm
E All GI structures from intermediate mesoderm
Answer: B — Epithelium from endoderm; smooth muscle from splanchnic mesoderm; mesentery from splanchnic mesoderm

The embryologic architecture of the GI tract reflects three germ layer contributions: (1) Endoderm (foregut endoderm → esophagus, stomach, duodenum, liver, pancreas; midgut endoderm → jejunum, ileum, cecum, ascending colon, proximal 2/3 transverse colon; hindgut endoderm → distal transverse colon, descending colon, sigmoid, rectum, upper anal canal): forms all mucosal epithelium and glandular structures. (2) Splanchnic mesoderm (visceral layer of lateral plate mesoderm): surrounds the foregut/midgut/hindgut and differentiates into smooth muscle (circular and longitudinal layers), connective tissue, and blood vessels. The dorsal mesentery is also derived from splanchnic mesoderm and carries the blood/lymphatic vessels. Why other options fail: Ectoderm gives rise to epidermis and nervous system, not GI epithelium; neural crest gives rise to ENS (not smooth muscle proper); paraxial mesoderm gives rise to somites (skeletal muscle, bone, dermis); peritoneum and intermediate mesoderm are not the primary sources.
Question 17 · Ventral Mesentery and Portal Triad
A surgeon is dissecting the hepatogastric ligament during a Whipple procedure (pancreaticoduodenectomy). As she elevates the lesser omentum and hepatoduodenal ligament, she carefully identifies and preserves the three main structures that run through this ligament: the common bile duct (anterior), the hepatic artery (anterior-left), and the portal vein (posterior).

What is the embryologic origin of the hepatoduodenal ligament and its mesentery?
A Dorsal mesentery of the foregut (like the mesentery of the jejunum and ileum)
B Ventral mesentery of the foregut (derived from the falciform ligament, which attaches the liver to the anterior abdominal wall and carries the ligamentum venosum/ductus venosus)
C Fusion of dorsal and ventral mesenteries around the lesser omentum
D Splanchnic mesoderm between the stomach and duodenum
E Parietal peritoneum reflected from the greater omentum
Answer: B — Ventral mesentery of the foregut (falciform ligament derivative)

The ventral mesentery is a mesentery that exists ONLY around the foregut (ventral to the dorsal mesentery). It attaches the foregut organs to the anterior abdominal wall and divides into: (1) Falciform ligament: attaches the liver to the anterior body wall and linea alba, carries the ligamentum venosum/ductus venosus; (2) Hepatoduodenal ligament (part of the lesser omentum): connects the liver to the duodenum and contains the portal triad (hepatic artery, portal vein, common bile duct); (3) Hepatogastric ligament: connects the liver to the lesser curvature of the stomach; (4) Lesser omentum: (hepatogastric + hepatoduodenal). Only the foregut has a ventral mesentery; the midgut and hindgut have dorsal mesentery only. Why other options fail: Dorsal mesentery is separate from the ventral mesentery; fusion is not the embryologic origin; splanchnic mesoderm is the tissue type, not a specific structure; parietal peritoneum is not derived from ventral mesentery.
Question 18 · Gastric Rotation and Anatomical Consequences
A radiology resident is interpreting a barium study of the upper GI tract and notes the characteristic anatomy of the stomach: the fundus is positioned in the left upper quadrant posterior to the spleen, the greater curvature is elongated and positioned inferiorly and anterolaterally, the lesser curvature is shortened and positioned superiorly and anteromedially. The student is asked: "Why does the stomach have this anatomically unusual orientation?"

What embryologic process explains the final anatomical position of the stomach?
A The stomach remains in its original embryologic position within the foregut
B Differential growth rates between the greater and lesser curvatures
C 90° clockwise rotation of the stomach around its longitudinal axis (pylorus rotates to the right, cardia to the left) combined with anterior rotation around a transverse axis (anterior surface rotates to become the visceral surface facing the spleen)
D Counterclockwise rotation of the stomach (opposite to midgut rotation)
E The stomach descends caudally due to gravity during fetal development
Answer: C — 90° clockwise rotation around its longitudinal axis

During weeks 5-6, the stomach rotates 90° clockwise around its longitudinal axis: the pylorus (originally to the right) rotates to the right side, and the cardia (originally to the left) rotates to the left side. This rotation also causes the ventral mesentery (lesser omentum) to shorten and the dorsal mesentery (greater omentum) to lengthen, pulling the greater curvature and spleen anterolaterally and inferiorly. The result is that the stomach's original anterior surface becomes the visceral (posterior) surface facing the spleen and kidney, and the original posterior surface becomes the anterior surface facing the liver and anterior abdominal wall. This "SAD PUCKER" mnemonic helps remember the final anatomy: S=Spleen (posterior), A=Aorta (posterior), D=Diaphragm (posterior), P=Pancreas (posterior, derived from dorsal bud), U=Unchanged kidney position, C=Colon (inferior), K=Kidney (posterior), E=Esophagus (superior), R=Ribs (superior). Why other options fail: Stomach does rotate; differential growth alone doesn't explain the axis; counterclockwise rotation would produce the opposite anatomy; gravity is not the embryologic mechanism.
