LO 1 — Wall Layers
Name the 9 layers of the anterior abdominal wall from superficial to deep. Group by "Rules of 3s".
tap to flip
Answer
Superficial (3): Skin, Camper's fascia (fatty), Scarpa's fascia (membranous)
Middle (3): External oblique, Internal oblique, Transversus abdominis
Deep (3): Transversalis fascia, Extraperitoneal fat, Parietal peritoneum
LO 1 — Rectus Sheath
Describe rectus sheath composition ABOVE vs. BELOW the arcuate line.
tap to flip
Answer
Above arcuate line: Anterior = EO+IO, Posterior = TA+TF
Below arcuate line: Anterior = EO+IO+TA, Posterior = TF only
Arcuate line location: ~50% distance from pubis to xiphoid (~3 cm above ASIS)
LO 1 — Scarpa's Fascia
Scarpa's fascia: what does it continue into, and what does it NOT enter?
tap to flip
Answer
Continues as: Dartos muscle (scrotum) or round ligament sheath (females)
Does NOT continue into: Thigh (blocked by fascia lata) or down leg
Clinical: Straddle injuries spread into scrotum/perineum, NOT down leg
LO 1 — Inguinal Canal
Define the inguinal canal: length, path, locations of deep and superficial rings.
tap to flip
Answer
Length: ~4 cm
Path: Inferolaterally from deep ring (lateral to epigastric vessels) → superficial ring (medial to ASIS)
Deep ring: In transversalis fascia
Superficial ring: Defect in EO aponeurosis medial to ASIS
LO 1 — Inguinal Canal Walls
Name the 4 walls of the inguinal canal: anterior, posterior, superior, inferior.
tap to flip
Answer
Anterior: EO aponeurosis + part of IO
Posterior: Transversalis fascia + conjoint tendon
Superior (roof): Arching fibers of IO + TA
Inferior (floor): Inguinal ligament + lacunar ligament + pectineal ligament
LO 1 — Spermatic Cord
Enumerate the contents of the spermatic cord (mnemonic: 3 A's, 3 V's, 3 N's).
tap to flip
Answer
Arteries (3): Testicular (from aorta!), Artery to vas (IMA), Cremasteric (IEA)
Veins (3): Testicular (IVC/renal), Venae comitantes, Cremasteric
Nerves (3): Genital branch GF, Cremasteric (from GF), Sympathetic
Plus: Vas deferens, lymphatics, fibrous remnants
LO 2 — Cremasteric Reflex
Cremasteric reflex: afferent arc, efferent arc, and clinical significance?
tap to flip
Answer
Afferent: Genital branch GF nerve (L1-L2) from upper medial thigh
Efferent: Genital branch GF nerve → cremasteric muscle
Clinical: ABSENT reflex = red flag for testicular torsion; also absent above L1 (spinal shock)
LO 1 — Testicular Descent
Name 3 stages of testicular descent in males and the role of the gubernaculum.
tap to flip
Answer
Stage 1 (transabdominal): Testis descends to inguinal ring
Stage 2 (inguinal): Testis + gubernaculum traverse canal
Stage 3 (scrotal): Gubernaculum attaches to scrotum; testis pulled down
Females: Gubernaculum = ovarian ligament + round ligament

LO 3 — Peritoneum
Define visceral vs. parietal peritoneum. Name the main peritoneal cavities (males vs. females).
tap to flip
Answer
Visceral: Covers organs
Parietal: Lines wall
Males: Greater sac, omental bursa, 2 paracolic gutters, 2 pericolic gutters, rectovesical pouch
Females: Same BUT rectovesical = rectouterine pouch; tubes open into peritoneal cavity
LO 4 — Hesselbach's Triangle
Define Hesselbach's triangle (inguinal triangle) by its 3 borders. What hernia type occurs here?
tap to flip
Answer
Borders: Inferior epigastric vessels (lateral), Rectus abdominis edge (medial), Inguinal ligament (inferior)
Hernia type: DIRECT inguinal hernia
Mnemonic: R.I.P. = Rectus, Inferior epigastric, Poupart's ligament
LO 4 — Hernia Classification
Compare direct vs. indirect inguinal vs. femoral hernia: location, frequency, strangulation risk.
tap to flip
Answer
Indirect (60%): Lateral to epigastric vessels, passes through both rings, wider neck, lower strangulation risk
Direct (25%): Medial to epigastric vessels, only superficial ring, narrower neck, higher strangulation risk
Femoral (10-15%): Below inguinal ligament, HIGHEST strangulation risk (narrowest)
LO 4 — Other Hernias
Describe umbilical, epigastric, and incisional hernias: location, timing, and management.
tap to flip
Answer
Umbilical: Through linea alba at umbilicus, common in infants (close spontaneously), adults with ↑IAP
Epigastric: Through linea alba between xiphoid-umbilicus, small defect, high incarceration risk, needs repair
Incisional: Through surgical scar, risk ↑ with infection/poor healing/obesity
LO 2 — Abdominal Arteries
Name the arteries of the anterior abdominal wall: inferior epigastric, superficial epigastric, superficial circumflex iliac. Origins and territories?
tap to flip
Answer
Inferior epigastric: From EIA, courses medially in rectus sheath posterior wall
Superficial epigastric: From femoral artery, supplies lower abdomen skin
Superficial circumflex iliac: From femoral artery, supplies ASIS region
Clinical: Superior epigastric (from ITA) + inferior = landmark in hernia surgery
LO 2 — Innervation
Segmental dermatomes T7-T12, L1 of abdominal wall. What are the motor nerves?
tap to flip
Answer
Dermatomes: T7 at xiphoid, T10 at umbilicus, L1 at midpoint umbilicus-ASIS
Motor: T7-T11 intercostal nerves (between obliques), T12 subcostal, L1 iliohypogastric + ilioinguinal
Clinical: Thoracic blocks for upper wall; spinal anesthesia T10 blocks pain below umbilicus
LO 2 — Lymphatics
Lymphatic drainage of anterior abdominal wall: superficial vs. deep, and the watershed at umbilicus.
tap to flip
Answer
Superficial above umbilicus: Ipsilateral axillary nodes
Superficial below umbilicus: Inguinal nodes (Cloquet's node medial to femoral vein)
Deep: Para-aortic nodes
Watershed importance: Skin cancer location dictates spread (head/axilla vs. groin)
LO 2 — TRAM Flap
What is a TRAM flap, what is it used for, and what is the key contraindication?
tap to flip
Answer
TRAM: Transverse Rectus Abdominis Myocutaneous flap
Use: Breast reconstruction (uses rectus + overlying skin/fascia)
Contraindication: Prior hernia repair or liposuction in rectus sheath (disrupts epigastric vessels → flap necrosis)
Alternative: DIEP flap (preserves rectus muscle)

Test Trap Mode: Each statement is either TRUE or FALSE as written. Many are designed to catch common exam errors. Click T or F — then read the explanation carefully.
Trap 01 — LO 1
"Auerbach's plexus controls secretion, while Meissner's plexus controls motility."
FALSE. This is reversed! Auerbach's (myenteric) plexus = MOTILITY (between circular & longitudinal muscle). Meissner's (submucosal) plexus = SECRETION. Mnemonic: "My Sub" — Myenteric = Motility, Submucosal = Secretion.
Trap 02 — LO 1
"In Hirschsprung disease, the DILATED segment of colon is the aganglionic segment that cannot relax."
FALSE. Classic trap! The AGANGLIONIC segment is NARROW and tonically contracted. The DILATED segment is PROXIMAL to the obstruction — it has NORMAL ganglion cells, it's just backed up with stool because nothing can pass through below it.
Trap 03 — LO 2
"Gastrin, CCK, and secretin are secreted directly into the GI lumen where they act on food to promote digestion."
FALSE. GI hormones are ALWAYS secreted into the BLOOD (portal circulation), not the lumen. They travel systemically to reach target organs (e.g., gastrin travels from G cells in the antrum through blood to stimulate parietal cells). Only digestive enzymes go into the lumen.
Trap 04 — LO 3
"Vomiting leads to metabolic acidosis because stomach acid is lost."
FALSE. Vomiting causes METABOLIC ALKALOSIS. When HCl is lost from the stomach, the body loses H⁺ → blood becomes relatively more basic. The correct pairing: vomiting → metabolic alkalosis + hypokalemia; diarrhea → metabolic acidosis + hypokalemia (lose HCO₃⁻).
Trap 05 — LO 4
"A patient with complete vagotomy (all vagal fibers cut) will have no peristalsis because the ENS requires vagal input to function."
FALSE. The ENS can function INDEPENDENTLY of extrinsic innervation (vagus, spinal cord). Transplanted, extrinsically denervated intestinal segments still peristalse. The vagus MODULATES and amplifies ENS activity but is NOT required for basic GI function.
Trap 06 — LO 5
"Nitric oxide (NO) from ENS neurons causes contraction of the LES to maintain the barrier against acid reflux."
FALSE. NO causes RELAXATION, not contraction. In fact, NO is responsible for relaxing the LES to allow swallowed food to pass into the stomach. When inhibitory NO neurons at the LES are destroyed (as in achalasia), the LES FAILS TO RELAX → food cannot enter the stomach → progressive esophageal dilation.
Trap 07 — LO 6
"B12 is absorbed in the jejunum, where the majority of nutrient absorption occurs."
FALSE. B12 (and bile salts) are specifically absorbed in the TERMINAL ILEUM — not the jejunum. The jejunum is the major site of general nutrient absorption, but B12 requires intrinsic factor (from parietal cells) and is exclusively absorbed in the ileum. This is why ileal disease/resection causes B12 deficiency.
Trap 08 — LO 7
"Sympathetic stimulation of the GI tract increases peristalsis and secretion to facilitate digestion during periods of stress."
FALSE. Sympathetic stimulation ("fight or flight") DECREASES GI motility and secretion, INCREASES sphincter tone, and causes vasoconstriction in the gut. It essentially shunts blood and energy away from digestion during stress. It is PARASYMPATHETIC ("rest and digest") that promotes GI function.
