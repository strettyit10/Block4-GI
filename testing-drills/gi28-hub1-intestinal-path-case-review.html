LO 1 — Signs & Symptoms
What is the classic presentation of Crohn disease?
tap to flip
Answer
RLQ pain, abdominal cramping, microcytic anemia, occult GI bleeding

Young adult with skip lesions visible on colonoscopy; may have fistulas, aphthous ulcers, or cobblestone appearance.
LO 1 — Signs & Symptoms
How does ulcerative colitis typically present clinically?
tap to flip
Answer
Bloody diarrhea (may be profuse), abdominal pain, urgency, tenesmus

Continuous mucosal inflammation starting at rectum extending proximally; no skip lesions; increased CRC risk after 7 years.
LO 2 — Pathology
What are the pathologic hallmarks distinguishing Crohn from UC?
tap to flip
Answer
Crohn: transmural involvement, skip lesions, noncaseating granulomas, fistulas
UC: mucosa/submucosa only, continuous inflammation, crypt abscesses, pseudopolyps
LO 2 — Pathology
Define a skip lesion and why it occurs in Crohn disease.
tap to flip
Answer
Skip lesion: areas of normal bowel interspersed between inflamed segments

Occurs in Crohn (transmural) but NOT in UC (continuous inflammation from rectum). Classic finding on colonoscopy.
LO 2 — Pathology
What complications of Crohn disease result from transmural inflammation?
tap to flip
Answer
Fistulas, strictures, sinus tracts, abscess formation, perforation

Transmural means all bowel layers involved; explains why complications penetrate beyond mucosa. Can form fistulas to skin, bladder, other bowel loops.
LO 1 — Signs & Symptoms
What is pseudomembranous colitis and what causes it?
tap to flip
Answer
Caused by Clostridium difficile toxins A & B

Yellow pseudomembranes (necrotic mucosa), volcano lesions on colonoscopy. Risk factors: clindamycin, fluoroquinolones, cephalosporins, prolonged antibiotics.
LO 2 — Pathology
Describe the histologic findings in C. difficile colitis.
tap to flip
Answer
Volcano lesions: erupting necrotic tips of epithelium with pseudomembrane (fibrin, mucus, debris)

Acute inflammation, mucus depletion, cryptitis. Pseudomembrane can span multiple crypts.
LO 1 — Signs & Symptoms
What is the typical presentation of diverticulitis?
tap to flip
Answer
LLQ pain, fever, elevated WBC, nausea, constipation or diarrhea

Inflammation of colonic diverticula; most common in sigmoid (smallest radius → highest intraluminal pressure). Risk factors: low fiber, advanced age, NSAIDs.
LO 2 — Pathology
What anatomic structure do colonic diverticula herniate through, and why is the sigmoid most affected?
tap to flip
Answer
False diverticula herniate at vasa recta (blood vessel penetration sites)

Sigmoid has smallest luminal radius → highest intraluminal pressure by Laplace law → most prone to herniation and diverticulitis.
LO 2 — Pathology
What are the major complications of diverticulitis?
tap to flip
Answer
Pericolic abscess, perforation, fistulization (colovesical, colovaginal), stricture, peritonitis

Pericolic abscess most common complication; can be managed percutaneously. Perforation → free air, sepsis, emergency surgery.
LO 4 — Polyps
What are the three main histologic types of adenomatous polyps?
tap to flip
Answer
Tubular, villous, tubulovillous

Tubular = tubular glands (most common, lowest malignant potential). Villous = finger-like villi (highest risk). Tubulovillous = mixed features. Sessile serrated = different pathway (BRAF, CIMP, MSI).
LO 4 — Polyps
What does "Big, Villous, Bad" refer to in adenoma risk stratification?
tap to flip
Answer
Big: size >10mm
Villous: villous or tubulovillous histology
Bad: high-grade dysplasia

Any of these features raises malignant potential; guide surveillance intervals post-polypectomy.
LO 4 — Molecular
Describe the adenoma-carcinoma sequence (APC-KRAS-p53).
tap to flip
Answer
APC loss → KRAS activation → p53 mutation → CIN (chromosomal instability)

Early adenomas lose APC; accumulate additional mutations over 10–15 years; progression to carcinoma. Explains why CRC risk rises with age and screening detects adenomas before malignancy.
LO 4 — Molecular
What is the serrated pathway and what genes distinguish it from the adenoma-carcinoma sequence?
tap to flip
Answer
Serrated pathway: BRAF mutation → CIMP (CpG island methylator phenotype) → MSI (microsatellite instability)

Contrasts with CIN pathway. Sessile serrated polyps follow this route; can progress to CRC faster; higher risk of right-sided cancer and CIMP-high phenotype.
LO 4 — CRC
How do right-sided and left-sided colorectal cancers differ clinically?
tap to flip
Answer
Right-sided: anemia (iron-deficiency), occult blood, palpable mass, may be asymptomatic
Left-sided: apple-core strictures, obstruction, change in bowel habits, visible blood in stool

Right = larger lumen, proximal obstruction less likely. Left = narrower lumen, obstructive symptoms earlier.
LO 3 — Crohn vs UC
What is the "CHRISTMAS" mnemonic for Crohn disease complications?
tap to flip
Answer
C = Cobblestone, H = fistulas (Holes), R = Rectal bleeding, I = Ileitis, S = Strictures, T = Transmural, M = skip lesions (patchiness), A = Abdominal pain, S = Sigma (sigmoid) spared
LO 1 — Signs & Symptoms
What is toxic megacolon and why is it a medical emergency?
tap to flip
Answer
Dilation of colon (>6cm) during severe IBD flare, fulminant colitis, or C. difficile infection

Risk of perforation, sepsis, death. Presents with abdominal distension, pain, fever, altered mental status. Managed with NPO, IV fluids, antibiotics; surgery if perforation or no improvement in 48h.
LO 1 — IBD Surveillance
What are the surveillance recommendations for IBD-associated CRC?
tap to flip
Answer
Colonoscopy every 1–2 years after 7 years of IBD (earlier if extensive disease or primary sclerosing cholangitis)

PSC + UC = annual surveillance. p53 mutations appear early in IBD-associated CRC. Dysplasia detection is key to preventing malignancy.
LO 4 — Hereditary Syndromes
What gene is mutated in FAP, and what is the cancer risk?
tap to flip
Answer
APC (adenomatous polyposis coli) germline mutation

Hundreds to thousands of adenomas develop by age 20–30; nearly 100% CRC risk by age 50 if untreated. Screening begins age 10–12; prophylactic colectomy often performed by age 20–25.
LO 4 — Hereditary Syndromes
What is Lynch syndrome (HNPCC) and which genes are involved?
tap to flip
Answer
Lynch syndrome (Hereditary Nonpolyposis CRC): germline mismatch repair gene mutations (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2)

Causes microsatellite instability (MSI-high); ~70% lifetime CRC risk by age 70. Accounts for 2–4% of all CRCs. Colonoscopy every 1–2 years from age 20–25.

Trap 1
Ulcerative colitis can involve the entire GI tract, including the small bowel and proximal colon, with skip lesions.
FALSE. UC is characterized by continuous mucosal involvement starting at the rectum and extending proximally—no skip lesions. The terminal ileum is typically spared. Transmural involvement and skip lesions are hallmarks of Crohn disease, not UC.
Trap 2
Pseudomembranous colitis is always caused by antibiotic use.
FALSE. While antibiotic-associated C. difficile is most common, C. difficile colitis can occur without recent antibiotics—especially in hospitalized or immunocompromised patients. Antibiotics are a major risk factor but not required.
Trap 3
True diverticula include only the mucosa herniated through the muscular wall.
FALSE. False diverticula (most common in colon) include only mucosa + submucosa. True diverticula (found in small bowel) include all layers. Colonic diverticula are false—herniation occurs at vasa recta weak points.
Trap 4
The adenoma-carcinoma sequence is driven primarily by BRAF mutations and microsatellite instability.
FALSE. The classic adenoma-carcinoma sequence is driven by APC → KRAS → p53 mutations (CIN pathway). BRAF mutations and MSI characterize the serrated pathway (sessile serrated polyps), which is a different route to CRC.
Trap 5
Right-sided colorectal cancers typically present with obstructive symptoms and "apple-core" appearance on imaging.
FALSE. Left-sided CRCs present with obstruction and apple-core strictures because the left colon has a narrower lumen. Right-sided CRCs have a larger lumen and typically present with anemia and occult blood, not obstruction.
Trap 6
Tubular adenomas have the highest malignant potential and require the most aggressive surveillance.
FALSE. Villous and tubulovillous adenomas have higher malignant potential. Tubular adenomas are the most common but have lowest risk. The "Big, Villous, Bad" rule captures high-risk features: large size, villous histology, and high-grade dysplasia.
Trap 7
Toxic megacolon is a specific complication of Crohn disease and does not occur in ulcerative colitis.
FALSE. Toxic megacolon can occur in both Crohn disease and UC during severe, fulminant flares. It also occurs with fulminant C. difficile colitis. It is not pathognomonic for Crohn disease.
Trap 8
MSI-high colorectal cancers are responsive to checkpoint inhibitors like pembrolizumab.
TRUE. MSI-high tumors have high mutational burden and are responsive to immune checkpoint inhibitors (pembrolizumab, nivolumab). This is a key insight: serrated pathway CRCs with MSI may respond better to immunotherapy than CIN-pathway CRCs.
Crohn Disease vs Ulcerative Colitis Comparison
Feature Crohn Disease Ulcerative Colitis
Pattern of inflammation Skip lesions (patchy) Continuous from rectum
Depth of involvement Transmural (all layers) Mucosa + submucosa only
Location Terminal ileum > colon; can involve entire GI tract Colon + rectum; starts at rectum
Histology Noncaseating granulomas, fissuring ulcers Crypt abscesses, goblet cell loss, pseudopolyps
Key complications Fistulas, strictures, abscesses, perforation Toxic megacolon, fulminant colitis, CRC
Extraintestinal manifestations Arthropathy, erythema nodosum, aphthous ulcers, pyoderma, uveitis PSC (very common), arthropathy, erythema nodosum
Mnemonic CHRISTMAS (Cobblestone, Holes, Rectal bleed, Ileitis, Strictures, Transmural, Mucosal patchiness, Abdominal pain, Sigma spared) ULCCCERS (Ulceration, Loss of goblet cells, Continuous, Cryptitis, Crypt abscess, Epithelial necrosis, Rectum, Stricture rare)
Key distinction: Crohn = transmural + skip lesions + complications (fistula, stricture). UC = mucosal + continuous + CRC/PSC risk. Both are chronic inflammatory bowel diseases with extraintestinal manifestations.
Pseudomembranous Colitis (C. difficile) Essentials
Aspect Details
Causative organism Gram+ anaerobic spore-forming rod; toxigenic strains produce Toxins A & B
Risk factors (4 Cs) Clindamycin, Cephalosporins, fluoroquinolones, prolonged antibiotics (also vancomycin); Hospitalization; Age >65; Immunosuppression
Pathology Volcano lesions (necrotic epithelial eruptions), pseudomembranes (yellow-white fibrin), no granulomas
Diagnosis Stool NAAT/PCR (most sensitive), toxin detection (EIA A/B), colonoscopy with pseudomembranes
Treatment (mild-moderate) Oral vancomycin 125mg QID or fidaxomicin 200mg BID (not IV vanco—poor intraluminal levels)
Treatment (fulminant) Oral vancomycin + IV metronidazole; consider vancomycin enema; possible colectomy if perforation/toxic megacolon
Recurrent infection Fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) 85–90% cure rate
Teaching point: Oral vancomycin is preferred because high intraluminal concentrations are needed; IV vanco does not achieve adequate colon levels.
Diverticular Disease Summary
Feature Details
Definition False (pseudo) diverticula: herniation of mucosa + submucosa through muscular layer at vasa recta weak points
Epidemiology Sigmoid > left colon > right colon (West); right colon more common in Asia; increased with age, low fiber, NSAIDs
Pathophysiology Small luminal radius → high intraluminal pressure (Laplace law); taeniae coli create weak points; herniation at vasa recta
Diverticulitis presentation LLQ pain, fever, elevated WBC; may progress to abscess, perforation, fistula, or stricture
Diverticular bleeding Painless hematochezia; often stops spontaneously; managed conservatively; angiography or surgery if recurrent
Complications Pericolic abscess, perforation, peritonitis, colovesical/colovaginal fistula, stricture
Prevention High-fiber diet, adequate hydration, avoid NSAIDs
Clinical pearl: Most diverticulosis is asymptomatic and discovered incidentally. Acute diverticulitis is infection/inflammation of a diverticulum; uncomplicated cases managed medically; complicated cases (perforation, peritonitis) require surgery.
Adenoma-Carcinoma Progression & Polyp Classification
Polyp Type Histology Malignant Risk Molecular Pathway
Tubular adenoma Tubular glands, regular architecture Lowest APC → KRAS → p53 (CIN)
Villous adenoma Finger-like villi, complex architecture Highest APC → KRAS → p53 (CIN)
Tubulovillous Mixed tubular + villous Intermediate APC → KRAS → p53 (CIN)
Sessile serrated Saw-tooth pattern, shallow crypts Moderate-high (faster progression) BRAF → CIMP → MSI (serrated)
Hyperplastic Small, elongated crypts, minimal dysplasia Very low None (benign)
Big, Villous, Bad rule: Adenomas >10mm, villous histology, or high-grade dysplasia have higher CRC risk and shorter surveillance intervals (3 years or sooner). Sessile serrated lesions require aggressive follow-up due to faster progression.
