LO 1 — Anatomy
Name the four concentric wall layers of the GI tract from innermost to outermost.
tap to flip
Answer
Mucosa → Submucosa → Muscularis externa (propria) → Serosa/Adventitia

Mucosa has 3 sublayers: epithelium, lamina propria, muscularis mucosae.
LO 1 — Plexuses
What are the two ENS plexuses, their alternate names, locations, and primary functions?
tap to flip
Answer
Myenteric (Auerbach's): between circular & longitudinal muscle → MOTILITY

Submucosal (Meissner's): between circular muscle & submucosa → SECRETION

Mnemonic: "My Sub" — Myenteric = Motility, Submucosal = Secretion
LO 1 — Cell Types
Parietal cells: location, two secretory products, and mechanism of HCl production?
tap to flip
Answer
Location: Gastric fundus/body
Products: HCl (via H⁺/K⁺ ATPase) + Intrinsic factor (required for B12 absorption in ileum)

Stimulated by: gastrin (G cells), histamine (H2), ACh (M3)
LO 1 — Cell Types
Match: Chief cells, G cells, Paneth cells, Goblet cells — product and location for each.
tap to flip
Answer
Chief cells: gastric fundus → pepsinogen (→ pepsin at pH <3.5)
G cells: gastric antrum + duodenum → gastrin → ↑HCl
Paneth cells: intestinal crypt base → defensins/lysozyme (innate immunity)
Goblet cells: throughout SI + colon → mucus
LO 1 — Surface Area
Name the three levels of small intestine surface area amplification in order, and the approximate fold-increase each adds.
tap to flip
Answer
1. Plicae circulares — large folds of mucosa + submucosa; ×3
2. Villi — finger-like mucosal projections; ×10
3. Microvilli (brush border) — on each enterocyte; ×20

Total ≈ 600-fold amplification over a simple tube.
LO 2 — DEEP MASS
What does DEEP MASS stand for? List all 8 functions of the GI tract.
tap to flip
Answer
Digestion · Elimination · Endocrine · Protection
Motility · Absorption · Secretion · Storage

Key: GI tract = largest endocrine organ in the body (Endocrine function = "E")
LO 2 — Hormones vs. Enzymes
GI hormones vs. digestive enzymes — where does each get secreted, and why does it matter?
tap to flip
Answer
GI hormones (gastrin, CCK, secretin, GIP, GLP-1) → secreted into BLOOD → travel systemically → serum measurable

Digestive enzymes (amylase, pepsin, lipase, trypsin) → secreted into LUMEN → act locally on food
LO 3 — Fluid Balance
How much fluid enters the GI tract daily? How much is excreted in feces? Where is the majority absorbed?
tap to flip
Answer
Total input: ~9–10 L/day (2L ingested + 7–8L from secretions)
Small intestine absorbs: ~8.5 L
Colon absorbs: ~400 mL
Feces: ~100–200 mL

Staggering efficiency — almost all reclaimed.
LO 3 — pH Gradient
What is the approximate pH at: stomach, proximal duodenum, jejunum, ileum, and colon?
tap to flip
Answer
Stomach: 1.5–2.0 (HCl from parietal cells)
Proximal duodenum: 4–5 (acid chyme + pancreatic HCO₃⁻)
Jejunum: 6–7 (progressive neutralization)
Ileum: 7.0–7.5
Colon: 5.5–7.0 (bacterial fermentation lowers pH)
LO 4 — ENS
How many neurons does the ENS contain, and what is its defining functional property?
tap to flip
Answer
~500 million neurons (more than the spinal cord)

Defining property: functions completely independently of the CNS — can coordinate peristalsis, secretion, and absorption without brain/spinal cord input ("second brain")
LO 4 — ENS Neurotransmitters
What do excitatory ENS motor neurons release? What do inhibitory ENS motor neurons release?
tap to flip
Answer
Excitatory: Acetylcholine (ACh) + Substance P → smooth muscle CONTRACTION

Inhibitory: VIP (vasoactive intestinal peptide) + Nitric Oxide (NO) → smooth muscle RELAXATION
LO 5 — Peristaltic Reflex
Describe the two simultaneous limbs of the peristaltic reflex and the neurotransmitters involved in each.
tap to flip
Answer
Ascending excitation (oral/above bolus): ACh + Substance P → circular muscle CONTRACTS → propels content forward

Descending inhibition (aboral/below bolus): VIP + NO → circular muscle RELAXES → creates space for content to move into
LO 5 — Receptors
Name the three ENS sensory receptor types and the stimulus each detects.
tap to flip
Answer
Mechanoreceptors: detect wall stretch/distension → trigger peristalsis
Chemoreceptors: detect pH, nutrients, bile acids → trigger hormone release (CCK, secretin)
Osmoreceptors: detect luminal osmolarity → regulate gastric emptying rate
LO 6 — Blood Supply
Which artery supplies each GI division: foregut, midgut, hindgut? Give a structure served by each.
tap to flip
Answer
Celiac trunk: foregut (stomach, duodenum 1st/2nd, liver, spleen, pancreas)
SMA: midgut (duodenum 3rd/4th → proximal 2/3 transverse colon)
IMA: hindgut (distal 1/3 transverse colon → upper rectum)
LO 7 — Autonomics
Compare parasympathetic vs. sympathetic effects on GI motility, secretion, and sphincter tone.
tap to flip
Answer
Parasympathetic (vagus/pelvic nerve): ↑motility, ↑secretion, ↓sphincter tone ("rest and digest")

Sympathetic (splanchnic nerves): ↓motility, ↓secretion, ↑sphincter tone, vasoconstriction ("fight or flight")
LO 7 — Cephalic Phase
What is the cephalic phase of digestion, what nerve mediates it, and what does it prepare the stomach to do?
tap to flip
Answer
Cephalic phase: sight, smell, taste, or thought of food triggers anticipatory GI activation BEFORE food arrives

Mediated by: vagus nerve (CN X) → ACh → stimulates parietal cells (HCl), chief cells (pepsinogen), gastric motility

Prepares the stomach to receive and begin digesting food

⚠️ Test Trap Mode: Each statement is either TRUE or FALSE as written. Many are designed to catch common exam errors. Click T or F — then read the explanation carefully.
Trap 01 — LO 1
"Auerbach's plexus controls secretion, while Meissner's plexus controls motility."
FALSE. This is reversed! Auerbach's (myenteric) plexus = MOTILITY (between circular & longitudinal muscle). Meissner's (submucosal) plexus = SECRETION. Mnemonic: "My Sub" — Myenteric = Motility, Submucosal = Secretion.
Trap 02 — LO 1
"In Hirschsprung disease, the DILATED segment of colon is the aganglionic segment that cannot relax."
FALSE. Classic trap! The AGANGLIONIC segment is NARROW and tonically contracted. The DILATED segment is PROXIMAL to the obstruction — it has NORMAL ganglion cells, it's just backed up with stool because nothing can pass through below it.
Trap 03 — LO 2
"Gastrin, CCK, and secretin are secreted directly into the GI lumen where they act on food to promote digestion."
FALSE. GI hormones are ALWAYS secreted into the BLOOD (portal circulation), not the lumen. They travel systemically to reach target organs (e.g., gastrin travels from G cells in the antrum through blood to stimulate parietal cells). Only digestive enzymes go into the lumen.
Trap 04 — LO 3
"Vomiting leads to metabolic acidosis because stomach acid is lost."
FALSE. Vomiting causes METABOLIC ALKALOSIS. When HCl is lost from the stomach, the body loses H⁺ → blood becomes relatively more basic. The correct pairing: vomiting → metabolic alkalosis + hypokalemia; diarrhea → metabolic acidosis + hypokalemia (lose HCO₃⁻).
Trap 05 — LO 4
"A patient with complete vagotomy (all vagal fibers cut) will have no peristalsis because the ENS requires vagal input to function."
FALSE. The ENS can function INDEPENDENTLY of extrinsic innervation (vagus, spinal cord). Transplanted, extrinsically denervated intestinal segments still peristalse. The vagus MODULATES and amplifies ENS activity but is NOT required for basic GI function.
Trap 06 — LO 5
"Nitric oxide (NO) from ENS neurons causes contraction of the LES to maintain the barrier against acid reflux."
FALSE. NO causes RELAXATION, not contraction. In fact, NO is responsible for relaxing the LES to allow swallowed food to pass into the stomach. When inhibitory NO neurons at the LES are destroyed (as in achalasia), the LES FAILS TO RELAX → food cannot enter the stomach → progressive esophageal dilation.
Trap 07 — LO 6
"B12 is absorbed in the jejunum, where the majority of nutrient absorption occurs."
FALSE. B12 (and bile salts) are specifically absorbed in the TERMINAL ILEUM — not the jejunum. The jejunum is the major site of general nutrient absorption, but B12 requires intrinsic factor (from parietal cells) and is exclusively absorbed in the ileum. This is why ileal disease/resection causes B12 deficiency.
Trap 08 — LO 7
"Sympathetic stimulation of the GI tract increases peristalsis and secretion to facilitate digestion during periods of stress."
FALSE. Sympathetic stimulation ("fight or flight") DECREASES GI motility and secretion, INCREASES sphincter tone, and causes vasoconstriction in the gut. It essentially shunts blood and energy away from digestion during stress. It is PARASYMPATHETIC ("rest and digest") that promotes GI function.
