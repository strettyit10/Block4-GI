LO 1 — Infectious Esophagitis
Name the three major infectious causes of esophagitis and their typical patient populations.
tap to flip
Answer
HSV esophagitis: CD4 <50 · vesicles → shallow ulcers
CMV esophagitis: CD4 <200 · deeper ulcers · solitary · "apple core"
Candida esophagitis: immunocompromised or prolonged antibiotics · pseudomembranous plaques
LO 1 — Infectious Histology
What are the key histologic findings in HSV and CMV esophagitis?
tap to flip
Answer
HSV: Cowdry A intranuclear inclusions, multinucleated giant cells, epithelial necrosis
CMV: owl's eye appearance (intranuclear + eosinophilic cytoplasmic inclusions)
LO 2 — GERD Pathophysiology
What are the primary mechanisms of GERD?
tap to flip
Answer
LES dysfunction: decreased pressure or inappropriate relaxation
Increased gastric pressure: delayed gastric emptying, obesity, pregnancy
Reduced clearance: decreased motility, reduced saliva
LO 2 — GERD Histology
What histologic changes define reflux esophagitis?
tap to flip
Answer
Changes: basal zone hyperplasia, epithelial necrosis, intraepithelial lymphocytes
Complications: stricture, Barrett's metaplasia, adenocarcinoma
LO 2 — Barrett's Esophagus
Define Barrett's esophagus and its significance.
tap to flip
Answer
Definition: columnar metaplasia >3 cm above GE junction
Risk: adenocarcinoma 0.2–0.5% per year
Diagnosis: endoscopy with biopsy showing intestinal metaplasia
LO 3 — Esophageal SCC
What are major risk factors for esophageal squamous cell carcinoma?
tap to flip
Answer
Risk factors: tobacco, alcohol (synergistic), caustic injury, achalasia, HPV
Location: upper/middle third
Prognosis: poor (5-year ~25%) due to late diagnosis
LO 3 — Esophageal Adenocarcinoma
What is the relationship between Barrett's esophagus and adenocarcinoma?
tap to flip
Answer
Progression: Barrett's → dysplasia (low/high) → invasive cancer
Location: lower third / GE junction
Epidemiology: rising in white males, age 40–70
LO 4 — Acute Gastritis
Define acute gastritis and list major causes.
tap to flip
Answer
Definition: acute mucosal inflammation (edema + neutrophils, minimal fibrosis)
Causes: H. pylori (most common), NSAIDs, alcohol, stress, aspirin, caustic injury
Presentation: epigastric pain, nausea, hematemesis if erosive
LO 5 — Erosion vs. Ulcer
What is the histologic distinction between erosion and ulcer?
tap to flip
Answer
Erosion: limited to MUCOSA (epithelium ± lamina propria) · does NOT breach muscularis mucosae
Ulcer: extends PAST muscularis mucosae into submucosa/muscle
Healing: erosions heal without scar · ulcers leave scar
LO 6 — Chronic Gastritis Types
Compare Type A (autoimmune) vs. Type B (H. pylori) chronic gastritis.
tap to flip
Answer
Type A: body/fundus · anti-parietal cell + anti-IF antibodies · achlorhydria, pernicious anemia
Type B: antrum · H. pylori+ · hypersecretion acid early, then achlorhydria late
Type C: bile reflux/NSAID · antrum + body · minimal inflammation
LO 7 — H. pylori PUD Pathogenesis
How does H. pylori cause peptic ulcer disease?
tap to flip
Answer
Mechanism: colonizes antrum → antritis → ↑gastrin → ↑acid → ulcer
Duodenal ulcer: >90% H. pylori+
Gastric ulcer: 60–70% H. pylori+
Key: hypersecretion of gastric acid is the driver
LO 7 — NSAID PUD
What is the mechanism of NSAID-induced PUD?
tap to flip
Answer
Mechanism: inhibit COX → ↓prostaglandins → loss of mucosal protection, ↓mucus, ↓HCO3-
Location: usually gastric ulcers (antrum)
Risk factors: age >60, H. pylori co-infection, high-dose NSAIDs
LO 8 — PUD Complications
What are the major complications of peptic ulcer disease?
tap to flip
Answer
Hemorrhage: erosion into artery → hematemesis/melena
Perforation: free air under diaphragm → peritonitis
Penetration: erosion into pancreas → pancreatitis
Obstruction: scarring at pylorus → vomiting, weight loss
LO 8 — PUD Treatment
What are the main approaches to PUD treatment?
tap to flip
Answer
H. pylori: triple therapy (PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin) × 10–14 days
NSAID ulcers: stop NSAID, PPI or H2-blocker
Acid suppression: PPIs (most effective), H2-blockers
Surgery: for complications (uncontrolled bleed, perforation, obstruction)
LO 9 — Precancerous Gastric Lesions
What precancerous lesions predispose to gastric carcinoma?
tap to flip
Answer
Sequence: chronic gastritis → atrophic gastritis → intestinal metaplasia → dysplasia → adenocarcinoma (Correa cascade)
Key drivers: H. pylori (most important), pernicious anemia (type A)
LO 10 — Lauren Classification
Describe the Lauren classification of gastric adenocarcinoma.
tap to flip
Answer
Intestinal type: organized glandular structure · associated with H. pylori, metaplasia · better prognosis
Diffuse type: poorly cohesive cells, signet-ring morphology · worse prognosis, young patients · often CDH1 mutation
LO 11 — GIST
What is a GIST and what is its key genetic driver?
tap to flip
Answer
GIST: arises from Cajal cells (pacemaker cells) · mesenchymal, not epithelial
Genetics: KIT gene mutation (c-KIT tyrosine kinase) · responds to imatinib
Prognosis: depends on mitotic rate and size
LO 12 — MALT Lymphoma
Describe MALT lymphoma and its association with H. pylori.
tap to flip
Answer
MALT lymphoma: indolent B-cell lymphoma from mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue
H. pylori: ~90% of gastric MALT lymphomas are H. pylori+
Treatment: H. pylori eradication alone for early stages · chemotherapy for advanced
LO 13 — H. pylori Associations
What diseases are associated with H. pylori infection?
tap to flip
Answer
Upper GI: antral gastritis, duodenal ulcer (>90%), gastric ulcer (60–70%), gastric cancer (>80%)
Extra-GI: MALT lymphoma, ITP, IDA
Mechanism: chronic inflammation, hypersecretion (duodenal) vs. hypochlorhydria (gastric)

Trap 1
Barrett's esophagus inevitably progresses to adenocarcinoma in all patients.
FALSE. Barrett's carries increased risk (0.2–0.5%/year) but NOT all progress to cancer. Many remain stable or regress with PPI therapy. High-grade dysplasia = cancer until proven otherwise.
Trap 2
CMV esophagitis typically requires CD4 <200 cells/μL, whereas HSV requires CD4 <50 cells/μL.
TRUE. CMV reactivates at higher CD4 (~200 cells/μL), while HSV requires severe immunosuppression (<50 cells/μL). Key thresholds for board exams.
Trap 3
An erosion and an ulcer are histologically identical; the difference is only clinical severity.
FALSE. Erosions are limited to MUCOSA; ulcers penetrate PAST muscularis mucosae. This is a fundamental histologic distinction. Ulcers heal with scar; erosions do not.
Trap 4
H. pylori causes duodenal ulcers through decreased gastric acid production.
FALSE. H. pylori causes duodenal ulcers through INCREASED acid (not decreased). Antritis → ↑gastrin → ↑acid → ulcer.
Trap 5
NSAIDs and H. pylori cause PUD in equal proportions (each ~50%).
FALSE. H. pylori is responsible for >90% of duodenal ulcers and 60–70% of gastric ulcers. H. pylori dominates the epidemiology of PUD.
Trap 6
Intestinal-type gastric cancer has better prognosis than diffuse-type because of H. pylori association.
TRUE. Intestinal-type has better prognosis (~40% 5-year) vs. diffuse (<5% 5-year). Intestinal-type arises from H. pylori-induced atrophy/metaplasia.
Trap 7
GIST is an epithelial malignancy arising from gastric glandular cells.
FALSE. GIST is a mesenchymal tumor arising from Cajal cells (pacemaker cells), NOT epithelial cells. Driven by KIT mutations.
Trap 8
MALT lymphoma of the stomach responds to H. pylori eradication in early-stage disease.
TRUE. ~90% of gastric MALT lymphomas are H. pylori+. Eradication alone is sufficient for ~75% of early-stage (stage I) MALT lymphomas.
