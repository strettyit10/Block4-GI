Learning Objective 1
Know the organization and structure of the parts of the GI tract
Question 1 Structure Medium
A pathology student examines a cross-section of the GI tract and identifies the following layers from lumen to outer surface: epithelium → loose connective tissue → thin band of smooth muscle → denser connective tissue containing a nerve plexus → inner circular muscle → outer longitudinal muscle → serosal membrane. The "thin band of smooth muscle" identified at this layer is:
✓ Correct Answer: B — Muscularis mucosa
AThe lamina propria is the loose connective tissue immediately under the epithelium — it contains glands, blood vessels, and immune cells but is NOT muscle.
B✓ The muscularis mucosa is a thin band of smooth muscle at the deepest part of the mucosa (below the lamina propria). It helps with local mucosal movement and folding. Together with the epithelium and lamina propria, it constitutes the mucosa.
CThe inner circular layer is the first (inner) layer of the muscularis externa — it is a thick, prominent layer responsible for peristaltic contractions, not a thin band at this position.
DThe submucosa is the connective tissue layer between the muscularis mucosa and the muscularis externa — it contains the Meissner's (submucosal) plexus, as described in the question stem.
EThe serosa is the outermost layer (visceral peritoneum), not an inner smooth muscle band.
Teaching Point — GI Wall Layers (lumen → out): Mucosa (epithelium + lamina propria + muscularis mucosa) → Submucosa (Meissner's plexus) → Muscularis externa (Auerbach's/myenteric plexus between layers) → Adventitia/Serosa. The muscularis mucosa is a thin, often overlooked band that is the boundary between the mucosa and submucosa.
Question 2 Structure Medium
A researcher studying the enteric nervous system identifies two major intrinsic nerve plexuses of the GI tract. Plexus A lies in the submucosa and primarily regulates secretion and blood flow. Plexus B lies between the inner circular and outer longitudinal layers of the muscularis externa and primarily coordinates peristaltic contractions. Which of the following correctly names Plexus A and Plexus B, respectively?
✓ Correct Answer: B
B✓ Meissner's plexus (submucosal plexus) is in the submucosa → controls glandular secretion and blood flow. Auerbach's plexus (myenteric plexus) lies between the two layers of muscularis externa → coordinates peristalsis. Both are part of the enteric nervous system (ENS).
AThis reverses the two plexuses. Auerbach's is myenteric (between muscle layers), not submucosal.
EThis also reverses the locations. Meissner's = submucosal; Auerbach's = myenteric.
Mnemonic: "Meissner's = Mucosa-adjacent (submucosal) = secretion and blood flow." "Auerbach's = Action (peristalsis) between muscle layers." In Hirschsprung disease, the aganglionic segment lacks Auerbach's AND Meissner's ganglia in the distal colon → no relaxation → functional obstruction.
Learning Objective 2
Know the overall histologic features and functions of the different parts of the GI tract
Question 3 Histology Medium
On a histology slide, a student identifies a segment of the GI tract lined by non-keratinized stratified squamous epithelium. The outer coat of this segment is adventitia rather than serosa. These findings are MOST consistent with which of the following?
✓ Correct Answer: D — Esophagus
D✓ Two distinguishing features of the esophagus: (1) lined by non-keratinized stratified squamous epithelium (protective, not absorptive), and (2) surrounded by adventitia rather than serosa. This lack of serosa is clinically significant — esophageal cancers spread more readily to adjacent structures (trachea, aorta) than cancers in serosal-covered GI organs.
AThe stomach is lined by simple columnar epithelium with gastric pits and is covered by serosa.
B/EThe small intestine (jejunum, duodenum) is lined by simple columnar epithelium with villi and is covered by serosa.
CThe colon is lined by simple columnar epithelium (no villi, many goblet cells) and is covered by serosa.
Teaching Point: The esophagus is the only part of the GI tract covered primarily by adventitia (not serosa). The non-keratinized stratified squamous epithelium is protective — the esophagus has no absorptive or digestive function. The transition from squamous → columnar at the GEJ is the site of Barrett's metaplasia.
Question 4 Histology Medium
During a gross anatomy lab, a student notes that the stomach differs from the rest of the GI tract in the arrangement of its muscularis externa. A dissection of the stomach wall reveals three distinct muscle layers rather than the usual two. What is the additional layer unique to the stomach, and what is its functional advantage?
✓ Correct Answer: B
B✓ The stomach has an additional inner oblique layer (innermost, unique to stomach), in addition to the inner circular and outer longitudinal layers present throughout the GI tract. This third layer allows the stomach to perform churning and trituration — vigorous mechanical mixing that breaks food into chyme — a function that is more demanding than simple peristalsis.
EThe pyloric sphincter IS formed by a thickened circular muscle layer, but this is not described as an additional layer — it is a local modification of the existing circular layer, not a unique third layer.
Stomach muscularis layers (inner → outer): Oblique (unique) → Circular → Longitudinal. This 3-layer arrangement is what makes the stomach uniquely capable of churning food. This is a classic USMLE histology identification question.
Question 5 Histology Medium
A pathologist examining a gastric biopsy describes: "numerous shallow surface invaginations lined by tall columnar cells; these invaginations lead into deeper tubular structures in the lamina propria." The tall columnar cells lining the surface and the invaginations secrete an alkaline mucus that protects the gastric epithelium. These structures and cells are identified as:
✓ Correct Answer: C
C✓ Gastric pits are the surface invaginations of the stomach mucosa. They are lined by surface mucus cells — tall columnar cells that secrete alkaline mucus, forming the critical first layer of protection against self-digestion by HCl. Each pit leads to 3–7 gastric glands in the lamina propria.
AVilli are fingerlike projections in the small intestine (not stomach). Enterocytes have a microvillus brush border for absorption, not protection.
EChief cells are found at the BASE of gastric glands (not the surface pits). They secrete pepsinogen, an enzyme precursor, not surface mucus.
Teaching Point: The gastric mucosal barrier consists of two layers of mucin: (1) surface mucus cells (alkaline mucin → pits + surface), and (2) mucus neck cells (acidic mucin → gland neck). H. pylori and NSAIDs disrupt this barrier → increased susceptibility to ulceration.
Learning Objective 3
Be able to relate histologic features with functional differences among the parts of the GI tract
Question 6 Integration Hard
A 32-year-old woman with Crohn's disease undergoes resection of her terminal ileum. Postoperatively, her physician explains that she will need monthly vitamin B12 injections for the rest of her life and may require bile salt supplementation. Which histological feature of the terminal ileum is directly responsible for both of these absorptive functions?
✓ Correct Answer: D
D✓ The terminal ileum enterocytes express two key apical transporters unique to this segment: Cubilin (with amnionless) — absorbs the IF-B12 complex; and ASBT (apical sodium-dependent bile acid transporter) — reabsorbs conjugated bile salts (enterohepatic circulation). Resection of the terminal ileum removes both transporters permanently.
ABrunner's glands are in the duodenum submucosa (not ileum) and secrete alkaline mucus, not transporters for B12 or bile salts.
BPeyer's patches contain M cells that sample antigens for immune surveillance — they are not responsible for B12 or bile salt absorption.
Teaching Point: The terminal ileum is the ONLY site for: (1) IF-B12 complex absorption (cubilin/amnionless receptors) and (2) enterohepatic bile salt recirculation (ASBT). Crohn's ileitis or ileal resection causes both B12 deficiency (megaloblastic anemia) AND bile salt malabsorption (→ fat malabsorption → steatorrhea + fat-soluble vitamin deficiencies + cholesterol gallstones).
Question 7 Integration Medium
A student asks why the large intestine can reabsorb water without the specialized surface area features (villi, plicae) of the small intestine. Which of the following correctly explains how the large intestine achieves water reabsorption despite this simpler architecture?
✓ Correct Answer: C
C✓ The large intestine relies on: (1) deep crypts of Lieberkühn (absorptive surface); (2) slow transit — haustral contractions caused by teniae coli churn and expose contents to the mucosa for extended time periods; (3) active Na⁺ absorption (electrogenic Na⁺ transport via ENaC + Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase) drawing water osmotically. The design emphasizes slow contact time rather than massive surface area.
BThe large intestine definitively has NO villi — this is a fundamental histological distinction. Any "folds" seen (haustra) are formed by teniae coli and are not villi.
DBrunner's glands are uniquely found in the duodenal submucosa, not the colon.
Teaching Point: Understanding why each region lacks certain features is as important as knowing what's present. The large intestine uses slow haustral transit + Na⁺-driven water reabsorption. In diarrhea (e.g., cholera), the large intestine is overwhelmed by secretory diarrhea that exceeds its absorptive capacity, demonstrating its physiological limits.
Learning Objective 4
Identify and understand the various cell types in the GI epithelia and their specific contributions to GI function
Question 8 Cell Types Hard
A pathology exam slide shows two different cells from the base of a fundic gastric gland. Cell A is round, stains brightly eosinophilic (pink), and has a canalicular system lined by microvilli extending into its cytoplasm. Cell B is columnar, stains basophilic (blue-purple), and has abundant granules at its apex. Which of the following correctly identifies these cells and their secretory products?
✓ Correct Answer: D
D✓ Cell A = Parietal cell: eosinophilic (packed with mitochondria for H⁺/K⁺-ATPase); unique intracellular canaliculi lined by microvilli (↑ surface area for HCl secretion); secretes HCl + intrinsic factor. Cell B = Chief cell: basophilic (abundant RER for pepsinogen protein synthesis); apical zymogen granules containing pepsinogen.
AClassic swap trap — this reverses the staining of the two cells. Remember: Parietal = Pink (mitochondria); Chief = Colorless/blue (RER).
Mnemonic for gastric gland cell staining:
• Parietal cell = Pink (eosinophilic) — Packed with mitochondria for the Proton pump
• Chief cell = Cold blue (basophilic) — abundant Rough ER for zymogen synthesis
This is one of the most commonly tested histological distinctions on Step 1.
Question 9 Cell Types Medium
A 60-year-old woman with a 10-year history of poorly controlled autoimmune gastritis develops fatigue and difficulty walking. Laboratory findings: Hgb 7.5 g/dL, MCV 118 fL, hypersegmented neutrophils on smear, serum B12 <100 pg/mL. Anti-parietal cell antibodies are positive. The gastric cell type destroyed by this autoimmune process normally produces which of the following pair of secretory products?
✓ Correct Answer: B
B✓ Anti-parietal cell antibodies destroy parietal cells. Parietal cells are uniquely responsible for secreting both HCl (via H⁺/K⁺-ATPase) AND intrinsic factor (needed for B12 absorption in the terminal ileum). The loss of intrinsic factor leads to B12 deficiency → megaloblastic anemia (high MCV, hypersegmented PMNs) + neurological symptoms (subacute combined degeneration).
EIntrinsic factor is parietal cell product (correct), but pepsinogen is from chief cells (not parietal cells) — making this answer incorrect.
Teaching Point: Remember that parietal cells produce BOTH HCl and intrinsic factor. Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) reduce acid but do not directly affect intrinsic factor in short-term use. Autoimmune pernicious anemia destroys parietal cells → loses both products simultaneously. Treat with IM vitamin B12 (bypasses the need for IF-mediated absorption).
Question 10 Cell Types Medium
A researcher studying intestinal defense identifies cells located at the base of the intestinal crypts that have large, brightly eosinophilic apical granules. These cells are distinct from goblet cells (which have pale mucin) and from enterocytes (which have a brush border). The granules in these cells contain which of the following antimicrobial compounds?
✓ Correct Answer: C
C✓ These are Paneth cells — specialized secretory cells at the base of intestinal crypts (small and large intestine). Their eosinophilic granules contain zinc, lysozyme, and defensins (alpha-defensins/cryptdins). They protect the intestinal stem cell niche from luminal bacteria and modulate the commensal microbiota. Paneth cell dysfunction is implicated in Crohn's disease pathogenesis.
AHCl and pepsinogen are gastric (parietal cells and chief cells, respectively), not intestinal.
BIgA is secreted by plasma cells in the lamina propria (transcytosed by intestinal epithelium as secretory IgA), not by Paneth cells.
Teaching Point: Paneth cells = "Intestinal innate immune cells." Location: base of crypts of Lieberkühn (small > large intestine). Appearance: eosinophilic (pink) apical granules — similar to parietal cells in staining but in the intestine. Paneth cell metaplasia in chronic colitis is a sign of long-standing intestinal inflammation (a Step 2 clue for Crohn's vs UC).
Learning Objective 5
Be able to distinguish the small and large intestines with regard to organization, structure, and function
Question 11 Histology Medium
A pathologist reviews a biopsy and reports: "No villi identified; deep crypts present; very abundant goblet cells; outer longitudinal smooth muscle forms three discrete ribbon-like bands rather than a continuous layer." These findings are MOST consistent with which region of the GI tract?
✓ Correct Answer: D — Colon
D✓ The colon is uniquely identified by: (1) No villi (all small intestine segments have villi), (2) Very abundant goblet cells (more than anywhere in the small intestine), (3) Teniae coli — three thick bands of longitudinal smooth muscle (a modification of the muscularis externa unique to the colon). The teniae coli cause haustra (sacculations).
A/B/CAll three small intestine segments have villi and a continuous outer longitudinal muscle layer (NOT teniae coli). The absence of villi immediately rules out the small intestine.
EThe esophagus has no villi and no goblet cells in its normal state — but it is lined by stratified squamous epithelium (not columnar), has no crypts, and no teniae coli.
Teaching Point: Key differentiators — Small intestine has: villi + central lacteals + plicae circulares + Paneth cells + (Brunner's glands in duodenum or Peyer's patches in ileum). Large intestine has: no villi + teniae coli + very many goblet cells + deeper crypts + appendix (abundant lymphoid tissue). This distinction is tested repeatedly on Step 1 histology slides.
Question 12 Integration Hard
A medical student comparing histological slides of different small intestine segments notes the following: Slide X has branched mucus-secreting glands in the submucosa. Slide Y has tall, prominent villi with well-developed plicae circulares. Slide Z has prominent submucosal lymphoid nodule aggregates. Which of the following correctly matches each slide to its intestinal segment?
✓ Correct Answer: D
D✓ Slide X = Duodenum: Brunner's glands are uniquely in the SUBMUCOSA of the duodenum. Slide Y = Jejunum: The jejunum has the tallest villi and most prominent plicae circulares, maximizing absorption surface area. Slide Z = Ileum: Peyer's patches (submucosal lymphoid aggregates) are concentrated in the ileum.
A/B/C/EThe key distinguishing features of each segment are Brunner's glands (submucosa = duodenum ONLY), Peyer's patches (submucosa = ileum), and tall villi/plicae (jejunum). Mix-ups on any of these three is a common Step 1 error.
Summary — Small Intestine Segment Identifiers:
• Duodenum — Brunner's glands (submucosa); receives bile + pancreatic enzymes; broad leaf-shaped villi
• Jejunum — tallest villi; most plicae circulares; primary absorptive workhorse
• Ileum — Peyer's patches (submucosal lymphoid tissue); most goblet cells; absorbs B12 + bile salts; smaller/fewer villi
Goblet cells increase duodenum → ileum (inverse of villi height).
