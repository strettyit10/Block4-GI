Describe the basal electrical rhythm (BER) of the GI tract and discuss its role in GI motility.
Question 1 Physiology Medium
A physiology experiment records rhythmic membrane potential oscillations of −65 to −45 mV at 12 cycles/min in an isolated segment of duodenal smooth muscle. These oscillations persist after tetrodotoxin (a sodium channel blocker that silences neurons) but do NOT trigger contraction. When acetylcholine is bath-applied, action potentials appear superimposed on the oscillations and contraction follows. Which of the following BEST characterizes the cells generating the initial rhythmic oscillations?
✓ Correct answer: B — Interstitial cells of Cajal (ICC)
AEnteric neurons modulate motility but do NOT generate the BER. The BER persists after tetrodotoxin (which silences neurons), confirming it is myogenic — not neurogenic.
BCORRECT. ICC at the myenteric plexus are the pacemaker cells of GI smooth muscle. They generate the BER (slow waves) — rhythmic graded depolarizations that set the MAXIMUM frequency of contraction. ACh from ENS neurons depolarizes smooth muscle to threshold → action potentials ride the slow wave → Ca²⁺ influx → contraction.
CSmooth muscle cells are the EFFECTORS of contraction. They contract when action potentials arrive from ICC, but they do not generate the BER themselves.
DThe BER persists after vagotomy — it is NOT vagally dependent. ICC are intrinsically rhythmic.
EMeissner's plexus (submucosal) controls secretion and local reflexes — not pacemaking or the BER.
🎯 Step 1 Key PointICC = GI pacemakers. BER (slow waves) ≠ contraction by itself. ACh from ENS shifts the membrane to threshold → action potentials → Ca²⁺ → contraction. BER frequency: Duodenum 12/min > Ileum 8–9/min > Stomach 3/min. ICC dysfunction (e.g., in diabetes, scleroderma) → gastroparesis and impaired motility.
Question 2 Physiology Medium
A student is asked to arrange the following GI segments in order of basal electrical rhythm (BER) frequency from LOWEST to HIGHEST. Which of the following correctly ranks them?
✓ Correct answer: C — Stomach < Ileum < Duodenum
AIncorrect. Duodenum has the HIGHEST BER (12/min), not the lowest.
BIncorrect. The ileum (8/min) is intermediate — not the highest.
CCORRECT. Stomach = 3/min (lowest) → Ileum ≈ 8–9/min → Duodenum = 12/min (highest). This aborally increasing gradient ensures chyme is always "pulled" forward — the intestine beats faster than the stomach, preventing retrograde flow.
DThe ileum is NOT the lowest. Stomach (3/min) is the slowest segment.
EDuodenum is the FASTEST segment, not the slowest. This ranking is inverted.
🎯 Step 1 Mnemonic"3 – 8 – 12" → Stomach (3/min), Ileum (8/min), Duodenum (12/min). The duodenum's high BER = the physiological "aborad gradient" that drives chyme forward. The ileocecal region acts as a natural brake before the colon.
LO2
State the mechanism causing smooth muscle contraction in the GI tract.
Question 3 Physiology Medium
A second-year student is reviewing GI smooth muscle physiology. She notes that smooth muscle lacks troponin. Which of the following MOST accurately describes the sequence of events that leads from GI smooth muscle depolarization to cross-bridge cycling?
✓ Correct answer: C — Ca²⁺ → calmodulin → MLCK → myosin phosphorylation
ATroponin-based regulation is CARDIAC and SKELETAL muscle. GI smooth muscle uses calmodulin-MLCK instead of troponin.
BNa⁺ influx generates the action potential but does NOT directly activate myosin ATPase. It is Ca²⁺ — not Na⁺ — that drives smooth muscle contraction.
CCORRECT. GI smooth muscle contraction: Action potential → voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open → Ca²⁺ influx → Ca²⁺ binds calmodulin (4:1 ratio) → Ca-calmodulin complex activates MLCK (myosin light chain kinase) → phosphorylates myosin light chain → cross-bridge cycling → contraction. More depolarization = more action potentials = more Ca²⁺ = stronger contraction.
DcAMP/PKA signaling generally RELAXES smooth muscle (lowers MLCK activity). ACh signals via Gq → IP3/DAG, not via cAMP.
ESmooth muscle does use IP3-mediated SR Ca²⁺ release, but NOT via ryanodine receptors. Ryanodine receptors are used in cardiac/skeletal muscle for CICR. Also, smooth muscle does not use troponin.
🎯 Step 1 Key PointSmooth muscle contraction cascade: Ca²⁺ → calmodulin → MLCK → myosin phosphorylation → contraction. Relaxation: MLCP (myosin light chain phosphatase) dephosphorylates myosin → relaxation. SNS/VIP/NO → ↑cAMP or ↑cGMP → activates MLCP → relaxation. This is how nitrates and β-agonists produce smooth muscle relaxation.
LO3
Define achalasia and compare it to normal esophageal function.
Question 4 Pathology Hard
A 42-year-old woman presents with a 6-month history of dysphagia to both solids AND liquids, regurgitation of undigested food, and a 12 lb weight loss. Barium swallow shows a "bird-beak" tapering at the gastroesophageal junction with dilation of the proximal esophagus. Manometry reveals failure of LES relaxation with swallowing and absent peristalsis in the distal esophageal body. Which of the following BEST explains the pathophysiology?
✓ Correct answer: D — Loss of inhibitory NO/VIP neurons → unopposed LES contraction
AUES dysfunction is not the mechanism of achalasia. Achalasia involves the LES (lower esophageal sphincter), controlled by the myenteric plexus of the distal esophagus — not cranial motor neurons.
BLeiomyoma causes extrinsic obstruction and dysphagia primarily to solids, not liquids. Achalasia causes dysphagia to both solids AND liquids — a key distinguishing feature from mechanical obstruction.
CGastrin actually INCREASES LES tone. Excess gastrin would worsen achalasia, not cause it. The pathology here is NEUROLOGICAL (loss of inhibitory neurons), not hormonal.
DCORRECT. Achalasia = loss of inhibitory myenteric neurons (NO- and VIP-releasing) at the LES → LES fails to relax during swallowing → functional obstruction. Additionally, the absence of coordinated peristalsis in the distal esophagus means food cannot be propelled normally. Result: progressive dilation of the esophagus proximal to the hypertonic LES.
EACh INCREASES LES tone. A deficiency of AChE (too much ACh) would cause LES hypertension — which actually can worsen achalasia symptoms, but the primary mechanism is loss of inhibitory neurons, not AChE deficiency.
🎯 Step 1 Achalasia PearlsAchalasia = "failure to relax" (Greek). Triad: (1) dysphagia to solids + liquids, (2) failed LES relaxation on manometry, (3) absent peristalsis in distal esophagus. Barium swallow → "bird beak" at GEJ. Risk of esophageal squamous cell carcinoma (not adenocarcinoma) due to stasis. Treat with: pneumatic dilation, Heller myotomy, or botulinum toxin injection (blocks ACh → reduces LES tone). Compare to GERD: LES tone is TOO LOW; in achalasia, LES tone is TOO HIGH.
LO4
Define the effects of PNS and SNS stimulation on motility and contractions in the GI tract.
Question 5 Physiology Medium
A 26-year-old man is involved in a high-speed motor vehicle accident and arrives in the ER in a state of severe physiological stress with elevated catecholamines. Which of the following BEST describes the expected effects on his GI tract?
✓ Correct answer: B — Decreased peristalsis, decreased secretions, increased sphincter tone
AThis describes parasympathetic (PNS) effects — "rest and digest." Stress activates the SNS, which has the opposite effect.
BCORRECT. SNS = "fight or flight" = shunts blood away from GI → ↓ peristalsis, ↓ secretions, ↑ sphincter tone (to prevent passage of contents). Norepinephrine acts on α2-adrenergic receptors on presynaptic ENS neurons to INHIBIT ACh release, and on α1 receptors on sphincter smooth muscle to contract them. VIP and NO (which cause smooth muscle relaxation) are also inhibited.
CSNS would not increase peristalsis. Increased sphincter tone with decreased peristalsis is consistent, but increased peristalsis is wrong.
DDecreased peristalsis is correct, but SNS decreases (not increases) secretions and increases (not decreases) sphincter tone.
ESNS clearly affects both peristalsis (decreases it) and secretions (decreases them). This choice is too minimal.
🎯 Step 1 Key PointPNS (vagus + sacral) = ↑ motility + ↑ secretions + ↓ sphincter tone (rest and digest). SNS = ↓ motility + ↓ secretions + ↑ sphincter tone (fight or flight). Clinically: opioids activate μ-receptors on ENS → inhibit ACh → ↓ motility → opioid-induced constipation. Anticholinergic drugs (atropine) → ↓ PNS → ↓ GI motility (antidiarrheal use).
LO5
Define and state the importance of the enteric nervous system (ENS).
Question 6 Neural Medium
A surgeon performs a complete vagotomy on a patient to treat a peptic ulcer. Postoperatively, the patient still has coordinated peristaltic contractions in the small intestine. Which of the following BEST explains why GI motility persists despite vagal transection?
✓ Correct answer: D — ENS functions autonomously as an independent "mini-brain"
AThe SNS INHIBITS GI motility — it does not coordinate peristalsis. SNS activity decreases gut function.
BSacral parasympathetics innervate the descending colon, sigmoid, and rectum — NOT the upper GI tract or small intestine. They cannot compensate for vagotomy in the small bowel.
CWhile GI hormones (CCK, gastrin) modulate motility, they cannot independently generate the coordinated neural reflexes needed for peristalsis. Motility requires the ENS.
DCORRECT. The ENS is sometimes called the "second brain" — it contains ~100 million neurons and can coordinate peristalsis, segmentation, secretory reflexes, and sphincter control INDEPENDENTLY of the CNS. Myenteric plexus (Auerbach's) = controls muscular activity and peristalsis. Submucosal plexus (Meissner's) = controls secretions and local blood flow. Vagal input modulates ENS activity but is NOT required for basic motility.
EICC set the pacemaker rhythm (BER) but do NOT coordinate peristaltic reflexes. The peristaltic reflex requires ENS interneurons and efferent neurons to coordinate oral contraction + aboral relaxation.
🎯 Step 1 ENS SummaryENS = myenteric plexus (motility) + submucosal plexus (secretion). ENS uses ACh, NO, VIP, substance P, serotonin. Clinical: Hirschsprung's = absent ganglion cells in myenteric plexus → no ENS → tonic contraction → megacolon. Achalasia = absent ENS neurons at LES → no relaxation. ENS neurotransmitter importance: ACh = excitatory; NO/VIP = inhibitory (relax smooth muscle, open sphincters).
LO6
Describe and compare the different types of motility in the small and large intestines.
Question 7 Physiology Medium
A student studies small intestinal motility and notes two primary movement patterns. Pattern X causes circular muscle rings to appear at regularly spaced intervals, creating back-and-forth mixing of intestinal contents without significant net aboral movement. Pattern Y involves coordinated oral contraction and aboral relaxation that propels chyme forward in a wave. Which of the following CORRECTLY identifies Patterns X and Y and their primary physiological roles?
✓ Correct answer: B — X = Segmentation; Y = Peristalsis
AThe roles are reversed. "Ring-like contractions creating back-and-forth mixing without net forward movement" = SEGMENTATION, not peristalsis.
BCORRECT. Segmentation = rhythmic ring-like contractions at intervals that mix chyme with digestive enzymes and maximize mucosal contact for absorption — little net forward movement. Peristalsis = coordinated oral circular muscle contraction + aboral longitudinal muscle contraction → wave-like forward propulsion. Both occur simultaneously in the postprandial small intestine.
CHaustration and mass movement are COLONIC motility patterns — not small intestinal. Haustration = slow churning in sacs (hausturae) for water absorption. Mass movement = large propulsive waves in colon.
DNeither description matches reverse peristalsis or the MMC. The MMC occurs during fasting and sweeps the entire GI tract — it is not simply "oral-to-aboral" propulsion during digestion.
EThe functions are swapped. Segmentation provides LOCAL mixing, not forward propulsion; peristalsis provides forward propulsion, not local mixing.
🎯 Step 1 Key PointSmall intestine: Segmentation (mixing) + Peristalsis (propulsion) — both postprandial. Large intestine: Haustration (slow churning for water absorption) + Mass movement (3–4×/day, triggered by gastrocolic reflex) + Defecation reflex. Note: colon has NO villi and relies on haustration for efficient water/electrolyte absorption across a large mucosal surface area.
Question 8 Physiology Medium
A 70-year-old man undergoes colonic manometry and the tracing shows a region of sustained, powerful contractions propagating rapidly from the right colon to the sigmoid colon. His gastroenterologist notes this occurred 20 minutes after breakfast. Which colonic motility pattern is being recorded, and what is the PRIMARY physiological mechanism that triggered it?
✓ Correct answer: C — Mass movement triggered by gastrocolic reflex
AHaustration is slow, localized churning that creates sac-like pockets for water absorption — NOT the large-scale rapid propulsive wave described here.
BSegmentation is a SMALL INTESTINAL pattern, not colonic. And the manometry description (rapid long-distance propagation) is not consistent with segmentation.
CCORRECT. Mass movements (high-amplitude propagating contractions, HAPCs) = 3–4 large propulsive waves per day that sweep contents from the right colon to the sigmoid/rectum. They are triggered by the GASTROCOLIC REFLEX — food entering the stomach activates vagal and hormonal signals (gastrin, CCK) → colonic contraction. This explains the urge to defecate after eating (a normal physiological response).
DMMC Phase III occurs during FASTING (3–4 hours after eating) — NOT postprandially. Also, the MMC operates in the STOMACH and SMALL INTESTINE, not primarily in the colon.
EReverse peristalsis occurs in the context of vomiting. The SNS suppresses GI motility — a postprandial surge in SNS activity contradicts normal physiology.
🎯 Step 1 Key PointGastrocolic reflex = food in stomach → colonic mass movement (via vagus + GI hormones). This is why people often defecate after meals. Clinically amplified in IBS and diarrhea-predominant conditions. Decreased in hypothyroidism, opioid use, and autonomic neuropathy → constipation.
LO7
Define the migrating myoelectric complex (MMC), what stimulates Phase III contractions, and discuss its importance.
Question 9 Physiology Medium
A researcher studying the interdigestive state measures serum hormone levels in a fasting volunteer at regular intervals. At hour 4 of fasting, she observes a surge in one hormone that correlates precisely with the appearance of strong, sweeping contractions that travel from the stomach through the terminal ileum over approximately 90 minutes. Which hormone is elevated, and what is the primary FUNCTION of the motor activity it initiates?
✓ Correct answer: C — Motilin triggers MMC Phase III
AGastrin is elevated POSTPRANDIALLY (released by G cells in response to food, vagal stimulation, and amino acids). It falls during fasting.
BCCK is released by I-cells of the duodenum/jejunum in RESPONSE TO FATS AND PROTEIN — postprandial, not interdigestive.
CCORRECT. Motilin (released by M-cells of the duodenum/jejunum) rises cyclically every 75–120 min during FASTING to initiate the MMC. MMC Phase III = strong, sweeping peristaltic contractions ("housekeeping waves") that travel from the stomach/duodenum to the terminal ileum, clearing residual food debris, dead cells, and bacteria — preventing small bowel bacterial overgrowth (SIBO). The MMC is suppressed IMMEDIATELY when eating resumes.
DSecretin is released by S-cells in response to duodenal ACIDIFICATION — postprandial. Its role is to stimulate pancreatic bicarbonate secretion, not to initiate fasting motility.
EGIP (glucose-dependent insulinotropic peptide) is released postprandially in response to carbohydrates and fats to amplify insulin release. It does not control interdigestive motility.
🎯 Step 1 MMC PearlsMMC = "housekeeper of the gut." Phases: I (quiescence, ~40 min), II (irregular contractions, ~50 min), III (intense sweeping contractions, ~10 min). Trigger: motilin from M-cells. Suppressed by eating. Clinical: disrupted MMC (e.g., from opioids, autonomic neuropathy, diabetes) → SIBO (small intestinal bacterial overgrowth) → bloating, diarrhea, malabsorption. Treatment of gastroparesis: erythromycin (motilin agonist) mimics motilin → restores Phase III MMC activity.
Question 10 Pharmacology Medium
A 58-year-old woman with long-standing type 2 diabetes presents with nausea, early satiety, and vomiting of food eaten several hours ago. A gastric emptying scintigraphy confirms gastroparesis. Her physician prescribes a medication that acts as a motilin receptor agonist to promote gastric emptying. This drug has a primary indication as an antibiotic but is used here off-label as a prokinetic agent. Which drug is being described?
✓ Correct answer: D — Erythromycin
AMetoclopramide IS a prokinetic (D2 antagonist + 5-HT4 agonist) and is FDA-approved for gastroparesis, BUT it is NOT a motilin agonist and is NOT an antibiotic. The question specifies "antibiotic used off-label as a motilin agonist."
BDomperidone is a peripheral D2 antagonist prokinetic. It is NOT an antibiotic and does NOT act as a motilin agonist.
CPrucalopride is a 5-HT4 agonist used for chronic constipation. It enhances colonic motility but is NOT a motilin agonist and is NOT an antibiotic.
DCORRECT. Erythromycin is a macrolide antibiotic that ALSO acts as a motilin receptor agonist. At low doses (below antibiotic levels), it stimulates motilin receptors → initiates Phase III MMC-like contractions → accelerates gastric emptying. Used off-label for gastroparesis (especially diabetic). Caution: tachyphylaxis (tolerance develops) with prolonged use and risk of QT prolongation.
EOndansetron is a 5-HT3 antagonist used as an ANTIEMETIC. It reduces nausea/vomiting but does NOT promote gastric emptying or act as a prokinetic or motilin agonist.
🎯 Step 1 Prokinetics SummaryErythromycin = motilin agonist → gastric emptying. Metoclopramide = D2 antagonist + 5-HT4 agonist → gastric emptying + antiemetic (risk: tardive dyskinesia with long-term use). Prucalopride = 5-HT4 agonist → colonic motility → used for chronic constipation. Remember: motilin → MMC initiation → important for preventing SIBO and clearing gastric residue.
LO8
Identify the major neural, hormonal, and chemical factors controlling the motility of the small bowel.
Question 11 Physiology Hard
A pharmacology student is studying neurotransmitters that regulate GI smooth muscle tone. She evaluates three signaling molecules: Substance P, VIP (vasoactive intestinal peptide), and NO (nitric oxide). Which of the following CORRECTLY matches each molecule to its effect on GI smooth muscle?
✓ Correct answer: B — Substance P excitatory; VIP and NO inhibitory
ASubstance P is excitatory (causes contraction), not relaxation. This is the opposite of the correct answer.
BCORRECT. Substance P (a tachykinin) = EXCITATORY → depolarizes smooth muscle → contraction. VIP = INHIBITORY → activates adenylyl cyclase → ↑cAMP → activates MLCP → smooth muscle relaxation + sphincter opening. NO = INHIBITORY → activates guanylyl cyclase → ↑cGMP → MLCP activation → relaxation. VIP and NO are the key mediators of the "descending relaxation" in the peristaltic reflex (aboral relaxation ahead of the bolus). Loss of VIP/NO neurons = achalasia, Hirschsprung's.
CVIP causes RELAXATION, not contraction. Confusing VIP with substance P is a classic error.
DSubstance P is EXCITATORY (causes contraction) and NO is INHIBITORY (causes relaxation) — this choice has both reversed.
EAll roles are reversed in this choice.
🎯 Step 1 Neurotransmitter TableEXCITATORY (↑ motility): ACh (muscarinic), Substance P, Serotonin (5-HT4), Gastrin, Motilin. INHIBITORY (↓ motility / relax sphincters): NO, VIP, Serotonin (5-HT3 on vagal afferents inhibits ENS), Sympathetic NE (via α2 presynaptic). Note: NO and VIP = critical for LES relaxation, pyloric relaxation, and the descending relaxation phase of peristalsis.
LO9 + LO10
Describe the gastrocolic and defecation reflexes and how they regulate intestinal motility. Describe factors involved in the defecation reflex and its voluntary modulation.
Question 12 Physiology Medium
A 24-year-old woman reliably experiences an urge to defecate shortly after eating breakfast each morning. She is concerned this may be abnormal, but her gastroenterologist reassures her it is a physiological reflex. Which of the following BEST describes the reflex and its mediators?
✓ Correct answer: C — Gastrocolic reflex via vagus + GI hormones
AThe enterogastric reflex goes in the OTHER direction — it's triggered by fat/acid in the duodenum and slows GASTRIC emptying. It does not explain post-meal defecation urge.
BThe ileocecal reflex controls passage from ileum to cecum — not post-meal colonic motility and not the defecation urge.
CCORRECT. The GASTROCOLIC REFLEX = food entering the stomach → vagal and hormonal (gastrin, CCK) signals → colonic mass movements → propel feces into rectum → trigger defecation reflex. This is a normal physiological reflex that clears old colonic contents to "make room" for incoming chyme. It is amplified in IBS-D and can be diminished in constipation, hypothyroidism, and diabetes with autonomic neuropathy.
DThe defecation reflex is triggered by RECTAL DISTENSION — not by gastric distension directly. The gastrocolic reflex → mass movement → rectal filling → THEN the defecation reflex is triggered. These are sequential, not identical.
EThe peristaltic reflex is a local ENS reflex triggered by luminal stretch (serotonin from EC cells → ENS activation). It is responsible for individual peristaltic waves, not the large-scale post-meal colonic response.
🎯 Step 1 Reflex CascadeGastrocolic reflex → Mass movement → Rectal distension → Defecation reflex (internal anal sphincter relaxes involuntarily via ENS/sacral PNS) → Urge to defecate → External anal sphincter: voluntary control (pudendal nerve, S2-S4 skeletal muscle). Clinically: injury above the sacral cord → voluntary control lost, but reflex defecation preserved. Sacral cord or pudendal nerve injury → both reflexes impaired.
Question 13 Integration Hard
A 32-year-old man sustains a complete spinal cord injury at T10. Evaluation reveals that reflex defecation can still be elicited by digital rectal stimulation, but the patient has no voluntary control over defecation timing. Which of the following BEST explains this dissociation?
✓ Correct answer: C — Sacral reflex arc intact; corticospinal voluntary control severed
AT10 is ABOVE the sacral cord (S2–S4). The sacral cord and its parasympathetic center are INTACT, which is why reflex defecation still occurs. T10 injury disrupts descending control from the cortex, not the sacral arc itself.
BThe ENS is intrinsic to the gut wall and is entirely independent of the spinal cord. Spinal cord injury does NOT eliminate ENS activity.
CCORRECT. The DEFECATION REFLEX ARC: rectal distension → sacral afferents (S2–S4) → sacral parasympathetic efferents → internal anal sphincter relaxes (involuntary, smooth muscle) + peristaltic contractions. This arc is BELOW T10 and remains INTACT. VOLUNTARY CONTROL requires: cortex → corticospinal tract → pudendal motor neurons (S2–S4) → external anal sphincter (skeletal muscle). T10 injury severs corticospinal tracts → external sphincter cannot be voluntarily contracted to delay defecation.
DThe internal anal sphincter is smooth muscle innervated by the ENS and sacral autonomics — NOT severed by T10 injury. And it is the internal sphincter that relaxes reflexively (not the external sphincter) in the defecation reflex.
EWhile T10 injury can disrupt SNS tone to the colon (SNS originates at T5-L2), this is not the primary mechanism here. The key distinction is voluntary vs. reflexive control of the external sphincter.
🎯 Step 1 Sphincter Key PointOnly 2 places in the GI tract have SKELETAL MUSCLE: (1) Upper esophagus / UES, (2) External anal sphincter. Both require intact somatic motor control. Internal anal sphincter = smooth muscle = involuntary = controlled by ENS + sacral PNS. External anal sphincter = skeletal muscle = voluntary = controlled by pudendal nerve (S2–S4) via corticospinal tracts. Spinal cord injury above S2 → voluntary incontinence (reflexive preserved). Sacral cord or pudendal nerve injury → flaccid incontinence (both lost).
LO11
Describe the consequences of an aganglionic region (no nerves) in the lower colon.
Question 14 Pathology Medium
A male newborn fails to pass meconium within the first 48 hours of life. Physical exam shows a distended abdomen; rectal exam reveals an empty rectum with a gush of stool on withdrawal of the examining finger. Abdominal X-ray shows dilated proximal colon. A full-thickness rectal biopsy reveals absence of ganglion cells in both the submucosal and myenteric plexuses. Which of the following BEST explains the pathophysiological mechanism of the bowel obstruction?
✓ Correct answer: C — Loss of inhibitory neurons → tonic contraction → functional obstruction
AIn Hirschsprung's, the problem is ABSENT ganglion cells (aganglionosis) — there are NO ENS neurons in the affected segment, hypertrophied or otherwise. The obstruction is due to lack of INHIBITORY neurons, not excess excitatory ones.
BICC cells may be present in Hirschsprung's — the primary issue is ABSENT ganglion cells (ENS neurons), not ICC loss. The BER may persist, but coordinated relaxation cannot occur without ENS inhibitory neurons.
CCORRECT. Hirschsprung's disease = congenital aganglionosis (absent ganglion cells in myenteric + submucosal plexuses). The aganglionic segment lacks inhibitory neurons (NO-releasing, VIP-releasing) → smooth muscle is tonically contracted (no inhibitory tone) → functional obstruction. The PROXIMAL colon fills with stool → dilates → megacolon. The DISTAL aganglionic segment is narrow and appears normal but is actually the pathological zone.
DThe SNS does promote smooth muscle tone, but Hirschsprung's is specifically due to failure of neural crest cell migration to the colon — it is a PRIMARY ENS defect, not an autonomic imbalance problem.
EThe smooth muscle in Hirschsprung's disease is structurally NORMAL. The muscle itself develops properly — it is the NERVES (ganglion cells) that are absent. Without inhibitory neural input, normal smooth muscle remains in tonic contraction.
🎯 Step 1 Hirschsprung's PearlsEmbryology: failure of neural crest cell migration to colonize the distal colon. The aganglionic segment is the DISTAL, NARROW portion. Megacolon forms PROXIMAL to the obstruction (normal ganglionic bowel dilates from backup). Presentation: neonate fails to pass meconium in first 24–48h; explosive stool on rectal exam. Diagnosis: suction rectal biopsy (absent ganglion cells + hypertrophied nerve fibers on acetylcholinesterase stain). Treatment: surgical resection of aganglionic segment. Compare: acquired megacolon → e.g., Chagas disease (T. cruzi destroys ENS) → same mechanism in adults.
Question 15 Integration Hard
A 3-year-old boy with Hirschsprung's disease involving the rectosigmoid region has not undergone surgical repair. His parents note he has not had a bowel movement in 5 days despite laxatives. On examination, the abdomen is massively distended. An abdominal X-ray shows markedly dilated loops of colon with a transition zone at the rectosigmoid junction. Which of the following MOST likely explains the radiographic finding of colonic dilation PROXIMAL to — but not at — the aganglionic segment?
✓ Correct answer: D — Tonically contracted distal segment → dilation of proximal normal colon
AThe DISTAL segment is aganglionic (tonically contracted/narrow). The PROXIMAL bowel is ganglionic (normal) and dilates because stool cannot pass through the contracted distal segment.
BHaustral contractions occur in the proximal (ganglionic) colon and may actually be increased as the bowel tries to overcome the distal obstruction. SNS tone is not the primary issue here.
CICC density changes are not a significant feature of Hirschsprung's pathology. The transition zone represents the border between aganglionic (distal, narrow) and ganglionic (proximal, dilated) bowel.
DCORRECT. The aganglionic segment lacks inhibitory ENS neurons → tonic smooth muscle contraction → NARROW, non-distensible distal segment (functional obstruction). Stool accumulates in the GANGLIONIC proximal colon, which has normal ENS and can distend → megacolon (abnormal dilation). The "transition zone" on barium enema or X-ray is the border between dilated proximal (normal) and narrowed distal (aganglionic) colon — this is the key diagnostic landmark.
EThe gastrocolic reflex may be present but simply cannot overcome the mechanical obstruction posed by the tonically contracted aganglionic segment. The reflex is not the primary problem.
🎯 Step 1 Transition Zone ConceptIn Hirschsprung's: aganglionic segment = NARROW (tonically contracted) → proximal bowel = DILATED (megacolon). The transition zone on barium enema = cone-shaped transition from dilated to narrow = diagnostic finding. Remember: the dilated segment is the NORMAL colon (upstream backup), NOT the pathological zone. Surgical treatment = pull-through procedure (remove aganglionic segment, anastomose normal ganglionic bowel to anus). Complication if untreated: Hirschsprung-associated enterocolitis (HAEC) — bacterial overgrowth + mucosal ischemia → life-threatening.
