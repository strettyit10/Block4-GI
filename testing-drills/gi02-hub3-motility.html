Question 1 of 5 — LO1 · Electrical Basis
A 52-year-old man with gastroesophageal reflux disease presents for esophageal pH monitoring. During the study, his esophageal smooth muscle is found to have resting membrane potentials of −60 mV with regular slow-wave oscillations at 3 cycles per minute. He is given a proton-pump inhibitor. Which of the following best describes the ionic basis of the slow waves (basic electrical rhythm) that were recorded?
ANa+ influx during depolarization phase with K+ efflux during repolarization
BRhythmic oscillations in membrane potential independent of action potentials, generated by interstitial cells of Cajal
CSpontaneous opening of ryanodine receptors leading to calcium-induced calcium release
DRepetitive depolarization due to persistent tetrodotoxin-resistant sodium channels
EPacemaker activity from the myenteric plexus neurons firing at regular intervals
B — Correct. The basic electrical rhythm (BER) or slow waves are rhythmic changes in resting membrane potential that occur INDEPENDENTLY of action potentials. They are generated by interstitial cells of Cajal (ICC), not the smooth muscle or neurons. The slow-wave frequency varies by location (esophagus ~3/min, duodenum ~12/min, colon ~2–6/min) and sets the MAXIMUM possible contraction rate—action potentials can only occur at the peaks of slow waves. Choice A describes skeletal muscle APs (which use Na+ for rapid depolarization). Choice C involves cardiac muscle ryanodine receptors. Choice D is incorrect because slow waves are not due to persistent Na+ channels. Choice E incorrectly attributes the rhythm to myenteric neurons rather than ICC.
Question 2 of 5 — LO2 · Action Potential Mechanism
A 67-year-old woman is being studied for gastric dysmotility. Gastric smooth muscle biopsies show normal slow-wave frequency (3–5/min) but very few action potentials despite normal nerve innervation. Her serum calcium is 7.2 mg/dL (normal: 8.5–10.5). Which ion is MOST critical for the generation of action potentials in gastrointestinal smooth muscle?
ASodium ions (Na+)
BPotassium ions (K+)
CCalcium ions (Ca2+)
DMagnesium ions (Mg2+)
EChloride ions (Cl−)
C — Correct. GI smooth muscle action potentials depend on L-type voltage-gated Ca2+ channels for depolarization (NOT Na+ as in skeletal muscle). The plateau phase is sustained by slow Ca2+ influx. In this patient, hypocalcemia impairs Ca2+ entry → fewer/smaller APs → reduced smooth muscle contraction despite normal slow waves and normal nerve input. Normal slow waves confirm the ICC are functioning; the problem is at the level of Ca2+ influx into smooth muscle. K+ efflux contributes to repolarization but is not the rate-limiting step. Na+ plays a minor role in GI smooth muscle APs.
Question 3 of 5 — LO3 · Parasympathetic Control
A 45-year-old man undergoes vagotomy (surgical cutting of the vagus nerve) for peptic ulcer disease. Postoperatively, he experiences severe constipation and early satiety. Which of the following BEST explains why parasympathetic denervation causes these symptoms?
ALoss of inhibitory neurons in the submucosal plexus causes mucosal secretion to increase
BVagus nerve carries parasympathetic fibers that release acetylcholine, depolarizing smooth muscle and increasing motility
CVagal stimulation directly activates enteric neurons to release nitric oxide for sphincter relaxation
DLoss of motilin release from the duodenum impairs migrating myoelectric complex propagation
EIncreased sympathetic tone unopposed by parasympathetic input inhibits all GI secretion
B — Correct. The parasympathetic nervous system (vagus nerve, CN X) is the primary "rest-and-digest" system. Vagal preganglionic fibers synapse with enteric neurons that release acetylcholine (ACh), which binds M3 receptors on smooth muscle → depolarization → increased APs → muscle contraction → increased motility. Vagotomy removes this stimulation → decreased motility → constipation and early satiety. Choice C is incorrect because vagus works through ENS neurons, not directly on smooth muscle. Choice D: Motilin is released by M cells in the duodenum during fasting but is not directly controlled by the vagus. Choice E overstates the role of sympathetic tone.
Question 4 of 5 — LO4 · Sympathetic Inhibition
A 58-year-old woman with long-standing type 2 diabetes presents with postprandial bloating, early satiety, and nausea. Imaging rules out mechanical obstruction. A diagnosis of gastroparesis is made. Which of the following medications would be MOST contraindicated in this patient due to worsening her underlying pathophysiology?
AMetformin
BLisinopril
CAtropine (anticholinergic)
DMetoclopramide
EDomperidone
C — Correct. Anticholinergic drugs like atropine block M3 muscarinic receptors on GI smooth muscle, preventing ACh-mediated depolarization and contraction. In gastroparesis (already impaired stomach emptying), anticholinergics would FURTHER decrease gastric motility and worsen symptoms. The parasympathetic nervous system is responsible for promoting GI motility; blocking it exacerbates dysmotility. Choice D (metoclopramide) and E (domperidone) are both prokinetic agents that would HELP. Metformin and lisinopril are not direct GI motility inhibitors.
Question 5 of 5 — LO5 · Enteric Inhibitory Neurons
A 3-year-old boy presents with failure to pass meconium, abdominal distension, and enterocolitis. Biopsy of the aganglionic colon reveals complete absence of ganglion cells and loss of acetylcholinesterase staining. This condition results in impaired function of which type of enteric neuron?
AMechanoreceptor neurons (primary sensory neurons)
BInhibitory motorneurons releasing VIP and nitric oxide
CEnteroendocrine cells secreting cholecystokinin
DSmooth muscle cells expressing voltage-gated calcium channels
EInterstitial cells of Cajal generating slow-wave pacemaker activity
B — Correct. This is Hirschsprung disease (aganglionic megacolon), caused by congenital absence of enteric ganglion cells (aganglionosis). Loss of inhibitory motorneurons (which normally release VIP and nitric oxide) means the affected segment cannot relax. The internal anal sphincter (IAS) fails to relax reflexively → functional obstruction. The loss of acetylcholinesterase staining is pathognomonic—normal nerve plexuses stain positive for AChE. Choice A and C are types of neurons/cells present in normal tissue but not relevant to the primary defect. Choice D and E: smooth muscle and ICC are structurally NORMAL in Hirschsprung; it is the NERVES that are absent.

Question 6 of 5 — LO6 · Motility Pattern Classification
A 24-year-old graduate student preparing for exams complains of bloating and cramping after meals without diarrhea or weight loss. Esophagogastroduodenoscopy is normal. Antroduodenal manometry shows preserved regular contractions at the duodenum (12/min) but infrequent, disorganized activity in the proximal jejunum. This pattern is MOST consistent with impaired which type of motility?
APeristalsis: organized aboral propulsion with descending inhibition
BSegmentation: mixing contractions that are aboral but variable in propagation
CHaustration: formation and emptying of sacs in the colon
DMigrating myoelectric complex: housekeeping contractions during fasting
EReceptive relaxation: accommodation of the stomach to incoming food
B — Correct. Segmentation is the dominant motility pattern in the small intestine during FEEDING. It involves circular muscle contractions on both sides of the bolus with relaxation in the middle → forms pockets that mix and slowly propel food. The duodenum has the highest BER frequency (12/min), allowing organized segmentation. Loss of segmentation in the proximal jejunum (beyond the duodenum) while duodenal activity is preserved suggests a selective motility dysfunction in the jejunum. The infrequent, disorganized activity indicates impaired segmentation (not complete peristaltic failure). Choice A: Peristalsis is more organized and propagates more uniformly than segmentation. Choice C: Haustration is a colon-specific pattern. Choice D: MMC occurs during FASTING, not after meals. Choice E: Receptive relaxation is a gastric phenomenon.
Question 7 of 5 — LO7 · MMC & Fasting Motility
A 38-year-old man is undergoing small bowel capsule endoscopy after 12 hours of fasting. The endoscopy captures regular, high-amplitude contractions sweeping from the duodenum into the jejunum and ileum over several minutes, then the bowel is quiet for 5 minutes before the pattern repeats. Which hormone is PRIMARILY responsible for initiating this contractile pattern?
ACholecystokinin (CCK)
BMotilin
CSecretin
DGastrin
EGlucose-dependent insulinotropic peptide (GIP)
B — Correct. The pattern described is the migrating myoelectric complex (MMC), the GI "housekeeping wave" that occurs during FASTING. Motilin is released by M cells in the duodenum during the interdigestive period and triggers Phase II and III of the MMC (Phase III is the high-amplitude contractions shown here). When food enters the stomach, motilin secretion stops and the MMC is suppressed, replaced by postprandial segmentation/peristalsis. CCK (choice A) is released postprandially in response to nutrients and promotes gallbladder contraction and pancreatic secretion—not the MMC. Choices C, D, E have minor roles in GI motility.
Question 8 of 5 — LO8 · Swallowing Reflex
A 72-year-old man with a recent left hemispheric stroke presents with difficulty swallowing (dysphagia). During the pharyngeal phase of swallowing, he has nasal regurgitation and coughing on liquids but maintains normal esophageal peristalsis. Which of the following is MOST likely impaired?
AEsophageal smooth muscle contraction
BSoft palate elevation and glottic closure via CN X
COral stage tongue propulsion via CN XII
DLower esophageal sphincter relaxation via vagus nerve
EUpper esophageal sphincter contraction via CN V
B — Correct. The pharyngeal stage of swallowing is controlled by the medulla via CN IX (glossopharyngeal) and CN X (vagus). Key protective reflexes include: (1) soft palate elevation (CN X) prevents nasal entry, and (2) glottic closure (CN X) via vocal cord adduction prevents aspiration. Nasal regurgitation + cough on swallowing indicates failure of these protective mechanisms—most consistent with CN X dysfunction affecting pharyngeal motor control. A left stroke affecting the swallowing center in the medulla would impair CN X output. The esophageal peristalsis is preserved (choice A is correct), so the problem is in the pharyngeal phase. Choice C: CN XII innervates the tongue (oral stage), but the problem is described as pharyngeal. Choice D: LES relaxation would cause dysphagia to solids primarily, not aspiration. Choice E: CN V is trigeminal; the UES is primarily controlled by CN X.
Question 9 of 5 — LO9 · Peristaltic & Defecation Reflexes
A 41-year-old woman with advanced scleroderma (systemic sclerosis) presents with severe constipation and fecal incontinence. Anorectal manometry shows elevated resting internal anal sphincter pressure with impaired relaxation reflex and reduced external anal sphincter squeeze pressure. Which of the following BEST explains her dual presentation of constipation AND incontinence?
ALoss of smooth muscle in the colon → reduced propulsive force with overflow incontinence
BImpaired rectosphincteric reflex (IAS relaxation) prevents normal defecation; weak EAS cannot maintain continence against proximal pressure
CSpinal cord lesion impairing pudendal nerve function
DMegacolon with chronic fecal retention overflowing past the internal anal sphincter
EScleroderma-induced myopathy of the external anal sphincter only
B — Correct. The rectosphincteric reflex (defecation reflex) normally works as follows: rectal distension → vagal afferents → medulla → vagal efferents → IAS relaxation (involuntary, mediated by inhibitory neurons releasing VIP/NO). The external anal sphincter (EAS) is skeletal muscle under voluntary pudendal nerve control. In scleroderma, fibrosis affects both smooth muscle (IAS) and skeletal muscle (EAS). Impaired IAS relaxation prevents stool from reaching the EAS → constipation. However, the weak EAS cannot maintain continence when intra-abdominal pressure is high or when stool is forced past → incontinence. This "paradoxical" presentation of both constipation and incontinence is characteristic. Choice A: Scleroderma causes fibrosis, not loss of muscle. Choice C: A spinal cord lesion would cause urinary dysfunction too. Choice D: Megacolon is more typical of Hirschsprung/Chagas. Choice E: Both sphincters are affected in scleroderma, not just EAS.
Question 10 of 5 — LO10 · Receptive Relaxation & Gastric Accommodation
A 35-year-old woman who underwent bariatric surgery (vertical sleeve gastrectomy) 6 months ago reports persistent early satiety, severe bloating after eating small portions, and weight loss plateau despite adequate caloric reduction. Postoperatively, she lacks the normal receptive relaxation of the gastric fundus. What is the PRIMARY mechanism by which loss of receptive relaxation causes early satiety?
ADestruction of gastric parietal cells reduces acid secretion, impairing protein digestion
BReduced gastric volume capacity → small food intake causes rapid intragastric pressure rise → vagal satiety signals
CImpaired VIP/vagal signaling prevents fundic relaxation → mechanical stretch receptors activated quickly → early fullness sensation
DLoss of CCK receptor expression in the stomach reduces nutrient signaling
EPyloric sphincter fibrosis prevents normal gastric emptying
C — Correct. Receptive relaxation is the reflex relaxation of the gastric fundus/body in anticipation of incoming food, mediated by vagus nerve release of VIP (a relaxing neurotransmitter). This reflex allows the stomach to accommodate a large meal volume WITH MINIMAL INCREASE in intragastric pressure. Loss of receptive relaxation (as occurs in some bariatric procedures) → stomach cannot relax normally → small food volume causes rapid pressure rise → mechanical stretch receptors → early satiety signal → reduced food intake (the intended weight-loss mechanism). Choice B is partially true but emphasizes the consequence rather than the mechanism. Choice A: Acid secretion is separate from motility. Choice D: CCK regulates gallbladder contraction, not gastric stretch sensation. Choice E: Pyloric function may be affected but is not the primary defect in receptive relaxation.

Question 11 of 5 — LO11 · Achalasia Pathophysiology
A 58-year-old man with 6 months of progressive dysphagia to solids AND liquids undergoes upper endoscopy. The endoscopist notes a "bird's beak" appearance of the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) on retroflexed view and finds an air-fluid level in the dilated esophagus. High-resolution esophageal manometry shows elevated LES pressure (50 mmHg, normal <20) with impaired relaxation (residual pressure >15 mmHg). Which of the following BEST describes the underlying pathology?
AMechanical obstruction of the LES by an infiltrating esophageal carcinoma
BPrimary hypertrophic smooth muscle of the LES causing mechanical narrowing
CLoss of inhibitory enteric neurons (VIP/NO-releasing) at the LES preventing relaxation
DSpasm of the upper esophageal sphincter (UES) with secondary LES dysfunction
EGastroesophageal reflux-induced stricture at the gastroesophageal junction
C — Correct. Achalasia is the failure of the LES to relax due to selective loss of inhibitory enteric neurons (those releasing VIP and nitric oxide). The LES itself is structurally normal smooth muscle, but unopposed excitatory (cholinergic) tone → elevated resting pressure and inability to relax. The classic findings are dysphagia to both solids and liquids (not just solids as in stricture), a "bird's beak" appearance on barium swallow, and elevated LES pressure with impaired relaxation on manometry. Achalasia can be primary (idiopathic) or secondary to Chagas disease (Trypanosoma cruzi destroys Auerbach's plexus). Choice A: Carcinoma would show a mass, not purely functional LES dysfunction. Choice B: The smooth muscle is normal; it's the neuronal innervation that is defective. Choice D: Primary UES dysfunction causes difficulty initiating swallowing, not achalasia. Choice E: Stricture from reflux would show mucosal inflammation/injury, not pure neural loss.
Question 12 of 5 — LO11 · Chagas Disease & Motility
A 42-year-old man from Argentina with a 30-year history of Chagas disease (Trypanosoma cruzi infection) develops progressive dysphagia and is found on biopsy to have massive dilatation of the esophagus (megaesophagus) with complete absence of ganglion cells in Auerbach's plexus. He also has megacolon and cardiomyopathy. Which of the following is the MOST likely mechanism of tissue destruction in Chagas disease?
ADirect parasitic invasion of smooth muscle cells leading to myofiber necrosis
BParasite tropism for enteric neurons (Auerbach's plexus) → immune-mediated destruction of ganglion cells
CToxin-induced apoptosis of esophageal epithelial cells
DChronic ischemia due to vasculitis of esophageal blood vessels
EDysplastic transformation of esophageal tissue leading to stricture formation
B — Correct. Chagas disease (caused by Trypanosoma cruzi) has a tropism for enteric neurons, particularly those in Auerbach's plexus (myenteric plexus). The parasite causes chronic inflammation and immune-mediated destruction of ganglion cells → progressive loss of neuronal function → dysmotility similar to achalasia. The result is megaesophagus, megacolon, and megaduedenum. The smooth muscle itself is normal; it's the innervation that is destroyed. Additionally, Chagas disease causes cardiomyopathy (parasites also target cardiac muscle directly). Choice A: Direct parasitic invasion of smooth muscle does occur in Chagas but is NOT the primary mechanism of megaesophagus; neural destruction is. Choice C, D, E: These are not the primary pathophysiology of Chagas-induced motility disorders.
Question 13 of 5 — LO11 · Scleroderma & GI Involvement
A 52-year-old woman with limited cutaneous scleroderma (lcSSc) presents with severe GERD refractory to proton-pump inhibitors, dysphagia, and early satiety. High-resolution esophageal manometry shows severely reduced LES pressure (8 mmHg, normal 15–30) with normal relaxation. Which of the following is the PRIMARY mechanism of LES dysfunction in scleroderma?
AAutoantibodies against vagus nerve fibers impairing parasympathetic innervation
BFibrosis and replacement of smooth muscle in the LES and esophageal body with collagen
CLoss of ganglion cells similar to achalasia
DChronic acid reflux causing stricture at the LES
EUnopposed sympathetic tone causing excessive smooth muscle relaxation
B — Correct. Scleroderma (systemic sclerosis) is characterized by excessive collagen deposition and fibrosis in multiple organs. In the GI tract, scleroderma causes smooth muscle fibrosis and replacement with collagen in the esophageal body and LES. The LES becomes patulous (weak) due to loss of contractile muscle → reduced LES pressure with normal relaxation (unlike achalasia where pressure is HIGH but relaxation is impaired). The result is severe GERD. Patients also develop reduced esophageal peristalsis (fibrosis of body smooth muscle) and gastric dysmotility. The neuronal innervation is structurally normal; it's the muscle itself that is replaced by scar tissue. Choice A: Autoimmune attack on vagus nerve is not the primary mechanism. Choice C: Neural loss is achalasia, not scleroderma. Choice D: Acid is a consequence, not the primary cause. Choice E: Sympathetic tone would actually increase sphincter pressure.
Question 14 of 5 — LO11 · Opioid-Induced Constipation Mechanism
A 68-year-old man on chronic high-dose morphine for pancreatic cancer pain develops severe constipation with abdominal pain and diarrhea alternating with hard stools (diarrhea due to fecal overflow around impacted stool). His attending physician states that morphine acts on the mu (μ) opioid receptors in the enteric nervous system to cause dysmotility. Which of the following is the MOST direct mechanism by which mu-receptor activation causes opioid-induced constipation?
AMu-receptors on smooth muscle cause direct muscle paralysis
BMu-receptor activation increases sympathetic outflow via the CNS
CMu-receptors on enteric neurons inhibit acetylcholine (ACh) release → decreased smooth muscle contraction
DMu-receptor agonism increases secretion of somatostatin inhibiting GI motility
EMu-receptors desensitize the myenteric plexus to normal feeding signals
C — Correct. Mu (μ) opioid receptors are present on enteric neurons (presynaptic terminals) throughout the myenteric and submucosal plexuses. Mu-receptor activation → inhibits acetylcholine (ACh) release from excitatory neurons (via inhibitory G-protein signaling) → reduced ACh → decreased smooth muscle depolarization → impaired contraction → decreased propulsive motility → constipation. Additionally, opioid receptors may enhance inhibitory neurotransmission (VIP/NO release) further suppressing motility. Choice A: Opioids do NOT cause direct muscle paralysis; they act via neuronal signaling. Choice B: Central sympathetic effects occur but are secondary to ENS effects. Choice D: Somatostatin can inhibit motility but is not the direct mechanism of opioid action. Choice E: Desensitization is not the primary acute mechanism.
Question 15 of 5 — LO11 · Gastroparesis Pharmacotherapy
A 45-year-old woman with type 1 diabetes mellitus presents with nausea, vomiting 2–3 hours after eating, and early satiety. Gastric emptying scintigraphy confirms delayed gastric emptying (gastroparesis). Upper endoscopy rules out mechanical obstruction and pyloric stricture. Her physician prescribes domperidone (a peripheral dopamine antagonist that does NOT cross the blood-brain barrier). Which of the following BEST explains why domperidone improves gastric motility in gastroparesis?
ADopamine antagonism increases acetylcholine (ACh) availability in the synaptic cleft via monoamine oxidase inhibition
BPeripheral dopamine antagonism increases vagal outflow to the stomach via the medulla
CDopamine normally inhibits enteric neurons; blocking dopamine receptors relieves inhibition → increased ACh/muscle contraction
DDomperidone blocks H2-receptors on parietal cells, increasing stomach pH and motility
EDomperidone is a motilin receptor agonist that directly stimulates Phase III MMC contractions
C — Correct. Dopamine is a neurotransmitter in the ENS that acts via dopamine receptors to INHIBIT enteric neurons and decrease ACh release → reduced motility. Dopamine antagonists (like domperidone, metoclopramide) block dopamine receptors on inhibitory neurons → relieve the inhibition → allow normal ACh-mediated contraction. Because domperidone does NOT cross the BBB, it acts purely at the peripheral ENS level. Choice A: MAO inhibition is not how dopamine antagonists work. Choice B: Domperidone is peripheral and does not affect central vagal outflow in the way described. Choice D: H2-blockers are acid suppressants, not prokinetics. Choice E: Motilin agonists (like erythromycin) directly trigger MMC, but that's a different class of drug than dopamine antagonists.
