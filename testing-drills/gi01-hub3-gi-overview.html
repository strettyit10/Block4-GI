Block A — Multiple Choice Questions

Q1 of 10 Physiology LO 1 Easy
A first-year medical student is studying the layers of the GI tract wall. She correctly identifies that there are two distinct plexuses of the enteric nervous system. Which of the following correctly states the location and primary function of the submucosal plexus?
✓ Correct Answer: C
Teaching Point: The submucosal (Meissner's) plexus lies between the circular muscle layer and the submucosa. Its primary function is regulating secretion and local mucosal blood flow. The myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus (choice A) lies between the circular and longitudinal muscle layers and controls motility/peristalsis. Mnemonic: "My Sub" — Myenteric = Motility, Submucosal = Secretion.
Q2 of 10 Pathology LO 1 Medium
A 2-week-old boy is brought to the pediatrician because he has not had a bowel movement since birth. His abdomen is distended and tympanitic. Digital rectal examination causes an explosive release of stool and gas. Rectal biopsy shows absence of ganglion cells in the segment biopsied. The patient's father asks about the embryological cause. Which of the following best explains the pathogenesis?
✓ Correct Answer: C
Teaching Point — Hirschsprung Disease: Results from failure of neural crest cells to migrate caudally into the distal colon during embryogenesis. This leaves the distal segment without ganglion cells (aganglionic) in both the myenteric and submucosal plexuses → tonically contracted segment → functional obstruction. The dilated segment seen visually is PROXIMAL and has normal ganglia — it is dilated from stool backup above the obstruction. High-yield: biopsy confirming absent ganglia is diagnostic.
Q3 of 10 Physiology LO 2–3 Medium
A physiology professor asks students which of the following statements about GI fluid dynamics is correct. An estimated 9–10 liters of fluid enters the small intestine daily. Which of the following best describes the fate of this fluid?
✓ Correct Answer: C
Teaching Point — GI Fluid Distribution: The small intestine is extraordinarily efficient — it absorbs approximately 8.5 L of the ~9–10 L entering daily (driven primarily by Na⁺-coupled cotransport). The colon absorbs the remaining ~400 mL, leaving only 100–200 mL in feces. This is why even modest disruption of small intestine absorption (e.g., enterotoxin-mediated secretion in cholera) causes massive diarrhea — losing even 1% of 8.5 L = 85 mL extra loss.
Q4 of 10 Physiology LO 4 Easy
A 35-year-old man undergoes a complete bilateral vagotomy as part of treatment for refractory peptic ulcer disease. Postoperatively, which of the following GI changes is most expected?
✓ Correct Answer: C
Teaching Point — Vagotomy Effects: The vagus nerve provides extrinsic parasympathetic input that amplifies GI function — but the ENS can maintain basal peristalsis and secretion independently. After vagotomy: (1) gastric acid ↓ (vagal stimulation of parietal cells is lost — the therapeutic goal); (2) the cephalic phase of digestion is disrupted; (3) gastric emptying is slowed. However, peristalsis continues throughout the gut because the ENS is intrinsically autonomous. "Denervated intestinal grafts still peristalse" is the classic proof of ENS independence.
Q5 of 10 Pathology LO 5 Medium
A 48-year-old woman with a 10-year history of difficulty swallowing both liquids and solids presents for evaluation. She denies heartburn. Upper endoscopy shows a dilated esophagus with food retention, and barium swallow reveals a smooth "bird-beak" narrowing at the gastroesophageal junction. Esophageal manometry shows absent peristalsis and incomplete LES relaxation. Which of the following is the most direct pathophysiologic mechanism?
✓ Correct Answer: C
Teaching Point — Achalasia: Caused by loss of inhibitory neurons (VIP + NO) at the LES. Without NO-mediated relaxation, the LES remains tonically contracted → food cannot enter the stomach → progressive proximal esophageal dilation. Key features: dysphagia for BOTH solids and liquids from the outset (distinguishes from mechanical obstruction which starts with solids only), bird-beak on barium swallow, absent LES relaxation on manometry. Choice E (nicotinic antibodies) describes myasthenia gravis, which affects the upper esophagus (skeletal muscle), not the LES.
Q6 of 10 Pharmacology LO 2 + Pharm Medium
A 62-year-old man with metastatic pancreatic cancer on high-dose opioid therapy develops severe constipation and abdominal distension. Abdominal X-ray shows diffuse colonic gas without a transition point. His pain is currently well-controlled on the current regimen. Which of the following mechanisms best explains why methylnaltrexone can relieve his constipation without compromising his analgesia?
✓ Correct Answer: C
Teaching Point — PAMORAs: Methylnaltrexone is a peripherally-acting μ-opioid receptor antagonist (PAMORA) that does not cross the blood-brain barrier. It therefore blocks opioid-induced suppression of ENS peristalsis (μ receptors on myenteric neurons → ↓ACh → ↓peristalsis) without reversing the CNS analgesic effect. Giving systemic naloxone (a BBB-penetrant antagonist) would reverse both the GI effects AND the analgesia — making methylnaltrexone the preferred agent for opioid-induced constipation.
Q7 of 10 Physiology LO 3 Medium
A 24-year-old Peace Corps volunteer in sub-Saharan Africa develops profuse watery diarrhea after eating contaminated food. She describes "rice-water" stools. Within 12 hours she has produced an estimated 8 liters of stool. Her physicians start oral rehydration therapy (ORS) containing sodium, glucose, and water. Which statement best explains why ORS works in this setting?
✓ Correct Answer: C
Teaching Point — ORS Mechanism: V. cholerae toxin permanently activates adenylyl cyclase → ↑↑cAMP → massive Cl⁻ secretion into the lumen → water follows osmotically. However, the Na⁺-glucose cotransporter SGLT1 uses a different mechanism (Na⁺ electrochemical gradient, not cAMP) and remains fully functional. By giving glucose + sodium together in ORS, SGLT1 co-transports both Na⁺ and glucose into enterocytes → water follows by osmosis. This elegantly bypasses the toxin's cAMP-mediated disruption. ORS saves millions of lives annually at near-zero cost.
Q8 of 10 Physiology LO 1 Easy
A 58-year-old woman with long-standing scleroderma (systemic sclerosis) develops progressive lower esophageal dysphagia with heartburn. Manometry shows absent smooth muscle peristalsis in the lower esophagus and an incompetent (low-pressure) LES. Her husband, who has myasthenia gravis, develops difficulty initiating swallowing. Which of the following best explains why the location of dysphagia differs between these two patients?
✓ Correct Answer: B
Teaching Point — Esophageal Muscle Type: The upper 1/3 of the esophagus (and pharynx) is skeletal muscle; the lower 2/3 and LES are smooth muscle. Myasthenia gravis destroys nicotinic ACh receptors at the NMJ → skeletal muscle weakness → dysphagia in the upper esophagus and pharynx. Scleroderma causes fibrosis/atrophy of smooth muscle → lower esophagus and LES weakness → GERD and smooth muscle dysphagia. These two diseases beautifully illustrate why the muscle type distribution in the esophagus matters clinically.
Q9 of 10 Integration LO 5–6 Hard
A 65-year-old woman with a history of chronic liver disease (Child-Pugh Class B cirrhosis) presents with hematemesis. Upper endoscopy reveals large esophageal varices with active bleeding. Her total bilirubin is 4.2 mg/dL, albumin 2.8 g/dL, and INR 1.8. Octreotide is administered. Which physiologic principle explains why portal hypertension leads to esophageal variceal formation in this patient?
✓ Correct Answer: B
Teaching Point — Portal Hypertension & Varices: Cirrhosis → hepatic fibrosis → ↑resistance to portal flow → portal hypertension. Blood seeks alternative pathways (portosystemic anastomoses) to bypass the liver. The key pathway for esophageal varices: left gastric (coronary) vein → azygous vein → SVC. Thin-walled submucosal esophageal veins become engorged → varices → rupture risk. Other portosystemic shunts: caput medusae (paraumbilical veins), hemorrhoids (inferior mesenteric → inferior rectal). Octreotide (somatostatin analog) reduces splanchnic blood flow → lowers portal pressure acutely.
Q10 of 10 Integration LO 7 Hard
A 32-year-old man is training for a marathon. During a 20-mile training run, he notices progressive abdominal cramps and urgency to defecate. His coach explains this is a known phenomenon related to exercise physiology. Which of the following best explains the GI changes occurring during intense physical exercise?
✓ Correct Answer: B
Teaching Point — Exercise & GI Function: During intense exercise, sympathetic activation: (1) redistributes blood away from the GI tract to skeletal muscle (splanchnic vasoconstriction); (2) theoretically decreases motility, but combined with mechanical jostling, heat, and dehydration, the net result is GI distress. Reduced gut perfusion impairs enterocyte barrier function → bacterial products may translocate → local inflammation → diarrhea. This is "runner's trots," affecting up to 50% of long-distance runners. Key concept: sympathetic = ↓GI function + vasoconstriction; paradoxically still causes GI symptoms through ischemia + barrier disruption.

SAQ 1LO 1–4Medium
A medical student correctly states: "The ENS is the only part of the peripheral nervous system that can function completely independently of the CNS."

Explain why this is true — including (a) the structural basis for ENS autonomy, (b) clinical proof that this is the case, and (c) why extrinsic innervation still matters if it's not required for basic function.
(a) Structural basis: The ENS contains ~500 million neurons arranged in complete reflex arcs within the gut wall — sensory neurons, interneurons, and motor neurons. Unlike other PNS ganglia (which are only post-ganglionic neurons requiring pre-ganglionic input), the ENS has all the circuitry needed to independently detect stimuli (stretch, chemicals, osmolarity), integrate them (interneurons), and produce motor output (peristalsis, secretion) — entirely within the wall of the gut.

(b) Clinical proof: (1) Intestinal segments transplanted with all extrinsic connections severed still undergo peristalsis; (2) patients with complete spinal cord transection have preserved (though altered) GI motility; (3) infants born without a functioning ENS (Hirschsprung) do NOT have normal motility — proving it is the ENS, not the extrinsic ANS, that is indispensable for peristalsis.

(c) Why extrinsic innervation matters: The vagus (parasympathetic) amplifies peristalsis and secretion, coordinates the cephalic phase (food anticipation before arrival), and integrates GI function with the brain (gut-brain axis, satiety signals). Sympathetic input suppresses GI activity during stress/exercise. Together they fine-tune ENS activity for the body's needs — the ENS provides basal function, autonomics provide adaptability.
SAQ 2LO 2–3Medium
A 19-year-old college student with anorexia nervosa has been purging (self-induced vomiting) for 3 years. Her ABG shows pH 7.52, pCO₂ 50, HCO₃⁻ 39. Serum K⁺ is 2.6 mEq/L.

Explain the step-by-step pathophysiology producing her acid-base disorder and hypokalemia, linking it to GI fluid physiology.
Step 1 — GI Fluid Loss: Vomiting expels gastric contents (HCl — from parietal cell H⁺/K⁺ ATPase). Each mole of HCl lost = 1 mole of H⁺ removed from body fluids.

Step 2 — Primary Metabolic Alkalosis: Loss of H⁺ → serum pH rises. Kidneys compensate by retaining CO₂ (hypoventilation) → pCO₂ rises to 50 (appropriate respiratory compensation). Serum HCO₃⁻ 39 = primary elevation. This is primary metabolic alkalosis with appropriate respiratory compensation.

Step 3 — Hypokalemia (two mechanisms): (a) Gastric juice contains K⁺ — direct GI loss; (b) Volume depletion from vomiting → ↑aldosterone → principal cells exchange Na⁺ reabsorption for K⁺ secretion in urine → renal K⁺ wasting → hypokalemia.

Step 4 — Paradoxical Aciduria: Despite systemic alkalosis, the kidneys excrete acidic urine because volume depletion drives Na⁺ reabsorption; to maintain electroneutrality the kidney simultaneously excretes H⁺ and K⁺. This is "paradoxical aciduria in metabolic alkalosis."

Key USMLE pearl: Vomiting = metabolic alkalosis + hypokalemia. Diarrhea = metabolic acidosis + hypokalemia. Both cause hypokalemia but via entirely different mechanisms.
SAQ 3LO 5Hard
Explain the full mechanism of the peristaltic reflex, starting from stimulus detection through to propulsion of content. Include the neurotransmitters involved in both limbs and explain why this directionality is essential for normal GI function.
Step 1 — Stimulus Detection: A bolus of food (or gas) distends the GI wall → mechanoreceptors (stretch receptors) in the mucosa and muscle layers are activated → sensory neurons in the ENS fire.

Step 2 — ENS Integration: Sensory neurons synapse with interneurons in the myenteric plexus → two simultaneous divergent signals: one traveling ORALLY (ascending) and one ABORALLY (descending).

Step 3 — Ascending Excitation (oral/above bolus): Excitatory motor neurons release ACh + Substance P → circular muscle CONTRACTS proximal to the bolus → creates a "pusher" squeeze behind the food.

Step 4 — Descending Inhibition (aboral/below bolus): Inhibitory motor neurons release VIP + Nitric Oxide → circular muscle RELAXES distal to the bolus → creates a "receptive relaxation" space for the food to move into.

Step 5 — Net Effect: Push (contraction above) + pull (relaxation below) propels content in the aboral direction (mouth → anus).

Why directionality is essential: Without coordinated polarity, content could move in either direction → no net progression of food → failure to digest, absorb, and eliminate. This is exactly what goes wrong in Hirschsprung disease (no neurons to detect stretch and initiate the reflex) and opioid-induced ileus (opioids block excitatory neurotransmitter release → ascending excitation fails → no peristalsis).
SAQ 4LO 3Medium
A patient asks their gastroenterologist why the pH in the stomach is around 1.5–2.0, but by the time chyme reaches the jejunum the pH is 6–7. What anatomical and physiological mechanisms produce this dramatic pH shift, and why does it matter clinically?
Step 1 — Gastric Acidification: Parietal cells in the gastric fundus/body secrete HCl via the H⁺/K⁺ ATPase pump → gastric pH drops to 1.5–2.0. Stimulated by gastrin (G cells), histamine (ECL cells), and ACh (vagus).

Step 2 — Duodenal Acid Sensing: Acid chyme exits the pylorus → enters the proximal duodenum (pH falls to ~4–5). Duodenal chemoreceptors detect low pH → stimulate S cells to release secretin into the bloodstream.

Step 3 — Pancreatic Bicarbonate Neutralization: Secretin → pancreatic ductal cells secrete large volumes of HCO₃⁻-rich fluid into the duodenum via the ampulla of Vater → neutralizes acid chyme → pH rises to 6–7 by the jejunum.

Clinical Significance:
(1) Peptic ulcer disease: Excess acid damages duodenal/gastric mucosa — PPIs target the H⁺/K⁺ ATPase
(2) Pancreatic insufficiency: Without HCO₃⁻, acid remains → inactivates pancreatic lipase → fat malabsorption (steatorrhea)
(3) ZES (gastrinoma): Excess gastrin → excess HCl → acid overwhelms bicarbonate neutralization → ulcers extend into jejunum
(4) Enteric-coated drugs: Designed to dissolve only at pH ≥5–6 — would be destroyed if acid is not neutralized first
SAQ 5IntegrationHard
Using the concept of DEEP MASS, explain how a patient with complete exocrine pancreatic insufficiency (e.g., from chronic pancreatitis) would have impairment in at least FOUR of the eight GI functions. Be specific about the mechanism for each.
Exocrine pancreatic insufficiency = loss of pancreatic enzyme secretion (lipase, amylase, trypsin, chymotrypsin) + bicarbonate. This impairs at minimum:

1. Digestion (D): Most obvious. Without pancreatic lipase → fat cannot be digested → steatorrhea (bulky, greasy, foul-smelling stools). Without amylase → carbohydrate maldigestion. Without proteases (trypsin, chymotrypsin) → protein maldigestion → azotorrhea. Result: malabsorption of all macronutrients.

2. Secretion (S): Pancreatic secretions (HCO₃⁻, zymogens) account for ~1.5 L/day of the 9–10 L entering the GI tract. Loss of this secretory function directly impairs the fluid/enzymatic contribution to digestion.

3. Absorption (A): Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) require lipid micelles for absorption — without lipase and bile salt function, these vitamins are malabsorbed → deficiencies (vitamin K → bleeding; vitamin D → bone disease; vitamin A → night blindness).

4. Protection (P): Without pancreatic HCO₃⁻, duodenal pH remains low → mucosal damage → duodenal ulcers (peptic damage to epithelium undermines the mucosal barrier).

Bonus — Motility (M): Unabsorbed nutrients in the ileum trigger the "ileal brake" (PYY, GLP-1 release) → slow gastric emptying → bloating + nausea.
