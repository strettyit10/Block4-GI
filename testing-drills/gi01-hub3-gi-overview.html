Question 1 of 5 — LO 1 · Histology
A 54-year-old man with a long-standing history of chronic bile reflux undergoes an upper endoscopy. A biopsy of his gastric mucosa reveals replacement of the normal mucosal architecture with columnar epithelium containing goblet cells, consistent with intestinal metaplasia. This finding reflects pathologic changes to which of the following tissue layers of the stomach?
AMucosa
BSubmucosa
CMuscularis propria
DSerosa
EMesentery
A — Mucosa. The stomach wall has four concentric layers: mucosa (innermost), submucosa, muscularis propria, and serosa. The mucosa consists of epithelium, lamina propria, and muscularis mucosae. Chronic bile reflux damages the mucosal epithelium, triggering metaplastic transformation where gastric glands are replaced by intestinal-type columnar epithelium with goblet cells. This occurs within the mucosal layer only. The deeper layers (submucosa, muscularis propria, serosa) remain structurally intact. This intestinal metaplasia is a pre-malignant finding that increases gastric cancer risk.
Question 2 of 5 — LO 1 · Cell Types
A 68-year-old woman presents with recurrent peptic ulcer disease despite appropriate PPI therapy. Laboratory studies reveal elevated serum gastrin (450 pg/mL; normal less than 100) and fasting gastric pH of 1.2. The ulcers recur in an unusual location along the duodenojejunal junction, and imaging reveals a pancreatic mass. Which gastric epithelial cell type is most likely producing the excessive acid that drives this clinical presentation?
AChief cells
BParietal cells
CG cells
DPaneth cells
EGoblet cells
B — Parietal cells. This is a classic presentation of Zollinger-Ellison syndrome (gastrinoma). The pancreatic mass is a gastrin-secreting tumor that stimulates parietal cells in the fundus to produce excessive HCl via the proton pump. Although G cells secrete gastrin, it is the downstream target—parietal cells—that execute the acid secretion. Chief cells secrete pepsinogen, Paneth cells secrete defensins, and goblet cells secrete mucus.
Question 3 of 5 — LO 4 · ENS Physiology
During a surgical procedure, a surgeon identifies that a 15-year-old patient has an absence of ganglion cells in the terminal ileum and rectum, confirmed by histology showing absent nerve plexuses in these regions. The patient experienced chronic constipation since infancy. Which of the following neurotransmitter pairs is most important for explaining why this patient cannot generate normal peristalsis in the affected segment?
ASerotonin and dopamine
BGABA and glutamate
CAcetylcholine and nitric oxide
DNorepinephrine and VIP
ESubstance P and cholecystokinin
C — Acetylcholine and nitric oxide. This presentation describes Hirschsprung disease, characterized by congenital absence of enteric neurons (aganglionosis). Normal peristalsis requires the peristaltic reflex: ascending excitation (ACh + Substance P → contraction above the bolus) coupled with descending inhibition (NO + VIP → relaxation below the bolus). Without ENS neurons, neither ascending excitation via acetylcholine nor descending inhibition via nitric oxide can occur. Diagnosis requires rectal suction biopsy showing absent ganglion cells.
Question 4 of 5 — LO 1 · Vasculature
A 62-year-old man presents with acute severe abdominal pain and bloody diarrhea. Imaging reveals acute thrombosis of the artery supplying the proximal two-thirds of the transverse colon. Which of the following arteries is most likely occluded?
ACeliac trunk
BSuperior mesenteric artery (SMA)
CInferior mesenteric artery (IMA)
DRight colic artery
ELeft colic artery
B — Superior mesenteric artery (SMA). GI blood supply follows embryologic origin: Celiac trunk supplies foregut, SMA supplies midgut (duodenum 3rd/4th through proximal 2/3 transverse colon), IMA supplies hindgut. The proximal two-thirds of the transverse colon is midgut territory, so SMA occlusion causes ischemic injury. This presents with severe pain and bloody diarrhea. Acute thrombosis is a surgical emergency requiring revascularization or resection of infarcted bowel.
Question 5 of 5 — LO 5 · Peristaltic Reflex
An experimental pharmacology study administers a novel antagonist that selectively blocks the N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptor on inhibitory ENS motor neurons. A research assistant then stimulates the intestinal mucosa with a mechanical probe. Which of the following findings would be expected in the region immediately distal (downstream) to the stimulus?
AUnopposed excitatory signaling and sustained contraction
BLoss of relaxation and failure of peristaltic progression
CIncreased sensitivity to substance P
DEnhanced nitric oxide release
EUpregulation of parietal cell secretion
B — Loss of relaxation and failure of peristaltic progression. The peristaltic reflex depends on two simultaneous, coordinated limbs: ascending excitation (above bolus: ACh/Substance P → contraction) and descending inhibition (below bolus: NO/VIP → relaxation). The NMDA receptors on inhibitory neurons mediate descending relaxation. Blocking NMDA receptors disables the inhibitory motor neurons' ability to release NO/VIP, eliminating downstream relaxation. Without this, the peristaltic wave cannot propagate.

Question 6 of 15 — LO 3 · Fluid Balance
A 38-year-old woman with diabetic ketoacidosis and acute gastroenteritis presents with severe dehydration. Laboratory analysis reveals that she has lost approximately 3.5 liters of fluid through vomiting and diarrhea. Given that approximately 10 liters of fluid enters the GI tract daily, approximately how much fluid would normally have been absorbed by her small intestine if she had not developed these acute losses?
A2 liters
B4 liters
C8 liters
D9.8 liters
E10 liters
C — 8 liters. Approximately 10 liters of fluid enter the GI tract daily from various secretions. Of this, the small intestine absorbs about 8 L (75-80% of luminal fluid). The colon then absorbs an additional 2 L, leaving only 100-200 mL in feces under normal conditions. Diarrhea overwhelms colonic absorption capacity, causing the observed fluid loss.
Question 7 of 15 — LO 2 · Hormonal Regulation
A 45-year-old patient with suspected pancreatic insufficiency undergoes a secretin stimulation test. The test measures pancreatic secretion response by measuring bicarbonate concentration in duodenal aspirate. Based on physiology, which of the following best explains why measuring duodenal bicarbonate (rather than just measuring pancreatic juice in the pancreatic duct) is the appropriate clinical marker for secretin's effect?
ABicarbonate is produced by the stomach in response to gastric acid
BSecretin directly stimulates pancreatic ductal cells to secrete bicarbonate into the duodenum
CThe duodenum produces bicarbonate to neutralize pancreatic acid
DBicarbonate is a marker of bile duct function, not pancreatic function
EOnly duodenal fluid accurately reflects systemic hormone levels
B — Secretin directly stimulates pancreatic ductal cells to secrete bicarbonate into the duodenum. Secretin is released by S cells in the duodenum in response to low pH. Secretin's primary target is pancreatic ductal epithelial cells, which respond by secreting bicarbonate-rich fluid to neutralize stomach acid. The bicarbonate appears in the duodenum and is the measurable product of secretin's action on pancreatic function.
Question 8 of 15 — LO 3 · pH Gradient
A 52-year-old man with a 20-year history of GERD suffers erosion of his teeth due to repeated acid exposure. His esophagus is now exposed to stomach contents with a pH of approximately 1.5-2. Given that the proximal duodenum normally has a pH of 6-7 (after being neutralized by pancreatic bicarbonate), what is the primary mechanism by which the duodenum is protected from acid-induced erosion despite receiving acidic gastric contents?
ADuodenal mucosa is lined with stratified squamous epithelium
BPancreatic bicarbonate secretion neutralizes gastric acid in the duodenum
CDuodenal cells secrete H+ to buffer stomach acid
DThe ileocecal valve prevents acid reflux from the jejunum
EBile salts inactivate gastric acid
B — Pancreatic bicarbonate secretion neutralizes gastric acid in the duodenum. The duodenum faces a hostile challenge: it receives highly acidic chyme (pH 1.5-2) from the stomach, yet duodenal epithelium cannot tolerate extreme acidity. The solution: pancreatic bicarbonate secretion rapidly neutralizes gastric acid, raising pH to 6-7. Without adequate pancreatic bicarbonate (in severe pancreatitis or CF), duodenal ulcers develop despite normal gastric acid production.
Question 9 of 15 — LO 7 · Autonomic Control
A 35-year-old woman presents with acute anxiety and is given a short-acting beta-2 agonist inhaler for hyperventilation. Within 30 minutes of the dose, she develops mild diarrhea and increased gastric motility. A medical student explains this by saying, "The sympathetic nervous system was inhibited, allowing parasympathetic effects to dominate." Is this explanation physiologically correct? Why or why not?
AYes, correct. Sympathetic activity normally inhibits GI motility, so reducing sympathetic tone allows parasympathetic activation to increase motility and secretion
BNo, incorrect. Beta-2 agonists directly stimulate parasympathetic neurons
CNo, incorrect. Sympathetic activation increases GI motility
DNo, incorrect. The diarrhea is caused by reduced pancreatic secretion
EYes, but only if the patient is in a fasted state
A — Yes, correct. Parasympathetic activation (via vagus nerve) increases GI motility and secretion, while sympathetic activation inhibits motility and secretion. With reduced sympathetic inhibition, parasympathetic (cholinergic) effects become relatively unopposed, promoting gastric emptying, intestinal propulsion, and increased secretion. The key physiology: sympathetic = inhibitory, parasympathetic = stimulatory for GI function.
Question 10 of 15 — LO 6 · Cephalic Phase
A 42-year-old man with severe vagal nerve injury (CN X palsy) from a skull base tumor is unable to taste or smell food normally. When food enters his stomach, his gastric acid secretion increases minimally compared to healthy individuals. Which of the following best explains why this patient has impaired gastric acid secretion?
ALoss of sensory input from the esophagus prevents food detection
BThe cephalic phase of digestion (mediated by the vagus nerve) is lost, preventing anticipatory gastric acid secretion before food arrives
CParietal cells are denervated and cannot respond to any stimuli
DG cells in the antrum no longer produce gastrin
EThe stomach cannot move food, preventing mechanical activation of acid secretion
B — The cephalic phase of digestion (mediated by the vagus nerve) is lost, preventing anticipatory gastric acid secretion before food arrives. Digestion has three overlapping phases: cephalic (sight, smell, taste, thought of food), gastric (food in stomach), and intestinal (chyme in small intestine). The cephalic phase is mediated by the vagus nerve (CN X): afferent vagal fibers carry sensory signals to trigger efferent vagal signaling that stimulates parietal cells before food arrives. Vagal injury eliminates the cephalic phase but does not eliminate gastric phase secretion (when food directly stimulates G cells). So acid secretion is reduced but not absent.

Question 11 of 15 — LOs 1, 2 · Anatomy & Physiology Integration
A 28-year-old medical student is studying for the USMLE Step 1 exam and learns that parietal cells secrete both HCl and intrinsic factor. She reads that parietal cells are located in the gastric fundus, while another source mentions parietal cells in the gastric body. She is confused about whether these are different populations or the same cells. Which of the following statements best resolves this confusion?
AThe fundus and body are histologically similar regions both containing fundic glands, so parietal cells in either location are functionally equivalent
BParietal cells in the fundus produce HCl, while parietal cells in the body produce intrinsic factor
CThe body and fundus are different organs, with separate blood supplies
DParietal cells exist only in the fundus; the body contains only chief cells
EParietal cells migrate from fundus to body during digestion
A — The fundus and body are histologically similar regions both containing fundic glands, so parietal cells in either location are functionally equivalent. The stomach is divided into cardia, fundus, and pyloric (antral) regions. The fundus and body are histologically continuous and both contain fundic glands—collections of parietal cells, chief cells, and G cells. Thus, parietal cells throughout the fundus and body are the same population, producing identical quantities of HCl and intrinsic factor. The distinction is purely anatomic/positional, not functional.
Question 12 of 15 — LOs 3, 4 · Pathophysiology
A 19-year-old patient with cystic fibrosis presents with recurrent abdominal pain and evidence of pancreatic atrophy on imaging. Despite having adequate gastric acid secretion and normal intrinsic factor, the patient develops vitamin B12 deficiency anemia. Given the pathophysiology of CF, which of the following best explains why pancreatic insufficiency leads to B12 deficiency?
AThe stomach fails to produce intrinsic factor
BThe small intestine loses its ability to absorb any nutrients
CPancreatic proteases are needed to degrade the R-protein that binds B12, allowing intrinsic factor to bind B12 for ileal absorption
DLack of pancreatic enzymes prevents absorption of intrinsic factor itself
EPancreatic amylase is required to synthesize B12 in the terminal ileum
C — Pancreatic proteases are needed to degrade the R-protein that binds B12, allowing intrinsic factor to bind B12 for ileal absorption. B12 absorption requires a sophisticated multi-step process: B12 binds to R-protein in the stomach. In the duodenum, pancreatic proteases degrade R-protein, releasing B12. The freed B12 then binds to intrinsic factor (IF), forming the B12-IF complex that is absorbed in the terminal ileum. In CF, pancreatic atrophy eliminates proteases, so B12 remains trapped on R-protein and cannot bind IF for absorption.
Question 13 of 15 — LOs 2, 5, 6 · Pharmacology & Mechanism
A 56-year-old man with severe acid reflux is prescribed a proton pump inhibitor (PPI). He is also evaluated for H. pylori infection, and the test is surprisingly negative. However, unbeknownst to the patient, he started PPI therapy 2 weeks before the H. pylori serologic test was performed. Which of the following best explains why the H. pylori test might incorrectly appear negative despite active infection?
APPIs directly kill H. pylori bacteria
BPPIs increase gastric pH, reducing the bacterial load below the detection threshold
CPPIs reduce acid-dependent H. pylori activation, decreasing urease production and reducing bacterial detection by urea breath test or antigen assays
DPPIs enhance the immune response, clearing the infection entirely
EPPIs block H. pylori from crossing the gastric mucosa
C — PPIs reduce acid-dependent H. pylori activation, decreasing urease production and reducing bacterial detection by urea breath test or antigen assays. PPIs work by irreversibly blocking the H+/K+-ATPase proton pump on parietal cells, reducing gastric acid production and raising gastric pH. H. pylori produces urease (ammonia) to neutralize acid in response to acidic conditions. When PPI therapy raises pH, bacteria sense less acid stress and reduce urease production. Urea breath tests and antigen assays rely on detecting urease activity, so reduced enzyme expression gives false-negative results. Best practice: discontinue PPIs 2 weeks before H. pylori testing.
Question 14 of 15 — LOs 1, 3, 4 · Complex Integration
A 34-year-old woman undergoing a total gastrectomy for gastric cancer is counseled on expected postoperative complications. She is told she will require lifelong B12 supplementation, may experience "dumping syndrome," and has reduced sensation of fullness after eating. Which single anatomic/physiologic change best explains all three of these complications?
ALoss of parietal cells → reduced intrinsic factor → B12 malabsorption only
BComplete removal of the stomach eliminates intrinsic factor (B12 deficiency), removes the gastric reservoir function (rapid emptying → dumping), and eliminates mechanoreceptors that mediate satiety signaling (reduced fullness sensation)
CDamage to the vagus nerve during surgery
DLoss of G cells → reduced gastrin → impaired acid secretion in the small intestine
EIncreased CCK release from the duodenum
B — Complete removal of the stomach eliminates intrinsic factor (B12 deficiency), removes the gastric reservoir function (rapid emptying → dumping), and eliminates mechanoreceptors that mediate satiety signaling (reduced fullness sensation). The stomach serves three critical functions: (1) intrinsic factor secretion for B12 absorption, (2) reservoir function that regulates gastric emptying—fast emptying overwhelms small intestine absorption, causing dumping syndrome (osmotic diarrhea, vasomotor flushing from rapid glucose absorption), and (3) mechanoreceptor signaling that triggers satiety. Gastrectomy eliminates all three, explaining the triad.
Question 15 of 15 — LOs 1-7 · Comprehensive Synthesis
A 51-year-old man with a 30-year history of uncontrolled GERD and a new diagnosis of Barrett's esophagus undergoes upper endoscopy. A suspicious ulcerated lesion is biopsied. Pathology reveals a gastrin-secreting neuroendocrine tumor (gastrinoma) in the gastric body. The patient is started on high-dose PPI therapy, and his ulcers heal within weeks. However, the tumor is not surgically resectable. Over the next 6 months, despite adequate PPI therapy, the patient develops severe diarrhea. Given knowledge of GI physiology, which of the following best explains the diarrhea?
AThe gastrinoma is growing and metastasizing to the small intestine
BHigh-dose PPI therapy is directly causative of diarrhea
CContinued gastric acid secretion overwhelms intestinal buffer capacity, inactivating pancreatic enzymes (trypsin) and disrupting optimal pH for lipase function, causing fat malabsorption and osmotic diarrhea
DThe tumor secretes additional hormones (VIP or motilin) that cause diarrhea
EUncontrolled gastric acid destroys parietal cells, eliminating intrinsic factor
C — Continued gastric acid secretion overwhelms intestinal buffer capacity, inactivating pancreatic enzymes (trypsin) and disrupting optimal pH for lipase function, causing fat malabsorption and osmotic diarrhea. Zollinger-Ellison syndrome (gastrinoma) produces massive amounts of gastrin, driving relentless parietal cell acid secretion. Even on high-dose PPIs, the sustained acid production may exceed the PPI's suppressive capacity. Excess acid in the duodenum overwhelms the small intestine's buffer capacity. The resulting low pH inactivates pancreatic proteases (trypsin requires pH >4) and disrupts pancreatic lipase function. Reduced lipase activity leads to fat malabsorption and osmotic diarrhea. Paradoxically, severe acid hypersecretion causes diarrhea rather than constipation because undigested fats osmotically draw water into the intestine.
