LOs 1 & 3 — Innervation, Referred Pain, Meckel's
Enteric Nervous System — Two Plexuses
PlexusLocation in WallFunction
Myenteric (Auerbach's)
Between the circular and longitudinal smooth muscle layers
Controls smooth muscle contraction → peristalsis and GI motility (Myenteric = Muscle)
Submucosal (Meissner's)
In the submucosa (between mucosa and circular muscle layer)
Controls glandular secretions and mucosal blood flow (Submucosal = Secretions)
Hirschsprung Disease
Ganglion cells (both myenteric + submucosal plexus neurons absent); distal colon and rectum (aganglionic segment)
Transition zone (the key diagnostic finding); narrow aganglionic segment; proximal dilated colon (megacolon)
Neural crest cell migration failure (fail to colonize distal gut during development); RET proto-oncogene mutation (also associated with MEN2A)

Practice Questions — GI08 USMLE-Style
Q1. During cholecystectomy, the surgeon compresses the hepatoduodenal ligament between thumb and finger to stop liver bleeding (Pringle maneuver). Which 3 structures are occluded?
A. Portal vein, hepatic artery, common bile duct
B. Portal vein, IVC, cystic duct
C. Hepatic veins, hepatic artery, cystic duct
D. SMA, portal vein, cystic duct
E. Hepatic artery, celiac trunk, common bile duct
✅ A — Portal vein, hepatic artery proper, and common bile duct (portal triad). The hepatoduodenal ligament is the free edge of the lesser omentum and forms the anterior border of the omental foramen. All three structures in it are temporarily occluded to control hepatic inflow.
Q2. Which list contains ONLY retroperitoneal structures?
A. Stomach, duodenum D1, sigmoid colon, rectum
B. Suprarenal glands, aorta, duodenum D2–D4, pancreas body/tail
C. Suprarenal glands, kidneys, duodenum D2–D4, ascending colon
D. Liver, gallbladder, pancreas head, cecum
E. Spleen, transverse colon, sigmoid colon, appendix
✅ C — SAD PUCKER: Suprarenal glands, Aorta/IVC, Duodenum D2–D4, Pancreas (except tail), Ureters, Colon (ascending & descending), Kidneys, Esophagus, Rectum. Pancreas tail is intraperitoneal (splenorenal ligament). Stomach, spleen, transverse colon, sigmoid colon, liver, and gallbladder are intraperitoneal.
Q3. During trauma laparotomy, a surgeon enters the lesser sac. Through which structure did they most likely pass?
A. Through the greater omentum directly
B. Via the epiploic foramen (foramen of Winslow), posterior to the hepatoduodenal ligament
C. Through the left gastroepiploic vessels
D. Via the falciform ligament
E. Through the transverse mesocolon
✅ B — The epiploic foramen (foramen of Winslow) is the natural communication between the greater and lesser peritoneal sacs. Bounded anteriorly by the hepatoduodenal ligament, posteriorly by IVC, superiorly by caudate lobe of liver, inferiorly by D1.
Q4. Endoscopy shows a dilated esophagus with bird-beak narrowing at the GEJ. Manometry confirms failure of the LES to relax. What cell type is lost?
A. Interstitial cells of Cajal
B. Myenteric plexus inhibitory nitrergic neurons
C. Submucosal plexus secretomotor neurons
D. Chief cells of the gastric fundus
E. Parietal cells of gastric body
✅ B — Achalasia = loss of inhibitory neurons (NO/VIP) in the myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus at the LES → tonic contraction → failure of relaxation → progressive dysphagia to BOTH solids and liquids from onset. Chagas disease (T. cruzi) destroys these neurons → secondary achalasia.
Q5. A 72-year-old woman with atrial fibrillation develops sudden abdominal pain and bloody diarrhea after colonoscopy shows ischemic changes at the splenic flexure. Why is this location vulnerable?
A. Splenic flexure has a long mesentery prone to volvulus
B. Watershed area between SMA and IMA territories with limited collateral supply
C. Splenic flexure receives blood exclusively from IMA with no collaterals
D. Retroperitoneal; compressed by left kidney
E. Site of transition between taeniae coli and continuous longitudinal muscle
✅ B — The splenic flexure is a watershed zone between SMA territory (middle colic artery) and IMA territory (left colic artery). The marginal artery of Drummond is often tenuous here → most vulnerable during low-flow states, embolism, or AAA repair.
Q6. A patient with cirrhosis vomits bright red blood. Endoscopy reveals varices at the GEJ. These varices arise from dilation of which portosystemic anastomosis?
A. Superior rectal vein ↔ middle/inferior rectal veins
B. Paraumbilical veins ↔ superficial epigastric veins
C. Left gastric (coronary) vein ↔ azygos/esophageal veins
D. Splenic vein ↔ left renal vein
E. SMV ↔ inferior epigastric veins
✅ C — Esophageal varices form where the left gastric (coronary) vein (portal) meets the azygos/esophageal system (systemic). Most dangerous portosystemic shunt — rupture → massive hematemesis. Anorectal varices = superior rectal ↔ inferior/middle rectal; caput medusae = paraumbilical ↔ superficial abdominal.
Q7. A 3-day-old infant fails to pass meconium. Barium enema shows megacolon proximal to a narrow rectosigmoid segment. Rectal biopsy: absent ganglion cells. What is the underlying mechanism?
A. Neural crest cell migration failure → tonically constricted aganglionic segment
B. Hypertrophy of myenteric plexus → excessive peristalsis
C. Loss of pacemaker cells (ICC) in sigmoid colon
D. Overactivation of Meissner's plexus → hypersecretion
E. Absence of internal anal sphincter smooth muscle
✅ A — Hirschsprung disease: neural crest cells fail to migrate to the distal colon → absent myenteric AND submucosal plexus ganglion cells → aganglionic segment stays tonically contracted → functional obstruction → proximal colon dilates (megacolon). RET proto-oncogene mutation.
Q8. A 30-year-old man has painless rectal bleeding. Colonoscopy finds a 2 cm outpouching 2 feet proximal to the ileocecal valve with ectopic gastric and pancreatic tissue on biopsy. Where would a Meckel's scan show a hot spot?
A. Right upper quadrant
B. Left upper quadrant
C. Left lower quadrant
D. Right lower quadrant
E. Periumbilical region
✅ D — ⁹⁹ᵐTc-pertechnetate is taken up by ectopic gastric mucosa → hot spot in the RLQ where the terminal ileum lies (2 feet from ileocecal valve). Painless rectal bleeding in a child = Meckel's diverticulum until proven otherwise (Rule of 2's).
