Q1 — LO1: A 58-year-old man with alcoholic cirrhosis presents with hematemesis. Endoscopy confirms large esophageal varices. Which portal tributary is most directly responsible for the formation of esophageal varices in portal hypertension?
A) Right gastric vein
B) Left gastric (coronary) vein
C) Inferior mesenteric vein
D) Paraumbilical veins
B — Left gastric (coronary) vein. The left gastric vein drains the lesser curvature and lower esophagus into the portal system. In portal HTN, elevated pressure reverses flow through the left gastric vein, engorging submucosal esophageal veins → varices. Paraumbilical veins (D) → caput medusae; IMV (C) → anorectal/sigmoid varices.
Q2 — LO1: A surgeon planning a right hepatic lobectomy must identify Cantlie’s line to define the true functional midplane. Cantlie’s line runs between which two structures?
A) Falciform ligament and ligamentum teres
B) Left triangular ligament and porta hepatis
C) Gallbladder fossa and groove for the IVC
D) Right hepatic vein and left hepatic duct
C — Gallbladder fossa to IVC groove. Cantlie’s line is the true surgical midplane separating functional right and left lobes. The falciform ligament (A) is only a surface landmark — the quadrate and caudate lobes appear right-sided but are functionally left-sided by Couinaud classification. Never confuse the falciform with the functional midplane on Step 1.
Q3 — LO1: A surgeon performing laparoscopic cholecystectomy must achieve the “critical view of safety” within Calot’s triangle before clipping. What are the correct boundaries of Calot’s triangle?
A) Cystic duct, common bile duct, and portal vein
B) Cystic duct, hepatic artery proper, and liver surface
C) Cystic duct, common hepatic duct, and inferior liver surface
D) Common hepatic duct, right hepatic duct, and left hepatic duct
C — Cystic duct (inferior), common hepatic duct (medial), inferior liver surface (superior). The cystic artery (typically from the right hepatic artery) runs within this triangle and must be identified before clipping. Misidentifying the CBD as the cystic duct is the most feared complication of lap cholecystectomy.
Q4 — LO2: A 22-year-old man sustains blunt abdominal trauma in a motor vehicle collision. He has LUQ tenderness and left shoulder tip pain. CT shows a splenic laceration. The spleen lies deep to which ribs?
A) Ribs 5–7
B) Ribs 7–9
C) Ribs 9–11
D) Ribs 11–12
C — Ribs 9–11. The spleen lies in the LUQ deep to ribs 9–11 in the midaxillary line; its long axis follows the 10th rib. Left shoulder tip pain (Kehr’s sign) = blood under the left diaphragm irritating the phrenic nerve (C3–C5). Rib 9–11 fractures are the classic mechanism of splenic laceration. The spleen is the most commonly injured organ in blunt abdominal trauma.
Q5 — LO2: A 30-year-old woman with hereditary spherocytosis undergoes elective splenectomy. Six weeks later she develops fulminant sepsis; blood cultures grow S. pneumoniae. Which intervention should have been performed to prevent this complication?
A) Prophylactic anticoagulation to prevent splenic vein thrombosis
B) Vaccination against encapsulated organisms before splenectomy
C) Long-term antifungal prophylaxis
D) IVIG infusion to replace immunoglobulin production
B — Vaccination against encapsulated organisms (SHiN) before splenectomy. The spleen clears opsonized encapsulated bacteria (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae type b, N. meningitidis). Without it, OPSI can be fatal (50–70% mortality). Vaccines should ideally be given 2 weeks before elective splenectomy for optimal immune response.
Q6 — LO3: A 68-year-old man with a 3-month history of painless progressive jaundice, weight loss, and new-onset diabetes presents with a palpable, non-tender gallbladder. CT shows a 3 cm mass in the head of the pancreas. Which anatomical relationship explains the jaundice?
A) The tumor compresses the portal vein → portal hypertension
B) The CBD runs in a groove on the posterior surface of the pancreatic head → tumor compresses CBD
C) The tumor invades the duodenum → malabsorption of bilirubin
D) The splenic vein thrombosis → left-sided portal hypertension
B — CBD runs in a groove on the posterior surface of the pancreatic head. This is Courvoisier’s law: painless jaundice + palpable (distended, non-tender) gallbladder = pancreatic cancer until proven otherwise. The GB distends because slow obstruction allows gradual dilation; in gallstone disease, the GB wall is fibrotic and cannot distend. 70% of pancreatic cancers arise in the head.
Q7 — LO3: A 45-year-old woman with known cholelithiasis develops sudden epigastric pain radiating to the back, vomiting, and jaundice. Labs: lipase 3,400 U/L, direct bilirubin elevated, ALP elevated. Which single anatomical site explains all three findings?
A) Cystic duct obstruction
B) Common hepatic duct obstruction
C) Ampulla of Vater (hepatopancreatic ampulla)
D) Minor duodenal papilla obstruction
C — Ampulla of Vater. A gallstone impacted at the ampulla simultaneously obstructs both the CBD (→ obstructive jaundice, ↑ ALP, ↑ direct bilirubin) and the main pancreatic duct (→ pancreatitis, ↑ lipase). The ampulla is the narrowest point of the biliary tract — gallstones passing the CBD can become trapped here. This is why gallstones and alcohol are the two most common causes of acute pancreatitis.
Q8 — LO4: A medical student is reviewing digestive physiology. Which of the following correctly pairs each hormone with its primary stimulus and action?
A) CCK released by acid → stimulates pancreatic HCO₃⁻ secretion
B) Secretin released by fat → causes gallbladder contraction
C) CCK released by fat + protein → GB contraction + sphincter of Oddi relaxation + pancreatic enzyme secretion
D) Secretin released by fat → stimulates pancreatic acinar enzyme secretion
C — CCK released by fat + protein (I-cells) → GB contraction + SOO relaxation + enzyme secretion. Remember: CCK = fat+protein stimulus, I-cells, GB contracts, SOO relaxes, acinar enzyme secretion. Secretin = acid (H⁺) stimulus, S-cells, ductal HCO₃⁻ secretion (neutralizes chyme). Both work together to optimize fat digestion in the duodenum.
