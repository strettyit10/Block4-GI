Q1. A researcher records rhythmic oscillations in resting membrane potential from a strip of small intestinal smooth muscle suspended in a dish with no neural connections. These oscillations occur at a constant frequency, do NOT reach action potential threshold, and are present even when the muscle is completely relaxed. Which cells are responsible for generating these oscillations?
A. Enterochromaffin cells of the mucosal epithelium
B. Interstitial Cells of Cajal (ICC) in the myenteric plexus
C. Circular smooth muscle cells responding to stretch
D. ENS motor neurons in the submucosal plexus
E. Mast cells releasing histamine in response to distension
B — Interstitial Cells of Cajal (ICC)
ICC are the pacemaker cells of the GI tract, located in the myenteric plexus region between circular and longitudinal muscle layers. They generate the Basic Electrical Rhythm (BER) — rhythmic subthreshold slow wave oscillations. BER is always present and autonomous (does not require neural input, as demonstrated by this in-vitro setup). Slow waves do NOT cause contractions by themselves — contractions only occur when a slow wave peak reaches −40 mV, triggering an action potential. The key distinctions: slow waves = always present; APs = only when threshold reached; contractions = only with APs.
Q2. A new drug selectively opens K⁺ channels in GI smooth muscle cells, shifting the resting membrane potential from −65 mV to −75 mV. BER slow waves continue normally but no peristaltic contractions can be observed. Which of the following BEST explains why contractions are absent despite intact slow waves?
A. BER frequency is reduced, preventing pacemaker activation
B. Ca²⁺ channels are permanently inactivated by the drug
C. Slow wave peaks cannot reach the −40 mV AP threshold → no Ca²⁺ influx → no contraction
D. ACh release from ENS motor neurons is blocked
E. The drug destroys ICC, eliminating the BER signal
C — Slow wave peaks cannot reach the −40 mV AP threshold → no Ca²⁺ influx → no contraction
Hyperpolarizing the membrane (to −75 mV) means slow wave oscillations, even at their peak, remain below the AP threshold of −40 mV. Without an AP, voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels do not open, Ca²⁺ does not enter, calmodulin is not activated, MLCK is not phosphorylated, and myosin cross-bridge cycling cannot occur → no contraction. The BER is unchanged (slow waves are generated by ICC intrinsically). This is the mechanism by which sympathetic NE, VIP, and NO hyperpolarize smooth muscle to reduce motility — they shift the membrane potential away from AP threshold.
Q3. A 70-year-old patient with a brainstem stroke develops severe dysphagia. He aspirates during swallowing attempts. On evaluation, the oral stage of swallowing appears intact (he can manipulate food with his tongue). Which phase of swallowing has been MOST disrupted, and what is the anatomical basis?
A. Oral phase — cortical damage prevents voluntary tongue movement
B. Pharyngeal phase — medullary swallowing center damage → loss of involuntary coordination
C. Esophageal phase (primary peristalsis) — vagal nuclei destroyed
D. Esophageal phase (secondary peristalsis) — ENS plexuses affected by brainstem damage
E. LES relaxation — NTS damage prevents VIP/NO release
B — Pharyngeal phase — medullary swallowing center damage → loss of involuntary coordination
The pharyngeal phase is coordinated by the medullary swallowing center (nucleus tractus solitarius + dorsal motor nucleus of vagus). It is entirely involuntary. A brainstem stroke affecting the medulla disrupts: soft palate elevation (closure of nasopharynx), epiglottic closure (airway protection → aspiration), UES relaxation, and pharyngeal peristalsis. The oral phase is cortically controlled (voluntary) and is preserved. The esophageal phase requires vagal input for primary peristalsis but secondary peristalsis is ENS-driven (intrinsic) and may partially persist.
Q4. A 42-year-old woman has a 3-year history of progressive dysphagia to both solids AND liquids, regurgitation of undigested food, and recurrent aspiration episodes at night. Esophageal manometry reveals elevated resting LES pressure with failure to relax during swallowing, and aperistalsis in the distal esophagus. Barium swallow shows esophageal dilation with "bird-beak" narrowing at the GEJ. Which neurotransmitters are ABSENT, and which treatment temporarily inhibits the remaining excitatory input?
A. Excess VIP and NO → botulinum toxin blocks VIP release
B. Absent VIP and NO → botulinum toxin blocks ACh release at LES
C. Absent ACh and Substance P → metoclopramide restores excitatory drive
D. Excess ACh → atropine blocks muscarinic receptors at LES
E. Absent motilin → erythromycin activates remaining motilin receptors
B — Absent VIP and NO → botulinum toxin blocks ACh release at LES
Achalasia = selective loss of inhibitory ENS neurons releasing VIP and NO. These are required for LES relaxation ahead of each peristaltic wave. Without inhibitory input, unopposed excitatory ACh/Substance P keeps the LES tonically contracted. Treatment: (1) Pneumatic balloon dilation — mechanically disrupts LES muscle; (2) Heller myotomy — surgical LES cut; (3) Botulinum toxin — blocks ACh (excitatory) release at the LES → rebalances excitation/inhibition temporarily. Effect is temporary because nerve terminals regenerate. Key distinction: achalasia causes dysphagia to BOTH solids and liquids (motor defect), unlike mechanical obstruction (esophageal cancer) which is solids first.
Q5. A patient who underwent truncal vagotomy for peptic ulcer disease now complains of early satiety — feeling full after eating only a small amount. His gastric acid secretion is significantly reduced (the intended therapeutic effect). What physiologic mechanism BEST explains the early satiety symptom?
A. Vagotomy reduces gastrin secretion → decreased gastric motility
B. Vagotomy impairs receptive relaxation (VIP-mediated accommodation) → rapid intragastric pressure rise with small meals
C. Vagotomy prevents CCK release from duodenal I cells → faster gastric emptying → early fullness
D. Vagotomy disrupts the gastroileal reflex → ileal distension inhibits satiety centers
E. Vagotomy eliminates the cephalic phase → no anticipatory gastric relaxation before eating
B — Vagotomy impairs receptive relaxation (VIP-mediated accommodation) → rapid intragastric pressure rise with small meals
Receptive relaxation (gastric accommodation) is vagally-mediated: food entering the stomach → vagal efferents → ENS inhibitory neurons → VIP + NO release → fundus/corpus smooth muscle relaxes → stomach can accommodate 1–1.5 L with minimal pressure rise. After vagotomy, this reflex arc is broken. The stomach cannot accommodate food normally → intragastric pressure rises sharply with small volumes → mechanoreceptors in the gastric wall fire → satiety signals → early satiety. This is also the principle behind bariatric sleeve gastrectomy (removes fundus → no accommodation → restricted intake). Note: vagotomy also reduces acid secretion (intended), impairs primary esophageal peristalsis, and affects gastric emptying.
Q6. A 55-year-old man with chronic back pain on long-term opioid therapy develops small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO), confirmed by hydrogen breath testing. He denies any anatomic abnormality of his small intestine. His eating pattern consists of small snacks every 1–2 hours throughout the day. Which TWO mechanisms MOST directly explain his SIBO?
A. Opioids decrease gastric acid → bacteria not killed; excess dietary fiber feeds bacteria
B. Opioid μ-receptor activation in ENS → ↓MMC activity; continuous snacking → no fasting state → MMC never initiates
C. Opioids hyperstimulate 5-HT3 receptors → retrograde peristalsis introduces colonic bacteria into SI
D. Opioids block motilin receptors → MMC Phase III cannot occur; protein-rich diet promotes bacterial growth
E. Opioids stimulate gastrocolic reflex → bacteria rapidly pushed from colon into terminal ileum
B — Opioid μ-receptor activation in ENS → ↓MMC activity; continuous snacking → no fasting state → MMC never initiates
The MMC is the interdigestive "housekeeper" — it sweeps bacteria and debris from stomach → terminal ileum every 75–120 min ONLY during fasting. Two mechanisms are disrupting it here: (1) Opioids bind μ-receptors on ENS neurons → ↓ACh + ↓Substance P release → ↓peristalsis + ↓MMC amplitude/coordination. (2) Continuous snacking means the gut never enters the fasting (interdigestive) state → MMC cannot initiate. Without MMC Phase III sweeping contractions, bacteria accumulate in the small intestine → SIBO. Clinical note: Erythromycin (motilin agonist) can stimulate Phase III-like contractions. Structured meal timing (avoiding grazing) is recommended in SIBO patients to allow MMC to function.
Q7. A 4-week-old male infant is brought in with a 3-week history of failure to pass meconium, progressively worsening abdominal distension, and bilious vomiting. Barium enema shows a narrow rectosigmoid segment with proximal colonic dilation. Rectal biopsy reveals absent ganglion cells in the submucosa and myenteric plexus. Which of the following CORRECTLY explains why the colon is dilated PROXIMAL to the abnormal segment?
A. The aganglionic segment is hypotonic → feces flow freely into it and accumulate distally
B. The aganglionic segment maintains normal peristalsis but the sphincter of Oddi is obstructed
C. The aganglionic segment is tonically contracted (no VIP/NO inhibitory neurons) → functional obstruction → feces accumulate proximally → megacolon
D. The aganglionic segment is the site of ganglionic hypertrophy → feces diverted proximally
E. The proximal dilated segment has absent ganglia → cannot propel contents → dilation
C — The aganglionic segment is tonically contracted → functional obstruction → feces accumulate proximally → megacolon
Hirschsprung's disease = failure of neural crest cell migration → ENS ganglia absent in distal colon/rectum. Without inhibitory ENS neurons (VIP + NO), the aganglionic segment cannot relax → remains in tonic contraction → functional obstruction. Feces cannot pass through this narrow contracted segment → back up proximally → the normally-ganglionated proximal colon becomes massively dilated (megacolon). Key exam points: (1) Aganglionic segment = NARROW (tonic); (2) Megacolon = PROXIMAL (normal ganglia but overfilled); (3) Diagnosis = biopsy (absent ganglion cells) + manometry (absent rectosphincteric reflex — IAS does not relax). Treatment = surgical resection of aganglionic segment (pull-through procedure).
Q8. A marathon runner experiences severe abdominal cramping, urgency, and loose stools during a race. Her resting GI function is completely normal. During the race, which pattern of ANS activity BEST explains her GI symptoms, and what is the PARADOX of getting diarrhea despite sympathetic activation?
A. PSNS dominance → ↑peristalsis + ↓sphincter tone → loose stools; no paradox exists
B. SNS dominance → normally ↓GI motility, but mechanical vibration + gut ischemia + hormones override this → diarrhea despite SNS activity
C. SNS dominance → ↑peristalsis due to NE binding β receptors in GI smooth muscle
D. Equal ANS balance → neither PSNS nor SNS is dominant during exercise
E. PSNS withdrawal → ↓secretion → paradoxically ↑intraluminal osmolarity → diarrhea
B — SNS dominance → normally ↓GI motility, but mechanical vibration + gut ischemia + hormones override this → diarrhea despite SNS activity
During intense exercise, the SNS dominates. SNS effects on GI: ↓motility, ↓secretion, splanchnic vasoconstriction (blood to muscles), ↑sphincter tone. These effects SHOULD cause constipation — yet runner's diarrhea is common. The paradox is explained by: (1) Mechanical vibration from running stimulates colonic contractions; (2) Splanchnic vasoconstriction → gut ischemia → mucosal permeability ↑ → loose stools; (3) Exercise hormones (motilin, PYY, peptide YY) can promote colonic motility; (4) Reduced EAS voluntary control during intense exertion. Understanding this highlights that GI motility is regulated by multiple simultaneous inputs — the ANS does not operate in isolation.
