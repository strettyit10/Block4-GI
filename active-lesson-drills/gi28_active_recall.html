LOs 1–4
Q1: Crohn vs UC — histology
A 24-year-old man with chronic RLQ pain, diarrhea, perianal fistula, and iron deficiency undergoes colonoscopy. Terminal ileum biopsy shows transmural lymphoid aggregates and a non-caseating granuloma. Diagnosis?

A) Ulcerative colitis
B) Crohn disease
C) Intestinal TB
D) Behçet disease
E) Ischemic colitis
Correct Answer: B

Crohn disease — terminal ileum involvement, transmural inflammation, fistula, non-caseating granuloma. UC spares the terminal ileum (except backwash ileitis), is mucosa-limited, and never causes granulomas. TB causes caseating granulomas.
Q2: UC complication
A 39-year-old woman with 12-year history of UC presents with fever, severe abdominal distention, and bloody stools. AXR: transverse colon dilated to 8 cm. Which complication?

A) Fistula formation
B) Toxic megacolon
C) Terminal ileitis
D) Diverticulitis
E) Colonic volvulus
Correct Answer: B

Toxic megacolon = colonic dilation > 6 cm + systemic toxicity. Risk of perforation is high. Stop anti-motility agents, aggressive resuscitation, IV corticosteroids; colectomy if failing.
Q3: IBD extraintestinal — PSC
A 32-year-old man with long-standing UC develops pruritus and elevated ALP. MRCP: "beaded" intra- and extra-hepatic bile ducts. Diagnosis and antibody?

A) Primary biliary cholangitis; AMA
B) Primary sclerosing cholangitis; p-ANCA
C) Autoimmune hepatitis; ANA
D) Hepatocellular carcinoma; AFP
E) Cholangiocarcinoma; CA 19-9
Correct Answer: B

PSC — characteristic "onion-skin" fibrosis, beaded ducts on MRCP, strongly associated with UC, positive p-ANCA in ~70%. Increased risk of cholangiocarcinoma.
Q4: C. diff — treatment
A 65-year-old woman 5 days post-op on ceftriaxone develops 10 watery stools/day, WBC 18k, Cr stable. Stool PCR and toxin EIA positive. Best initial treatment?

A) IV metronidazole monotherapy
B) Oral vancomycin OR oral fidaxomicin
C) IV vancomycin
D) Loperamide
E) Rifaximin
Correct Answer: B

Current first-line for non-severe initial C. diff = oral vancomycin OR oral fidaxomicin. Fidaxomicin has lower recurrence. IV vanco does NOT penetrate colonic lumen. Loperamide is contraindicated.
Q5: Diverticulitis management
A 62-year-old woman presents with 3 days of LLQ pain, fever 38.5 °C, WBC 14k. CT: inflamed sigmoid diverticula with small pericolonic abscess (3 cm). Best management?

A) Emergent colectomy
B) Outpatient observation alone
C) IV antibiotics + bowel rest; CT-guided drainage if abscess enlarges
D) Colonoscopy during acute episode
E) Barium enema
Correct Answer: C

Hinchey Ib (pericolonic abscess) → IV antibiotics (cipro + metronidazole or pip-tazo) and bowel rest. Drain abscess if > 4 cm. Colonoscopy is CONTRAINDICATED in acute diverticulitis (perforation risk); do it 4–6 weeks later.
Q6: Villous adenoma
A 68-year-old man has copious watery mucoid diarrhea, K 2.8, and a 4-cm rectal mass. Biopsy: polyp composed of long finger-like projections of dysplastic epithelium. Which lesion carries the HIGHEST risk of malignant transformation?

A) Hyperplastic polyp
B) Tubular adenoma
C) Tubulovillous adenoma
D) Villous adenoma
E) Juvenile polyp
Correct Answer: D

Villous adenoma — highest malignant potential (> 30% when > 4 cm). Can cause secretory/hypokalemic diarrhea (McKittrick-Wheelock syndrome). Hyperplastic polyps have no malignant potential.
Q7: CRC — classic adenoma-carcinoma sequence
Which is the EARLIEST and most common mutation in the classic chromosomal instability pathway of sporadic CRC?

A) KRAS
B) p53
C) APC (tumor suppressor)
D) DCC / SMAD4
E) MLH1/MSH2 (MMR)
Correct Answer: C

APC loss is the earliest event (initiation → small adenoma). Sequence: APC → KRAS → p53. MMR mutations (MLH1/MSH2) define the Lynch/MSI pathway (serrated precursor or Lynch syndrome).
Q8: Right-sided CRC presentation
A 72-year-old man presents with fatigue; labs: Hgb 8.2, MCV 72, ferritin 9. No overt GI bleeding. Most likely diagnosis/location?

A) Left-sided CRC, obstructing
B) Right-sided CRC (occult GI bleed, iron-deficiency anemia)
C) Gastric ulcer
D) Hemorrhoids
E) Diverticular bleed
Correct Answer: B

Right-sided CRC typically presents with iron-deficiency anemia from chronic occult bleeding; the wide cecal lumen tolerates bulky exophytic ("fungating") growth without obstruction. Left-sided CRC tends to obstruct (napkin-ring) and present with change in bowel habits.
Q9: Lynch syndrome
A 45-year-old woman has colon cancer; her mother had endometrial cancer at 48 and her uncle had a right-sided colon cancer at 52. Germline testing shows a pathogenic MSH2 mutation. Which additional cancer is she most at risk for?

A) Osteosarcoma
B) Endometrial and ovarian cancer
C) Medullary thyroid cancer
D) Pheochromocytoma
E) Glioblastoma
Correct Answer: B

Lynch syndrome (HNPCC) — autosomal dominant MMR gene mutation (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2). Endometrial cancer is the 2nd most common after colon cancer (lifetime risk up to 60%). Also ovarian, gastric, small bowel, urothelial.
Q10: IBD surveillance
A 35-year-old woman with 8-year history of pancolitis UC and newly diagnosed PSC asks about colon cancer screening. What is the recommended strategy?

A) FIT every year starting now
B) Colonoscopy every 10 years starting at age 50
C) Surveillance colonoscopy now and every 1–2 years thereafter
D) CT colonography every 5 years
E) No additional screening needed
Correct Answer: C

Surveillance colonoscopy every 1–2 years after 8 years of pancolitis (or 15 years of left-sided colitis). If PSC is present, start surveillance at the time of PSC diagnosis and continue annually (highest risk group).
