LO 1 — The Unstirred Water Layer
Click any card to flip · 16 cards total
LO 1 · UWL
What is the unstirred water layer (UWL) and what creates it?
Tap to reveal →
Answer
The UWL is an aqueous zone immediately adjacent to the brush border of enterocytes. It forms because laminar flow near the gut wall is slow, and mucus + HCO₃⁻ secreted by goblet cells creates a stagnant water layer that chyme cannot penetrate by bulk flow.
LO 1 · UWL
Why can't dietary lipids cross the UWL on their own?
Tap to reveal →
Answer
Lipids are hydrophobic — they aggregate into large fat droplets in the aqueous lumen. These droplets cannot diffuse through the aqueous UWL. They need an amphipathic carrier (bile/micelles) to transport them to the enterocyte surface.
LO 1 · Bile Role
What is the "lipid taxi" concept and who is the driver?
Tap to reveal →
Answer
Bile forms micelles — spherical amphipathic aggregates with a hydrophobic core (holds dietary lipids) and a hydrophilic exterior (faces the aqueous UWL). The micelle is the "taxi"; bile is the driver — it carries lipids through water to the enterocyte.
LO 1 · Classic Trap
Does bile digest lipids? Explain.
Tap to reveal →
Answer
NO. Bile does NOT digest lipids. Digestion (hydrolysis of triglycerides) is performed by pancreatic lipase. Bile emulsifies fat droplets (↑ surface area) and forms micelles to transport lipids — but never chemically breaks them down.
LO 2 · Synthesis
Where is bile synthesized and from what backbone?
Tap to reveal →
Answer
Bile is synthesized exclusively in hepatocytes from a cholesterol backbone. The rate-limiting enzyme for cholesterol synthesis is HMG-CoA reductase — the target of statins. Bile also contains phospholipids, metabolic waste, and bilirubin.
LO 2 · Structure
What makes primary bile amphipathic? Name the two types.
Tap to reveal →
Answer
Conjugation with an amino acid adds a hydrophilic head to the lipophilic cholesterol backbone:
• Glycine → glycocholic acid
• Taurine → taurocholic acid
Result: amphipathic molecule — one end loves fat, one end loves water.
LO 2 · Primary vs Secondary
Distinguish primary vs secondary bile acids.
Tap to reveal →
Answer
Primary bile: made by hepatocytes; amphipathic; forms micelles in small intestine (e.g., cholic acid, chenodeoxycholic acid).
Secondary bile: primary bile deconjugated by colonic bacteria → mostly lipophilic; found in colon; cannot form micelles. (e.g., deoxycholic acid, lithocholic acid)
LO 2 · Bilirubin
Trace bilirubin from RBC destruction to stool color.
Tap to reveal →
Answer
Heme from RBC → unconjugated bilirubin (water-insoluble, albumin-bound) → liver conjugates with glucuronic acid → conjugated bilirubin (water-soluble) → secreted into bile → gut bacteria → urobilinogen (some excreted in urine) → stercobilin (brown stool color).
LO 3 · CCK
What triggers CCK release and what are its 3 biliary effects?
Tap to reveal →
Answer
Trigger: fat + protein in duodenum → I-cells release CCK.
3 biliary effects:
1. ↑ bile production by hepatocytes
2. Gallbladder contraction (releases stored bile)
3. Relaxation of the sphincter of Oddi (bile flows into duodenum)
LO 3 · Enterohepatic
Describe enterohepatic circulation — where, how, and how much is recycled?
Tap to reveal →
Answer
After micelle delivery, bile remains in the lumen → travels to terminal ileum → actively reabsorbed via ASBT transporter → portal circulation → liver reuses it. The body recycles bile 6–10×/day; only ~10% is lost in feces per cycle.
LO 3 · Gallstones
What are the 3 types of gallstones, their composition, and radiology?
Tap to reveal →
Answer
Cholesterol (~80%): crystallized cholesterol; radiolucent on X-ray; 5 F's risk factors.
Black pigment: calcium bilirubinate; radiopaque; chronic hemolysis (sickle cell).
Brown pigment: bilirubinate + bacteria; radiopaque; biliary infection (E. coli).
LO 4 · Micelle
Describe micelle structure and what lipid cargo it carries.
Tap to reveal →
Answer
Micelle = spherical aggregate of primary bile molecules:
• Lipophilic ends face inward → surround and trap lipid cargo
• Hydrophilic ends face outward → interact with the aqueous UWL
Cargo: free fatty acids, monoglycerides, fat-soluble vitamins (A,D,E,K), cholesterol.
LO 4 · Colipase
Why is colipase needed? How is it activated?
Tap to reveal →
Answer
Bile's hydrophilic shell blocks pancreatic lipase from accessing the lipid core. Colipase binds the hydrophilic ends of bile, creating a "door" for lipase.
Activation: pancreas secretes pro-colipase → trypsin cleaves it → active colipase.
LO 4 · Lipase Products
What does pancreatic lipase produce and where do the products go?
Tap to reveal →
Answer
Pancreatic lipase hydrolyzes triglycerides → 2 free fatty acids + 1 monoglyceride. These diffuse passively off the micelle into the enterocyte (they are lipophilic). Inside the enterocyte: re-esterified → chylomicrons → lacteals (lymph) → thoracic duct → bloodstream.
LO 4 · Steatorrhea
Define steatorrhea and list 4 causes.
Tap to reveal →
Answer
Steatorrhea: excess fat in stool (>7 g/day); greasy, foul-smelling, floating, hard to flush.
4 causes: ↓ bile (liver failure, obstruction), ↓ pancreatic lipase (CF, chronic pancreatitis), acid inactivation (ZES), terminal ileum resection (↓ bile reabsorption → depleted pool).
LO 4 · Fat Vitamins
Name the deficiencies from fat malabsorption — one clue per vitamin.
Tap to reveal →
Answer
Vitamin A: night blindness → xerophthalmia.
Vitamin D: rickets (children) / osteomalacia (adults).
Vitamin E: hemolytic anemia, ataxia, peripheral neuropathy.
Vitamin K: bleeding (↓ II, VII, IX, X, Protein C/S) → ↑ PT/INR.

True or False? These statements target the most common exam misconceptions on bile physiology. Select your answer to see the explanation.
Trap 1 · LO 1
"Bile digests dietary fat by breaking down triglycerides into fatty acids and glycerol."
FALSE. Bile does NOT digest fat. Digestion (hydrolysis of triglycerides) is performed by pancreatic lipase. Bile emulsifies fat droplets (increases surface area) and forms micelles to transport lipids through the UWL — but it has no enzymatic activity on lipids.
Trap 2 · LO 2
"Secondary bile acids are amphipathic and can form micelles in the colon."
FALSE. Secondary bile acids are formed when gut bacteria deconjugate primary bile in the colon — stripping the hydrophilic amino acid. Without the hydrophilic head, secondary bile is predominantly lipophilic and cannot form micelles. Only primary bile (in the small intestine) forms micelles.
Trap 3 · LO 2
"Statins reduce bile synthesis because HMG-CoA reductase directly synthesizes bile acids."
FALSE (nuance). HMG-CoA reductase synthesizes cholesterol, which is the backbone for bile acids. Statins indirectly reduce bile acid synthesis by lowering cholesterol availability. The direct rate-limiting step for bile acid synthesis FROM cholesterol is cholesterol 7α-hydroxylase — not HMG-CoA reductase.
Trap 4 · LO 3
"CCK causes contraction of the sphincter of Oddi to increase bile pressure in the gallbladder."
FALSE. CCK causes relaxation of the sphincter of Oddi — not contraction. Relaxation allows bile to flow freely from the common bile duct into the duodenum. Simultaneously, CCK causes gallbladder contraction to push stored bile out. Sphincter of Oddi contraction would obstruct bile flow.
Trap 5 · LO 3
"After delivering lipids to enterocytes, bile molecules are absorbed along with the fatty acids into the enterocyte."
FALSE. Bile molecules stay in the intestinal lumen after delivering their lipid cargo. They travel down to the terminal ileum, where they are actively reabsorbed via the ASBT transporter into the portal circulation — not into enterocytes with the lipids. This is enterohepatic circulation.
Trap 6 · LO 3
"Cholesterol gallstones are radiopaque and easily visible on plain abdominal X-ray."
FALSE. Cholesterol stones are radiolucent — pure cholesterol does not absorb X-rays well. They are not visible on plain X-ray and are best detected by ultrasound (echogenic foci with posterior acoustic shadowing). It is pigment stones (black and brown) that are radiopaque.
Trap 7 · LO 4
"Colipase is needed because pancreatic lipase cannot work in the presence of bile at all."
FALSE (nuance). Pancreatic lipase can work in the absence of bile (e.g., in the stomach where lingual/gastric lipase acts). However, when bile is present and forming micelles, the hydrophilic shell blocks lipase access. Colipase overcomes this bile-specific inhibition — it's not that lipase can never work without colipase.
Trap 8 · LO 4
"In Zollinger-Ellison syndrome, steatorrhea occurs because gastrin directly inhibits bile synthesis in the liver."
FALSE. Gastrin does not directly inhibit bile synthesis. In ZES, the gastrinoma produces massive amounts of gastrin → ↑↑ acid secretion → the duodenal pH drops so low that it inactivates bile salts and pancreatic lipase (both require pH ~6–7). Steatorrhea results from acid inactivation, not from any direct effect on bile synthesis.
