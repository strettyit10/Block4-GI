Practice Questions — GI14 USMLE-Style
Q1. A patient with acetaminophen overdose develops acute liver failure. Liver biopsy shows centrilobular necrosis. Which zone of the hepatic acinus is injured, and why is it preferentially affected?
A. Zone 1 — first to receive toxic metabolites via portal blood
B. Zone 2 — intermediate oxygen tension increases susceptibility
C. Zone 3 — highest CYP2E1 concentration + lowest O₂ tension
D. Zone 3 — direct toxin absorption from the central vein
E. Zone 1 — highest oxygen exposure activates APAP to NAPQI
✅ C — Zone 3 (centrilobular) is preferentially damaged by acetaminophen because it has the highest concentration of CYP2E1 (the enzyme that converts APAP to toxic NAPQI) and the lowest O₂ tension (last to receive blood from the portal triad). At therapeutic doses, NAPQI is detoxified by glutathione; overdose depletes glutathione → centrilobular necrosis. Zone 1 is preferentially damaged by viral hepatitis (first to encounter virus-containing portal blood).
Q2. A patient with cirrhosis develops confusion, asterixis, and a flapping hand tremor. Labs show NH₃ of 180 μg/dL. Which two medications would you prescribe and why?
A. Lactulose + rifaximin
B. Neomycin + vancomycin
C. Lactulose + metronidazole
D. Cholestyramine + lactulose
E. Rifaximin + proton pump inhibitor
✅ A — Hepatic encephalopathy results from impaired urea cycle → NH₃ accumulation → CNS toxicity. Lactulose: acidifies colon → converts NH₃ → NH₄⁺ (trapped, non-absorbable) + cathartic effect. Rifaximin: non-absorbable antibiotic → reduces ammonia-producing gut bacteria. Together they reduce serum ammonia. Rifaximin is preferred over neomycin (better evidence, less nephrotoxicity/ototoxicity).
Q3. A 45-year-old woman with cirrhosis has ascites (SAAG = 1.4 g/dL) and bilateral leg edema. A classmate says "the edema is from low albumin — remove albumin-rich fluid and she'll get better." What is wrong with this reasoning?
A. SAAG <1.1 indicates malignancy, not portal hypertension
B. Ascites requires portal hypertension in addition to ↓oncotic pressure; removing fluid doesn't address portal HTN
C. Bilateral leg edema in cirrhosis is always from DVT, not albumin
D. Albumin levels are always normal in cirrhosis because the liver compensates
E. Ascites in cirrhosis is due to infection, not portal hypertension
✅ B — SAAG ≥1.1 g/dL confirms portal hypertension etiology (SAAG = 1.4 here). Ascites requires portal hypertension as the primary driver — hypoalbuminemia alone (e.g., nephrotic syndrome) causes leg edema but NOT ascites. The triad for cirrhotic ascites: portal HTN + hypoalbuminemia + RAAS activation. Treating only albumin without addressing portal HTN is insufficient.
Q4. A physician prescribes atorvastatin to a patient with an LDL of 190 mg/dL. The patient asks why the drug helps. Which sequence correctly describes statin's mechanism?
A. Statin blocks LDLR → less LDL cleared → LDL falls because less is produced
B. Statin blocks HMG-CoA reductase → ↓hepatic cholesterol → upregulate LDLR → ↑LDL clearance from plasma
C. Statin blocks MRP2 → ↓cholesterol secretion into bile → LDL falls
D. Statin activates SREBP-1c → ↑cholesterol synthesis but ↑LDL receptor too → net LDL falls
E. Statin blocks lipoprotein lipase → TG cleared faster → LDL falls indirectly
✅ B — Statins competitively inhibit HMG-CoA reductase → ↓endogenous hepatic cholesterol → hepatocytes sense low intracellular cholesterol → SREBP-2 activated → ↑LDLR on hepatocyte surface → ↑LDL uptake from plasma → ↓serum LDL by 30–50%. Side effects: myopathy (↑CK), rhabdomyolysis (rare), transaminase elevation. FH = LDLR mutation → statins less effective alone.
Q5. A 28-year-old man has recurrent mild jaundice after fasting and a recent illness. Bilirubin = 3.0 mg/dL (all indirect). Direct bilirubin, LFTs, CBC, and hemolysis panel are all normal. What is the diagnosis and mechanism?
A. Dubin-Johnson syndrome — defective MRP2 secretion of conjugated bilirubin
B. Gilbert syndrome — mildly reduced UGT1A1 promoter activity triggered by fasting/stress
C. Crigler-Najjar Type II — severely reduced UGT1A1 activity
D. Hemolytic anemia — RBC destruction overwhelming conjugation
E. Rotor syndrome — defective OATP1B1/B3 uptake of bilirubin
✅ B — Gilbert syndrome: most common congenital unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia (5–10% of population). UGT1A1 promoter TATA box repeat → mildly reduced activity (30–50%) → ↑indirect bilirubin during fasting, illness, exercise, or stress. Completely benign — no treatment needed. Key distinguisher from hemolysis: no anemia, no ↑reticulocytes, no ↑LDH, normal haptoglobin.
Q6. A patient presents with dark urine, pale clay-colored stools, and intense pruritus for 3 weeks. Labs: total bilirubin 12 mg/dL (direct 10, indirect 2), ALP ↑↑↑, GGT ↑↑, ALT/AST mildly elevated. What type of jaundice is this, and what additional sign should you look for?
A. Hemolytic jaundice — look for splenomegaly and reticulocytosis
B. Obstructive jaundice — look for Courvoisier sign (palpable non-tender gallbladder)
C. Hepatocellular jaundice — look for spider angiomata and asterixis
D. Neonatal jaundice — look for unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia only
E. Gilbert syndrome — look for fasting as a precipitant
✅ B — Classic obstructive (post-hepatic) jaundice: ↑↑direct bili, dark urine (bilirubinuria), pale/clay stools (no stercobilin), pruritus (bile salt skin deposition), markedly ↑ALP (cholestasis marker). Courvoisier sign (painless jaundice + palpable non-tender gallbladder) = pancreatic head malignancy until proven otherwise (gallstones cause GB wall fibrosis → GB cannot distend — Courvoisier's law).
Q7. A patient undergoes extensive terminal ileal resection for Crohn's disease. Which of the following is NOT an expected consequence?
A. Steatorrhea
B. Fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (A, D, E, K)
C. Bile acid (cholerheic) diarrhea
D. Vitamin B₁₂ deficiency → megaloblastic anemia
E. Improved fat digestion due to ↑hepatic bile acid synthesis compensating for lost reabsorption
✅ E — The liver CANNOT adequately compensate through increased synthesis. Hepatic de novo capacity is limited (~0.5 g/day). Terminal ileal resection eliminates ASBT (95% reabsorption) → rapid pool depletion → insufficient intraluminal bile acid concentration → steatorrhea + fat-soluble vitamin deficiency + bile acid diarrhea + B₁₂ deficiency. Key distinction: cholecystectomy (reservoir only, pool intact) does NOT cause steatorrhea.
Q8. A medical student is tested on bile secretion regulation. She writes: "Both CCK and secretin contract the gallbladder." What is incorrect in her statement, and what does secretin actually do?
A. She's correct — both hormones contract the gallbladder
B. CCK relaxes the gallbladder; secretin contracts it
C. Only CCK contracts the gallbladder; secretin stimulates ductal HCO₃⁻ secretion but does NOT contract the gallbladder
D. Secretin contracts the gallbladder only in the fasted state
E. Neither CCK nor secretin contracts the gallbladder — vagal stimulation is the sole driver
✅ C — Classic trap: secretin does NOT contract the gallbladder. CCK (I cells; fat + protein stimulus) → contracts gallbladder + relaxes sphincter of Oddi → bile delivered to duodenum. Secretin (S cells; H⁺ stimulus) → cholangiocytes and pancreatic ductal cells secrete HCO₃⁻-rich fluid → alkalinizes duodenum. Secretin = "the bicarb hormone," not the bile-contracting hormone.
