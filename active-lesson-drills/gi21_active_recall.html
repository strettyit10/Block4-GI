Practice Questions
Q1: APAP overdose timing
A 22-year-old woman arrives 6 hours after ingesting ~30 extra-strength acetaminophen tablets. She is currently asymptomatic. What is the BEST next step?

A) Observe and discharge — she is asymptomatic
B) Draw a 4-hour level and plot on the Rumack-Matthew nomogram; start NAC if above treatment line
C) Begin NAC empirically now without labs
D) Give activated charcoal and discharge
E) Order LFTs only; if normal, no treatment needed
Correct Answer: B

A 4-hour post-ingestion APAP level plotted on the Rumack-Matthew nomogram is the standard. Phase 1 patients are classically asymptomatic — a normal exam does NOT rule out toxicity. NAC is most effective within 8 hours, so draw the level NOW (she's at 6h) and decide. If the level is above treatment line or timing uncertain (e.g., >8–10h), start NAC empirically without waiting. LFTs are normal in Phase 1.
Q2: Mixed acid-base in overdose
A 45-year-old man presents tachypneic with tinnitus, confusion, and vomiting. ABG shows pH 7.48, pCO₂ 22, HCO₃ 16. Anion gap is 22. What is the most likely intoxication?

A) Acetaminophen
B) Ethylene glycol
C) Salicylates (aspirin)
D) Tricyclic antidepressant
E) Opioid
Correct Answer: C

Classic salicylate presentation: mixed primary respiratory alkalosis (direct medullary stimulation → tachypnea) + anion gap metabolic acidosis (uncoupling of oxidative phosphorylation → lactate, ketones). Tinnitus is the pathognomonic early symptom. Treatment: urine alkalinization (NaHCO₃) → target serum pH ~7.5, urine pH >7.5. Dialyze if level >100 mg/dL, AMS, renal failure, or pulmonary edema. Ethylene glycol is anion gap only, no respiratory alkalosis.
Q3: Wide-complex tachycardia
A 30-year-old woman is brought in after a suicide attempt. She is somnolent, tachycardic (HR 130), hypotensive, with dry warm skin and mydriasis. ECG shows QRS 130 ms with a terminal R wave in aVR. What is the best immediate treatment?

A) Amiodarone IV
B) Sodium bicarbonate IV bolus
C) Cardioversion
D) Flumazenil IV
E) Lidocaine infusion
Correct Answer: B

TCA toxicity: anticholinergic features (mydriasis, dry skin, tachycardia) + Na-channel blockade (wide QRS, terminal R wave in aVR, right axis deviation). NaHCO₃ IV provides two benefits: (1) alkalinization reduces drug binding to Na channel, (2) sodium load overcomes the block. A/E worsen conduction. Flumazenil is contraindicated (seizure risk + could unmask TCA if coingested). QRS >100 ms = seizure risk; >160 ms = arrhythmia risk.
Q4: Hemodialysis indications
Which of the following toxins is MOST effectively removed by hemodialysis?

A) Digoxin
B) Tricyclic antidepressant
C) Lithium
D) Iron
E) Calcium channel blocker
Correct Answer: C

SLIME — Salicylates, Lithium, Isopropanol, Methanol/Metformin, Ethylene glycol. Dialyzable toxins: low MW, low protein binding, low Vd, water soluble. Lithium meets all criteria perfectly. Digoxin has huge Vd (bound to tissue), TCAs are highly protein bound + high Vd, iron is too large/complexed, CCBs have high Vd.
Q5: Cherry-red skin
A family of 4 is found obtunded in their home one morning in winter. The furnace has been malfunctioning. SpO₂ on pulse oximetry reads 99% but all members have cherry-red mucosa and lactic acidosis. What test confirms the diagnosis?

A) Arterial blood gas PaO₂
B) Standard pulse oximetry
C) Co-oximetry (carboxyhemoglobin level)
D) Methemoglobin level
E) Cyanide level
Correct Answer: C

Co-oximetry directly measures carboxyhemoglobin (CO-Hb), distinguishing it from O₂-Hb — which standard pulse oximetry CANNOT do (CO-Hb and O₂-Hb absorb light similarly at 660/940 nm). PaO₂ is normal in CO poisoning because dissolved O₂ is unchanged; the problem is Hb can't carry it. Treatment: 100% O₂; HBO for severe CNS symptoms, LOC, pregnancy, or CO-Hb >25% (>15% if pregnant since fetal Hb has even higher CO affinity).
Q6: Toxic alcohol
A 52-year-old man presents intoxicated but the ED smells no alcohol on his breath. He is acidotic with a HIGH anion gap (AG 28) and HIGH osmolar gap (25 mOsm/kg). Urinalysis shows calcium oxalate crystals. What is the best immediate treatment?

A) Ethanol infusion
B) Fomepizole IV
C) Naloxone IV
D) Activated charcoal
E) Pyridoxine IV
Correct Answer: B

Ethylene glycol poisoning: AG metabolic acidosis + elevated osmolar gap + calcium oxalate crystals on UA. Fomepizole is first-line (competitive alcohol dehydrogenase inhibitor) — prevents conversion to toxic glycolate/oxalate. Ethanol (A) is backup if fomepizole unavailable. Adjuncts: thiamine + pyridoxine (shunts to non-toxic metabolites). Hemodialysis if severe acidosis, renal failure, or high level. Methanol would give visual symptoms (blindness) + no oxalate crystals.
Q7: Charcoal contraindication
Activated charcoal is LEAST effective for which of the following ingestions?

A) Acetaminophen 1 hour ago
B) Aspirin 30 minutes ago
C) Tricyclic antidepressant 45 minutes ago
D) Lithium carbonate 1 hour ago
E) Phenytoin 30 minutes ago
Correct Answer: D

LAME Ca — Lithium, Alcohols, Metals (iron), Electrolytes, Caustics — are NOT effectively adsorbed by activated charcoal. Lithium is an ion (not organic) and doesn't bind well. Whole-bowel irrigation with PEG is preferred for lithium (especially sustained-release). Charcoal works best for organic, low-polarity compounds and is ideally given within 1 hour.
Q8: Cholinergic toxidrome
A 40-year-old farmworker is brought in after being sprayed with an unknown pesticide. He has copious oral secretions, miosis, bradycardia, bronchoconstriction, and fasciculations. What is the FIRST-line treatment?

A) Atropine IV titrated to dry secretions
B) Pralidoxime (2-PAM) IM only
C) Naloxone IV
D) Physostigmine
E) Activated charcoal
Correct Answer: A

Organophosphate poisoning (AChE inhibition) = cholinergic crisis (DUMBELS + SLUDGE). Atropine IV is first-line, titrated aggressively to DRY PULMONARY SECRETIONS (not HR/pupils). Pralidoxime (2-PAM) regenerates AChE and should be given WITH atropine, especially for nicotinic effects (muscle weakness/fasciculations) — but NOT as sole therapy. Give pralidoxime within 24–48h before "aging" of enzyme occurs. Physostigmine (D) is the WRONG direction — would worsen cholinergic excess; it's the antidote for ANTIcholinergic toxicity.
