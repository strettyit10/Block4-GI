Three GI sensory receptor types (LO5)
1. Mechanoreceptors
Distension/stretch of GI wall (intraluminal pressure or volume)
Primary trigger for peristaltic reflex; over-distension → vomiting reflex; gastric distension → satiety via vagal afferents
Surgical bowel handling stuns mechanoreceptors → ENS cannot initiate reflex → paralytic ileus even with intact ENS neurons
2. Chemoreceptors
Chemical composition of lumen: pH, fatty acids, amino acids, glucose, bile acids, bacterial products
Trigger hormone secretion: CCK (FA + AA in duodenum) + secretin (low pH) → downstream pancreatic/biliary effects
Low intraluminal pH (acid chyme) in duodenum → secretin → pancreatic HCO₃⁻ to neutralize and protect duodenum
3. Osmoreceptors
Osmolarity of luminal contents — most sensitive in duodenum and jejunum
Hyperosmolar chyme → slows gastric emptying; triggers fluid secretion into lumen to dilute content before absorption
Post-gastrectomy: rapid emptying of hyperosmolar chyme overwhelms osmoreceptors → fluid rushes in → hypovolemia + nausea + diarrhea

Peristaltic reflex — step by step (LO5)
Distension OR chemical stimuli → enterochromaffin cells release 5-HT → activates IPANs via 5-HT3 receptors
ACh + Substance P → circular muscle CONTRACTS above (proximal/oral side of) bolus → propels content forward
VIP + NO → circular muscle RELAXES below (distal/aboral side of) bolus → creates space for content
DESCENDING INHIBITION fails — VIP/NO neurons at LES destroyed (autoimmune) → LES cannot relax → bird-beak dilation; Tx: botulinum toxin, pneumatic dilation, or Heller myotomy
~90% of body's total serotonin; produced by enterochromaffin (EC) cells in intestinal mucosa
Ondansetron, granisetron — block 5-HT3 on vagal afferents → antiemetic; used for chemotherapy-induced and post-op nausea
Cisapride, prucalopride — ↑ACh release → prokinetic; used for gastroparesis and chronic constipation
EC cell tumor → massive 5-HT → liver overwhelmed → systemic 5-HT; right-sided heart fibrosis (tricuspid/pulmonary); pulmonary vasculature inactivates 5-HT → left heart spared; urine 5-HIAA elevated

Q1. A histology slide shows neurons located between the inner circular and outer longitudinal smooth muscle layers of the small intestine. Damage to these neurons would MOST directly impair which function?
A. Mucosal enzyme secretion and local blood flow
B. Coordinated peristalsis and segmentation
C. IgA production and mucosal immune defense
D. Absorption of long-chain fatty acids into lacteals
E. Pancreatic bicarbonate release in response to acid chyme
B — Coordinated peristalsis and segmentation
The myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus lies between the circular and longitudinal muscle layers and controls MOTILITY. The submucosal (Meissner's) plexus, located between circular muscle and submucosa, controls secretion and blood flow (choice A). Choices C–E involve immune, absorptive, and hormonal functions not directly controlled by this plexus location.
Q2. A 3-day-old neonate fails to pass meconium and develops progressive abdominal distension. Rectal biopsy reveals an absence of ganglion cells in the distal rectosigmoid. What embryological event failed, and what would you find in the PROXIMAL dilated segment on biopsy?
A. Failed mesodermal differentiation; absent muscularis mucosae proximally
B. Failed neural crest migration; normal ganglion cells present proximally
C. Failed neural crest migration; absent ganglion cells throughout the colon
D. Defective Wnt signaling; hyperplastic crypts proximally
E. Failed endodermal rotation; volvulus with normal ganglia throughout
B — Failed neural crest migration; normal ganglion cells present proximally
Hirschsprung disease = failure of neural crest cells to migrate into the distal colon during embryogenesis → aganglionic segment → tonic contraction (functional obstruction). The proximal dilated segment has NORMAL ganglia (it dilates because stool cannot pass through the contracted distal segment). This is a classic neonatal surgical emergency.
Q3. A patient is found to have metabolic alkalosis and hypokalemia. Which of the following processes is the MOST likely cause?
A. Profuse watery diarrhea from Vibrio cholerae
B. Prolonged nasogastric suctioning of gastric contents
C. Secretory diarrhea from a VIPoma
D. Pancreatic fistula draining into the peritoneum
E. Ileostomy with high output from the small intestine
B — Prolonged nasogastric suctioning of gastric contents
Loss of gastric fluid (HCl) → loss of H⁺ and Cl⁻ → metabolic ALKALOSIS + hypokalemia (K⁺ shifts into cells as H⁺ exits, and ↑aldosterone from volume depletion causes renal K⁺ wasting). All other choices cause diarrhea → loss of HCO₃⁻-rich intestinal/pancreatic fluid → metabolic ACIDOSIS + hypokalemia.
Q4. A patient on long-term IV opioids for cancer pain develops severe constipation and abdominal distension. The team wants to restore bowel function without reversing analgesia. Which of the following is the BEST option?
A. Naloxone IV (systemic opioid antagonist)
B. Metoclopramide (5-HT4 agonist / D2 antagonist)
C. Methylnaltrexone (peripherally-acting μ-opioid antagonist)
D. Atropine (muscarinic antagonist)
E. Ondansetron (5-HT3 antagonist)
C — Methylnaltrexone (peripherally-acting μ-opioid antagonist)
Opioids bind μ-receptors on ENS neurons → inhibit excitatory NT (ACh/Substance P) → ↓peristalsis + ↑sphincter tone. Methylnaltrexone does NOT cross the BBB, so it blocks the GI μ-receptors without reversing CNS analgesia. Naloxone (A) crosses BBB → would reverse analgesia. Metoclopramide (B) is a prokinetic but does not address the receptor-level cause. Atropine (D) would WORSEN constipation.
Q5. A 68-year-old patient with atrial fibrillation presents with sudden-onset severe periumbilical pain for 2 hours. His abdomen is soft on exam. CT angiography shows an embolus at the origin of the superior mesenteric artery. Which bowel segments are at GREATEST risk for ischemia?
A. Esophagus, stomach, and proximal duodenum
B. Descending colon, sigmoid colon, and upper rectum
C. Distal esophagus, liver, spleen, and gallbladder
D. Jejunum, ileum, cecum, ascending colon, and proximal transverse colon
E. Distal transverse colon, descending colon, and sigmoid colon only
D — Jejunum, ileum, cecum, ascending colon, and proximal transverse colon
The SMA supplies the MIDGUT: duodenum (distal 3rd/4th parts), jejunum, ileum, cecum, ascending colon, and proximal 2/3 of transverse colon. The "pain out of proportion to exam" is the classic Step 1 clue — ischemia causes severe visceral pain mediated by mesenteric afferents before transmural necrosis and peritoneal signs develop. AF = embolic source. Choice A = celiac territory; Choice B/E = IMA; Choice C = celiac.
Q6. A patient develops progressive dysphagia to both solids and liquids. Esophageal manometry reveals failure of the lower esophageal sphincter to relax during swallowing and absence of peristalsis in the distal esophagus. Barium swallow shows a "bird-beak" narrowing at the gastroesophageal junction. Which neurotransmitter deficit BEST explains the pathophysiology?
A. Loss of excitatory ACh + Substance P neurons at LES
B. Excess VIP release causing sustained smooth muscle relaxation
C. Loss of inhibitory VIP/NO neurons at LES → unopposed excitatory tone
D. Excess Substance P causing hypertensive peristalsis
E. Loss of 5-HT3 receptor signaling in ENS afferents
C — Loss of inhibitory VIP/NO neurons at LES → unopposed excitatory tone
Achalasia = selective destruction of inhibitory ENS neurons (nNOS/VIP-positive) at the LES. LES relaxation requires VIP + NO (descending inhibitory limb). Without inhibitory input, unopposed ACh/Substance P excitatory tone → LES stays contracted → food cannot enter stomach → progressive esophageal dilation. Treatments: botulinum toxin (blocks ACh to rebalance), pneumatic dilation, or surgical myotomy (Heller).
Q7. A patient with cirrhosis presents with hematemesis. Endoscopy reveals bleeding esophageal varices. Which statement BEST explains the anatomical basis for variceal formation?
A. Splanchnic arterial vasodilation increases hepatic arterial blood flow
B. Portal hypertension forces blood into portosystemic anastomoses between the left gastric vein and azygos veins
C. Hepatic venous outflow obstruction causes reversal of portal venous flow
D. Inferior mesenteric vein hypertension causes bleeding at the rectosigmoid junction
E. Chylomicron accumulation in the thoracic duct causes esophageal dilation
B — Portal hypertension forces blood into portosystemic anastomoses between the left gastric vein and azygos veins
Cirrhosis → ↑hepatic sinusoidal resistance → portal hypertension → blood backs up into portal tributaries → portosystemic shunts open. At the esophagus: left gastric (portal) ↔ esophageal submucosal veins ↔ azygos vein (systemic). These thin-walled esophageal varices are at high risk of rupture → most dangerous portal HTN complication. Other shunts: caput medusae (paraumbilical veins) and hemorrhoids (superior rectal ↔ inferior/middle rectal).
Q8. A patient smells a meal being prepared and begins salivating with audible stomach growling before eating anything. What is the physiologic name for this phenomenon, and what percentage of total meal-stimulated gastric acid production does it account for?
A. Gastric phase; 60–70% of acid response
B. Intestinal phase; 5–10% of acid response
C. Cephalic phase; 20–30% of acid response
D. Enteric phase; 40–50% of acid response
E. Cephalic phase; 60–70% of acid response
C — Cephalic phase; 20–30% of acid response
The cephalic phase occurs before food even enters the GI tract, triggered by sight/smell/taste/thought of food → cortical + hypothalamic activation → dorsal motor nucleus of vagus → vagal efferents → ENS → ↑HCl, pepsinogen, salivation, and gastric motility. Accounts for ~20–30% of total acid response. Gastric phase (distension + amino acids → gastrin) accounts for 60–70%. Intestinal phase (chyme in duodenum) = 5–10%. Pavlov's "psychic secretion" experiments demonstrated this in dogs.
