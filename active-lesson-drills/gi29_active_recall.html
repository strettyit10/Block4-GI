LOs 1–4
Q1: Retroperitoneal identification
During an emergency laparotomy for blunt trauma, a surgeon identifies blood behind the posterior peritoneum. Which of the following organs is retroperitoneal?

A) Stomach
B) Spleen
C) Pancreas (body and tail)
D) Transverse colon
E) Jejunum
Correct Answer: C

SAD PUCKER: Suprarenal, Aorta/IVC, Duodenum (2nd-4th), Pancreas (except tail), Ureters, Colon (ascending/descending), Kidneys, Esophagus (lower), Rectum (lower 2/3). Stomach, spleen, jejunum, and transverse colon are intraperitoneal.
Q2: Aortic branch level
A CT angiogram shows an atherosclerotic lesion at the origin of the superior mesenteric artery. At which vertebral level is this lesion located?

A) T8
B) T10
C) T12
D) L1
E) L3
Correct Answer: D

Celiac trunk = T12, SMA = L1 (just below celiac), IMA = L3. Aortic bifurcation = L4. "T12–L1–L3" is the 3-3-3-1 framework.
Q3: Nutcracker syndrome
A 28-year-old thin woman presents with left flank pain and hematuria. Imaging shows compression of the left renal vein between the aorta and superior mesenteric artery. What is the name of this syndrome?

A) SMA syndrome
B) Nutcracker syndrome
C) Median arcuate ligament syndrome
D) May-Thurner syndrome
E) Leriche syndrome
Correct Answer: B

Nutcracker syndrome — left renal vein compressed between aorta (posteriorly) and SMA (anteriorly). Causes L flank pain, hematuria, and varicocele in men (L gonadal v. drains into L renal v.). SMA syndrome = compression of 3rd part duodenum. MALS = celiac trunk compression.
Q4: Portosystemic anastomosis
A cirrhotic patient develops dilated tortuous veins radiating outward from the umbilicus. Which anastomosis is responsible?

A) Left gastric ↔ esophageal veins
B) Paraumbilical ↔ superficial epigastric veins
C) Superior rectal ↔ middle/inferior rectal
D) Splenic ↔ left renal
E) Retroperitoneal (of Retzius)
Correct Answer: B

Caput medusae = paraumbilical veins (portal) ↔ superficial epigastric veins (systemic). Esophageal varices = left gastric (portal) ↔ azygos. Anorectal varices = superior rectal (portal) ↔ middle/inferior rectal (systemic). These represent internal rectal varices — separate entity from hemorrhoids.
Q5: Diaphragm opening
The esophagus passes through the diaphragm alongside which other structure?

A) Thoracic duct
B) Azygos vein
C) IVC
D) Vagus nerves (anterior + posterior trunks)
E) Aorta
Correct Answer: D

T8 = IVC + right phrenic nerve. T10 = Esophagus + vagus nerves (anterior + posterior). T12 = Aorta + thoracic duct + azygos vein. "I 8 10 Eggs At 12."
Q6: Kehr sign
A 22-year-old man with blunt abdominal trauma complains of left shoulder tip pain. Vital signs are deteriorating. What is the most likely source of the shoulder pain?

A) Cardiac ischemia
B) Diaphragmatic irritation from splenic rupture
C) Rotator cuff tear
D) Clavicular fracture
E) Cervical nerve root impingement
Correct Answer: B

Kehr sign = referred shoulder pain from diaphragmatic irritation, classically from splenic rupture causing hemoperitoneum. Phrenic nerve (C3-5) shares dermatomal overlap with supraclavicular nerves → pain perceived at shoulder.
Q7: Psoas sign
A 19-year-old man with RLQ pain has worsened pain when his right hip is extended. What does this suggest?

A) Obturator hernia
B) Retrocecal appendicitis or psoas abscess
C) Ureteral stone
D) Crohn disease flare
E) Inguinal hernia
Correct Answer: B

Psoas sign = hip extension stretches the psoas, worsening pain if there is adjacent inflammation (classic retrocecal appendix, psoas abscess, or ileal pathology). Obturator sign (internal rotation of flexed hip) suggests pelvic/obturator pathology.
Q8: Left gonadal vein drainage
A 35-year-old man presents with a left-sided varicocele that does not reduce when supine. What diagnosis must be ruled out?

A) Bladder cancer
B) Renal cell carcinoma invading left renal vein
C) Testicular torsion
D) Lymphoma
E) Epididymitis
Correct Answer: B

Left gonadal (testicular) vein drains into the LEFT RENAL vein (at 90°) before reaching the IVC. A non-reducing left varicocele in an older man raises concern for RCC obstructing the left renal vein. Right gonadal v. drains directly into IVC. Nutcracker syndrome is another cause.
Q9: Diaphragmatic rupture
A patient involved in a motor vehicle collision has a chest X-ray showing bowel loops in the left chest cavity. Why is the left hemidiaphragm more commonly ruptured than the right?

A) The left diaphragm has thinner muscle fibers
B) The liver protects the right hemidiaphragm
C) The left diaphragm is congenitally weaker
D) The left phrenic nerve is shorter
E) The left crus is more mobile
Correct Answer: B

The liver cushions the right hemidiaphragm from trauma; the left is unprotected, making left-sided rupture ~3× more common. Stomach and bowel herniate into the left chest, mimicking a pneumothorax or pleural effusion on CXR.
Q10: Meralgia paresthetica
An obese patient who wears a tight belt reports burning numbness on the lateral aspect of the thigh. Which nerve is entrapped, and where?

A) Femoral nerve at the inguinal ligament
B) Lateral femoral cutaneous nerve at the inguinal ligament / ASIS
C) Obturator nerve in the obturator canal
D) Sciatic nerve at the piriformis
E) Genitofemoral nerve at the deep inguinal ring
Correct Answer: B

Meralgia paresthetica = lateral femoral cutaneous nerve (L2-L3) entrapment as it passes under the inguinal ligament near the ASIS. Causes pure sensory symptoms (pain, burning, numbness) on the lateral thigh. No motor deficits. Associated with obesity, tight belts, pregnancy, tool belts.
