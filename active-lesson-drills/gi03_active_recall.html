8 USMLE Step 1-style questions — commit to an answer before revealing
Q1. A 28-year-old man sustains blunt perineal trauma in a bicycle accident (straddle injury). His scrotum is swollen and ecchymotic, with bruising spreading up the anterior abdominal wall but NOT onto his thighs or into the gluteal region. Which fascial layer is confining this fluid collection?
A. Camper’s fascia
B. Scarpa’s fascia
C. Fascia lata
D. Transversalis fascia
E. External oblique aponeurosis
B — Scarpa’s fascia. Scarpa’s (membranous layer of superficial fascia) is continuous with Colles’ fascia (perineum), Dartos (scrotum/labia), and Buck’s fascia (penis). Fluid tracks within this fascial plane to scrotum, penis, and anterior abdominal wall. It fuses with fascia lata at the inguinal crease — preventing spread to the thigh or gluteal region. Camper’s (A) is fatty and provides no containment barrier.
Q2. A 55-year-old woman on warfarin develops a hematoma confined within the rectus sheath below the arcuate line. Which statement best explains why the posterior wall composition changes below the arcuate line?
A. Below the arcuate line, the posterior sheath is formed by transversus aponeurosis alone
B. Below the arcuate line, all three aponeuroses pass anterior to the rectus, leaving only transversalis fascia posteriorly
C. Below the arcuate line, the internal oblique aponeurosis splits to surround the rectus
D. Below the arcuate line, the rectus muscle is absent
E. Below the arcuate line, the posterior sheath is formed by external oblique only
B — Below the arcuate line, ALL 3 flat muscle aponeuroses shift to the ANTERIOR sheath. Only transversalis fascia covers the posterior surface — no aponeurotic barrier. Blood from a hematoma below the arcuate line can therefore spread more freely posteriorly. Above the arcuate line, the internal oblique splits (anterior + posterior leaves) creating a true posterior sheath.
Q3. A medical student is studying the inguinal canal. Which of the following correctly identifies the posterior wall of the inguinal canal and its medial reinforcement?
A. External oblique aponeurosis; reinforced by inguinal ligament
B. Transversalis fascia; reinforced medially by the conjoint tendon
C. Internal oblique aponeurosis; reinforced by lacunar ligament
D. Transversus abdominis aponeurosis; reinforced by pectineal ligament
E. Transversalis fascia; reinforced laterally by the conjoint tendon
B — Posterior wall = transversalis fascia (throughout). Reinforced medially by the conjoint tendon (inguinal falx = combined internal oblique + transversus abdominis aponeuroses at their insertion). Weakness of the conjoint tendon → direct inguinal hernia through Hesselbach’s triangle floor. The deep inguinal ring is a gap in the transversalis fascia lateral to the inferior epigastric vessels.
Q4. A physician strokes the inner right thigh of a male patient and observes ipsilateral elevation of the testis. Which pairing correctly identifies both limbs of this reflex?
A. Afferent: genitofemoral (genital branch); Efferent: ilioinguinal
B. Afferent: ilioinguinal; Efferent: genital branch of genitofemoral
C. Afferent: femoral nerve; Efferent: ilioinguinal
D. Afferent: pudendal nerve; Efferent: genitofemoral
E. Afferent: lateral femoral cutaneous; Efferent: ilioinguinal
B — Cremasteric reflex: Afferent = ilioinguinal nerve (L1) — detects medial thigh stroke; runs in inguinal canal OUTSIDE spermatic cord. Efferent = genital branch of genitofemoral nerve (L1–L2) — innervates cremaster; runs INSIDE spermatic cord. These are TWO DIFFERENT nerves — a classic Step 1 trap. Absent cremasteric reflex in acute scrotal pain = testicular torsion until proven otherwise.
Q5. A 45-year-old woman is scheduled for left TRAM flap breast reconstruction after mastectomy. Her surgical history includes CABG using the left internal mammary artery 3 years ago. What is the implication for reconstruction?
A. Right TRAM flap is also contraindicated
B. Left TRAM flap is contraindicated due to loss of the superior epigastric pedicle
C. TRAM flap is unaffected by prior CABG history
D. The inferior epigastric artery alone now supplies the flap and surgery can proceed
E. The internal thoracic artery regenerates; the flap can proceed after 2 years
B — Left TRAM flap is contraindicated. The TRAM flap pedicle is the superior epigastric artery (terminal branch of the internal thoracic/mammary artery). Prior CABG harvesting the left IMA sacrifices the left superior epigastric origin — eliminating the pedicle blood supply. The anastomosis with the inferior epigastric cannot compensate without the superior epigastric continuity. A right-sided or alternative reconstruction would be required.
Q6. A 72-year-old man with alcoholic cirrhosis has visible tortuous veins radiating outward from his umbilicus in all directions. This finding results from increased pressure in which vessel, causing reversal of flow through which anastomotic channels?
A. Inferior vena cava; retroperitoneal veins
B. Portal vein; paraumbilical veins
C. Superior mesenteric vein; inferior epigastric veins
D. Inferior mesenteric vein; superficial circumflex iliac veins
E. Splenic vein; short gastric veins
B — Portal hypertension → elevated portal vein pressure → reversal of flow through paraumbilical veins (via ligamentum teres to umbilicus) → blood radiates outward into abdominal wall veins (superior/inferior epigastric, superficial epigastric) = caput medusae. Flow is AWAY from umbilicus. Compare to IVC obstruction: collateral flow runs upward on both flanks — not umbilically centered.
Q7. A 42-year-old bodybuilder develops a groin bulge when lifting heavy weights that protrudes medial to the inferior epigastric vessels. On examination, a cough impulse is felt on the pad (not tip) of the finger placed in the superficial inguinal ring. What type of hernia is this?
A. Indirect inguinal hernia through the deep inguinal ring
B. Direct inguinal hernia through Hesselbach’s triangle
C. Femoral hernia through the femoral canal
D. Spigelian hernia through the semilunar line
E. Umbilical hernia through the umbilical ring
B — Direct inguinal hernia. Three clues: (1) Medial to inferior epigastric vessels → within Hesselbach’s triangle; (2) Impulse on pad of finger = hernia pushes straight forward (indirect = impulse felt at fingertip, travels along canal); (3) Easily reducible (wide neck). R.I.P. = Rectus (medial), Inferior epigastric (lateral), Poupart’s ligament (inferior). Direct hernias are acquired (transversalis fascia weakness); indirect hernias exit lateral to epigastrics.
Q8. A 68-year-old woman has a 3-day history of worsening right groin pain. Exam reveals a tender, non-reducible mass located below and lateral to the pubic tubercle. She is febrile (38.8°C), leukocytotic, and has not passed flatus in 24 hours. What is the most appropriate next step?
A. Outpatient elective repair of reducible inguinal hernia
B. Emergency surgical repair of strangulated femoral hernia
C. Manual reduction of incarcerated direct inguinal hernia under sedation
D. CT scan for lymph node staging
E. IV antibiotics and observation
B — Emergency surgical repair. Location below and lateral to pubic tubercle = femoral hernia (inguinal hernias are above and medial). Non-reducible + fever + leukocytosis + obstipation = strangulation (bowel ischemia). Femoral hernias have the highest strangulation risk because the femoral ring is bounded by rigid structures (inguinal lig, pectineal lig, lacunar lig, femoral vein — cannot expand). Manual reduction is contraindicated when strangulation is suspected — would reduce ischemic bowel into abdomen.
