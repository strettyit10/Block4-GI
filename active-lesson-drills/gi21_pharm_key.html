Epidemiology & Top Killers (NPDS)
Primary U.S. Source of Poisoning Epidemiology Data
National Poison Data System (NPDS) — annual report from America's Poison Centers; aggregates all calls to U.S. Poison Control hotlines.
Approx % of NPDS Exposures in Children ≤5 yrs
~40% of all reported exposures occur in children ≤5 (mostly unintentional/exploratory) — but most fatalities occur in adults from intentional OD.
Mnemonic — Top 4 NPDS Killers ("SOAP")
SSedatives/hypnotics (benzos, barbiturates) — #1, ~299 deaths in 2022
OOpioids — #3, ~244 deaths in NPDS (CDC totals much higher)
AAcetaminophen (APAP) — #2, ~270 deaths
PPoisons/alcohols — #4, ~231 deaths (includes methanol, ethylene glycol)
Why Most Childhood Exposures Are Low Fatality but Most Adult Deaths Are High Fatality
Children: small exploratory ingestions, often a single sip/lick of one substance, brought in early → high volume but very low fatality rate. Adults: intentional self-harm with large doses, polysubstance ingestion, often presenting late → much higher case-fatality rate.
Focused History — "WARS" Framework
Mnemonic — "WARS" for Poisoning History
WWhat — what agent(s) were involved? (bring pill bottles)
AAmount — how much was taken? (count pills missing)
RRoute — ingestion / inhalation / dermal / ocular / injection
SSymptoms — current symptoms + what was done at home (e.g., milk, ipecac)
2 Additional Critical History Items Beyond WARS
1.When — time since exposure (drives nomograms, antidote windows, decon decisions).
2.Who — patient weight + age (mg/kg dose, pediatric ingestion of "1 pill can kill" drugs).
U.S. Poison Control Hotline Number + When to Call
1-800-222-1222 (national hotline; routes to local Poison Control Center).
Call EARLY for any significant exposure and as needed throughout management — free, 24/7, expert toxicology consult.
Why Patient Weight + Age Matter for Risk Assessment
Toxic dose is mg/kg → small children reach toxic thresholds at much lower absolute doses; some agents ("one-pill-can-kill" — CCBs, TCAs, sulfonylureas, opioids, camphor, methyl salicylate) are lethal to a toddler at single adult-tablet doses.

🚩 TRAP — UDS LIMITATIONS
Standard UDS uses immunoassay → many false POSITIVES (fluoroquinolones → opiates; ranitidine → meth) and false NEGATIVES (misses fentanyl, tramadol, methadone in many panels). UDS does NOT detect APAP, salicylates, TCAs, digoxin, alcohol, or most "real killers." A negative UDS does NOT rule out poisoning — order specific drug levels for clinical concerns.
Targeted Labs & Osmol Gap
Osmol Gap — Formula + Cutoff + What It Suggests
Calculated Osm = 2[Na] + [glucose]/18 + [BUN]/2.8; Osmol gap = measured − calculated.
Normal <10 mOsm/kg.
Elevated osmol gap + AGMA → toxic alcohol (methanol, ethylene glycol) until proven otherwise. The parent alcohol raises osmol gap; metabolites cause AGMA.
EKG Findings → Toxin Mapping (4)
1.Wide QRS >120 ms → TCA / Na-channel blockers (flecainide, quinidine) → NaHCO₃.
2.QT prolongation → antipsychotics, methadone, Class IA/III antiarrhythmics → correct K/Mg, Mg for torsades.
3.Bradycardia + AV block → digoxin, β-blockers, CCBs → Dig-Fab / glucagon / calcium.
4.Bidirectional VT → digoxin toxicity (also aconitine) → Dig-Fab.
External Decontamination
Exposure RouteAction / Key Detail
👁️ OcularImmediate copious irrigation with water/saline ≥15 min (acid); for ALKALI, irrigate until pH normalizes (30–60 min). Remove contacts. Ophtho consult.
🫁 InhalationRemove from source (rescuer must wear PPE first); fresh air / supplemental O₂; support airway.
🖐️ Skin/DermalRemove all contaminated clothing/jewelry; copious water irrigation ≥15 min; gentle lavage (don't scrub); HCW PPE.
GI Decontamination — Activated Charcoal
Activated Charcoal — Standard Adult Dose + Time Window
1 g/kg (adult ≈ 50–100 g) PO/NG.
Within 1 hour of ingestion (efficacy drops sharply after 60 min).
Why Time Window Matters (MOA in 1 line)
Charcoal adsorbs toxin only while it's still in the GI lumen — once absorbed into systemic circulation it cannot pull it back; gastric emptying largely complete by 1h.
Single-Dose Charcoal Contraindications (≥3)
1.Unprotected airway / depressed LOC (aspiration risk → severe pneumonitis).
2.Caustic ingestion (acid/alkali — obscures endoscopy, no benefit).
3.Hydrocarbon ingestion (aspiration risk; not adsorbed); also bowel obstruction/ileus, late presentation.
Status of Ipecac in Modern Practice
Obsolete — no longer recommended. Provides no proven benefit, increases aspiration risk, delays administration of charcoal, and is contraindicated in caustic/hydrocarbon ingestion. Removed from AAP guidelines.
Mnemonic — Toxins NOT Adsorbed by Activated Charcoal ("LAME Ca")
"LAME Ca" — 5 Categories Charcoal Misses
LLithium — small ion, not adsorbed.
AAlcohols (ethanol, methanol, ethylene glycol, isopropanol) — small polar molecules.
MMetals (iron, lead, mercury) — heavy metals not adsorbed.
EElectrolytes / acid-base salts (K, Na, Mg, etc.).
CaCaustics (acids, alkalis) — doesn't adsorb; obscures endoscopy.
Whole-Bowel Irrigation (WBI) — 4 Indications
1.Iron overdose (not adsorbed by charcoal).
2.Lithium overdose.
3.Sustained-release / enteric-coated medications (delayed dissolution).
4.Body packers/stuffers (drug-filled packets in gut). Use PEG-electrolyte solution (GoLYTELY) until clear effluent.
🚩 TRAP — CAUSTIC INGESTION (NaOH, ACID)
Do NOT: induce emesis (re-injures mucosa), pass NG tube blindly (perforation), or "neutralize" with opposing pH (exothermic burn). DO: dilute with small amount of water/milk; secure airway; perform endoscopy at 12–24h to assess depth of injury (alkali → liquefactive necrosis — deeper; acid → coagulative necrosis). Surgical consult for full-thickness burns.
🛑 HARD TRAP — HYDROCARBONS
Hydrocarbons (gasoline, lighter fluid, mineral spirits) cause MINIMAL GI absorption — the real danger is ASPIRATION pneumonitis (low viscosity + low surface tension → aspirates easily). Inducing emesis or gastric lavage forces hydrocarbon back through pharynx → severe aspiration pneumonitis. Management is supportive: O₂, respiratory monitoring, CXR if symptomatic.
Enhanced Elimination — Urinary Alkalinization
2 Drugs Removed by Urinary Alkalinization + Target Urine pH
Salicylates (aspirin).
Phenobarbital (and methotrexate to some extent).
Urine pH 7.5–8.0 (achieved with IV NaHCO₃ infusion).
Mechanism (Ion Trapping) — 4 Steps
1.Weak acids (salicylate, phenobarbital) filter into the renal tubule.
↓
2.NaHCO₃ alkalinizes the urine → tubular pH rises above the drug's pKa.
↓
3.Drug becomes ionized (charged) → cannot cross the lipid tubular membrane.
↓
4.Drug is "trapped" in tubular fluid → reabsorption blocked → ↑ urinary excretion.
Mnemonic — Hemodialysis-Removable Toxins ("SLIME")
"SLIME" — 5 Toxins Removed by HD
SSalicylates (when severe — pH <7.2, AMS, renal failure, level >100).
LLithium (severe toxicity, renal failure, level >4 mEq/L).
IIsopropanol (rarely needed — usually self-resolves).
MMethanol + Metformin-induced lactic acidosis.
EEthylene glycol (especially with renal failure or severe acidosis).
Properties That Make a Toxin "Dialyzable" (4)
1.Low molecular weight (<500 Da).
2.Small volume of distribution (<1 L/kg) — stays in blood, not tissue.
3.Low protein binding.
4.Water-soluble (not lipophilic).
Multi-Dose Activated Charcoal (MDAC) — "CPSA"
"CPSA" — 4 Indications for MDAC
CCarbamazepine.
PPhenobarbital.
SSalicylates.
AAminophylline / Theophylline. (Also dapsone, quinine.)
MDAC Mechanism of Action ("Gut Dialysis")
Repeated charcoal doses maintain a concentration gradient across the gut mucosa → drug already absorbed into the blood diffuses BACK into the gut lumen along the gradient and is adsorbed by the charcoal. Also interrupts enterohepatic / enteroenteric recirculation. Effective for drugs with low Vd, low protein binding, and significant enterohepatic cycling.
🛑 HARD TRAP — DIGOXIN & HEMODIALYSIS
Digoxin has a SMALL molecular weight but a HUGE volume of distribution (~7 L/kg) — it sequesters in skeletal muscle and tissue, NOT freely circulating in plasma. HD removes only what's in blood → ineffective. Antidote: digoxin-specific Fab antibody fragments (Dig-Fab) — bind free dig in plasma → tissue dig redistributes back into blood → bound and excreted renally.
Anticholinergic Toxidrome — "Hot, Dry, Red, Mad"
Mnemonic — 4 "Hatter Signs" (Hot/Dry/Red/Mad)
HHot as a hare → hyperthermia (lost sweating).
DDry as a bone → anhidrosis, dry mucous membranes.
RRed as a beet → cutaneous flushing (vasodilation without sweating).
MMad as a hatter → AMS, agitation, delirium, hallucinations.
Mnemonic — "Can't ___, Can't ___, Can't ___, Can't ___" (4 peripheral signs)
1.Can't see → mydriasis (fixed dilated pupils), cycloplegia, blurred vision.
2.Can't spit → dry mouth, ↓ secretions.
3.Can't pee → urinary retention.
4.Can't 💩 → ileus, constipation, ↓ bowel sounds. Also: tachycardia.
Causative Agents (≥4 categories)
Atropine, scopolamine, benztropine; antihistamines (diphenhydramine, promethazine); TCAs (also Na-channel + α-1 blockade); antipsychotics (especially phenothiazines); plants — jimsonweed (Datura stramonium), deadly nightshade (Atropa belladonna).
Antidote + 1 Critical Contraindication
Physostigmine (acetylcholinesterase inhibitor — crosses BBB).
TCA overdose — risk of asystole + worsened seizures (TCAs already block Na channels and lower seizure threshold).

"DUMBELS" — Adds Life-Threatening Features (7)

BBradycardia + Bronchospasm + Bronchorrhea ("3 Bs" — fatal cause of death).

Nicotinic Effects (4)
1.Tachycardia + HTN (ganglionic stimulation — may mask muscarinic bradycardia).
2.Fasciculations (NMJ overstimulation).
3.Muscle weakness → paralysis (depolarizing block at NMJ).
4.Diaphoresis.
Causative Agents — 2 Drug Classes + Plant Source
Organophosphates (insecticides — malathion, parathion; nerve agents — sarin, VX) → IRREVERSIBLE AChE inhibition. Carbamates (aldicarb) → REVERSIBLE AChE inhibition. Direct agonists: pilocarpine, bethanechol. Plants/mushrooms: Clitocybe, Inocybe ("SLUDGE mushrooms").
Treatment — 2 Antidotes + Endpoint
1.Atropine — competitive muscarinic antagonist (large doses may be needed).
↓
2.Titrate to drying of pulmonary secretions (NOT heart rate) — endpoint = dry lungs.
↓
3.Pralidoxime (2-PAM) — reactivates AChE (regenerates the enzyme); for organophosphate, NOT carbamate.
Why Pralidoxime Timing Is Critical ("Aging")
Organophosphate-AChE bond starts as reversible but undergoes "aging" — irreversible covalent dealkylation over hours to days. Once aged, pralidoxime cannot reactivate the enzyme. Give 2-PAM EARLY. Not needed for carbamates (bond is spontaneously reversible).
Sympathomimetic Toxidrome
5 Hallmark Signs
1.Tachycardia.
2.Hypertension.
3.Hyperthermia.
4.Diaphoresis (WET skin) + mydriasis.
5.Agitation/paranoia + hyperactive bowel sounds.
Causative Agents (≥3)
Cocaine; amphetamines (methamphetamine, MDMA/ecstasy); pseudoephedrine, ephedrine; caffeine (very high doses); theophylline.
Treatment — 1st-Line + What to AVOID in Cocaine OD + Why
Benzodiazepines (treat agitation, seizures, tachycardia, HTN). External cooling for hyperthermia.
Beta-blockers in cocaine OD.
Unopposed α-1 stimulation → severe vasoconstriction, malignant HTN, coronary spasm. Use nitrates, CCBs, or phentolamine instead for cocaine-related coronary spasm.
Sympathomimetic vs Anticholinergic — KEY Discriminator (2 Findings)
1.Skin: sympathomimetic = WET (diaphoretic) vs anticholinergic = DRY (anhidrotic).
2.Bowel sounds: sympathomimetic = HYPERACTIVE vs anticholinergic = ↓/ABSENT (ileus).
Opioid (Narcotic) Toxidrome — Classic Triad
Classic Opioid Triad (3)
1.Altered mental status (sedation → coma).
2.Miosis (pinpoint pupils) ← KEY differentiator.
3.Respiratory depression (↓ rate, shallow). Plus: bradycardia, hypotension, hypothermia, hyporeflexia, ↓ bowel sounds.
Antidote + Why Repeat Dosing or Infusion May Be Needed
Naloxone (competitive μ-opioid antagonist — IV/IM/intranasal).
Naloxone t½ (30–90 min) is much shorter than most opioids (especially methadone and SR formulations) → resedation/recurrent respiratory depression after a single dose. May need repeat boluses or continuous infusion (~⅔ of reversal dose per hour). Buprenorphine binds with high affinity → poorly reversed at standard naloxone doses.

APAP (Acetaminophen) — 4-Phase Course
PhaseTimeFeatures
I0–24 hAsymptomatic or mild N/V — "silent phase." Patient looks well.
II24–72 hRUQ pain, rising LFTs (AST/ALT >1000), ↓ urine output.
III72–96 hPeak hepatotoxicity — AST/ALT in tens of thousands, coagulopathy, jaundice, AMS, renal failure.
IV4 d – 2 wkRecovery if survived, OR death from fulminant liver failure.
APAP Toxic Metabolite Pathway (Mechanism Chain)
1.APAP metabolized by CYP2E1 (minor pathway in therapeutic dosing).
↓
2.Toxic metabolite NAPQI (N-acetyl-p-benzoquinone imine) produced.
↓
3.Normally detoxified by hepatic glutathione (GSH) — but in OD, GSH stores are overwhelmed/depleted.
↓
4.Free NAPQI binds hepatocyte proteins → centrilobular (zone 3) hepatic necrosis.
Risk-Stratification Tool + Antidote + Time Window
Rumack-Matthew nomogram (serum APAP level vs hours post-ingestion).
N-acetylcysteine (NAC) — IV or PO.
Most effective if started within 8–10 h of ingestion; still beneficial up to 24+ h.
NAC Mechanism (2 actions)
1.Replenishes hepatic glutathione stores (NAC is a cysteine precursor for GSH synthesis).
2.Directly conjugates and detoxifies NAPQI; also has antioxidant + microcirculatory benefits in established liver injury.
Why "Looks Fine in the ED" Is the Wrong Reason to Skip Treatment
APAP toxicity is silent for 24–72 h. By the time RUQ pain or transaminitis appears (Phase II), NAC may no longer prevent fulminant liver failure. Always check APAP level + time → plot nomogram → treat per protocol, regardless of how well the patient looks.
Salicylate (ASA) Poisoning
Early Signs (≥3)
1.Tinnitus (often the earliest finding).
2.Nausea/vomiting.
3.Hyperpnea/tachypnea (direct stimulation of medullary respiratory center). Also: hyperthermia, diaphoresis.
Classic Acid-Base Pattern (Mixed Disorder)
MIXED respiratory alkalosis (hyperventilation, ↑ pH) PLUS anion-gap metabolic acidosis (uncoupled oxidative phosphorylation → lactate; ketones; salicylate anion itself). Adults often show alkalosis predominating early; severe/late disease and children show acidosis predominating. CSF acidosis worsens CNS toxicity even with normal serum levels.
Mechanism of Hyperthermia + Late Acidosis
1.Salicylates uncouple mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation.
↓
2.Energy from electron transport released as HEAT instead of being captured as ATP.
↓
3.Hyperthermia + anaerobic metabolism → lactate accumulation → anion-gap metabolic acidosis.
Treatment — 1st-Line Enhanced Elimination + When to Dialyze
Urinary alkalinization with IV NaHCO₃ (target urine pH 7.5–8.0).
Severe acidosis (pH <7.2), AMS / cerebral edema, pulmonary edema, renal failure, hemodynamic instability, level >100 mg/dL (acute) or >60 mg/dL (chronic).
Iron Poisoning — 5-Phase Course
PhaseTimeWhat Happens
1 — GI0–6 hVomiting, diarrhea, hematemesis, GI hemorrhage (direct mucosal corrosion).
2 — "Quiet"6–24 hApparent recovery — patient looks better. DECEPTIVE.
3 — Systemic12–48 hAnion-gap metabolic acidosis, shock, coagulopathy, multi-organ failure.
4 — Hepatic2–4 dHepatic necrosis → fulminant liver failure.
5 — Late/Scarring2–8 wkPyloric / proximal bowel obstruction from corrosive scarring.
Iron Antidote + Mechanism
Deferoxamine (IV chelator).
Chelates free (non-protein-bound) plasma iron → forms ferrioxamine, which is excreted in urine ("vin rosé" pink-red discoloration).
Key Lab Test + Optimal Timing Post-Ingestion
Serum iron + TIBC.
4–6 h post-ingestion (peak absorption); levels >500 μg/dL = severe toxicity.
🚩 TRAP — IRON PHASE 2 "APPARENT RECOVERY"
Patients can look completely well 6–24 h after ingestion, then abruptly crash into Phase 3 (shock, acidosis, multi-organ failure) and Phase 4 (liver failure). Do NOT discharge an iron-overdose patient who appears to improve. Get serum iron at 4–6 h, monitor for 24+ h, AXR for radiopaque tablets, treat with deferoxamine + WBI (charcoal does NOT bind iron — it's "M" in LAME Ca).
Opioid Overdose
Receptor Target + Mechanism of Death
μ (mu)-opioid receptor (CNS).
Brainstem respiratory center suppression → respiratory depression → hypoxia → cardiac arrest. (Also CNS depression → loss of airway protection → aspiration.)
Opioids Particularly Tricky for Naloxone Reversal (≥2)
1.Methadone — very long t½ (~24 h) → recurrent respiratory depression after naloxone wears off; needs continuous infusion + extended monitoring.
2.Buprenorphine — high μ-receptor affinity (partial agonist) → poorly reversed at standard naloxone doses. Fentanyl, SR oxycodone also problematic.
TCA (Tricyclic Antidepressant) Overdose — 3 Receptor Effects
Receptor Effect 1 → EKG Finding
Sodium channel blockade.
Wide QRS (>120 ms), terminal R wave in aVR >3 mm, ventricular arrhythmias.
Receptor Effect 2 → Hemodynamic Finding
Alpha-1 adrenergic blockade.
Hypotension, peripheral vasodilation.
Receptor Effect 3 → Peripheral Findings
Anticholinergic (muscarinic blockade).
Tachycardia, dry mucous membranes, hyperthermia, urinary retention, mydriasis, AMS/agitation/delirium.
Why TCA Also Causes Seizures (4th mechanism)
GABA-A antagonism by TCAs → ↓ inhibitory tone → lowered seizure threshold. Combined with hypoxia from cardiac collapse, seizures are common and can be refractory.
Antidote + Mechanism + Target Serum pH
1.Sodium bicarbonate 1–2 mEq/kg IV bolus, then infusion.
↓
2.(a) Na⁺ load overcomes Na-channel blockade; (b) alkalinization ↑ TCA protein binding + ↓ free drug → narrows QRS.
↓
3.Goal serum pH 7.45–7.55. Monitor serial EKGs + ABG.
2 Drugs to AVOID in TCA OD + Why
1.Avoid: flumazenil → can precipitate seizures (removes benzo-mediated CNS protection).
2.Avoid: physostigmine → risk of asystole and worsened seizures with TCA's already-blocked Na channels.
Carbon Monoxide (CO) Poisoning
CO Mechanism — 2 Pathways of O₂ Deprivation
1.CO binds Hb with 200–250× greater affinity than O₂ → carboxyhemoglobin (COHb), ↓ O₂ carrying capacity.
↓
2.Leftward shift of O₂-Hb dissociation curve → bound O₂ not released to tissues.
↓
3.CO also inhibits cytochrome c oxidase (Complex IV) → cellular asphyxiation independent of O₂ delivery.
Diagnostic Test — Why Standard SpO₂ Fails
ABG with co-oximetry (measures COHb directly) — also serum lactate.
Pulse oximeter cannot distinguish COHb from oxyhemoglobin (similar absorption at ~660 nm) — it reads both as "saturated," giving a falsely normal reading despite severe hypoxia.
SeverityCOHb %Symptoms
Mild10–20%Headache, nausea, malaise — "flu without fever."
Moderate20–40%Severe headache, dizziness, confusion, chest pain, syncope.
Severe>40%Coma, seizures, cardiovascular collapse, death.
Treatment — Tier 1 vs Tier 2 (HBO Indications)
Remove from source + 100% O₂ via non-rebreather (↓ COHb t½ from 4–5 h to 60–90 min).
Loss of consciousness, COHb >25% (or >15% in pregnancy), neurologic symptoms, cardiac ischemia, persistent acidosis, pregnancy. HBO further reduces COHb t½ to ~20 min.
Why Cherry-Red Skin Is NOT a Reliable Sign
Late finding seen in <30% of CO patients (often only at autopsy). Do NOT rely on it to diagnose or rule out CO. The classic clinical scenario (multiple household members + pets sick simultaneously, flu without fever in winter) is much more sensitive.
🛑 HARD TRAP — CO + PREGNANCY
Fetal hemoglobin has a HIGHER affinity for CO than adult Hb → fetus accumulates more COHb and clears it more slowly than the mother. Maternal COHb level UNDERESTIMATES fetal exposure. Lower threshold for HBO in pregnancy (any symptomatic exposure or COHb >15%, regardless of maternal severity).

1st-Line Antidote + Mechanism + Backup if Unavailable
Fomepizole (4-MP).
Competitive inhibitor of alcohol dehydrogenase → blocks conversion of methanol/EG into their toxic metabolites. Also: HD if severe acidosis or end-organ damage; folate (methanol) and thiamine + pyridoxine (EG) shunt metabolites away.
IV ethanol (saturates ADH); maintain BAL ~100 mg/dL. Used only if fomepizole unavailable — more side effects (CNS depression, hypoglycemia, harder to titrate).

Supportive Care Cornerstones
Why Supportive Care Is the Cornerstone of Most Poisonings
Specific antidotes exist for only a minority of toxic exposures. For most poisonings, treatment is supportive — keeping the patient alive while the body metabolizes and eliminates the toxin. Even when antidotes exist, supportive care is always provided concurrently.
Indications to Intubate (≥3)
1.GCS ≤8.
2.Loss of airway protective reflexes (gag, cough).
3.Severe respiratory depression OR anticipated clinical deterioration.
First-Line Anticonvulsant in Toxic Seizures
Benzodiazepines (lorazepam, diazepam, midazolam). Escalate to phenobarbital or propofol if refractory. Avoid phenytoin in toxin-induced seizures (often ineffective). Pyridoxine for INH-related seizures.
Mandatory Step Before Discharge for ALL Intentional ODs
Psychiatric evaluation — assess suicide risk, intent, safety planning, need for inpatient psych admission. Medical clearance alone is INSUFFICIENT. Also rule out delayed toxins (APAP, salicylate levels) before declaring "medically cleared."
🛑 HARD TRAP — FLUMAZENIL CONTRAINDICATIONS
Avoid in (1) BENZODIAZEPINE-DEPENDENT patients — precipitates acute withdrawal seizures; (2) TCA OVERDOSE / suspected mixed-OD — removing benzo-mediated CNS protection unmasks TCA-induced seizures and arrhythmias. Flumazenil's t½ (~1 h) is shorter than most benzos → resedation. Not routinely recommended in unknown overdose.
