Parietal Cell Targets
3 Stimulatory Inputs to Parietal Cell (NT → Receptor → Cell of Origin)
1.ACh from vagal postganglionic neurons → M₃ receptor.
2.Histamine from ECL cells → H₂ receptor (Gs → ↑cAMP).
3.Gastrin from antral G cells → CCK-B receptor. All converge on H⁺/K⁺-ATPase.
Final Common Pump Blocked by PPIs/P-CABs
H⁺/K⁺-ATPase (proton pump) on the apical/canalicular membrane of the parietal cell.
Why Anticholinergics Are Rarely Used for Acid Suppression
Non-selective M blockade → prohibitive side effects: tachycardia, dry mouth, blurred vision, constipation, urinary retention, sexual dysfunction. Understand the target conceptually; not first-line.
Which Target Has the Most Potent Acid Suppression?
Proton pump (H⁺/K⁺-ATPase) — PPIs suppress 60–80% of acid output; targeting the final common step gives maximal efficacy.

🚩 TRAP — CIMETIDINE DRUG INTERACTIONS
Cimetidine inhibits CYP1A2, 2C19, and 2D6 → ↑ plasma levels of warfarin (bleeding), phenytoin (toxicity), benzodiazepines (sedation), theophylline. Also ↓ clopidogrel activation (prodrug requiring CYP2C19) → ↓antiplatelet effect. Famotidine does NOT have these interactions — switch if cimetidine interactions are a concern.
Proton Pump Inhibitors ("-prazoles")
MOA (Complete Chain — Prodrug → Activation → Binding Type)
1.Enteric-coated prodrug absorbed in small bowel.
↓
2.Accumulates in acidic parietal cell canaliculi; protonated to active sulfenamide.
↓
3.Covalently binds cysteine sulfhydryl groups on H⁺/K⁺-ATPase (IRREVERSIBLE).
↓
4.Permanent pump inactivation — new pumps (~48 hrs) required to restore acid secretion.
When Must PPIs Be Taken (Meal Timing)? Why?
30–60 minutes BEFORE a meal.
The meal activates proton pumps; only active pumps generate the acidic microenvironment needed to activate the PPI prodrug.
Why No Tolerance Develops (vs H₂ Blockers)
Irreversible covalent binding inactivates each pump permanently. Acid suppression depends on synthesis of NEW pumps, not receptor signaling — there's no feedback receptor upregulation.
PPI with LEAST CYP450 Interactions
Pantoprazole — cleanest interaction profile; preferred when patient takes multiple CYP-sensitive drugs (e.g., clopidogrel).
PPICYP Effects / Notes
Omeprazole / EsomeprazoleInhibit CYP2C19 + 3A4 → ↑ warfarin/oxycodone levels; ↓ clopidogrel activation (major interaction). Esomeprazole = S-enantiomer of omeprazole.
PantoprazoleLOWEST CYP450 interactions of all PPIs — preferred when clopidogrel or warfarin also prescribed.
Lansoprazole~80% bioavailable; intermediate CYP profile; shorter duration than omeprazole.
5 Long-Term PPI Adverse Effects
1.↑ C. difficile infection (gastric acid is a protective barrier).
2.Vitamin B12 deficiency (acid needed to release B12 from food proteins).
3.Bone fractures (impaired Ca²⁺ absorption — hip, spine, wrist).
4.Hypomagnesemia (can cause tetany, seizures, arrhythmias).
5.Community-acquired pneumonia + acute interstitial nephritis + rebound acid hypersecretion.
Mechanism — Why PPIs Cause B12 Deficiency
1.PPI suppresses gastric acid → achlorhydria.
↓
2.Acid + pepsin normally cleave B12 from dietary proteins — this step fails.
↓
3.↓ free B12 available for intrinsic-factor binding → ↓ ileal absorption → B12 deficiency.
🚩 TRAP — PPI + CLOPIDOGREL
Omeprazole and esomeprazole are potent CYP2C19 inhibitors. Clopidogrel is a prodrug activated by CYP2C19 → ↓ active metabolite → ↓ antiplatelet effect → ↑ stent thrombosis / MI risk. Preferred alternative: pantoprazole (minimal 2C19 inhibition) or an H₂ blocker (famotidine).

Hypercalcemia.
Metabolic alkalosis.
Acute kidney injury.
High-dose calcium carbonate (Tums) chronic use — especially with thiazides or vitamin D.
🚩 TRAP — ANTACID DRUG INTERACTIONS
Antacids raise gastric pH → ↓ absorption of drugs requiring acid: tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones, iron, ketoconazole/itraconazole, atazanavir. Also chelate divalent cations. Rule: separate dosing by ≥2 hours.
H. pylori — Virulence & Diagnosis
Key Virulence Enzyme + Mechanism of Acid Survival
UREASE.
Converts urea → NH₃ + CO₂; NH₃ locally buffers gastric acid → allows bacterial survival in mucosal layer. Urease is also the target of the urea breath test.
Long-Term Complications of Untreated H. pylori (2)
1.Gastric adenocarcinoma (intestinal type — strong causal link).
2.MALT lymphoma (marginal zone B-cell lymphoma — often regresses with H. pylori eradication alone). Also causes chronic gastritis and PUD.
Gold-Standard Non-Invasive Dx Test + How It Works
Urea breath test.
Patient ingests ¹³C- or ¹⁴C-labeled urea → bacterial urease splits it → labeled CO₂ detected in exhaled breath. Highly sensitive + specific; confirms active infection.
Why Serology (IgG) Is NOT Used to Confirm Eradication
IgG antibodies persist for months–years after bacteria cleared — cannot distinguish active from past infection. Use urea breath test or stool antigen for eradication confirmation.
Treatment Regimens
RegimenDrugs (all 4 / all 3)Duration / When Used
Quadruple Therapy (preferred, 1st-line)PPI + Bismuth subsalicylate + Metronidazole + Tetracycline.10–14 days. First-line due to rising clarithromycin resistance; preferred in most populations.

Why Is Triple Therapy Falling Out of Favor?
Rising clarithromycin resistance (>15% in many regions) → treatment failure rates climbing. Quadruple therapy covers resistance and is now preferred empirically.
PPI's Dual Role in H. pylori Therapy
1.Suppresses acid → promotes ulcer healing and symptom relief.
2.Raises gastric pH → improves efficacy of acid-labile antibiotics (amoxicillin, clarithromycin) at the mucosal surface.
Post-Treatment Retesting: When? Which Test(s)?
4 weeks AFTER completing antibiotics; stop PPI ≥2 weeks before testing to avoid false negatives.
Urea breath test or stool antigen — NOT serology.
Bismuth in Quadruple Therapy — Why Added? (2 roles)
1.Intrinsic antimicrobial activity against H. pylori (disrupts bacterial wall + inhibits urease).
2.Forms protective coating over ulcer base and reduces inflammation. (Side effect: harmless black stools and black tongue.)

🛑 HARD TRAP — MISOPROSTOL IN PREGNANCY
Misoprostol is a PGE₁ analog and potent uterotonic — induces uterine contractions and cervical ripening. Category X for ulcer indications. Used therapeutically in obstetrics ONLY for: labor induction (term), medical abortion (with mifepristone), postpartum hemorrhage prevention/treatment. NEVER prescribe for NSAID ulcer prophylaxis in pregnant women — use a PPI instead.
GERD Pathophysiology
Primary Mechanical Defect in GERD
Reduced lower esophageal sphincter (LES) tone → retrograde movement of gastric contents into the esophagus → mucosal inflammation.
Normal LES Resting Pressure (mmHg)
~20 mmHg (range 10–30 mmHg) — high enough to prevent reflux at rest.
3 Normal Esophageal Defense Mechanisms
1.LES tone (mechanical barrier).
2.Esophageal peristalsis (clears refluxed material).
3.Salivary bicarbonate (neutralizes residual acid).
Progression: Chronic GERD → ??? → ??? (name both)
1.Barrett's esophagus (intestinal metaplasia — columnar epithelium with goblet cells).
2.Esophageal adenocarcinoma.
Factors That Reduce LES Pressure (Drug/Food)
CategoryExamples
Dietary (≥4)Fatty foods, caffeine, chocolate, alcohol, citrus, peppermint.
Drugs (≥5 classes)Calcium channel blockers, anticholinergics, nitrates, β-agonists, α-antagonists, opioids (morphine), benzodiazepines, barbiturates, progesterone.
Systemic diseaseScleroderma (impairs esophageal motility and acid clearance).
PhysiologicPregnancy (progesterone ↓ LES tone + gravid uterus ↑ abdominal pressure); obesity.
Stepwise Treatment
Step 1 (Lifestyle)
Weight loss, elevate head of bed, avoid late meals (≥3 hrs before supine), avoid trigger foods, smoking cessation, smaller meals.
Step 2 (On-Demand Drug Class)
Antacids (or alginate) — rapid, symptomatic relief of infrequent/mild heartburn.
Step 3 (Mild/Intermittent Symptoms)
H₂ blocker (famotidine) — daily or BID for more persistent symptoms.
Step 4 (Frequent/Severe or Erosive)
PPI once daily — standard for erosive esophagitis and frequent symptoms.
Step 5 (Refractory / Failed BID PPI)
P-CAB (vonoprazan) — for severe erosive esophagitis or PPI-refractory GERD.
Surgical Option for Refractory GERD
Nissen fundoplication (wrapping gastric fundus around distal esophagus to recreate a competent LES).
🚩 TRAP — DRUG-INDUCED GERD
Common offenders: (1) Calcium channel blockers; (2) Anticholinergics (antihistamines, TCAs); (3) Nitrates; (4) Opioids (morphine); (5) Benzodiazepines. Recognize drug-induced GERD before adding yet another acid suppressant — deprescribe the offender when possible.

Why Is the CTZ Uniquely Accessible to Blood-Borne Toxins?
The area postrema is a circumventricular organ — it lacks a blood-brain barrier, so circulating drugs, toxins, and metabolites directly stimulate its chemoreceptors. This is why chemo, opioids, and uremia cause N/V via the CTZ.
Which 2 Receptors Dominate Motion Sickness (Vestibular)?
1.H₁ (histamine) — blocked by meclizine/dimenhydrinate.
2.M₁ (muscarinic) — blocked by scopolamine.

🛑 HARD TRAP — METOCLOPRAMIDE IN PARKINSON'S
Metoclopramide crosses the blood-brain barrier and blocks central D₂ receptors in the nigrostriatal pathway — this directly worsens parkinsonian motor symptoms and can precipitate a parkinsonian crisis. CONTRAINDICATED in Parkinson's disease. Also avoid in mechanical bowel obstruction (prokinetic effect may cause perforation). Safer alternative for gastroparesis: trimethobenzamide or dietary measures.

🛑 HARD TRAP — LOPERAMIDE IN C. DIFFICILE / EHEC
Slowing gut motility in toxin-mediated or invasive infectious diarrhea prolongs mucosal toxin exposure → ↑ risk of toxic megacolon, hemolytic uremic syndrome (EHEC O157:H7), and sepsis. Contraindicated in C. difficile, EHEC, Shigella, and any bloody/febrile diarrhea. Rehydration + targeted antibiotics instead.

Lactulose Dual Use — Laxative AND ???
Hepatic encephalopathy treatment: colonic bacteria ferment lactulose → acidification of gut lumen → converts NH₃ → NH₄⁺ (charged, non-absorbable) → trapped and excreted in stool → ↓ serum ammonia.
Why Do PAMORAs NOT Reverse Central Analgesia?
They are highly polar/charged or actively pumped out by P-glycoprotein at the blood-brain barrier → cannot cross into CNS → block only peripheral (enteric) μ-opioid receptors. Relieves constipation without reversing central pain control.
IBD Treatment Pyramid (Bottom = Mild → Top = Severe)
Step 1 (Mildest)
5-ASAs (mesalamine, sulfasalazine) — induction and maintenance, primarily in UC.
Step 2 (Induction only — NOT maintenance; why?)
Corticosteroids (prednisone, budesonide). Used only for induction of remission — chronic use causes Cushingoid toxicity (osteoporosis, DM, infection, cataracts, adrenal suppression). Budesonide has high first-pass metabolism → fewer systemic effects.
Step 3 (Immunomodulators — name 2)
1.Azathioprine / 6-mercaptopurine (purine antimetabolites).
2.Methotrexate (dihydrofolate reductase inhibitor — more effective in Crohn's disease).
Step 4 (Biologics — 3 target categories)
1.Anti-TNF-α (infliximab, adalimumab, certolizumab, golimumab).
2.Anti-integrin (vedolizumab α4β7; natalizumab α4).
3.Anti-IL-12/23 (ustekinumab) or anti-IL-23 alone (risankizumab). Plus: JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib) and S1P modulators (ozanimod).
Step 5 (Last-Line)
Surgery — colectomy (curative for UC), segmental resection or strictureplasty (CD; recurrence common).
5-ASAs (Mesalamine & Related)
MOA (Local Anti-Inflammatory)
1.Topically active in colonic lumen (minimal systemic absorption).
↓

More Effective in UC or CD? Why?
Ulcerative colitis.
Disease limited to colon, where 5-ASAs are delivered and act locally. Less effective in Crohn's because CD often affects small bowel (proximal to colonic release).
Sulfasalazine — What Is the 2nd Component? ADR It Causes?
Sulfapyridine (sulfa antibiotic) — cleaved by colonic bacteria to release 5-ASA. Sulfapyridine causes the sulfa-related ADRs: rash/Stevens-Johnson, hemolysis in G6PD deficiency, and REVERSIBLE OLIGOSPERMIA (male infertility).
Key Renal ADR of Mesalamine
Interstitial nephritis (check baseline + periodic creatinine).

🛑 HARD TRAP — NATALIZUMAB & PML
Natalizumab blocks α4 integrin non-selectively → reduces CNS lymphocyte trafficking → allows JC virus to reactivate in brain → progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML — often fatal demyelinating disease). Screen anti-JC virus antibody BEFORE starting and periodically during therapy; highest risk with JCV-positive patients on prolonged therapy + prior immunosuppression. Vedolizumab (α4β7, gut-selective) does NOT cross into CNS trafficking → no PML risk.
Immunomodulators
Azathioprine / 6-MP — What Enzymes to Check Before Starting? Why?
TPMT (thiopurine methyltransferase) and NUDT15 activity/genotype.
Deficiency → excess active 6-thioguanine nucleotides → severe myelosuppression and potentially fatal pancytopenia.
Azathioprine / 6-MP Key ADRs (3)
1.Myelosuppression (dose-related; monitor CBC).
2.Pancreatitis (idiosyncratic, usually early).
3.Hepatotoxicity. (Plus ↑ lymphoma/skin cancer risk, especially in combination with anti-TNF.)
Methotrexate — Preferred in UC or CD?
Crohn's disease — effective for maintenance of remission. Limited efficacy in UC.
MTX Pre-Screening Before Starting for IBD (Tests)
Baseline CBC, LFTs, creatinine, hepatitis B/C serology, pregnancy test (MTX is teratogenic — contraindicated in pregnancy). Chest X-ray if pulmonary risk. Supplement folic acid to ↓ ADRs.
Anti-TNF Pre-Screening & ADRs
Mandatory Infectious Pre-Screens Before Anti-TNF (2)
1.Latent TB screen — PPD or IGRA (QuantiFERON); treat latent TB before/with therapy.
2.Hepatitis B serology (HBsAg, anti-HBc) — risk of reactivation fulminant hepatitis; treat or monitor with antivirals.
Malignancy Risk Increased with Anti-TNF + Thiopurine Combo
Hepatosplenic T-cell lymphoma (rare but often fatal) — classically in young males on combined anti-TNF + azathioprine/6-MP. Also ↑ non-melanoma skin cancers.
FMT (Fecal Microbiota Transplant)
Gold-Standard Indication for FMT
Recurrent (multiple-episode) C. difficile infection refractory to standard antibiotics — cure rates >85%.
Emerging (Non-Standard) Indication
Ulcerative colitis — some randomized trials show benefit for induction of remission; not yet standard-of-care.
