Discontinuous endothelial cells with large fenestrations (100–200 nm)

Wider and more irregular than typical capillaries

Ito cell activation — 6-step cascade in cirrhosis
Chronic injury: alcohol, viral hepatitis, NASH, iron, toxins → hepatocyte damage
Kupffer cells activated → release TGF-β, PDGF, TNF-α
Stellate cells proliferate in Space of Disse
Lose vitamin A → transform into myofibroblasts

4 clinical consequences of cirrhosis
Portal hypertension → varices, caput medusae, splenomegaly, ascites
Loss of synthetic function → ↓ albumin, ↓ clotting factors (INR ↑)

LOs 1–5
Q1: Liver directionality
In the classic hepatic lobule, what is the direction of blood flow and bile flow, respectively?

A) Both flow from central vein → portal triad
B) Both flow from portal triad → central vein
C) Blood: triad → central vein; Bile: hepatocyte → triad (opposite directions)
D) Blood: central vein → triad; Bile: triad → hepatocyte
E) Both flow in canaliculi
Correct Answer: C

Blood flows INTO the lobule at the portal triad (from hepatic a. + portal v.), through sinusoids, out via central vein → hepatic veins → IVC. Bile flows OPPOSITE — hepatocyte → canaliculus → bile ductule (canals of Hering) → bile duct in portal triad.
Q2: Acetaminophen zone
A 22-year-old woman presents with fulminant hepatic failure after acetaminophen overdose. Biopsy will most likely show necrosis in which region?

A) Zone 1 (periportal)
B) Zone 2 (midzonal)
C) Zone 3 (centrilobular, around central vein)
D) Space of Disse
E) Portal triad
Correct Answer: C

Zone 3 is farthest from oxygenated portal blood → most susceptible to ischemic and toxic injury. Also the zone with the most CYP450 → converts acetaminophen to the toxic NAPQI metabolite. Acetaminophen, ischemia ("shock liver"), and alcohol all target zone 3.
Q3: Sinusoid features
The hepatic sinusoid is classified as which type of capillary?

A) Continuous with tight junctions
B) Fenestrated with intact basement membrane
C) Sinusoidal (discontinuous) — fenestrations + NO basement membrane
D) Lymphatic
E) Arteriole
Correct Answer: C

Hepatic sinusoids are discontinuous capillaries — large fenestrations AND no basement membrane → allows plasma proteins and macromolecules free access to hepatocytes across the Space of Disse.
Q4: Ito cell function
Which cell type, located in the Space of Disse, stores vitamin A in its quiescent state and transforms into collagen-producing myofibroblasts in cirrhosis?

A) Kupffer cell
B) Hepatocyte
C) Cholangiocyte
D) Hepatic stellate (Ito) cell
E) Pit cell (liver NK)
Correct Answer: D

Ito (hepatic stellate) cells — store vitamin A in lipid droplets (why chronic vit A toxicity → ↑ cirrhosis). When activated by Kupffer cell cytokines (TGF-β, PDGF), they transform into myofibroblasts → deposit type I collagen → fibrosis & cirrhosis.
Q5: Gallbladder wall
Which layer is ABSENT from the gallbladder wall (distinguishing it from typical GI tract)?

A) Mucosa
B) Submucosa (and muscularis mucosae)
C) Muscularis externa
D) Serosa
E) Adventitia
Correct Answer: B

The gallbladder lacks a submucosa and muscularis mucosae. It has only 3 layers: mucosa (simple columnar w/ microvilli), muscularis externa, and serosa/adventitia. This means mucosal cancers quickly invade deep — one reason gallbladder adenocarcinoma has such poor prognosis.
Q6: Rokitansky-Aschoff sinuses
A cholecystectomy specimen shows mucosal herniations extending through the muscularis externa. What are these called?

A) Crypts of Lieberkühn
B) Brunner glands
C) Rokitansky-Aschoff sinuses
D) Peyer patches
E) Plicae circulares
Correct Answer: C

Rokitansky-Aschoff sinuses = outpouchings of mucosa through the muscularis externa, characteristic of chronic cholecystitis. Think "cholesterolosis" or gallbladder "diverticula."
Q7: Acinar cell H&E
A pancreatic acinar cell shows intensely BASOPHILIC base and EOSINOPHILIC apex on H&E. What accounts for the basophilia at the base?

A) Zymogen granules
B) Rough endoplasmic reticulum (abundant rRNA)
C) Lipofuscin pigment
D) Mitochondria
E) Glycogen
Correct Answer: B

Basal basophilia = extensive rough ER (for protein/enzyme synthesis) — rRNA is acidic and binds basic dyes. Apical eosinophilia = zymogen granules (pre-packed digestive enzymes). This "polarity" is the hallmark of a serous secretory acinar cell.
Q8: T1DM histology
An autopsy of a 14-year-old boy with a 2-year history of type 1 diabetes shows islets with a lymphocytic infiltrate and profound loss of one cell type. Which cells are destroyed?

A) α-cells (glucagon)
B) β-cells (insulin)
C) δ-cells (somatostatin)
D) PP cells (pancreatic polypeptide)
E) Acinar cells
Correct Answer: B

Type 1 DM = autoimmune destruction of β-cells → insulitis (lymphocytic infiltrate, especially CD8+ T cells). α, δ, and PP cells preserved. Type 2 DM → amyloid deposition (islet amyloid polypeptide / amylin) within islets, β-cell dysfunction.
Q9: Zollinger-Ellison
A patient has refractory peptic ulcers in unusual locations (e.g., jejunum), chronic diarrhea, and elevated serum gastrin. Which islet cell tumor is responsible?

A) Insulinoma (β-cell)
B) Gastrinoma (non-β islet / duodenal)
C) Glucagonoma (α-cell)
D) VIPoma
E) Somatostatinoma
Correct Answer: B

Zollinger-Ellison syndrome = gastrinoma (often duodenal wall or pancreas), causing hypergastrinemia → ↑ gastric acid → multiple, recurrent, distally located ulcers + diarrhea. ~25% of gastrinomas occur in MEN1 (3 P's: Pituitary, Parathyroid, Pancreas).
Q10: PDAC molecular
A 68-year-old smoker presents with painless jaundice, weight loss, and a mass in the pancreatic head. Which is the most commonly mutated oncogene in pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma?

A) BRAF V600E
B) KRAS
C) EGFR
D) APC
E) BCR-ABL
Correct Answer: B

KRAS activating mutation is present in >90% of PDAC — the earliest driver. Subsequent losses: CDKN2A (p16), TP53, SMAD4/DPC4. PDAC marker = CA 19-9. Classic signs: painless jaundice, Courvoisier sign (palpable non-tender gallbladder), Trousseau sign (migratory thrombophlebitis).
