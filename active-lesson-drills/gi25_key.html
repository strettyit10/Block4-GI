LOs 1–3
Q1: Traveler's diarrhea pathogen
A 28-year-old medical student returns from a 2-week trip to Mexico with 3 days of profuse watery diarrhea, abdominal cramps, and no blood. Stool studies show a non-inflammatory profile. Which pathogen is most likely?

A) EHEC (O157:H7)
B) Shigella
C) ETEC
D) Campylobacter jejuni
E) Norovirus
Correct Answer: C

ETEC is the #1 cause of traveler's diarrhea — watery, secretory, non-inflammatory (LT toxin ↑ cAMP; ST toxin ↑ cGMP). EHEC and Shigella cause dysentery. Campylobacter is possible but less common. Norovirus is plausible but less likely in this classic traveler presentation.
Q2: HUS after undercooked beef
A 5-year-old develops bloody diarrhea 4 days after a barbecue. Five days later he is pale, lethargic, with petechiae, oliguria, and Cr 2.9. Smear shows schistocytes. Which organism and toxin?

A) Shigella + Shiga toxin
B) EHEC + Shiga-like toxin (Stx)
C) ETEC + heat-labile toxin
D) Salmonella + LPS
E) Campylobacter + cytolethal distending toxin
Correct Answer: B

EHEC O157:H7 + Shiga-like toxin → HUS. Undercooked beef classic. Avoid antibiotics and anti-motility agents.
Q3: Preformed toxin — fast onset
Six people eat at a picnic; 3 hours later all develop vomiting and cramps. Recover in 24 h.

A) Salmonella
B) Campylobacter
C) S. aureus
D) EHEC
E) C. difficile
Correct Answer: C

S. aureus preformed enterotoxin → 2–6 h onset, vomiting predominant.
Q4: Invasive vs. toxigenic
34 M with bloody diarrhea, fever, leukocytosis after undercooked chicken. Stool WBCs +; curved gram-negative rods at 42 °C.

A) ETEC — toxigenic
B) Campylobacter jejuni — invasive
C) Vibrio cholerae — toxigenic
D) Listeria — invasive
E) Norovirus — viral
Correct Answer: B

Campylobacter jejuni (curved GNR at 42 °C). Post-infectious: GBS + reactive arthritis.
Q5: C. difficile first-line
67 M inpatient on ceftriaxone → 10 watery stools/day; toxin PCR + EIA positive; afebrile, WBC 14 k.

A) IV metronidazole alone
B) Oral vancomycin OR oral fidaxomicin
C) Oral ciprofloxacin
D) Loperamide
E) IV cefepime
Correct Answer: B

First-line is oral vancomycin OR fidaxomicin; metronidazole no longer first-line. Stop inciting antibiotic; never use loperamide.
Q6: SBP
58 M with cirrhosis/ascites, fever, abdominal pain. Paracentesis: PMN 320/μL, gram-negative rods on Gram stain.

A) Secondary peritonitis — polymicrobial
B) SBP — E. coli
C) CAPD peritonitis — S. epidermidis
D) TB peritonitis
E) Pancreatitis
Correct Answer: B

SBP: PMN ≥250; E. coli most common. Ceftriaxone × 5–7 d; long-term prophylaxis.
Q7: Amebic liver abscess
33 M from Mexico with bloody diarrhea, RUQ pain, fever; US shows single right-lobe lesion; E. histolytica serology +.

A) Percutaneous drainage alone
B) Metronidazole, then paromomycin
C) Ceftriaxone + metronidazole
D) Surgical resection
E) Oral vancomycin
Correct Answer: B

Metronidazole (tissue), then luminal agent (paromomycin) to eliminate cysts. Drainage usually unnecessary.
Q8: H. pylori triple therapy
Duodenal ulcer + positive urea breath test. Standard first-line?

A) Amox + metronidazole + bismuth (no PPI)
B) PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin
C) PPI alone × 8 wk
D) PPI + tetracycline alone
E) H2 blocker + clarithromycin alone
Correct Answer: B

Classic triple therapy: PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin × 14 d. Quadruple (PPI + bismuth + metro + tetracycline) for high clarithromycin resistance.
