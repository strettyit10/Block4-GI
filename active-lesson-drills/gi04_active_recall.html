Practice Qs — LOs 1–5
USMLE Step 1 & In-House Style — attempt all before revealing answers
Q1 (LO1) A 65-year-old man has a rectal polyp biopsy showing adenocarcinoma penetrating through the muscularis mucosae into the submucosa. Crossing which layer most significantly changes his oncologic staging?
A. Lamina propria  B. Muscularis mucosae  C. Submucosa  D. Muscularis propria  E. Serosa
B — Muscularis mucosae. This is the key oncologic staging landmark in GI cancers. Tumor above it (T1a, intramucosal) has minimal lymph node risk; below it (T1b, into submucosa) the rich submucosal lymphatic network allows nodal spread (~10–15% risk) — changing management to consider surgical resection.
Q2 (LO1) A 3-week-old infant fails to pass meconium for 48 hours. Rectal biopsy shows no ganglion cells in the rectosigmoid. Which best describes the obstruction mechanism?
A. Dilated proximal colon cannot contract  B. Aganglionic segment lacks inhibitory neurons → tonic contraction → functional obstruction  C. Hypermotility from excess ACh  D. Only proximal colon is aganglionic  E. Meissner’s alone is absent; Auerbach’s spared
B — Aganglionic segment lacks inhibitory ENS neurons. Without VIP/NO-releasing inhibitory neurons, the distal segment remains in tonic spasm. The narrow segment is diseased; the dilated megacolon proximal is the normal one backed up with stool. Both Meissner’s and Auerbach’s are absent.
Q3 (LO2) A 52-year-old woman with 12-year GERD history has biopsy 3 cm above the GEJ showing simple columnar epithelium with goblet cells. What is this finding and its significance?
A. Normal GEJ histology  B. Reflux esophagitis (neutrophilic only)  C. Barrett’s esophagus — intestinal metaplasia predisposing to esophageal adenocarcinoma  D. Squamous cell carcinoma in situ  E. Normal cardiac mucosa variant
C — Barrett’s esophagus. Goblet cells in columnar epithelium proximal to the GEJ = intestinal metaplasia = Barrett’s. Goblet cells are the required histologic criterion. Normal GEJ columnar mucosa does NOT contain goblet cells. Barrett’s is the major risk factor for esophageal adenocarcinoma.
Q4 (LO2) A 40-year-old has epigastric pain. Duodenal biopsy reveals branched tubular glands in the submucosa secreting alkaline mucus. These glands protect primarily against what?
A. Mechanical abrasion  B. Bacterial translocation  C. Acidic chyme from the stomach  D. Bile reflux  E. Pancreatic protease autodigestion
C — Acidic chyme (Brunner’s glands). Brunner’s glands are the ONLY intestinal submucosal glands. They secrete alkaline bicarbonate-rich mucin to neutralize gastric acid entering the duodenum. In Zollinger-Ellison syndrome, massive acid overload overwhelms their capacity → multiple duodenal ulcers.
Q5 (LO3) A 30-year-old with chronic diarrhea, steatorrhea, and elevated anti-tTG IgA has small intestinal biopsy showing villous atrophy and crypt hyperplasia. Why is fat malabsorption prominent?
A. Loss of Brunner’s glands reduces bicarbonate for lipase  B. Crypt hyperplasia accelerates transit  C. Villous atrophy eliminates the primary surface area amplification for absorption  D. Intraepithelial lymphocytes sequester fatty acids  E. Loss of goblet cells exposes enterocytes to bile detergent injury
C — Villous atrophy (celiac disease). Villi provide ~10× surface area amplification. Their gluten-triggered immune destruction drastically reduces absorptive surface. Duodenum and proximal jejunum are most severely affected. Crypt hyperplasia is compensatory, not destructive.
Q6 (LO4) A 62-year-old woman has fatigue, glossitis, difficulty walking, MCV 114 fL, and positive anti-parietal cell antibodies. What is the sequence of pathophysiology?
A. Parietal loss → ↓HCl → bacterial overgrowth → B12 deficiency  B. Parietal loss → ↓intrinsic factor → impaired B12 absorption (terminal ileum) → demyelination  C. Chief cell loss → ↓pepsinogen → B12 malabsorption  D. G cell hyperplasia → ↑HCl → B12 denaturation  E. Parietal loss → ↓HCl → iron deficiency → neurological symptoms
B — Pernicious anemia. Parietal cells produce HCl AND intrinsic factor. Autoimmune destruction eliminates IF → B12 cannot be absorbed in the terminal ileum → megaloblastic anemia + subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord (posterior columns + lateral corticospinal tracts). Treatment: parenteral (IM) B12.
Q7 (LO4) A 22-year-old with typhoid fever (Salmonella typhi confirmed) has rose spots and bacteremia. Which specialized cell type allows S. typhi to cross the intestinal epithelium?
A. Goblet cells (transcytosis via mucin binding)  B. Paneth cells (defensin granule pathway)  C. M cells overlying Peyer’s patches in the terminal ileum  D. Enterocytes via brush border endocytosis  E. Enteroendocrine cells via serotonin receptors
C — M cells overlying Peyer’s patches. M cells lack a brush border and transcytose luminal antigens/microorganisms to the underlying lymphoid tissue. S. typhi exploits this for invasion. Other pathogens using M cells: Yersinia, poliovirus, Listeria. Week 3 of typhoid: Peyer’s patches can ulcerate → bowel perforation.
Q8 (LO5) A pathology resident sees a slide: flat mucosal surface with no villi, deep straight crypts packed with goblet cells, outer longitudinal muscle condensed into three discrete bands. Which segment?
A. Duodenum  B. Jejunum  C. Ileum  D. Ascending colon  E. Anal canal
D — Ascending colon. The triad of (1) no villi, (2) very goblet-cell-rich deep crypts, and (3) teniae coli (3 bands of outer longitudinal muscle) is pathognomonic for the colon. All three SI segments have villi. Anal canal would show stratified squamous epithelium.
