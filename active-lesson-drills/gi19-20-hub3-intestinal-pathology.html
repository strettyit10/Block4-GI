Q1 · Congenital Diverticula · Meckel Diverticulum
A 3-year-old boy presents to the ED with painless hematochezia (bright red blood per rectum) that has occurred three times in the past month. Hemoglobin is 8.9 g/dL. Physical exam is unremarkable. Endoscopy reveals a normal-appearing colon and terminal ileum. A technetium-99m pertechnetate scan shows focal uptake in the distal ileum. Which tissue type is responsible for the positive radionuclide scan?
AHeterotopic pancreatic tissue secreting digestive enzymes
BEctopic gastric mucosa secreting hydrochloric acid
CInflamed true diverticulum with normal ileal tissue
DAdenomatous polyp arising from residual duct tissue
EPseudo-diverticulum with herniated submucosa
Correct: B — Ectopic gastric mucosa in Meckel diverticulum
A positive Tc-99m pertechnetate scan (Meckel scan) localizes ectopic gastric mucosa within a Meckel diverticulum. This heterotopic tissue secretes hydrochloric acid, which can ulcerate the adjacent ileal mucosa, causing bleeding. Meckel's diverticulum is a remnant of the vitelline duct with Rule of 2's: 2% of population, 2 feet from ileocecal valve, 2 inches long, 2 types of ectopic tissue (gastric or pancreatic), 2x more common in males, presents before age 2 in most symptomatic cases.

A— Pancreatic heterotopia may cause obstruction but does not concentrate pertechnetate.
C— Diverticulitis alone would not concentrate the radionuclide.
D— Adenoma is not the classic bleeding source in Meckel.
E— Pseudo-diverticula are common in the colon but do not explain Meckel.
Q2 · Congenital Diverticula · Hirschsprung Disease
A newborn male does not pass meconium within 48 hours despite adequate feeding. Abdominal X-ray shows proximal small bowel distension with a narrowed distal segment. Contrast enema reveals a transition zone in the distal colon. Rectal biopsy shows absence of ganglion cells in the muscularis propria and submucosa. Which embryologic defect explains this presentation?
AFailure of mesodermal invagination during weeks 3–5
BIncomplete cranial-to-caudal migration of neural crest cells
CAbnormal differentiation of endoderm into epithelial lining
DExcessive apoptosis of splanchnic mesoderm
EHyperplasia of submucosal nerve fibers
Correct: B — Incomplete neural crest cell migration causes aganglionosis
Hirschsprung disease (congenital aganglionic megacolon) results from failure of neural crest cells to migrate completely from the proximal intestine toward the rectum during weeks 5–12 of gestation. This leads to a segment lacking ganglion cells (aganglionosis), most commonly the distal rectum and sigmoid. Without enteric ganglia, peristalsis cannot propel stool, causing functional obstruction. The RET gene (receptor tyrosine kinase) is implicated in short-segment disease.

A— Mesodermal issues would affect muscularis, not ganglia specifically.
C— Endodermal differentiation produces the epithelium, not ganglia.
D— Mesoderm apoptosis would not explain selective absence of ganglia.
E— Hirschsprung is characterized by absence, not hyperplasia of nerves.
Q3 · Intestinal Obstruction · Intussusception
A 18-month-old girl presents with intermittent colicky abdominal pain, vomiting, and one episode of currant jelly-like stools. Abdominal ultrasound shows a characteristic "doughnut" appearance in transverse view and a "pseudokidney" sign in longitudinal view. Which pathophysiologic mechanism best explains the currant jelly appearance of the stool?
AUlceration of the intussusceptum from gastric acid exposure
BVenous congestion and mucosal bleeding from compression of mesenteric vessels
CAcute bacterial translocation through necrotic bowel wall
DMassive mucosal shedding from hypermotility of the involved segment
EConversion of hemoglobin to methemoglobin by bacterial toxins
Correct: B — Venous congestion causes mucosal bleeding (currant jelly stool)
Intussusception is the telescoping of a proximal bowel segment (intussusceptum) into a distal segment (intussuscipiens), dragging the mesentery with it. This compression obstructs venous return, leading to venous congestion and mucosal edema. Continued pressure compromises capillary flow, causing bleeding into the bowel lumen. The mixture of blood and mucus produces the characteristic currant jelly stools. Peak incidence is 3 months to 3 years; most cases are idiopathic (viral prodrome → lymphoid hyperplasia as lead point in terminal ileum). Air or hydrostatic reduction is often successful if performed within 24–48 hours.

A— Gastric acid exposure is not the primary mechanism.
C— Early intussusception causes bleeding before translocation.
D— Hypermotility does not cause this specific blood-and-mucus pattern.
E— Methemoglobin conversion is not relevant to intussusception.
Q4 · Ischemic Bowel Disease · Acute Mesenteric Ischemia
A 72-year-old man with atrial fibrillation (not anticoagulated) presents with acute severe postprandial abdominal pain out of proportion to abdominal exam findings. CT angiography with IV contrast shows an abrupt occlusion of the superior mesenteric artery (SMA) at its origin. Which complication is most likely to develop if reperfusion is not urgently achieved?
ABacterial overgrowth leading to protein malabsorption
BChronic stricture formation from submucosal fibrosis
CTransmural bowel necrosis with perforation and sepsis
DCarcinoid syndrome from hyperplasia of enteroendocrine cells
ESecondary lactase deficiency from reduced brush border enzyme synthesis
Correct: C — Transmural necrosis with perforation and sepsis
Acute mesenteric ischemia from SMA thromboembolism (often from AFib) is a surgical emergency. The small bowel supplied by the SMA is extremely vulnerable to ischemic necrosis because it has limited collateral circulation. Without prompt reperfusion (within hours), the bowel undergoes transmural infarction, leading to perforation, bacterial peritonitis, and sepsis. The classic presentation is severe pain with minimal exam findings ("pain out of proportion to exam"). Risk factors include AFib, hypercoagulable states, atherosclerosis, and low-flow states.

A— Overgrowth occurs in chronic ischemia, not acute necrosis.
B— Strictures develop after recovery from chronic ischemic colitis.
D— Carcinoid syndrome is not a complication of mesenteric ischemia.
E— Secondary lactase deficiency is seen in mucosal injury, not primary to acute ischemia.
Q5 · Ischemic Bowel Disease · Ischemic Colitis Watershed Zones
A 68-year-old woman with a history of coronary artery disease and recent hypotensive episode (MAP ~60 mmHg for 2 hours during sepsis) develops sudden left-sided abdominal pain with bloody diarrhea. Colonoscopy shows segmental mucosal ulceration and inflammation with a relative sparing of the rectum. Which region of colon is most vulnerable during this watershed ischemia?
AAppendix and cecum supplied by ileocolic artery
BSplenic flexure (Griffith's point) between SMA and IMA territories
CHepatic flexure supplied by superior mesenteric artery alone
DRectum and anal canal supplied by IMA exclusively
ETransverse colon proximal to hepatic flexure
Correct: B — Splenic flexure (Griffith's point) is a watershed zone
The splenic flexure (Griffith's point) and rectosigmoid junction (Sudeck's point) are watershed zones — areas of relatively tenuous vascular supply at the junction of major arterial territories (SMA and IMA). During systemic hypotension or low-flow states, these zones are the first to become ischemic because they depend on collateral flow. Ischemic colitis classically spares the rectum because it has dual blood supply from the IMA and hypogastric arteries. The sigmoid and descending colon are also vulnerable. Most cases of ischemic colitis are reversible if perfusion is restored quickly.

A— Cecum is supplied by the ileocolic artery (well-perfused).
C— Hepatic flexure is supplied by SMA branches (less vulnerable).
D— Rectum is dual-supplied; relatively spared.
E— Transverse colon proximal to flexure has better collaterals.
Q6 · Malabsorption · Celiac Disease Pathology
A 6-year-old girl presents with chronic diarrhea, weight loss, abdominal distension, and iron-deficiency anemia. Serum tissue transglutaminase (tTG) IgA is markedly elevated. Small intestinal biopsy shows villous atrophy with increased intraepithelial lymphocytes (IELs). Which gluten epitope peptide is the primary target of tissue transglutaminase?
Aα-gliadin, modified by tTG to become immunogenic
BDeamidated gluten peptides bound to HLA-DQ2/DQ8
CIntact gluten proteins without modification
DTransglutaminase enzyme itself (molecular mimicry)
EZonula occludens-1 (ZO-1) tight junction protein
Correct: B — Deamidated gluten peptides bound to HLA-DQ2/DQ8
In celiac disease, the enzyme tissue transglutaminase (tTG) deamidates glutamine residues in gluten peptides to glutamic acid, making them highly immunogenic. The deamidated peptides then bind to HLA-DQ2 or HLA-DQ8, which are present in nearly 95% of celiac patients. This triggers a CD4+ T cell response and B cell production of IgA antibodies against both the deamidated gluten and tTG itself. The pathology shows villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, and increased IELs. Diagnosis requires serology (anti-tTG IgA) and biopsy; treatment is a gluten-free diet.

A— tTG modifies the gluten, but the target is deamidated peptide.
C— Intact unmodified gluten is not recognized as an antigen.
D— While anti-tTG is produced, the peptide itself is the primary target.
E— ZO-1 is not the antigen in classic celiac disease.
Q7 · Malabsorption · Dermatitis Herpetiformis
A 32-year-old man presents with intensely pruritic vesicles and papules grouped in clusters on the extensor surfaces of the elbows and knees. Direct immunofluorescence of a skin biopsy shows granular IgA deposits at the tips of dermal papillae. Which intestinal finding is most closely associated with this cutaneous manifestation?
ACrypt abscess formation with normal villi
BVillous atrophy and increased intraepithelial lymphocytes (celiac-like histology)
CTransmural inflammation with fissuring ulcers
DThickened muscularis propria with stricture formation
ELoss of colonic crypt architecture with surface ulceration
Correct: B — Villous atrophy and increased IELs (celiac-associated)
Dermatitis herpetiformis (DH) is a cutaneous manifestation of gluten-sensitive enteropathy (celiac disease). Nearly all DH patients have celiac-type small intestinal histology (villous atrophy, increased IELs) even if asymptomatic. The skin shows granular IgA deposits at dermal papilla tips on direct immunofluorescence — a pathognomonic finding. DH is associated with anti-endomysial antibodies and anti-tTG IgA. Treatment is a gluten-free diet, and the skin lesions improve within weeks to months as the intestine heals.

A— Crypt abscess is seen in ulcerative colitis, not DH-associated disease.
C— Transmural inflammation is characteristic of Crohn disease, not DH.
D— Strictures develop in chronic Crohn disease.
E— Colonic crypt loss is seen in UC, not associated with DH.
Q8 · IBD — Crohn Disease Pathology
A 24-year-old woman with a 3-year history of right lower quadrant pain, nonbloody diarrhea, and weight loss undergoes colonoscopy. The mucosa shows patchy areas of inflammation separated by normal-appearing segments ("skip lesions"). Biopsy of an inflamed region reveals granulomatous infiltration extending through all layers of the bowel wall (muscularis propria and submucosa). Which complication is this patient at highest risk for developing?
AToxic megacolon with perforation
BEnterocutaneous fistula or perianal fistula
CFulminant colitis with universal colon involvement
DMassive hemorrhage from mucosal erosions
EAcute pseudomembranous colitis from C. difficile
Correct: B — Enterocutaneous/perianal fistulas from transmural disease
Crohn disease is characterized by skip lesions (discontinuous inflamed segments) and transmural inflammation affecting all layers of the bowel. The transmural involvement can penetrate the bowel wall, creating fistulas — abnormal connections to adjacent bowel (internal fistula), skin (enterocutaneous), or perirectal tissue (perianal/anorectal). Biopsy shows noncaseating granulomas in ~30–50% of cases. The mesentery becomes thickened ("creeping fat"). Most common sites: terminal ileum and right colon.

A— Toxic megacolon is a complication of UC, not Crohn.
C— Fulminant colitis with universal involvement is UC, not Crohn.
D— Massive hemorrhage is more typical of UC.
E— C. difficile infection is not a defining complication of Crohn.
Q9 · IBD — Ulcerative Colitis Features
A 28-year-old woman with a 5-year history of bloody diarrhea, urgent bowel movements (8–10/day), and tenesmus undergoes colonoscopy. The mucosa is friable and hyperemic from the rectum through the sigmoid colon with a sharp demarcation between inflamed and normal mucosa at the descending colon. Biopsy shows crypt abscesses with surface ulceration limited to the mucosa and submucosa. Which finding distinguishes this disease from Crohn disease?
APresence of noncaseating granulomas on biopsy
BContinuous mucosal inflammation without skip lesions; limited to mucosa/submucosa
CInvolvement of terminal ileum as the primary site
DDevelopment of fistulous tracts and perianal disease
ERequirement for total colectomy to achieve cure
Correct: B — Continuous mucosal inflammation without skip lesions
Ulcerative colitis (UC) is distinguished by: (1) continuous inflammation without skip lesions; (2) limited to mucosa and submucosa (non-transmural); (3) always involves the rectum and extends proximally; (4) crypt abscesses and crypt distortion on histology. UC increases risk of colorectal cancer and is associated with primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC). Complications include toxic megacolon, perforation, and systemic manifestations (uveitis, pyoderma gangrenosum, PSC).

A— Granulomas are seen in Crohn, not UC (though UC may have rare granulomas in UC; Crohn is more typical).
C— UC does not involve small bowel; Crohn commonly affects terminal ileum.
D— Fistulas are a hallmark of Crohn, not UC.
E— While total colectomy can cure UC, it is not the only distinguishing feature.
Q10 · IBD — Toxic Megacolon Complication
A 35-year-old man with 2 years of ulcerative colitis is admitted with a 3-day history of worsening bloody diarrhea (12–15 BMs/day), fever (39.2°C), and severe abdominal pain with distension. Abdominal X-ray shows a dilated colon with a diameter >6 cm and absence of colonic haustra ("lead pipe" appearance). Which medication should be AVOIDED in this patient to prevent perforation?
AAnticholinergic agents and opioid antiperistaltic medications
BIntravenous corticosteroids
CBiologic agents targeting TNF-α
DAminosalicylates (5-ASA) as maintenance therapy
EOral iron supplementation
Correct: A — Avoid anticholinergics and opioids (risk of perforation)
Toxic megacolon is a serious complication of IBD (more common in UC) characterized by colonic dilation >6 cm with loss of haustra and signs of systemic toxicity (fever, tachycardia, dehydration, electrolyte abnormalities). It is triggered by severe inflammation and can progress to perforation with peritonitis and sepsis. Anticholinergic agents and opioid antidiarrheals are contraindicated because they reduce colonic propulsive activity, increasing intraluminal pressure and perforation risk. Management includes IV fluids, electrolyte repletion, broad-spectrum antibiotics, high-dose IV corticosteroids, and often urgent colectomy.

B— IV steroids are indicated to reduce inflammation.
C— TNF-α inhibitors can be used in some cases.
D— 5-ASA is standard maintenance therapy.
E— Iron supplementation is safe and may be needed for anemia.
Q11 · Diverticular Disease · Diverticulitis Acute
A 72-year-old woman presents with left lower quadrant pain, fever (38.8°C), and mild leukocytosis (WBC 12.5 K/µL). Contrast-enhanced CT shows segmental colonic wall thickening in the sigmoid colon with focal infiltration of the pericolic fat and a small collection of fluid adjacent to the inflamed segment. Which pathophysiologic process distinguishes this complication from simple diverticulosis?
AProgressive stricture formation from collagen deposition
BObstruction and perforation of a diverticulum with inflammation and abscess
CBacterial overgrowth causing osmotic diarrhea
DChronic venous stasis and mesenteric ischemia
EAdenocarcinoma arising within a diverticular pouch
Correct: B — Obstruction/perforation of diverticulum with inflammation and abscess
Diverticulitis occurs when a pseudo-diverticulum (outpouching of mucosa and submucosa through weakened muscular points where blood vessels penetrate) becomes obstructed by fecal matter or inspissated debris. This leads to bacterial overgrowth within the diverticulum, mucosal ulceration, and perforation. Inflammation extends into the pericolic fat, causing fever and leukocytosis. Small contained perforations form paracolic or pericolic abscesses. Sigmoid colon is most commonly affected (especially in Western populations with low-fiber diet). Risk increases with age.

A— Strictures develop as a long-term sequela, not acutely.
C— Overgrowth alone causes diarrhea, not acute inflammation.
D— Mesenteric ischemia is not the primary mechanism.
E— Carcinoma may coexist but is not the cause of acute diverticulitis.
Q12 · Diverticular Disease · Diverticular Bleeding
A 68-year-old man with chronic diverticulosis presents with painless hematochezia (bright red blood per rectum) lasting 2 hours. He is hemodynamically stable. Colonoscopy shows a solitary diverticulum in the right colon with an exposed blood vessel at its base. Which anatomic feature of colonic pseudo-diverticula explains the bleeding risk?
AEctopic gastric mucosa within the diverticulum producing acid ulceration
BHerniation of mucosa/submucosa adjacent to penetrating blood vessels
CInflammation extending into the muscularis propria
DVolvulus of the diverticular segment with ischemia
EMalignant transformation of diverticular epithelium
Correct: B — Herniation adjacent to penetrating blood vessels
Colonic pseudo-diverticula are formed by herniation of mucosa and submucosa through weakened areas of the colonic wall at sites where blood vessels penetrate the muscularis. The blood vessel runs through the wall of the diverticulum. Erosion of the diverticulum or mucosal ulceration can expose and erode this vessel, causing painless bleeding (often brisk and requiring transfusion). Right-sided diverticula bleed more frequently than left-sided. Most diverticular bleeding stops spontaneously, but severe hemorrhage may require angiographic intervention or surgical resection.

A— Ectopic gastric mucosa is found in Meckel diverticula, not colonic pseudo-diverticula.
C— Inflammation causes diverticulitis, not typical painless bleeding.
D— Volvulus is not a feature of colonic diverticulosis.
E— Malignancy does not cause diverticular bleeding.
Q13 · Colonic Polyps · Adenoma Cancer Risk
A 58-year-old woman undergoes screening colonoscopy and is found to have a 15 mm sessile polyp in the descending colon. Pathology shows a tubulovillous adenoma with mild dysplasia. The pathologist notes that a purely villous adenoma with the same size would have carried substantially higher cancer risk. What is the primary reason villous adenomas have increased malignant potential compared to tubular adenomas?
AVillous adenomas secrete excess mucus leading to osmotic diarrhea and epithelial turnover
BVillous architecture creates a larger surface area for bacterial colonization
CVillous structure exposes more dysplastic epithelium directly to luminal carcinogens
DVillous adenomas arise from hyperplastic polyps which have inherent genetic instability
ETubular adenomas produce protective mucin coating against carcinogens
Correct: C — Villous architecture exposes dysplastic epithelium to carcinogens
Adenomatous polyps are classified histologically as: (1) Tubular (lowest cancer risk ~5%); (2) Tubulovillous (intermediate ~10–20%); (3) Villous (highest cancer risk ~40%). Villous adenomas have finger-like surface projections that expose a larger area of dysplastic epithelium directly to the colonic lumen and carcinogenic fecal contents. The increased surface area and prolonged contact with carcinogens increase the risk of malignant transformation. All adenomas are presumed to arise via the adenoma-carcinoma sequence (APC mutation → K-RAS → p53 loss).

A— Mucus secretion is not the primary factor in cancer risk.
B— Bacterial colonization does not explain cancer risk differences.
D— Hyperplastic polyps are distinct from adenomas and have very low cancer risk.
E— There is no evidence of protective mucin in tubular adenomas.
Q14 · Colonic Polyps · Peutz-Jeghers Syndrome
A 16-year-old boy is referred for evaluation of mucocutaneous pigmentation (dark brown macules on lips and buccal mucosa) and recurrent abdominal pain with episodes of intussusception. Capsule endoscopy reveals numerous small bowel polyps. Genetic testing confirms a mutation in the STK11 (LKB1) gene. Which pathologic feature distinguishes these polyps from adenomatous polyps?
ADysplastic epithelium with mucus-producing signet ring cells
BBenign hamartomatous polyps with arborizing smooth muscle and normal epithelium
CInflammatory pseudopolyps from regenerating mucosa post-ulceration
DHyperplastic polyps with minimal dysplasia and normal adenoma-carcinoma sequence
ESessile serrated polyps with irregular crypt patterns
Correct: B — Benign hamartomas with arborizing smooth muscle and normal epithelium
Peutz-Jeghers syndrome (PJS) is an autosomal dominant condition caused by STK11 mutations. The syndrome features: (1) mucocutaneous pigmentation (dark macules on lips, buccal mucosa, genitals); (2) benign hamartomatous polyps throughout the GI tract (especially small bowel), characterized by arborizing (tree-like) smooth muscle bundles and normal mucosa (not dysplastic); (3) intussusception (polyps act as lead points). Despite the benign histology of individual polyps, PJS carries increased cancer risk (gastric, small bowel, colon, pancreatic, breast, lung) due to the underlying STK11 mutation.

A— Dysplasia is not a feature of hamartomas.
C— Inflammatory pseudopolyps are post-ulcerative, not congenital.
D— Hyperplastic polyps are a separate entity with very low malignant potential.
E— Sessile serrated polyps have MLH1 methylation, not STK11 mutations.
Q15 · Colonic Polyps · Sessile Serrated Polyp (SSP)
A 62-year-old woman is found on screening colonoscopy to have a 8 mm sessile polyp in the cecum with irregular serrated crypt pattern on histology. The pathologist notes abnormal methylation of the MLH1 promoter region. Which mechanism explains the increased colorectal cancer risk associated with this polyp?
AInactivation of the APC tumor suppressor gene via chromosomal deletion
BMLH1 promoter methylation → mismatch repair deficiency → microsatellite instability
CActivation of K-RAS oncoprotein through point mutation
DLoss of p53 function preventing apoptosis of dysplastic cells
EMicrosatellite instability from germline Lynch syndrome mutations
Correct: B — MLH1 methylation causes mismatch repair deficiency and MSI
Sessile serrated polyps (SSPs) are flat or slightly raised polyps with irregular serrated crypt architecture. They are characterized by MLH1 promoter methylation (epigenetic silencing), which inactivates the MLH1 mismatch repair protein. This leads to microsatellite instability (MSI-H) — defective repair of repetitive DNA sequences — accelerating progression to cancer. SSPs are part of the serrated pathway to colorectal cancer and account for ~20–30% of colorectal cancers. They often occur in the right colon and have been missed on older screening techniques.

A— APC inactivation is typical of traditional adenomas, not SSPs.
C— K-RAS activation occurs but is not the primary mechanism of SSP cancer risk.
D— p53 loss occurs late in progression but is not the distinguishing feature.
E— Germline Lynch mutations cause hereditary MSI, not sporadic SSP MLH1 methylation.
Q16 · Colorectal Carcinoma · Right-sided CRC
A 67-year-old man presents with fatigue and dyspnea. Labs show hemoglobin 7.2 g/dL with microcytic indices and iron studies consistent with iron-deficiency anemia. Fecal occult blood test is positive. Colonoscopy reveals an exophytic, fungating mass in the cecum. Which pathologic and clinical feature is characteristic of right-sided colorectal carcinoma?
ANapkin-ring narrowing of the colonic lumen with apple-core appearance
BExophytic/fungating tumor with iron-deficiency anemia from chronic bleeding
CAcute presentation with change in bowel habits and pencil-thin stools
DObstruction with perforation leading to fecal peritonitis
ERectal bleeding with visible blood on toilet paper
Correct: B — Exophytic tumor with chronic iron-deficiency anemia
Right-sided colorectal carcinoma (cecum, ascending colon) typically presents with: (1) exophytic, fungating growth (projecting into the lumen); (2) chronic occult bleeding → iron-deficiency anemia (presenting symptom); (3) **fatigue, dyspnea, palpitations** from anemia. The right colon has a larger lumen, so masses may grow large before causing obstruction. In contrast, left-sided CRC (rectosigmoid) often presents with change in bowel habits, obstruction, and napkin-ring appearance. Right-sided tumors have slightly worse prognosis (often stage III–IV at presentation).

A— Napkin-ring appearance is characteristic of left-sided CRC.
C— Change in bowel habits is more typical of left-sided disease.
D— Obstruction/perforation are complications of left-sided disease.
E— Visible bleeding suggests distal (rectal) involvement.
Q17 · Colorectal Carcinoma · Left-sided CRC
A 71-year-old man reports a 3-month history of intermittent constipation alternating with diarrhea, along with visible blood in his stool. Abdominal exam reveals a palpable mass in the left lower quadrant. CT shows a narrowed segment of the sigmoid colon with focal wall thickening and shouldering at the margins. Which pathologic feature explains the patient's change in bowel habits?
AExophytic mass reducing colonic diameter gradually
BNapkin-ring stenosis causing mechanical obstruction and altered motility
CVillous adenoma secreting excess mucus and electrolytes
DFistulization to adjacent bowel with bypass of the tumor
EMucinous differentiation causing localized peritoneal inflammation
Correct: B — Napkin-ring stenosis causes mechanical obstruction
Left-sided colorectal carcinoma (descending and sigmoid colon) commonly presents with: (1) circumferential "napkin-ring" or "apple-core" appearance — a constricting annular lesion that narrows the lumen; (2) change in bowel habits (constipation, straining, pencil-thin stools from narrowing); (3) **visible bright red bleeding** (distal location); (4) **obstruction** if advanced. The stricturing nature causes progressive narrowing and partial obstruction, leading to alternating constipation and diarrhea as stool bypasses the stenotic segment.

A— Exophytic growth is more typical of right-sided CRC.
C— Adenomas do not present as carcinomas in this setting.
D— Fistulization is not the primary mechanism of obstruction.
E— Mucinous differentiation is not the cause of obstruction.
Q18 · Colorectal Carcinoma · Familial Adenomatous Polyposis
A 28-year-old man is discovered on screening colonoscopy to have hundreds of adenomatous polyps throughout the colon (estimated >500), with multiple polyps showing high-grade dysplasia. He reports that his father died of colorectal cancer at age 42. Genetic testing confirms an APC gene mutation. Which extraintestinal manifestation is this patient at increased risk for?
AHereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer (Lynch syndrome)
BDesmoid tumors, osteomas, and congenital hypertrophy of retinal pigment epithelium
CEndometrial cancer and ovarian cysts
DGastric intestinal metaplasia and gastric cancer
EPrimary sclerosing cholangitis and bile duct cancer
Correct: B — Desmoid tumors, osteomas, and congenital hypertrophy of RPE
Familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP) is an autosomal dominant syndrome caused by APC (adenomatous polyposis coli) gene mutations. Features: (1) **hundreds to thousands of colonic adenomas** by age 10–20; (2) **virtual 100% risk of CRC** by age 50 if untreated (requires prophylactic total colectomy); (3) extraintestinal manifestations: desmoid tumors (invasive fibromatosis), osteomas (benign bone lesions), congenital hypertrophy of retinal pigment epithelium (CHRPE), and increased risk of gastric, duodenal, and pancreatic cancers.

A— Lynch syndrome is due to mismatch repair genes, not APC.
C— Endometrial cancer and ovarian cysts are associated with Lynch, not FAP.
D— Gastric intestinal metaplasia is seen in some FAP families but is not a major feature.
E— PSC is associated with ulcerative colitis and Lynch syndrome, not FAP.
Q19 · Colorectal Carcinoma · Lynch Syndrome Genetics
A 35-year-old woman is found to have colorectal cancer on surveillance colonoscopy. Her family history is notable for: mother with endometrial cancer (age 48), brother with CRC (age 42), and paternal grandmother with ovarian cancer (age 56). Genetic testing reveals a pathogenic variant in the MLH1 gene. Which mechanism explains the increased cancer susceptibility in this family?
ALoss of APC tumor suppressor allowing uncontrolled adenoma formation
BMismatch repair deficiency → microsatellite instability → accelerated mutation accumulation
CEpigenetic MLH1 promoter methylation (somatic, not germline)
DDefective DNA mismatch repair at tumor suppressor genes only, not oncogenes
EEnvironmental exposure to carcinogens without genetic predisposition
Correct: B — Mismatch repair deficiency causes microsatellite instability
Lynch syndrome (hereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer, HNPCC) is caused by germline mutations in mismatch repair (MMR) genes: MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2. These genes normally repair DNA replication errors. Deficiency leads to microsatellite instability (MSI-H) — accumulation of mutations in repetitive DNA sequences — dramatically accelerating cancer development. Cancers are often multiple and early-onset. Amsterdam Criteria identify Lynch syndrome families: ≥3 relatives with cancer, ≥2 generations, ≥1 before age 50, and FAP excluded. Associated cancers: CRC (most common), endometrial (second most common), ovarian, gastric, ureter/renal pelvis.

A— APC mutations cause FAP, not Lynch.
C— MLH1 methylation is somatic (not germline) and occurs in sporadic cancers.
D— MMR defects affect all genes, not selectively.
E— Lynch is hereditary, not purely environmental.
Q20 · Colorectal Carcinoma · Anal Canal Carcinoma
A 52-year-old man with a history of receptive anal intercourse and untreated HIV infection (CD4+ 95 cells/µL) presents with anal pain, tenesmus, and visible bleeding. Biopsy of an anal lesion shows squamous cell carcinoma with high-risk HPV-16 detected by PCR. Which factor most significantly increases cancer risk in this patient?
AChronic smoking and alcohol use inducing p53 mutations
BSevere immunosuppression (low CD4+) impairing HPV clearance and increasing viral persistence
CUnderlying familial polyposis predisposition
DAdenomatous polyps in the rectum progressing to SCC
EPrior diverticulitis causing chronic inflammation
Correct: B — Severe immunosuppression impairs HPV clearance and allows persistence
Anal canal carcinoma is predominantly squamous cell carcinoma (unlike rectal/colonic CRC which is adenocarcinoma). It is strongly associated with human papillomavirus (HPV) infection (especially high-risk types 16 and 18). Risk factors include: (1) receptive anal intercourse; (2) HIV/AIDS (especially CD4+ <100); (3) history of anal intraepithelial neoplasia (AIN); (4) smoking. The severely immunosuppressed state (CD4+ <100) prevents normal immune clearance of HPV, allowing persistent infection and malignant transformation. Antiretroviral therapy and immune reconstitution significantly reduce anal cancer risk.

A— Smoking accelerates cancer risk but is not the primary mechanism without HPV.
C— Familial polyposis does not increase anal cancer risk.
D— Adenomatous polyps arise in the colon, not anal canal.
E— Diverticulitis is not associated with anal SCC.
