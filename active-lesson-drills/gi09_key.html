Practice Questions — LO 1–14 Integration

Select the best answer, then reveal the explanation.

Q1. A 28-year-old woman with obesity undergoes Roux-en-Y gastric bypass. Six months later she reports markedly reduced hunger. Which of the following best explains the hormonal changes responsible?



C — Decreased ghrelin + increased PYY/GLP-1. The bypassed fundus produces less ghrelin (less hunger drive). Food passes rapidly to the distal gut → robust L-cell stimulation → ↑ PYY + GLP-1 → satiety. Six months post-op she has lost weight → less fat → less leptin (opposite of D). Choice A reverses the ghrelin direction.
Q2. A researcher collects saliva at rest (low flow) and after maximal gustatory stimulation (high flow). Which correctly describes how electrolytes change with increasing flow rate?



B — Na+ increases; K+ decreases slightly; always hypotonic. At low flow, ducts have more time to reabsorb Na+ and secrete K+ → very low Na+, very high K+. At high flow, less ductal modification → Na+ rises toward plasma levels, K+ falls slightly. Saliva is always hypotonic regardless of flow rate — it never reaches isotonicity.
Q3. A 65-year-old woman prescribed oxybutynin (anticholinergic) for overactive bladder develops dry mouth. What is the mechanism?



B — M3 muscarinic blockade → xerostomia. Parasympathetic ACh acting on M3 receptors (IP3/Ca2+) is the dominant driver of salivary secretion. Anticholinergics block M3 → eliminate sustained watery saliva → xerostomia. Note: vagus (CN X) does NOT control salivary glands — that's CN VII (submandibular/sublingual) and CN IX (parotid).
Q4. A 52-year-old woman has macrocytic anemia, subacute combined degeneration, and markedly elevated serum gastrin. Anti-parietal cell antibodies are positive. Why is her gastrin elevated?



B — Loss of HCl removes the normal acid feedback on G cells. Anti-parietal cell antibodies destroy parietal cells → no HCl (achlorhydria) + no intrinsic factor. Normally pH <3 triggers D cells → somatostatin → inhibits G cells. Without HCl, pH stays high → no somatostatin → G cells tonically active → hypergastrinemia. This is pernicious anemia. Choice A (gastrinoma) would not show anti-parietal cell antibodies.
Q5. A new acid-suppressing drug is a prodrug activated by gastric acid that then irreversibly forms a disulfide bond with its target. Which target and mechanism does this describe?



D — H+/K+-ATPase (PPI mechanism). PPIs are prodrugs activated by acid in the secretory canaliculi of parietal cells → sulfenamide form → irreversibly binds cysteine residues of H+/K+-ATPase. The proton pump is the rate-limiting step in HCl secretion — no H+ reaches the lumen without it. PPIs are the most effective acid suppressants because they target the final, obligatory step. H2 blockers block histamine signaling upstream of the pump.
Q6. Sham feeding (seeing and smelling food without eating) significantly increases gastric acid. Which phase is this, and what happens after truncal vagotomy?



C — Cephalic phase; completely eliminated by truncal vagotomy. The cephalic phase is 100% vagal (CN X): cortex → dorsal vagal nucleus → vagus → stomach → ACh + GRP → parietal cells + G cells → ↑ gastrin. Contributes 30–40% of meal acid. Truncal vagotomy severs CN X → no cephalic-phase acid response. The gastric phase has a vagal component but local ENS and gastrin can maintain it; the intestinal phase is primarily hormonal.
Q7. A patient develops acute pancreatitis secondary to a gallstone obstructing the pancreatic duct. Which of the following best explains why hypocalcemia develops?



B — Phospholipase A2 activation → fat necrosis → saponification → Ca2+ sequestration. In pancreatitis, zymogens activate prematurely inside the pancreas. Phospholipase A2 digests fat cells → fatty acids are released → Ca2+ binds fatty acids forming calcium soaps (saponification) → Ca2+ sequestered in the peritoneum → hypocalcemia → Chvostek's + Trousseau's signs. Elastase separately digests vessel walls → hemorrhagic pancreatitis → Cullen's sign (periumbilical) or Grey Turner's sign (flank).
Q8. A patient has a pancreatic tumor causing watery diarrhea (8 L/day), hypokalemia, and achlorhydria. What is the diagnosis and what is the specific molecular mechanism of diarrhea?



C — VIPoma (WDHA syndrome). VIP binds VPAC receptors → ↑ adenylate cyclase → ↑↑ cAMP → PKA → CFTR channels open → massive Cl- secretion → Na+ + water follow → voluminous secretory diarrhea. VIP also: inhibits parietal cells → achlorhydria; stimulates colonic K+ secretion → hypokalemia. WDHA = Watery Diarrhea, Hypokalemia, Achlorhydria. Treatment: octreotide (somatostatin analog) + surgical resection. This is also called "pancreatic cholera" because the mechanism mirrors cholera toxin's effect on CFTR.
