LOs 1–3
Q1: Traveler's diarrhea pathogen
A 28-year-old medical student returns from a 2-week trip to Mexico with 3 days of profuse watery diarrhea, abdominal cramps, and no blood. Stool studies show a non-inflammatory profile. Which pathogen is most likely?

A) EHEC (O157:H7)
B) Shigella
C) ETEC
D) Campylobacter jejuni
E) Norovirus
Correct Answer: C

ETEC is the #1 cause of traveler's diarrhea — watery, secretory, non-inflammatory (LT toxin ↑ cAMP; ST toxin ↑ cGMP). EHEC and Shigella cause dysentery (bloody). Campylobacter is possible but less common in Mexico-origin traveler's diarrhea. Norovirus is plausible but less likely in this classic traveler presentation.
Q2: HUS after undercooked beef
A 5-year-old develops bloody diarrhea 4 days after a barbecue. Five days later he is pale, lethargic, with petechiae, oliguria, and Cr 2.9. Smear shows schistocytes. Which organism and toxin?

A) Shigella + Shiga toxin
B) EHEC + Shiga-like toxin (Stx)
C) ETEC + heat-labile toxin
D) Salmonella + LPS
E) Campylobacter + cytolethal distending toxin
Correct Answer: B

EHEC O157:H7 produces Shiga-like toxin → HUS triad (microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, AKI). Undercooked ground beef / unpasteurized dairy is classic. Avoid antibiotics and anti-motility agents (worsen HUS). Shigella also produces Shiga toxin but HUS is rare compared with EHEC.
Q3: Preformed toxin — fast onset
Six people eat at a picnic; 3 hours later all develop vomiting and cramps. They recover within 24 hours. Which pathogen?

A) Salmonella
B) Campylobacter
C) S. aureus (preformed enterotoxin)
D) EHEC
E) C. difficile
Correct Answer: C

S. aureus produces a heat-stable preformed enterotoxin. Onset is 2–6 hours (toxin is already in the food — no time needed for replication). Vomiting predominates; resolution within 24–48 hours. B. cereus emetic form (fried rice) is the other classic fast-onset option.
Q4: Invasive vs. toxigenic classification
A 34-year-old man has bloody diarrhea, fever, and leukocytosis after eating undercooked chicken. Stool WBCs positive; stool culture grows curved gram-negative rods with corkscrew motility at 42°C. Which organism + category?

A) ETEC — toxigenic
B) Campylobacter jejuni — invasive
C) Vibrio cholerae — toxigenic
D) Listeria — invasive
E) Norovirus — viral
Correct Answer: B

Campylobacter jejuni = curved/comma-shaped gram-negative rods, motile, grows at 42°C. Classic exposure = undercooked poultry/unpasteurized milk. Invasive → bloody diarrhea + fever + stool WBCs. Post-infectious: Guillain-Barré (molecular mimicry with gangliosides) and reactive arthritis.
Q5: C. difficile first-line therapy
A 67-year-old inpatient on IV ceftriaxone develops 10 watery stools/day. Stool PCR positive for C. difficile; toxin EIA positive. He is afebrile with normal vitals and WBC 14k. What is the first-line treatment?

A) IV metronidazole alone
B) Oral vancomycin OR oral fidaxomicin
C) Oral ciprofloxacin
D) Loperamide for symptom control
E) IV cefepime
Correct Answer: B

Current first-line for non-severe/initial C. diff is oral vancomycin OR oral fidaxomicin (metronidazole has fallen out of first-line due to inferior cure rates). Fidaxomicin has lower recurrence. Stop the inciting antibiotic if possible. Loperamide is contraindicated (toxin trapping).
Q6: Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis
A 58-year-old man with alcoholic cirrhosis and ascites presents with fever and abdominal pain. Paracentesis: PMN 320/μL, single organism on gram stain (gram-negative rods). What is the diagnosis and most likely organism?

A) Secondary peritonitis — polymicrobial
B) SBP — E. coli
C) CAPD peritonitis — S. epidermidis
D) Tuberculous peritonitis — M. tuberculosis
E) Pancreatitis — sterile
Correct Answer: B

SBP is defined by ascitic PMN ≥250/μL in the setting of cirrhosis without a surgical source. Most common organism = E. coli, then Klebsiella, then S. pneumoniae. Treat with ceftriaxone × 5–7 d; long-term ciprofloxacin or TMP-SMX prophylaxis after first episode or in high-risk patients.
Q7: Amebic liver abscess
A 33-year-old man returns from Mexico with 2 weeks of bloody diarrhea, RUQ pain, and fever. Ultrasound shows a single right-lobe liver lesion. Serology for E. histolytica is positive. Best initial treatment?

A) Percutaneous drainage alone
B) Metronidazole, followed by a luminal agent (paromomycin)
C) Ceftriaxone + metronidazole
D) Surgical resection of the abscess
E) Oral vancomycin
Correct Answer: B

Amebic liver abscess: metronidazole (tissue amebicide) FIRST, then a luminal agent (paromomycin or iodoquinol) to clear intraluminal cysts and prevent relapse. Drainage usually not required. Classic "anchovy paste" abscess contents, right lobe, history of travel to endemic area.
Q8: H. pylori eradication
A 45-year-old with a duodenal ulcer tests positive for H. pylori on urea breath testing. Which of the following is a standard first-line triple-therapy regimen?

A) Amoxicillin + metronidazole + bismuth (no PPI)
B) PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin
C) PPI alone for 8 weeks
D) PPI + tetracycline (no other antibiotic)
E) H2 blocker + clarithromycin monotherapy
Correct Answer: B

Classic triple therapy: PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin for 14 days. Quadruple therapy (PPI + bismuth + metronidazole + tetracycline) is preferred in areas of high clarithromycin resistance or penicillin allergy. Confirm eradication ≥4 weeks after finishing antibiotics with urea breath test or stool antigen.
