Practice Qs — LOs 1–4
USMLE Step 1 & In-House Style — attempt all before revealing answers
Q1 (LO1) A researcher finds that dietary lipids cannot cross a stagnant aqueous zone adjacent to enterocytes without a carrier. Why can’t lipids cross this zone unaided?
A. Too large for aquaporin channels  B. Hydrophobic → aggregate in aqueous lumen → cannot diffuse through the aqueous UWL  C. Require ATP-dependent transporters  D. Negatively charged, repelled by glycocalyx  E. Acidic mucus repels them
B — Hydrophobic aggregation prevents UWL crossing. The UWL is aqueous. Lipids aggregate into large hydrophobic droplets and cannot diffuse through this aqueous zone. They need an amphipathic carrier — the micelle. Key trap: once lipids reach the enterocyte membrane they diffuse right through it (lipophilic → loves lipid bilayer) — the problem is crossing the aqueous UWL first.
Q2 (LO1/2) A student asks: "If bile can’t chemically digest fat, why is it essential for fat absorption?" Which best describes bile’s role?
A. Bile hydrolyzes triglycerides into fatty acids  B. Bile activates pancreatic lipase by phosphorylation  C. Bile emulsifies fat globules and forms micelles to carry lipids through the UWL  D. Bile provides the alkaline pH for lipase  E. Bile transports fatty acids via receptor-mediated endocytosis
C — Bile emulsifies + forms micelles. Bile is a detergent (emulsifier) — NOT a digestive enzyme. It (1) breaks large fat globules into smaller ones (emulsification → ↑ surface area for lipase) and (2) packages lipid fragments into micelles to carry them through the aqueous UWL. Triglyceride hydrolysis is done by pancreatic lipase. The alkaline pH comes from HCO₃⁻ in bile + pancreatic secretions.
Q3 (LO2) A 52-year-old man is started on atorvastatin for hypercholesterolemia. The drug blocks the rate-limiting enzyme in cholesterol synthesis. Name the enzyme and explain its relevance to primary bile acid production.
A. Fatty acid synthase; reduces phospholipid component of bile  B. HMG-CoA reductase; directly reduces cholesterol backbone of primary bile acids  C. Cholesterol 7α-hydroxylase; reduces bilirubin secretion  D. HMG-CoA synthase; reduces triglyceride component of bile  E. Acetyl-CoA carboxylase; reduces glycine conjugate
B — HMG-CoA reductase; cholesterol backbone of primary bile acids. Primary bile acids (glycocholic acid, taurocholic acid) are made from a cholesterol backbone. HMG-CoA reductase is rate-limiting for cholesterol synthesis → statins inhibit this enzyme → ↓ intracellular cholesterol → hepatocytes upregulate LDL receptors → ↓ serum LDL. Also reduces primary bile synthesis. Side effect: myopathy (↑ CK); risk ↑ with fibrates.
Q4 (LO2) A 45-year-old woman has progressive jaundice, pruritus, xanthelasma, and elevated alkaline phosphatase. Biopsy: granulomatous destruction of intrahepatic bile ducts. Labs: positive anti-mitochondrial antibody (AMA). Diagnosis + effect on fat absorption?
A. PSC; normal fat absorption  B. PBC; steatorrhea and fat-soluble vitamin deficiency  C. Autoimmune hepatitis; protein malabsorption only  D. Biliary atresia; corrected by UDCA  E. Wilson disease; copper accumulation causing fat malabsorption
B — PBC; steatorrhea + fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (ADEK). PBC = autoimmune destruction of intrahepatic bile ducts in middle-aged women; AMA is the hallmark antibody. Destroyed bile ducts → cholestasis → ↓ bile delivery → impaired micelle formation → steatorrhea + vitamin ADEK deficiency. PSC (young men + UC + p-ANCA + "beads on string" ERCP) is a different entity. Treatment: ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA).
Q5 (LO3) A 28-year-old woman with gallstones develops RUQ pain after eating a high-fat meal. Which hormone triggered her gallbladder contraction, and what are its three biliary effects?
A. Secretin; ↑ HCO₃⁻, relaxes sphincter of Oddi, contracts gallbladder  B. Gastrin; ↑ acid, stimulates bile, relaxes pylorus  C. CCK; ↑ bile production, contracts gallbladder, relaxes sphincter of Oddi  D. GIP; inhibits acid, relaxes gallbladder, ↓ bile  E. Motilin; ↑ motility, contracts gallbladder, ↓ bile reabsorption
C — CCK; three biliary effects: ↑ bile production, gallbladder contraction, sphincter of Oddi relaxation. Fat + protein in duodenum → I-cells release CCK → (1) ↑ bile production at liver, (2) rhythmic gallbladder contraction (releases stored bile), (3) relaxes sphincter of Oddi (bile enters duodenum). CCK also stimulates pancreatic enzyme secretion (lipase, proteases, amylase). Gallbladder contracting against a cystic duct stone = biliary colic.
Q6 (LO3) A patient with Crohn’s disease undergoes resection of 120 cm of terminal ileum. Postoperatively she has watery diarrhea and steatorrhea. What is the primary mechanism of diarrhea?
A. Bacterial overgrowth → protein fermentation  B. Bile acid malabsorption → bile acids enter colon → osmotic/secretory diarrhea  C. Loss of ileocecal valve → rapid transit  D. ↓ Gastric acid from loss of ileal enterocytes  E. Loss of disaccharidase enzymes → carbohydrate malabsorption
B — Bile acid malabsorption → bile acids enter colon → watery diarrhea. Terminal ileum = primary site for active bile acid reabsorption (ASBT transporter). After resection, unabsorbed bile acids enter the colon → osmotic + secretory diarrhea. Also: depleted bile pool → steatorrhea; B₁₂ deficiency (IF-B₁₂ absorbed here); fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (ADEK). Treat bile acid diarrhea with cholestyramine.
Q7 (LO4) A researcher finds that pancreatic lipase cannot digest triglycerides within micelles because the hydrophilic ends of bile form a blocking shell. What mechanism overcomes this?
A. Secretin cleaves pro-colipase → colipase displaces bile  B. Trypsin cleaves pro-colipase → colipase binds hydrophilic ends of bile → opens access for pancreatic lipase  C. Enterokinase directly activates colipase → digests bile conjugates  D. CCK activates pancreatic lipase by phosphorylation  E. Bile spontaneously deconjugates at the micelle surface
B — Trypsin → colipase → opens door for pancreatic lipase. Pro-colipase (from pancreas) is activated by trypsin. Active colipase binds the hydrophilic ends of bile molecules on the micelle surface, displacing them and creating a "door" for pancreatic lipase. Lipase hydrolyzes triglycerides → 2 fatty acids + 1 monoglyceride. Without colipase, fat digestion in the SI fails even if bile and lipase are both present.
Q8 (LO4) A 48-year-old alcoholic has greasy floating stools, easy bruising, and elevated PT/INR. Which fat-soluble vitamin deficiency best explains the coagulopathy?
A. Vitamin A  B. Vitamin D  C. Vitamin E  D. Vitamin K  E. Vitamin C
D — Vitamin K deficiency. Vitamin K is required for γ-carboxylation of coagulation factors II, VII, IX, X + protein C and S. Deficiency → ↑ PT/INR → easy bruising and bleeding. Alcoholism → liver disease + fat malabsorption (pancreatitis, impaired bile) → steatorrhea → malabsorption of all fat-soluble vitamins. Vitamin A = night blindness; Vitamin D = osteomalacia/rickets; Vitamin E = ataxia/hemolytic anemia. Vitamin C is water-soluble (causes scurvy, not elevated PT).
