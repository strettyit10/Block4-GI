▼ Defining Diarrhea & Big-Picture Pathogen Classes

⭐ Infectious diarrhea is defined by frequency (>3 stools/day), volume (>200 g/day in adults), and form (“takes the shape of its container”). Acute = <2 weeks duration.

📏 Clinical Definition

  • Frequency: >3 stools/day
  • Volume: >200 g/day in adults on a Western diet
  • Form: Stool takes the shape of its container (watery/unformed)
  • Acute diarrhea: illness lasting <2 weeks

📊 Frequency of Bacterial Isolates in Stool Cultures

  • Campylobacter: ~42% (most common)
  • Salmonella: ~32%
  • Shigella: ~19%
  • E. coli O157:H7: ~0.14% without visible blood; rises to ~7.8% when blood is visible
⚡ High-Yield:
  • Viruses are the most common cause of gastroenteritis overall, but bacteria dominate when diarrhea is severe or bloody.
  • “Most common bacterial cause of diarrhea in the US” = Campylobacter.
  • If the stem has visible blood in the stool, think bacteria (especially EHEC, Shigella, Campylobacter, Salmonella) or Entamoeba.
⚠️ TRAP: “Most common” depends on what you’re counting ▼
  • Most common gastroenteritis overall = viral (Norovirus in adults, Rotavirus in unvaccinated kids).
  • Most common bacterial cause = Campylobacter.
  • Most common cause of traveler’s diarrhea = ETEC.
  • Don’t conflate these — exam writers love this distinction.

▼ Viral Pathogens

⭐ Viruses are the most common cause of gastroenteritis. Classic Step-1 picks: Rotavirus (unvaccinated kids), Norovirus (outbreaks on cruise ships, dorms, nursing homes).

💈‍♂️ Rotavirus

  • Structure: Segmented dsRNA virus (Reoviridae).
  • Who gets it: The single most important viral cause of severe dehydrating diarrhea in children worldwide. Adults usually only mildly affected (parents, daycare workers, healthcare personnel).
  • Transmission: Fecal-oral route.
  • Incubation: <48 hours.
  • Clinical: Vomiting, non-bloody watery diarrhea, low-grade fever. 30–50% also have respiratory symptoms. Ranges from asymptomatic shedding to severe dehydration, seizures, even death.
  • Diagnosis: EIA on stool (commonly used); PCR available.
  • Prevention: Live oral rotavirus vaccine is routine in the US.

🚢 Norovirus

  • Structure: Non-enveloped ssRNA virus (Caliciviridae).
  • Epidemiology: Most common cause of viral gastroenteritis in adults in the US. Infamous on cruise ships and college dorms — outbreaks spread rapidly.
  • Contagiousness: An infected person sheds billions of viral particles; as few as 18 particles can infect another person.
  • Incubation: 12–48 hours.
  • Clinical: Abrupt-onset vomiting (“winter vomiting syndrome”), watery non-bloody diarrhea with abdominal cramps, nausea. Symptoms last 24–72 hours.
  • Complication: Dehydration — serious in young children, elderly, and immunocompromised.
  • Diagnosis: RT-qPCR on stool.
  • Management: Hydration + supportive care.

🕵️ Other Viral Causes (know they exist)

  • Adenovirus (types 40, 41) — pediatric diarrhea.
  • Astrovirus, Calicivirus, Enterovirus, Coronavirus — generally mild self-limited disease.
⚠️ TRAP: Rotavirus vs. Norovirus ▼
  • Rotavirus = unvaccinated infant/toddler, severe dehydration, dsRNA segmented (Reovirus family).
  • Norovirus = adults on a cruise ship/dorm outbreak, vomiting predominant, ssRNA non-enveloped (Calicivirus family).
  • Both give non-bloody watery diarrhea — if the stem has blood, it’s not a virus.

🍗 Campylobacter jejuni — Deep Dive

  • Morphology: Gram-negative curved/S-shaped rod. Cultured at 42°C on special selective media.
  • Sources: Chicken (huge one), unpasteurized milk, contaminated water, puppies.
  • Clinical spectrum: Loose stools → massive watery OR grossly bloody diarrhea. Fever, malaise, crampy abdominal pain.
  • Typical course: Self-limited; empiric antibiotics reserved for severe cases.
  • Prolonged disease in patients with hypogammaglobulinemia.

Major complications (very high-yield):

  • Guillain-Barré syndrome — ascending flaccid paralysis; incidence ~1:1000 infections. Especially C. jejuni serotype O:19 and C. upsaliensis.
  • Reactive arthritis (Reiter’s syndrome) — rare.
  • Septicemia with C. fetus — debilitated/immunocompromised hosts (alcoholics, DM, malignancy).

🥚 Salmonella — Non-Typhoidal vs. Typhoidal

▼Non-Typhoidal (S. typhimurium, enteritidis, choleraesuis)
  • Sources: Raw/undercooked poultry, eggs (transovarial contamination of intact shells!), reptile pets (turtles, iguanas).
  • Onset: 6–48 hours after ingestion.
  • Clinical: Fever, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea (may be bloody), abdominal cramps.
  • Septicemia: Especially with S. choleraesuis; highest risk in pediatric, geriatric, and AIDS patients. Osteomyelitis in sickle cell disease is a classic association.
  • Treatment: Do not give antibiotics routinely — they prolong the carrier state. Reserve for severely ill, >50 y/o, immunocompromised, or those with sepsis.
▼Typhoid Fever (S. typhi / paratyphi)
  • Reservoir: Humans only (person-to-person). No animal reservoir.
  • Inoculum: Very small — easily transmitted. Rapidly multiplies if food left at room temperature.
  • Pathogenesis: Bacteria translocate through intestinal wall → engulfed by macrophages → hematogenous spread to liver, spleen, bone marrow. Gallbladder becomes a reservoir and reseeds the intestine.
  • Clinical: High fevers, chills, headache, malaise, anorexia; diarrhea OR constipation; rose spots on the abdomen; relative bradycardia; hepatosplenomegaly; neuropsychiatric signs (delirium, “picking at bedsheets”).
  • Complications: Intestinal perforation at 3–4 weeks.
  • Diagnosis: Blood and bone marrow cultures (bone marrow has the highest yield).
  • Treatment: Fluoroquinolones or broad-spectrum cephalosporins (ceftriaxone).
  • Prevention: S. typhi vaccine available (~65% efficacy).
  • High-risk carriers: Patients with biliary disease; women (“Typhoid Mary”).

▼

Typhoid = FRBD

  • Fever (high, stepwise)
  • Rose spots on the abdomen
  • Bradycardia (relative, despite fever)
  • Deranged sensorium (delirium) & hepatosplenomegaly

🦿 Shigella

  • Species: S. sonnei (most common in developed countries), S. flexneri (developing countries), S. dysenteriae (most severe disease, produces the classic Shiga toxin), S. boydii (rare).
  • Reservoir: Humans only. Spread person-to-person by fecal-oral route.
  • Inoculum: Extremely low (as few as 10 organisms) — explains high secondary attack rate in daycares.
  • At-risk groups: Young children in daycare/nurseries, their siblings and parents, institutionalized patients, men who have sex with men.
  • Clinical: Begins as watery diarrhea → within 1–2 days progresses to abdominal cramps, tenesmus, and bloody mucoid stools (classic dysentery).
  • Treatment: Unlike non-typhoidal Salmonella, antibiotics shorten symptom duration and fecal shedding.

⚡ EHEC Diagnosis Pearl:
  • Sorbitol-MacConkey (SMAC) agar: EHEC does not ferment sorbitol → colorless colonies (normal E. coli ferments sorbitol and is pink).
  • Ingestion of fewer than 100 organisms is sufficient to cause disease.
  • Confirm with serogrouping for O157:H7 or immunoassay for Shiga-like toxin.
  • Do NOT give antibiotics for EHEC — they increase HUS risk.

🌽 Yersinia enterocolitica / pseudotuberculosis

  • Morphology: Gram-negative rod in the Enterobacteriaceae family.
  • Lab note: Requires cold enrichment — not routinely isolated on standard stool cultures.
  • Sources: Undercooked pork, chitterlings, tofu, unpasteurized milk; exposure in child-care settings.
  • Presentations:
    • Pseudoappendicitis: RLQ pain, fever, leukocytosis — mimics appendicitis in children.
    • Enteric fever-like syndrome.
    • Septicemia in iron-overloaded patients (thalassemia, hemochromatosis) because Yersinia scavenges iron.
  • Treat septicemia with fluoroquinolones or third-generation cephalosporins.

🌊 Vibrio Species

▼V. cholerae
  • Gram-negative comma-shaped rod, oxidase-positive.
  • Not routinely cultured; requires TCBS selective media (yellow colonies).
  • Sources: contaminated water, fish, shellfish, street vendors in endemic areas.
  • Cholera toxin ADP-ribosylates Gs → persistent activation of adenylate cyclase → ↑cAMP → chloride & water secretion.
  • Profuse watery “rice-water” diarrhea with flecks of mucus; vomiting; rapid progression to severe dehydration, metabolic acidosis, hypokalemia, hypovolemic shock.
  • Treatment: aggressive oral rehydration (glucose-salt solution exploits Na+-glucose cotransport); tetracycline/doxycycline shortens duration.
▼V. parahaemolyticus
  • Source: raw/undercooked seafood, especially shellfish.
  • Self-limited explosive watery diarrhea, nausea/vomiting, abdominal cramps, headache, low-grade fever.
  • Can also cause wound infections after contaminated salt-water exposure.
▼V. vulnificus
  • Raw oysters / salt-water exposure.
  • Devastating wound infection: erythema → hemorrhagic bullae → tissue necrosis, septicemia.
  • Cirrhotics and iron-overloaded patients are at very high risk for fulminant sepsis and death.

🧄 Listeria monocytogenes

  • Morphology: Short Gram-positive rod. Tumbling motility at room temperature. Grows at refrigeration temperatures (~3°C) — the classic “cold enrichment” bug.
  • Sources: Hot dogs, deli meats, fresh soft cheeses (queso fresco, brie), unpasteurized milk, ice cream.
  • At-risk populations: Pregnant women, neonates, elderly, immunocompromised (especially impaired cell-mediated immunity), diabetics.
  • Clinical syndromes:
    • Febrile gastroenteritis in healthy adults (mild, self-limited).
    • Pregnancy: “flu-like” illness with fever → transplacental transmission → neonatal infection (granulomatosis infantiseptica, neonatal sepsis, meningitis).
    • Immunocompromised: bacteremia, meningoencephalitis (classic adult meningitis pathogen at extremes of age).
  • Treatment: Ampicillin (± gentamicin).
⚠️ TRAP: Antibiotics make things worse for two bugs ▼
  • Non-typhoidal Salmonella: antibiotics prolong the fecal carrier state.
  • EHEC (O157:H7): antibiotics trigger SOS-response release of Shiga-like toxin → ↑risk of HUS.
  • For both, the right answer on exam is usually “supportive care / hydration only.”

🍖 S. aureus Food Poisoning — Details

  • One of the most common food-borne illnesses in the US.
  • Toxin is preformed in the food — reheating food does not destroy the heat-stable enterotoxin.
  • Classic foods: processed meats (ham, salted pork), custard-filled pastries, potato salad, ice cream — anything handled by a colonized worker and left at room temperature.
  • ~50% of outbreaks traced to asymptomatic carriers (especially nasal/oropharyngeal).
  • Onset mean ~4 hr; symptoms resolve within 24 hr.
  • Treatment: supportive only — no antibiotics.

🧹 Clostridium Food Poisoning Highlights

  • C. perfringens: incubation 8–24 hr; abdominal cramps + watery diarrhea; no fever, nausea, or vomiting. Resolves 24–48 hr. Typical source: meat kept warm too long (cafeteria-style buffets).
  • C. botulinum: ingestion of preformed botulinum toxin in home-canned goods (or spores in honey for infants) → descending flaccid paralysis. Not a diarrhea syndrome — know it as a differential.
⚠️ TRAP: Know the incubation-period fingerprint ▼
  • <6 hr + vomiting dominates → preformed toxin (S. aureus or B. cereus emetic).
  • 8–16 hr + diarrhea dominates → C. perfringens or B. cereus diarrheal.
  • >16 hr, bloody, with fever → invasive bacterial (Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter, EHEC/EIEC).
  • Fried rice at a Chinese restaurant + vomiting in 2 hours = Bacillus cereus (emetic).

▼ Protozoa & Parasites — At a Glance

⭐ Protozoal infections are covered in depth in GI26, but Dr. Kumar wants you to know which bugs belong in the “non-bacterial, non-viral” infectious-diarrhea bucket.

🧬 Quick-Hit Protozoal Pathogens

  • Entamoeba histolytica — bloody diarrhea (amebic dysentery), flask-shaped colonic ulcers, anchovy-paste liver abscess. Travel to endemic region.
  • Giardia intestinalis (lamblia) — fatty/foul-smelling diarrhea, bloating, cramps after camping/drinking stream water. Trophozoites and cysts on stool.
  • Cryptosporidium parvum — severe chronic watery diarrhea in AIDS (CD4 <100); acid-fast oocysts.
  • Cyclospora cayetanensis — imported berries/produce; prolonged watery diarrhea.
  • Isospora (Cystoisospora) belli — AIDS-associated watery diarrhea; oocysts on stool.
  • Microsporidia (Septata, Enterocytozoon) — chronic diarrhea in AIDS.
  • Roundworms — Ascaris, Strongyloides, hookworms (Necator, Ancylostoma).
⚠️ TRAP: Don’t confuse Entamoeba with Giardia ▼
  • Entamoeba = bloody dysentery + liver abscess. Invasive to colonic mucosa (flask ulcers).
  • Giardia = non-bloody, fatty, foul-smelling stool. Non-invasive; stays in the small intestine.

🧠 Testing Your Understanding!

Testing Your Understanding — LO 1, Question 1

A 7-year-old presents with 2 days of bloody diarrhea after eating at a backyard cookout. Stool culture on sorbitol-MacConkey agar shows colorless (non-fermenting) colonies. She develops anemia, thrombocytopenia, and an acute rise in creatinine. Why would antibiotics be the wrong treatment here, and what pathogen is responsible?

Consider: SMAC sorbitol-negative, Shiga-like toxin, HUS triad, SOS response

✓ Answer: EHEC (E. coli O157:H7). Antibiotics increase toxin release via the SOS response and worsen HUS risk.

EHEC is the only Shiga-toxin-producing E. coli you’ll see on Step 1 in the US context. It’s sorbitol-negative on SMAC (colorless colonies), whereas normal stool E. coli ferments sorbitol to pink colonies. The Shiga-like toxin (Stx) inhibits the 28S rRNA of the 60S ribosomal subunit on endothelial cells, damaging renal and cerebral microvasculature and producing the HUS triad: microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, and acute kidney injury. Antibiotics that induce the bacterial SOS response (especially TMP-SMX and fluoroquinolones) up-regulate the lambdoid prophage carrying the stx gene and can paradoxically increase toxin release. Management is supportive hydration and dialysis as needed; eculizumab may be considered in severe atypical cases.

Testing Your Understanding — LO 1, Question 2

Two pregnant women are in clinic. One ate a salad with queso fresco two weeks ago and now has fever, myalgias, and mild diarrhea. The other ate raw oysters at a seafood bar; she has cirrhosis and now presents with fever and hemorrhagic skin bullae. Identify each organism and explain why each host population is uniquely vulnerable.

Consider: Gram-positive cold grower, transplacental transmission, iron/cirrhosis susceptibility

✓ Answer: (1) Listeria monocytogenes in pregnancy; (2) Vibrio vulnificus in cirrhosis.

Listeria is a Gram-positive rod that uniquely grows at refrigeration temperatures — soft cheeses and deli meats are the classic vectors. It invades intracellularly and is controlled by cell-mediated immunity, which is physiologically blunted in pregnancy. Transplacental spread causes granulomatosis infantiseptica or neonatal meningitis/sepsis. Treat with ampicillin (± gentamicin).

Vibrio vulnificus is a Gram-negative comma-shaped rod acquired from raw oysters or salt-water wounds. Cirrhotics and patients with iron overload (hemochromatosis, thalassemia) are strikingly susceptible because free iron fuels the organism’s virulence. It produces a characteristic hemorrhagic bullous skin lesion and can progress rapidly to septic shock with >50% mortality. Treat with doxycycline + a third-generation cephalosporin; aggressive debridement may be needed.

🔧 Secretory vs. Invasive vs. Osmotic Mechanisms

  • Secretory (non-inflammatory): Toxin-mediated ↑cAMP or ↑cGMP drives active Cl– and water secretion. High volume, no WBCs, no blood. Examples: cholera toxin, ETEC LT/ST, C. difficile toxins.
  • Invasive (inflammatory): Organism breaches epithelium → inflammation, bleeding, WBCs in stool. Examples: Shigella, EIEC, Campylobacter, invasive Salmonella.
  • Osmotic: Unabsorbed solute draws water in (e.g., post-infectious lactase deficiency, Giardia-associated fat malabsorption).
⚠️ TRAP: Stool WBCs are the key discriminator ▼
  • WBCs + blood in stool → inflammatory (invasive) diarrhea → think Shigella, Campylobacter, EHEC, EIEC, Salmonella, Entamoeba.
  • No WBCs, no blood, huge volumes → secretory → cholera, ETEC, viruses.
  • EHEC is the trap: bloody but often no fever and no fecal WBCs (because it’s toxin-mediated endothelial injury, not inflammation).

▼ Watery (Secretory / Non-Inflammatory) Diarrhea

⭐ Voluminous, non-bloody, high-volume losses. The core issue is secretion, not inflammation — dehydration is the main threat.

🌊 Cholera (V. cholerae)

  • Hallmark: Abrupt-onset “rice-water” stool — colorless, flecks of mucus, fishy odor; can exceed 1 L/hour.
  • Mechanism: Cholera toxin ADP-ribosylates Gs → constitutively active adenylate cyclase → ↑cAMP → sustained Cl– secretion through CFTR.
  • Sequelae: Hypovolemic shock, metabolic acidosis, hypokalemia.
  • Management: Aggressive oral rehydration solution (glucose-salt: exploits SGLT1 Na+-glucose cotransporter, which is intact even when CFTR is hijacked). Doxycycline/tetracycline shortens illness.

✈️ ETEC

  • Two toxins: heat-Labile (LT) acts like cholera toxin (↑cAMP); heat-Stable (ST) activates guanylate cyclase (↑cGMP).
  • Watery diarrhea + cramps, low-grade fever; usually self-limited within 3–4 days.
  • Most common cause of traveler’s diarrhea worldwide.

🦠 Viral Gastroenteritis

  • Rotavirus: Severe dehydrating non-bloody diarrhea in young children; vomit + fever common; respiratory symptoms in 30–50%.
  • Norovirus: Explosive vomiting + watery diarrhea; classic outbreaks on cruise ships, dorms; short, intense illness 24–72 hr.

🧬 Protozoal Watery Diarrhea

  • Giardia: Greasy, foul-smelling, non-bloody stools; bloating; after camping or daycare exposure.
  • Cryptosporidium: Severe chronic watery diarrhea in AIDS (CD4 <100) — may lose 10–20 L/day.
⚠️ TRAP: ORS still works in cholera — here’s why ▼
  • Cholera toxin activates CFTR-mediated Cl– secretion, but SGLT1 (Na+/glucose cotransport) remains fully functional.
  • Oral rehydration solution with glucose + salt drives Na+ absorption via SGLT1 → water follows.
  • This is why the classic ORS is “salty sugar water” — cheap, life-saving, Nobel-level insight.

▼ Dysentery (Inflammatory Bloody Diarrhea)

⭐ Dysentery = small-volume, frequent, bloody stools with tenesmus and fever. Always think invasion of the colonic mucosa.

🦿 Shigellosis

  • Very low inoculum (~10 organisms) → daycare outbreaks.
  • 1–2 days of watery diarrhea → progresses to abdominal cramps, tenesmus, scant bloody-mucoid stools.
  • Shiga toxin (especially S. dysenteriae) can precipitate HUS analogous to EHEC.
  • Antibiotics shorten duration and reduce shedding (fluoroquinolones, TMP-SMX, azithromycin).

🥨 EHEC (O157:H7)

  • Starts watery; progresses to grossly bloody “hemorrhagic colitis” with severe abdominal pain.
  • Notably often afebrile — distinguishes from Shigella/Campylobacter.
  • 5–10% develop HUS (microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, AKI), especially children.
  • Classic exposures: undercooked ground beef, unpasteurized apple cider, petting zoos, contaminated leafy greens.
  • Treat supportively; avoid antibiotics and antimotility agents.

🍗 Campylobacter

  • Most common US bacterial cause of diarrhea.
  • Crampy pain, fever, watery ±bloody stools; self-limited over a week.
  • Guillain-Barré (most common post-infectious GBS trigger worldwide) and reactive arthritis are classic sequelae.

🥚 Invasive Salmonella & Entamoeba

  • Salmonella (non-typhoidal): may cause bloody diarrhea with fever; watch for osteomyelitis in sickle-cell disease.
  • Entamoeba histolytica: bloody/mucoid stool, flask-shaped colonic ulcers, possible “anchovy paste” liver abscess. Treat with metronidazole + luminal agent (paromomycin) to eradicate cysts.
⚠️ TRAP: Bloody diarrhea without fever → think EHEC ▼
  • Shigella, Campylobacter, Salmonella, and EIEC all cause fever + bloody diarrhea.
  • EHEC is the outlier: severe abdominal pain + gross blood but classically afebrile — the Shiga-like toxin directly injures endothelium without inducing significant mucosal inflammation.

▼ Food Poisoning (Preformed Toxin Syndromes)

⭐ Key features: very short incubation (hours), vomiting usually predominates, no fever, symptoms resolve in <48 hr.

🍖 S. aureus Food Poisoning

  • Incubation mean ~4 hr (classically 1–8 hr).
  • Severe vomiting > diarrhea, nausea, cramping. No fever.
  • Classic foods: mayo-based salads, cream-filled pastries, potato salad, ham, ice cream — anything handled by a carrier and left unrefrigerated.
  • Self-limited within 24 hr. Supportive care only — no antibiotics.

🍚 Bacillus cereus

  • Emetic form: <6 hr incubation after reheated fried rice; preformed heat-stable cereulide toxin. Vomiting dominates.
  • Diarrheal form: 6–15 hr incubation after meats/vegetables; heat-labile enterotoxin produced in gut. Watery diarrhea + cramps.
  • Both self-limited.

🧹 C. perfringens

  • 8–24 hr after reheated meats/gravy (buffets, catered events).
  • Watery diarrhea + abdominal cramps; no fever, no vomiting.
  • Resolves in 24–48 hr.

😵 Botulism (differentiator)

  • Preformed botulinum toxin (home-canned goods); infant botulism from spores in honey.
  • Not a diarrhea syndrome — produces descending flaccid paralysis (diplopia, dysphagia, dysarthria), then progressive limb weakness.
  • Treat with equine antitoxin (adults) or human-derived BabyBIG (infants).
⚠️ TRAP: Reheating doesn’t fix preformed toxins ▼
  • S. aureus enterotoxin and B. cereus cereulide are heat-stable. Reheating the food kills the bacteria but leaves the toxin intact.
  • This is why sitting food at room temperature is so dangerous — a few hours at warm temperature lets bacteria grow and secrete toxin.

▼ Traveler’s Diarrhea

⭐ Defined as ≥3 loose stools/day in a traveler to a developing region. ETEC is #1. Most cases are watery, self-limited, and resolve in 3–5 days.

🛫️ Top Pathogens by Frequency

  • ETEC (most common overall)
  • EAEC
  • Campylobacter — increasingly prominent in Southeast Asia
  • Norovirus (cruise ships, resorts)
  • Salmonella, Shigella (if dysenteric features)
  • Protozoa (Giardia, Cryptosporidium, Cyclospora, Entamoeba) if chronic >2 weeks

💊 Prevention & Treatment

  • Prevention: Bottled/treated water; avoid raw vegetables, unpeeled fruits, ice, street food. Bismuth subsalicylate can decrease incidence.
  • Empiric therapy for moderate-severe cases in adults: azithromycin (preferred in Southeast Asia due to Campylobacter FQ-resistance) or ciprofloxacin; loperamide added for symptom control if no fever/bloody stools.
  • Avoid loperamide in dysenteric (bloody/febrile) disease — risk of toxic megacolon.
⚠️ TRAP: Traveler ≠ EIEC ▼
  • Default assumption for a stem featuring traveler’s diarrhea should be ETEC.
  • If the vignette emphasizes bloody, invasive, dysentery-like symptoms, pivot to Shigella, Campylobacter, Salmonella, EIEC, or Entamoeba — not ETEC.
  • Chronic (>2 weeks) watery traveler’s diarrhea after camping or freshwater exposure → think Giardia.

▼ Clostridioides difficile Infection (CDI)

⭐ The quintessential antibiotic-associated diarrhea. Gram-positive anaerobic spore-former that overgrows after normal flora are wiped out.

💉 Risk Factors & Offending Antibiotics

  • Classically implicated antibiotics: Clindamycin (historic), fluoroquinolones, penicillins, cephalosporins — but essentially any antibiotic can precipitate CDI.
  • Other risks: hospitalization, advanced age, PPIs, inflammatory bowel disease, chemotherapy.
  • Hypervirulent strain: NAP1/BI/027 produces increased toxin and a binary toxin → severe disease, outbreaks.

🫂 Clinical Presentation

  • Watery diarrhea within days to weeks of antibiotic exposure; often with fever, leukocytosis, ±blood in stool.
  • Severe disease: marked leukocytosis (>15,000), elevated creatinine, pseudomembranous colitis on endoscopy (yellow-white plaques on the colonic mucosa).
  • Fulminant CDI: hypotension, ileus, toxic megacolon, colonic perforation.
  • Recurrence in 10–25% of treated patients.

🔍 Diagnosis

  • Primary test: Detection of toxins A and B in stool — commonly by EIA (enzyme immunoassay); NAAT (PCR) for the tcdB gene is highly sensitive.
  • Two-step algorithm: GDH (glutamate dehydrogenase antigen) screen → confirm with toxin EIA or PCR.
  • Endoscopic finding: pseudomembranous colitis — adherent yellow-white plaques.
  • Do not test asymptomatic patients (carriage is common).

💊 Management

  • Discontinue the offending antibiotic when possible.
  • Mild-moderate (initial episode): oral vancomycin 125 mg PO QID ×10 days, or oral fidaxomicin (lower recurrence).
  • Severe/fulminant: oral vancomycin (higher dose) + IV metronidazole; consider colectomy for toxic megacolon/perforation.
  • Recurrent CDI: tapered vancomycin, fidaxomicin, or fecal microbiota transplantation; bezlotoxumab (anti-toxin B mAb) to prevent recurrence.
  • Elderly and hospitalized patients are at highest risk for complications.
🧠 Mnemonic: C. diff Culprit Antibiotics — “4 C’s”
▼

The 4 C’s of C. difficile:

  • Clindamycin
  • Cephalosporins (especially 3rd-gen)
  • Ciprofloxacin / Fluoroquinolones
  • Carbapenems / any broad-spectrum antibiotic

(Historically also “Penicillins” — so really “4 C’s + P”.)

⚠️ TRAP: Metronidazole is no longer first-line for most CDI ▼
  • Per IDSA (2017→) and ACG guidelines, oral vancomycin or fidaxomicin are first-line for both initial and recurrent CDI in adults.
  • Metronidazole is now reserved for scenarios where vanco/fidaxo unavailable, or as IV adjunct in severe/fulminant disease with ileus.
  • Old First Aid editions still listed metronidazole — don’t get tricked on updated questions.

Testing Your Understanding — LO 2, Question 1

Testing Your Understanding — LO 2, Question 2

A 72-year-old inpatient finishes a 5-day course of ceftriaxone for pneumonia and develops frequent watery diarrhea, fever, and a WBC of 22,000. Stool NAAT is positive for tcdB. Flexible sigmoidoscopy shows yellow-white adherent plaques on the colon. What drug should be started, what drug should be avoided, and what mechanism underlies pseudomembrane formation?

✓ Answer: Start oral vancomycin (or fidaxomicin). Avoid antimotility agents like loperamide. Pseudomembranes form when toxins A and B glucosylate Rho-family GTPases, disrupting the actin cytoskeleton → enterocyte death, fibrin/mucin/neutrophil plaques.

C. difficile toxin B (and to a lesser extent toxin A) inactivates Rho/Rac GTPases by monoglucosylation, collapsing actin filaments and tight junctions — cells round up and slough off, and the resulting inflammatory exudate forms the classic yellow-white pseudomembrane. In a patient with severe features (WBC >15,000, creatinine rise, hypotension), you can add IV metronidazole to oral vancomycin. Loperamide prolongs toxin contact time and can precipitate toxic megacolon. Recurrence is common (10–25%) — fidaxomicin has lower recurrence rates; refractory/recurrent cases often get fecal microbiota transplantation.

▼ Mouth-to-Anus: Normal Flora & Potential Pathogens

⭐ When the GI mucosa is breached (perforation, trauma, surgery, necrosis), commensal organisms seed deeper spaces and become pathogens. Knowing the normal flora tells you what antibiotic coverage you need.

👄 Normal Mouth Flora (Potential Pathogens)

  • Aerobic Gram-positive: Viridans streptococci, Streptococcus pneumoniae.
  • Anaerobes: Fusobacterium, Veillonella, Prevotella, Peptostreptococcus.

Clinical relevance: dental infections, aspiration pneumonia, mouth/neck abscesses, Ludwig’s angina.

🪺 Normal Gut Flora (Potential Pathogens)

  • Aerobic Gram-negative: E. coli (dominant), Klebsiella, Enterobacter, Proteus.
  • Aerobic Gram-positive: Enterococcus.
  • Anaerobes: Bacteroides fragilis (dominant anaerobe), Clostridium, Peptostreptococcus.

Clinical relevance: when you spill gut flora into the peritoneum (perforated diverticulum, appendicitis, surgical anastomotic leak), you need anaerobic + Gram-negative coverage.

💉 Empiric Coverage for Intraabdominal Infections

  • Options: piperacillin/tazobactam, ceftriaxone + metronidazole, or a carbapenem (for severe/hospital-onset disease).
  • Key principle: Cover enteric Gram-negatives + anaerobes. Add anti-enterococcal coverage in healthcare-associated or severe disease.
  • Practical advice from Dr. Kumar’s slides: “Give anaerobic coverage even if no anaerobes grow on cultures” — anaerobes are hard to culture.
⚠️ TRAP: Match the bug to the breach site ▼
  • Upper GI/biliary source → enteric Gram-negatives + Enterococcus dominate (fewer anaerobes).
  • Distal small bowel or colon source → anaerobes (Bacteroides fragilis) + E. coli dominate.
  • Always cover anaerobes empirically for intraperitoneal abscesses — they seed from colonic flora and are frequently culture-negative.

▼ Helicobacter pylori

⭐ Curved Gram-negative urease-positive rod that colonizes the gastric mucus layer; the dominant infectious cause of peptic ulcer disease and gastric cancer worldwide.

📑 Epidemiology & Disease Associations

  • Causes ~80% of all gastric ulcers and ~90% of duodenal ulcers.
  • Major risk factor for:
    • Gastric adenocarcinoma of the body/antrum (not the cardia).
    • Gastric MALT lymphoma (low-grade MALToma often regresses after eradication).
  • Paradox: H. pylori is protective against GERD and lower esophageal/gastric cardia adenocarcinoma — eradicating asymptomatic colonization is therefore controversial.

🧪 Pathogenesis

  • Urease generates ammonia from urea, neutralizing gastric acid and enabling colonization of the mucus layer.
  • Flagella + spiral shape permit burrowing into mucus.
  • Virulence factors: CagA (cancer-associated), VacA (vacuolating cytotoxin) → epithelial damage → chronic active gastritis → ulcer, metaplasia, carcinoma.

🔍 Diagnosis

  • Test only if you plan to treat. Indications: active PUD or history of PUD without documented cure, low-grade MALT lymphoma, early gastric cancer, unexplained iron-deficiency anemia.
  • Non-invasive tests:
    • Urea breath test: highly sensitive and specific; detects urease activity by breath 13CO2.
    • Stool antigen: sensitivity and specificity ~95%.
  • Invasive (endoscopic) tests:
    • Rapid urease (CLO) test on biopsy — fast, inexpensive.
    • Gastric biopsy histology: Warthin-Starry silver stain is the gold standard (100% sensitivity and specificity).
    • Culture — rarely performed.
  • Hold PPIs / antibiotics / bismuth before testing (can false-negative urea breath and stool antigen).
  • Confirm eradication post-treatment with urea breath or stool antigen (repeat endoscopy if ulcer was complicated).

💊 Treatment

  • First-line (quadruple therapy):
    • Bismuth quadruple: PPI + bismuth + metronidazole + tetracycline × 14 days — preferred where clarithromycin resistance is high (>15%).
    • Concomitant (non-bismuth quadruple): PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin + metronidazole × 14 days.
  • Avoid clarithromycin-based triple therapy if prior macrolide exposure or regional resistance.
  • Penicillin-allergic? Substitute levofloxacin-based regimen or tetracycline + metronidazole.
⚠️ TRAP: H. pylori location determines risk ▼
  • Antrum-predominant colonization → ↑gastrin → hyperchlorhydria → duodenal ulcer.
  • Body/corpus-predominant colonization → atrophic gastritis with hypochlorhydria → gastric adenocarcinoma and ulcer.
  • H. pylori-associated adenocarcinoma affects the body and antrum, NOT the cardia — cardia cancers are associated with GERD and Barrett’s, which H. pylori protects against.

▼ Pyogenic Liver Abscess

⭐ Most common type of visceral abscess. Predominant source is biliary tract or portal seeding from a bowel source.

🪺 Bacteriology

  • Gut flora (~85%) — biliary/portal seeding from cholangitis, diverticulitis, appendicitis.
    • Enterobacterales (E. coli, Klebsiella — especially K. pneumoniae in Asian populations).
    • Streptococci (milleri group).
    • Anaerobes (Bacteroides fragilis).
  • Skin/mouth flora — from bacteremia, IV drug use, endocarditis.
  • Entamoeba histolytica — “anchovy-paste” abscess in travelers from endemic regions; right lobe predominant.

🧗‍♂️ Clinical & Diagnosis

  • Symptoms: Fever, chills, RUQ pain, anorexia, weight loss, hiccups, shoulder pain (diaphragmatic irritation).
  • Labs: Leukocytosis, elevated alkaline phosphatase (hepatobiliary marker), ±transaminitis, elevated inflammatory markers.
  • Imaging: CT or ultrasound — shows a fluid-filled lesion ±internal septations.
  • Blood cultures positive in ~50%.

💊 Management

  • Drainage (image-guided CT/US percutaneous drain, surgery for multiloculated).
  • Empiric antibiotics: cover streptococci, enteric Gram-negatives, and anaerobes — typically ceftriaxone + metronidazole, or piperacillin/tazobactam.
  • Duration: 4–6 weeks depending on drainage and clinical response.
  • Practical pearl: Cover anaerobes even if cultures are negative.
  • Amebic abscess — treat with metronidazole + luminal agent (paromomycin). Usually does not require drainage unless >10 cm or imminent rupture.
⚠️ TRAP: Pyogenic vs. amebic liver abscess ▼
  • Pyogenic: often multiple, from biliary source; mixed gut flora; requires drainage + antibiotics.
  • Amebic: usually single, right lobe, in traveler from endemic area; serology for Entamoeba; treat with metronidazole (drainage often unnecessary).
  • Don’t aspirate pure amebic abscesses routinely — they’re classically culture-negative (“anchovy paste” fluid is sterile).

▼ Intraperitoneal Abscess

⭐ Localized walled-off infection within the peritoneal cavity. Forms after perforation, diverticulitis, appendicitis, or post-operative leak.

🧗‍♀️ Clinical Features

  • Persistent fever, chills, abdominal pain, mass effect (early satiety, urinary frequency, obstructive symptoms).
  • Leukocytosis; elevated inflammatory markers.
  • Classic locations: subphrenic, subhepatic, pelvic (pouch of Douglas), inter-loop.

🔍 Diagnosis

  • CT with contrast is the test of choice.
  • Ultrasound for pelvic or superficial abscess.

💊 Management

  • Source control = drainage. Image-guided percutaneous drain whenever possible; surgery for inaccessible or multiloculated collections.
  • Empiric antibiotics: streptococci + enteric Gram-negatives + anaerobes — pip-tazo, carbapenems, or ceftriaxone + metronidazole.
  • Antibiotics are adjunctive; source control is the key to cure.
⚠️ TRAP: Antibiotics alone don’t cure abscesses ▼
  • Abscesses have low pH, low O2, and thick fibrin walls — antibiotics penetrate poorly.
  • If a stem describes a defined abscess cavity that hasn’t been drained, the next best step is percutaneous drainage, not another antibiotic.

▼ Peritonitis — Primary, Secondary, & CAPD-Associated

⭐ Inflammation of the peritoneum — classified by route of infection. Each type has a different bug profile and management.

🧬 Three Classifications

🥄 Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP)

  • Diagnosis: ascitic fluid PMN ≥250 cells/mm³ OR positive ascites culture (usually monomicrobial, unlike secondary peritonitis).
  • Treatment: ceftriaxone or cefotaxime × 5 days.
  • Primary prophylaxis: norfloxacin or TMP-SMX in high-risk cirrhotics (low ascitic protein, prior SBP, GI bleed).
  • Outcomes: mortality ~20%; recurrence >50% within 1 year without secondary prophylaxis.

👔 Secondary Peritonitis

  • Polymicrobial — cover Gram-negatives, anaerobes, ±Enterococcus.
  • Requires surgical source control (repair perforation, drain abscess, resect ischemic bowel) in addition to antibiotics.
  • Clue on ascitic tap: multiple organisms growing, high protein, often glucose <50 mg/dL, LDH elevated — suggests secondary cause (look for perforation).

🧪 CAPD (Peritoneal Dialysis) Peritonitis

  • Cloudy effluent + abdominal pain in PD patient.
  • Dialysate WBC >100/mm³ with >50% PMNs is diagnostic.
  • Gram-positive skin flora predominate — empiric vancomycin + a cephalosporin (or aminoglycoside) intraperitoneally.
  • Persistent infection → catheter removal.
⚠️ TRAP: Monomicrobial vs. polymicrobial culture ▼
  • SBP (primary) = almost always monomicrobial. A polymicrobial ascitic culture in a cirrhotic should prompt an aggressive hunt for a perforation (i.e., secondary peritonitis).
  • Other secondary-peritonitis ascites clues: glucose <50, protein >1 g/dL, LDH above the upper limit of normal (Runyon’s criteria).

▼ Other Intraabdominal Infections (Quick Hits)

⭐ Dr. Kumar’s second half also surveys these — know the setting and the offending bug.

🧬 Site-by-Site Recap

  • Cholecystitis / Cholangitis: Ascending infection from biliary obstruction — E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterococcus, anaerobes. Charcot’s triad (fever + RUQ pain + jaundice) → cholangitis; Reynolds pentad adds hypotension + confusion for suppurative cholangitis.
  • Appendicitis: Polymicrobial luminal obstruction → mural ischemia → perforation. E. coli + Bacteroides. Treat with appendectomy + perioperative Gram-negative/anaerobic antibiotics.
  • Diverticulitis: Microperforation of a colonic diverticulum → left lower quadrant pain, fever. Mixed colonic flora. Uncomplicated: outpatient metronidazole + ciprofloxacin (or amoxicillin/clavulanate). Complicated (abscess, perforation): hospitalization ±drainage ±surgery.
  • Neutropenic typhlitis (cecitis): Necrotizing inflammation of cecum in chemotherapy/neutropenic patients — mixed enteric flora; treat with broad-spectrum antibiotics, antifungal coverage if refractory.
  • Proctitis (STI-related): Gonorrhea, chlamydia, herpes, syphilis in receptive anal intercourse; tenesmus, rectal discharge.
⚠️ TRAP: Cholangitis is a surgical/procedural emergency ▼
  • Charcot’s triad = ascending cholangitis: treat with fluids, broad-spectrum antibiotics, and emergent biliary decompression via ERCP.
  • Reynolds pentad (add hypotension + altered mental status) signals suppurative cholangitis — ICU-level care; mortality is very high without urgent drainage.

Testing Your Understanding — LO 3, Question 1

A 58-year-old man with alcoholic cirrhosis and chronic ascites presents with two days of diffuse abdominal pain, low-grade fever, and new confusion. Paracentesis shows ascitic PMNs of 750 cells/mm3, glucose 95 mg/dL, protein 0.8 g/dL, and a monomicrobial E. coli culture. Explain the most likely diagnosis, empiric therapy, and a key secondary-prevention strategy.

Consider: SBP criteria, gut translocation, ceftriaxone, norfloxacin prophylaxis

✓ Answer: Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP). Empiric ceftriaxone (or cefotaxime) × 5 days. Long-term oral fluoroquinolone (norfloxacin) prophylaxis is indicated after the first episode.

SBP is diagnosed by ascitic fluid PMNs ≥250/mm3 in a cirrhotic with ascites; the fluid is typically low-protein (impaired opsonin delivery) and monomicrobial. E. coli is the #1 organism because translocation of gut flora across a leaky intestinal barrier is the pathogenesis. Third-generation cephalosporins are standard empiric therapy. Add IV albumin (1.5 g/kg day 1, 1 g/kg day 3) in patients with creatinine >1 mg/dL, BUN >30, or bilirubin >4 — this reduces the risk of hepatorenal syndrome and improves survival. Recurrence exceeds 50% in the year after a first episode, so long-term norfloxacin (or TMP-SMX or ciprofloxacin) prophylaxis is indicated. Polymicrobial growth or very high ascitic protein should prompt evaluation for secondary (surgical) peritonitis.

Testing Your Understanding — LO 3, Question 2

A 54-year-old woman with 6 months of epigastric pain develops melena. EGD reveals a clean-based duodenal ulcer. A rapid urease test on antral biopsy is positive. Six weeks after completing bismuth quadruple therapy, you are deciding how to confirm eradication. Why is simple repeat serology useless, what is the preferred non-invasive test, and what is the theoretical downside of treating her?

Consider: IgG persistence, urea breath/stool antigen, H. pylori protective effects

✓ Answer: Serology stays positive for years after cure (IgG persistence). Confirm eradication with a urea breath test or stool antigen, holding PPIs/antibiotics/bismuth beforehand. Downside: eradicating H. pylori may raise her risk of GERD and lower esophageal/gastric cardia adenocarcinoma.

H. pylori serology (IgG) reflects past or present infection and cannot discriminate active colonization from eradicated disease; it remains positive long after successful treatment. Urea breath test relies on labeled-CO2 generation by active urease in the stomach and is rapidly accurate once treatment works. Stool antigen is a similarly reliable alternative. Hold PPIs for 2 weeks and antibiotics/bismuth for 4 weeks before either test to prevent false-negatives. The “H. pylori dilemma” is that colonization protects against GERD, Barrett’s, and gastric-cardia adenocarcinoma by lowering acid output (atrophic body gastritis) — this is why we don’t screen asymptomatic patients. In patients with PUD, MALT lymphoma, early gastric cancer, or unexplained iron-deficiency anemia, though, the benefits of eradication clearly outweigh the risks.

(B) describes Norovirus, which causes cruise-ship/adult outbreaks. (C) is Listeria, (D) is Campylobacter, (E) is Cryptosporidium — all wrong demographics/presentations.

Question 3

A 25-year-old develops bloody diarrhea, fever, and crampy abdominal pain 3 days after eating undercooked chicken at a cookout. Stool culture at 42°C on selective medium grows a curved Gram-negative rod. Three weeks later he develops ascending flaccid paralysis. The most likely organism also causes which additional complication?

A. Hemolytic uremic syndrome

B. Ascending cholangitis

C. Reactive arthritis (Reiter’s)

D. Typhoid fever

E. Pseudoappendicitis

✓ Correct: C — Campylobacter jejuni is classically linked to Guillain-Barré syndrome AND reactive arthritis.

Campylobacter (curved Gram-negative rod, 42°C selective culture, chicken source) is the most common US bacterial diarrhea and the most common worldwide antecedent of Guillain-Barré. It is also a member of the “SSCY(C)” reactive arthritis group (Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter, Yersinia, Chlamydia). HUS (A) is EHEC/Shigella. Cholangitis (B) is biliary obstruction with gut flora. Typhoid (D) is S. typhi. Pseudoappendicitis (E) is Yersinia.

Question 4

A 6-year-old child with sickle cell disease presents with fever and right femur pain. Blood cultures grow a Gram-negative H2S-producing rod. Which of the following best explains why this organism is particularly problematic in this patient population?

A. Production of IgA protease

B. Splenic autoinfarction impairs clearance of encapsulated and invasive organisms

C. Low inoculum transmissibility from person to person

D. Direct invasion via iron scavenging

E. Preformed enterotoxin in contaminated food

✓ Correct: B — Functional asplenia in sickle cell disease predisposes to invasive Salmonella infections, classically osteomyelitis.

Non-typhoidal Salmonella species cause osteomyelitis in sickle-cell patients because splenic microinfarcts eliminate clearance of bacteremia, and Salmonella has a tropism for infarcted bone. In the general population, S. aureus is the top cause of osteomyelitis; Salmonella is the outlier for sickle cell. IgA protease (A) is a feature of S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, and Neisseria. Low-inoculum transmission (C) is Shigella. Iron scavenging (D) describes Yersinia in iron-overloaded hosts. Preformed toxin (E) is not the mechanism.

LO 2 — Clinical Syndromes (Q5–Q8)

Question 5

A 29-year-old eats leftover fried rice from dinner the night before. Two hours later he develops severe vomiting and cramping; no fever. He feels better within 10 hours. What is the causative organism?

A. Staphylococcus aureus

B. Bacillus cereus (emetic form)

C. Clostridium perfringens

D. Norovirus

E. Salmonella enteritidis

✓ Correct: B — The reheated-fried-rice + short-incubation emetic picture is classic Bacillus cereus (cereulide toxin).

Cereulide is a heat-stable preformed toxin; re-heating does not destroy it. S. aureus food poisoning (A) also has a short incubation and vomiting, but the classic foods are custard pastries, mayo salads, and processed meats. C. perfringens (C) has an 8–24 hr incubation and is diarrhea-predominant. Norovirus (D) can also produce vomiting but with a longer 12–48 hr incubation and typically an outbreak context. Salmonella (E) is invasive, fever-positive, and has a 6–48 hr incubation.

Question 6

A 4-year-old previously healthy girl presents after 4 days of watery diarrhea that turned bloody. She has abdominal pain but no fever. Labs show Hb 8 g/dL with schistocytes, platelets 55,000, BUN 65, creatinine 2.2. Which of the following is the most appropriate initial management?

A. Empiric ciprofloxacin

B. Oral metronidazole

C. IV fluids and supportive care

D. Loperamide and oral rehydration

E. TMP-SMX and bismuth

✓ Correct: C — EHEC with HUS is treated with supportive care; antibiotics and antimotility agents worsen toxin release and HUS severity.

The triad of microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, and AKI in a child with preceding bloody diarrhea is hemolytic uremic syndrome, classically post-EHEC (O157:H7). Antibiotics (especially fluoroquinolones, TMP-SMX) induce the bacterial SOS response → up-regulate the lambdoid prophage carrying stx → increased toxin release → worse HUS. Loperamide increases toxin contact time and is contraindicated. Provide aggressive IV fluid resuscitation, monitor renal function, transfuse judiciously, and consider dialysis as needed.

Question 7

A 68-year-old nursing home resident on day 5 of ceftriaxone for pneumonia develops 8 watery stools per day and a WBC of 24,000. Stool toxin EIA is positive. Sigmoidoscopy reveals adherent yellow-white plaques. Which agent is first-line?

A. IV metronidazole alone

B. Oral ciprofloxacin

C. Oral vancomycin

D. Rifaximin

E. IV vancomycin

✓ Correct: C — Oral vancomycin (or fidaxomicin) is first-line for CDI, per updated IDSA/ACG guidance.

Oral vancomycin reaches therapeutic luminal concentrations (it is not systemically absorbed), exactly where C. difficile resides. IV vancomycin (E) does NOT reach the colonic lumen and is inappropriate. Metronidazole (A) is no longer preferred for initial CDI but may be added IV in severe/fulminant disease with ileus. Fluoroquinolones (B) are often causative, not therapeutic. Rifaximin (D) is used for hepatic encephalopathy and traveler’s diarrhea, not CDI.

Question 8

A 52-year-old relief worker returns from Haiti with a single day of “rice-water” diarrhea exceeding 10 L and now has hypotension, hypokalemia, and metabolic acidosis. The toxin responsible acts by which mechanism?

A. Activates guanylate cyclase via heat-stable enterotoxin

B. ADP-ribosylates Gs, leading to persistent ↑cAMP and Cl– secretion

C. Inactivates EF-2 via ADP-ribosylation

D. Cleaves SNARE proteins at the neuromuscular junction

E. Glucosylates Rho GTPases, disrupting actin cytoskeleton

✓ Correct: B — Cholera toxin ADP-ribosylates Gs → locked-on adenylate cyclase → ↑cAMP → CFTR-mediated Cl– and water secretion.

This drives the massive “rice-water” stool. Treat with aggressive oral rehydration salts (the glucose-Na+ SGLT1 cotransporter remains functional) + doxycycline. (A) is ETEC heat-stable toxin. (C) is diphtheria toxin and Pseudomonas exotoxin A. (D) is botulinum/tetanus toxin. (E) is C. difficile toxins A and B.

LO 3 — Intraabdominal Infections (Q9–Q12)

Question 9

A 64-year-old with Child-Pugh B cirrhosis presents with low-grade fever, diffuse abdominal pain, and new encephalopathy. Paracentesis shows 500 PMNs/mm3 and grows a single organism on culture. Which empiric antibiotic is most appropriate?

A. Vancomycin + aztreonam

B. Ceftriaxone

C. Oral vancomycin

D. Piperacillin/tazobactam + vancomycin

E. TMP-SMX

✓ Correct: B — Ceftriaxone (or cefotaxime) is standard empiric therapy for SBP.

SBP (primary peritonitis) is defined by ascitic PMN ≥250/mm3 in a cirrhotic; monomicrobial growth (usually E. coli) supports the diagnosis. Third-generation cephalosporins cover the typical enteric Gram-negatives. Add IV albumin to reduce risk of hepatorenal syndrome. Oral vancomycin (C) is for CDI. Broader coverage (A, D) is reserved for nosocomial or healthcare-associated peritonitis. TMP-SMX (E) is a prophylactic option, not primary therapy.

Question 10

A 48-year-old man is referred for dyspepsia. Endoscopy shows a duodenal ulcer; rapid urease test is positive. He is treated with bismuth quadruple therapy. Why should clarithromycin-based triple therapy have been avoided as empiric first-line today in many US regions?

A. Clarithromycin is ineffective in achlorhydric stomachs

B. Rising clarithromycin resistance (>15%) has compromised cure rates

C. Clarithromycin does not reach therapeutic levels in gastric mucus

D. Clarithromycin is known to eradicate protective Lactobacillus species

E. Clarithromycin accelerates bismuth metabolism

✓ Correct: B — Regional clarithromycin resistance >15% has dropped cure rates below acceptable thresholds; current guidelines favor bismuth or non-bismuth quadruple therapy empirically.

Triple therapy (PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin) was once standard but is now reserved for patients without prior macrolide exposure in low-resistance regions. Quadruple regimens (PPI + bismuth + metronidazole + tetracycline, or PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin + metronidazole) achieve higher eradication rates × 14 days. Confirm eradication with urea breath test or stool antigen 4 weeks post-treatment (off PPI).

Question 11

A 34-year-old man returned from Mexico 2 months ago. He now presents with RUQ pain, low-grade fever, and an 8-cm right-hepatic-lobe fluid collection on ultrasound. Percutaneous drainage yields thick reddish-brown fluid with no organisms on Gram stain. What is the best therapy?

A. Ceftriaxone + metronidazole

B. Oral vancomycin

C. Metronidazole + paromomycin

D. Amphotericin B

E. Praziquantel

✓ Correct: C — Amebic liver abscess (Entamoeba histolytica): metronidazole clears tissue forms, then paromomycin (or iodoquinol) eliminates luminal cysts.

The right-lobe single abscess with reddish-brown (“anchovy paste”) sterile fluid in a returned traveler is pathognomonic. Serology (antibodies to E. histolytica) confirms. Antibiotics like ceftriaxone + metronidazole (A) would fit a pyogenic abscess — but pyogenic collections are usually polymicrobial with gut flora on Gram stain, not sterile. Paromomycin is essential to eradicate luminal cysts and prevent recurrence/transmission.

Question 12

A 71-year-old man with a perforated sigmoid diverticulum is taken to the OR. Peritoneal fluid grows E. coli + Bacteroides fragilis + Enterococcus faecalis. What is the key mechanistic principle explaining the empirical antibiotic choice?

A. Cover only Gram-negative organisms because anaerobes rarely matter

B. Cover anaerobes empirically even if cultures are negative, since gut flora from the colon reliably seed these infections

C. Antibiotics alone are adequate if they cover all three organisms

D. Use narrow-spectrum therapy to preserve microbiome

E. Prophylactic antifungals are always required

✓ Correct: B — Secondary peritonitis from a colonic perforation is polymicrobial with reliable anaerobic involvement; empiric anaerobic coverage (metronidazole, pip-tazo, carbapenems) is essential regardless of culture results.

Anaerobes, especially Bacteroides fragilis, are notoriously hard to culture but nearly always present in colonic perforations. “Give anaerobic coverage even if anaerobes don’t grow” is a core teaching point. Surgical or percutaneous source control is also required — antibiotics alone cannot cure a walled-off abscess or fix a perforation. Narrow-spectrum therapy (D) is inappropriate at initial empiric coverage. Enterococcal coverage (ampicillin, vancomycin) is added for healthcare-associated or severe disease. Antifungals (E) are not routine unless risk factors (TPN, prolonged ICU, immunosuppression) are present.
