▼1. Entamoeba histolytica

Flask-shaped ulcer Anchovy-paste abscess RUQ pain + returned traveler Gal/GalNAc lectin Metronidazole → paromomycin

Organism & Life Cycle

  • Pathogen: Entamoeba histolytica (invasive protozoan). Morphologically identical species E. dispar, E. moshkovskii, E. bangladeshi are commensals only.
  • Transmission: fecal-oral — contaminated water/food; oral-anal sex (MSM).
  • Life cycle: ingest quadri-nucleate cyst → excystation in bowel lumen → uni-nucleate trophozoite (invasive form) → trophozoite either re-encysts (asymptomatic passer) or adheres via Gal/GalNAc-specific lectin → invades colonic mucosa → can spread to liver → abscess.
HIGH-YIELD:
  • Gal/GalNAc lectin = the adhesin that mediates mucosal invasion. Classic Step 1 detail.
  • Cysts = quadri-nucleate (4 nuclei); trophozoites = uni-nucleate, often with ingested RBCs on stool microscopy.

Epidemiology / Exposure

  • ~34–50 million cases worldwide, 100,000 deaths/yr.
  • Endemic in Central/South America, Africa, India.
  • 3rd most common parasitic infection in the U.S. (immigrants, travelers, MSM).
  • More severe disease in: young children, elderly, steroids/immunocompromised, pregnancy.

Clinical Manifestations

SyndromePresentation
Asymptomatic cyst passer~80% of E. histolytica infections; all E. dispar / E. moshkovskii infections. Risk of downstream transmission.
Amebic colitis / dysenteryBloody mucoid diarrhea, crampy abdominal pain, tenesmus. Complications: toxic megacolon, ameboma (inflammatory mass mimicking cancer). Classic path: flask-shaped ulcer.
Amebic liver abscessFever, RUQ pain, N/V, elevated LFTs, leukocytosis. Right lobe in 80%, more common in males. Often WITHOUT concomitant colitis. Aspirate → thick "anchovy-paste" reddish-brown sterile fluid.
Thoracic amebiasis~10% of liver-abscess pts. R-sided pleural effusion or empyema; 3% get pericarditis.
Cerebral abscess0.6–4.7% of liver-abscess patients.
⚠️ EXAM TRAP — Amebic vs. Pyogenic Liver Abscess ▼
  • Amebic: solitary, R lobe, returned traveler (Mexico/C. South America), sterile “anchovy-paste” aspirate, positive serology → metronidazole + paromomycin.
  • Pyogenic: often multiple, usually polymicrobial (E. coli, Klebsiella, anaerobes), positive Gram stain/cultures → ceftriaxone + metronidazole + percutaneous drainage.
  • Dead giveaway in a vignette: "thick reddish-brown fluid, no organisms on Gram stain" = amebic.

Diagnosis

TestColitisLiver Abscess
Stool microscopy (O&P)25–60%10–40%
Stool antigen detection80%~40%
Serum antigen detection65%>95%
Stool / abscess PCR>95%>95%
Serology (indirect hemagglutination)Acute 70% / Conv. >90%Acute 70–80% / Conv. >90%

Workflow: for suspected liver abscess → CT/US + serology (most useful — positive in >90% convalescent). Abscess Gram stain is sterile.

Treatment

  • Invasive disease (colitis or liver abscess): Metronidazole (or tinidazole) — kills tissue trophozoites.
  • Follow with a luminal agent to eradicate cysts: paromomycin (or diloxanide furoate, iodoquinol). Cysts do NOT respond to metronidazole — ~40–60% persist.
  • Asymptomatic cyst passer: paromomycin alone.
🧠 MNEMONIC — "Metro first, Paro last"▼

Metronidazole = Mucosal/tissue (trophozoites).

Paromomycin = Persistent cysts in the lumen.

Miss the luminal step = relapse + continued transmission.

▼2. Giardia lamblia

Beaver fever Camping / stream water Fatty, foul-smelling diarrhea "Falling leaf" trophozoite Metronidazole / tinidazole

Organism & Life Cycle

  • Pathogen: Giardia lamblia (a.k.a. G. intestinalis, G. duodenalis). Flagellated protozoan; trophozoites have a classic "falling leaf" motility with a central pear-shaped body and 2 nuclei ("smiley face").
  • Transmission: fecal-oral ingestion of cysts.
  • Life cycle: cyst → excystation in duodenum → trophozoites adhere to duodenal/jejunal villi via ventral sucking disk (no invasion!) → blunt villi → malabsorption → new cysts pass in stool.

Epidemiology / Exposure

  • Most commonly identified intestinal parasite in the U.S. (4–7% of stools).
  • Transmission: recreational fresh water (lakes, streams, untreated well water) — beavers are a classic reservoir.
  • Other: day care centers, MSM sexual transmission, international travel.
  • Host risk factors: IgA deficiency / CVID, hypo/achlorhydria (PPI, post-gastrectomy).

Clinical Manifestations

  • Incubation 1–2 weeks.
  • Syndromes: asymptomatic cyst passage → acute self-limited diarrhea → chronic diarrhea with malabsorption.
  • Classic features: foul-smelling, greasy, non-bloody diarrhea, bloating, flatulence, crampy abdominal pain, weight loss.
  • Post-giardia lactose intolerance is common (disaccharidase loss from villous blunting).
HIGH-YIELD:
  • No eosinophilia (protozoan, not a helminth).
  • No dysentery / no bloody stool (non-invasive).
  • Classic vignette: hiker drinks stream water → 2 weeks later has bloating, foul-smelling greasy diarrhea.

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis:
    • Stool O&P (1 stool 60–80%; 3 stools >90%) — shedding is intermittent.
    • Stool Giardia antigen EIA (85–95% sens / 90–100% spec) — often first-line now.
  • Treatment: tinidazole or metronidazole (first line); alternatives: nitazoxanide, albendazole.

▼3. Cryptosporidium parvum & hominis

AIDS + CD4 <50 Chronic watery diarrhea Chlorine-resistant oocyst Modified acid-fast stain Nitazoxanide

Organism & Life Cycle

  • Pathogen: Cryptosporidium parvum and C. hominis (most common human species).
  • Transmission: ingestion of infectious oocysts (already sporulated, infectious on excretion).
  • Life cycle: oocyst → sporozoites released → invade intestinal epithelial cells but remain in a parasitophorous vacuole at the microvillus border (intracellular but extracytoplasmic) → asexual → sexual cycles → both thick-walled oocysts (environmental spread) and thin-walled oocysts (autoinfection).

Epidemiology / Exposure

  • Oocysts are infectious at very low inocula and resistant to chlorine — why it causes large water-park / municipal water outbreaks.
  • Classic U.S. outbreak: Milwaukee 1993 (~403,000 infected via city water supply).
  • Sources: agricultural runoff, well water, pools/water parks, day care centers, person-to-person.
  • Most severely affected: children and patients with depressed cellular immunity (AIDS, CD4 <100).

Clinical Manifestations

  • Immunocompetent: self-limited watery diarrhea, cramps, nausea, low-grade fever; lasts 5–10 days.
  • Immunocompromised (HIV/AIDS, CD4 <50): chronic, severe, secretory diarrhea → weight loss, malabsorption, electrolyte abnormalities.
  • Extraintestinal disease (AIDS): AIDS cholangiopathy (sclerosing-cholangitis-like biliary disease), pancreatitis, pneumonitis.

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis: not seen on routine O&P. Order:
    • Modified acid-fast (Ziehl-Neelsen) stain of stool → small (~5 µm) red oocysts.
    • Cryptosporidium stool antigen / PCR.
  • Treatment:
    • Immunocompetent: supportive care; nitazoxanide shortens illness.
    • AIDS: antiparasitics alone are not curative — ART to restore CD4 is the definitive treatment.

▼4. The Modified Acid-Fast Trio

All three cause watery diarrhea and stain with modified AFB:

OrganismOocyst SizeClassic ExposureTreatment
Cryptosporidium~5 µmContaminated water, AIDSNitazoxanide (+ ART)
Cystoisospora belli (formerly Isospora)~25 µm ovalCentral/South America, Africa, India; AIDS diarrheaTMP-SMX
Cyclospora cayetanensis~8–10 µmContaminated produce — raspberries, snow peas, cilantro (esp. Guatemala)TMP-SMX
🧠 MNEMONIC — "C-C-C → AFB"▼

Crypto, Cystoisospora, Cyclospora = acid-fast oocysts.

Crypto is the smallest (5 µm), Cyclospora is double-sized (8–10 µm), Cystoisospora is the biggest oval (~25 µm).

Treat Crypto with nitazoxanide (or ART); treat Cystoisospora & Cyclospora with TMP-SMX.

⚠️ EXAM TRAP — Which one is it? ▼
  • AIDS + chronic watery diarrhea + biliary tree involvement → Cryptosporidium.
  • Raspberries/snow peas/cilantro outbreak (often Guatemala) → Cyclospora.
  • AIDS + eosinophilia + diarrhea in a traveler from Haiti/Central America → Cystoisospora.

🧪 Testing Your Understanding

TYU 1

A 28-year-old returns from Mexico with 3 weeks of intermittent bloody diarrhea and 1 week of right upper quadrant pain. CT shows a single 8-cm fluid collection in the right hepatic lobe. Aspirate is sterile and reddish-brown. What is the best next step?

✓ Correct: C — Amebic liver abscess. Treat invasive tissue trophozoites with metronidazole, then eradicate luminal cysts with paromomycin to prevent relapse and transmission.

Metronidazole alone fails in ~40–60% because it does not kill cysts. Ceftriaxone + metronidazole (A) covers a pyogenic abscess, which would be polymicrobial with organisms on Gram stain.

TYU 2

A camper returns from a hike with 10 days of greasy, foul-smelling diarrhea, bloating, and flatulence. There is no blood and no fever. Stool O&P × 1 is negative. What is the best next step?

✓ Correct: A — Classic Giardia. Shedding is intermittent, so single O&P is only 60–80% sensitive; 3 samples get to >90%, and stool Giardia antigen EIA is ~90% sens/spec.

Giardia is a non-invasive protozoan — no blood, no fever, no eosinophilia. Treat with tinidazole or metronidazole.

TYU 3

An HIV-positive man with CD4 count 38 presents with 3 months of watery diarrhea and 15-lb weight loss. Stool modified acid-fast stain reveals small ~5-µm red oocysts. What is the most important therapy?

✓ Correct: D — Cryptosporidium in AIDS. Antiparasitics alone are not curative at CD4 <50; immune reconstitution via ART is the definitive treatment. Nitazoxanide may be added but is of limited benefit.

Don't miss AIDS cholangiopathy (sclerosing-cholangitis-like picture from Crypto in the biliary tree).

▼1. Leishmania

Sandfly bite Non-healing ulcer Kala-azar pentad Amastigotes in macrophages Liposomal ampho B

Organism & Life Cycle

  • Transmission: bite of the female sandfly (Phlebotomus Old World; Lutzomyia New World).
  • Life cycle: sandfly injects promastigotes → taken up by macrophages → transform into amastigotes (no flagellum) inside phagolysosomes → replicate → infect other reticuloendothelial cells.
  • Histopath buzz: amastigotes in macrophages on Giemsa = small round bodies with a rod-shaped kinetoplast next to the nucleus.

Epidemiology / Exposure

FormSpeciesGeography
Visceral (Kala-azar)L. donovani (Old), L. infantum/chagasi (New)India, Nepal, Bangladesh, Sudan, Brazil
Cutaneous — Old WorldL. major, L. tropicaMiddle East (Iran, Syria, Iraq, Afghanistan), North Africa — classic “Baghdad boil” in Iraq war vets
Cutaneous — New WorldL. mexicana, L. braziliensisMexico, Central/South America
Mucosal (espundia)L. braziliensis complexBolivia, Brazil, Peru

Clinical Manifestations

Cutaneous Leishmaniasis
  • Painless non-healing ulcer at bite site with raised/rolled indurated borders.
  • Weeks-to-months after exposure; often on exposed skin (face, arms).
Mucosal Leishmaniasis (Espundia)
  • Erosive destruction of mucosal surfaces (nose, oropharynx, larynx) — can follow cutaneous disease years later.
  • Predominantly South America (L. braziliensis).
Visceral Leishmaniasis (Kala-azar)
HIGH-YIELD — Classic Pentad:
  • Prolonged fever
  • Weight loss
  • Hepatosplenomegaly (massive)
  • Pancytopenia
  • Hypergammaglobulinemia

Severe in AIDS, transplant, and malnourished patients. May cause GI diarrhea in AIDS. Can be fatal if untreated.

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis:
    • Cutaneous/mucosal: biopsy or scraping with Giemsa stain → amastigotes; PCR/culture.
    • Visceral: splenic aspirate (highest yield) or bone marrow aspirate; serology (rK39 rapid test); PCR.
  • Treatment:
    • Visceral → liposomal amphotericin B (U.S. first line).
    • Alternatives: pentavalent antimony (stibogluconate; risk of pancreatitis, arrhythmia), miltefosine, pentamidine.
    • Selected cutaneous: fluconazole or ketoconazole.

▼2. Trypanosoma cruzi — Chagas Disease

Kissing / Reduviid / Triatomine bug Romaña's sign Cardiomyopathy + megacolon + megaesophagus Central/South America Benznidazole / Nifurtimox

Organism & Life Cycle

  • Pathogen: Trypanosoma cruzi.
  • Vector: blood-sucking triatomine (“kissing / reduviid”) bug.
  • Life cycle: bug bites face/mucous membranes and defecates at the bite; rubbing the feces into the wound introduces trypomastigotes → invade host cells → amastigotes replicate in muscle/neural tissue (heart, GI autonomic ganglia) → trypomastigotes recirculate.
  • Other transmission: blood transfusion, solid organ transplant, congenital.

Epidemiology

  • Endemic: Mexico, Central and South America (most prevalent in rural poverty).
  • Only 10–30% of infected patients develop chronic Chagas disease (often years to decades later).

Clinical Manifestations

Acute Chagas
  • Romaña's sign — unilateral painless periorbital/eyelid swelling from ocular inoculation.
  • Chagoma (indurated nodule at bite site), fever, lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly.
  • Rarely: myocarditis, meningoencephalitis (can be fatal, especially in children).
Chronic Chagas (10–30% of infected, >10 yrs later)
  • Dilated cardiomyopathy (apical aneurysm, heart block, arrhythmias, HF, sudden cardiac death). Typically right ventricle involved prominently.
  • Megaesophagus — dysphagia mimicking achalasia (destruction of enteric plexus).
  • Megacolon — chronic constipation, sigmoid volvulus risk.
HIGH-YIELD: Brazilian/Central American patient with new cardiomyopathy + megacolon or megaesophagus = Chagas until proven otherwise.

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis:
    • Acute: trypomastigotes on Giemsa-stained peripheral blood smear; PCR.
    • Chronic: serology (need 2 different assays for confirmation).
  • Treatment: benznidazole (first-line in U.S.) or nifurtimox — most effective in acute and early chronic disease.
  • Chronic cardiomyopathy / mega-syndromes managed symptomatically (pacemakers, surgery).

▼3. Malaria (Plasmodium)

Female Anopheles mosquito Cyclic fevers (tertian / quartan) Ring forms on thick/thin smear Cerebral malaria (P. falciparum) Primaquine for hypnozoites

Species & Epidemiology

SpeciesGeographyKey Features
P. falciparumSub-Saharan Africa, Papua New Guinea, Haiti, Indian subcontinentMost pathogenic; persistent fevers; infects RBCs of ALL ages; NO hypnozoites; cerebral malaria
P. vivaxC/S America, N Africa, Middle East, Indian subcontinentTertian (q48h) fever; infects reticulocytes only; hypnozoites → relapse; Duffy-antigen dependent (FyFy West Africans resistant)
P. ovaleWest AfricaTertian fever; reticulocytes; hypnozoites → relapse
P. malariaeWorldwide (scattered)Quartan (q72h); infects mature RBCs; chronic infection for years; nephrotic syndrome (immune complex)
P. knowlesiMalaysia / SE AsiaZoonotic from macaques; identical morphology to P. malariae but can cause severe disease

Life Cycle

  1. Female Anopheles bites (dusk–dawn) → injects sporozoites into bloodstream.
  2. Sporozoites travel to liver → invade hepatocytes → mature into tissue schizonts (or persist as hypnozoites in vivax/ovale).
  3. Schizont rupture releases 10,000–30,000 merozoites into blood → invade RBCs.
  4. Asexual blood cycle every 48h (falciparum, vivax, ovale) or 72h (malariae) → synchronized cytokine release = paroxysms (chills → fever → sweats).
  5. Some parasites form gametocytes → taken up by mosquito → sexual cycle → new sporozoites.
HIGH-YIELD:
  • Hypnozoites exist only in P. vivax and P. ovale → they are the reason you need primaquine (kills hypnozoites). Must check G6PD first — primaquine causes hemolysis in G6PD-deficient patients.
  • Duffy-antigen negative (FyFy) individuals (most West Africans) are resistant to P. vivax.
  • HbS (sickle trait), HbC, G6PD deficiency, and α-thalassemia are all protective against falciparum malaria — hence evolutionary selection in endemic regions.

Clinical Manifestations

  • General: cyclic fevers, chills, rigors, sweats, headache, hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, jaundice/abnormal LFTs, splenomegaly.
  • Tertian malaria (q48h): P. vivax, ovale, falciparum.
  • Quartan malaria (q72h): P. malariae.
  • P. falciparum typically has persistent/daily fevers (not truly cyclic).
Severe / Complicated P. falciparum (>5% parasitemia or organ dysfunction)
  • Cerebral malaria (altered mental status, seizures, coma).
  • Lactic acidosis.
  • Hypoglycemia.
  • ARDS.
  • Acute kidney injury / "Blackwater fever" (massive intravascular hemolysis with hemoglobinuria).

Why is falciparum so severe? RBCs cytoadhere and sequester in microvasculature of vital organs (brain, kidney) — and falciparum can infect RBCs of all ages, producing very high parasitemia.

Diagnosis

  • Thick & thin blood smears × 3 (Giemsa) — thick for screening/parasitemia, thin for species ID.
  • Rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs) detect HRP-2 or pLDH antigens.
  • PCR for species confirmation / low parasitemia.
Smear Clues
  • P. falciparum: small ring forms, multiple rings in one RBC, banana-shaped gametocytes, high parasitemia.
  • P. vivax / ovale: enlarged RBCs with Schüffner's dots; irregularly-shaped trophozoites.
  • P. malariae: band-form trophozoites.

Treatment

ScenarioFirst-Line Therapy
Uncomplicated P. falciparum (parasitemia <5%, no end-organ involvement)Artemether-lumefantrine (Coartem); alternatives: atovaquone-proguanil, quinine+doxycycline, mefloquine
Severe / complicated P. falciparumIV artesunate (IV quinidine if unavailable)
P. vivax / P. ovale (blood stage)Chloroquine (if sensitive)
P. vivax / P. ovale (hypnozoite radical cure)Primaquine (or tafenoquine) — check G6PD first!
  • Chloroquine-sensitive regions: Dominican Republic, Haiti, Middle East, Central America west of Panama Canal. Everywhere else assume resistance.
⚠️ EXAM TRAP — Primaquine & G6PD ▼

Always screen for G6PD deficiency before giving primaquine or tafenoquine. Severe oxidative hemolysis can occur in deficient patients (classic in African, Mediterranean, SE Asian men).

▼4. Babesia microti (Babesiosis)

Ixodes scapularis tick New England / Cape Cod / upper Midwest Hemolytic anemia + thrombocytopenia Maltese cross Atovaquone + azithromycin

Key Points

  • Pathogen: Babesia microti (intraerythrocytic protozoan).
  • Vector: Ixodes scapularis tick (same as Lyme & Anaplasma — co-infection common).
  • Geography: New England (esp. Cape Cod, Nantucket), Mid-Atlantic, upper Midwest, northern California.
  • Other transmission: blood transfusion.
  • Severe disease risk factors: asplenia, elderly, immunocompromised.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Fever, chills, myalgias, fatigue.
  • Hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, elevated AST/ALT.
  • Severe: ARDS, DIC, hemoglobinuria, AKI.
  • 20% co-infection with Borrelia burgdorferi (Lyme); also with Anaplasma.

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis: Giemsa-stained peripheral smear → intraerythrocytic ring forms; pathognomonic “Maltese cross” tetrads (4 merozoites in a cross). No RBC pigment (unlike malaria). PCR and serology available.
  • Treatment:
    • Mild/moderate: atovaquone + azithromycin.
    • Severe: quinine + clindamycin; consider RBC exchange transfusion if parasitemia >10% or organ failure.
⚠️ EXAM TRAP — Babesia vs. Malaria on Smear ▼
  • Both have intra-erythrocytic ring forms.
  • Babesia: no RBC pigment (hemozoin), no gametocytes, classic Maltese cross, exposure = Northeast US tick bite.
  • Malaria: pigment-filled schizonts/gametocytes, banana-shaped gametocytes in falciparum, exposure = travel to Africa/SE Asia.

🧪 Testing Your Understanding

TYU 1

A 40-year-old returns from a 2-week safari in Kenya with 4 days of fever and headache. He is now confused and unresponsive. Labs show hemolytic anemia, AKI, thrombocytopenia. Peripheral smear shows numerous small ring forms inside RBCs, with multiple rings per cell. What is the most likely pathogen?

✓ Correct: C — P. falciparum with cerebral malaria.

Key clues: Africa travel, severe multi-organ disease, multiple ring forms per RBC (falciparum infects RBCs of all ages → high parasitemia). Treat with IV artesunate. Babesia would be expected with Northeast U.S. exposure and Maltese-cross tetrads.

TYU 2

A 55-year-old Brazilian woman presents with 2 weeks of dyspnea and lower-extremity edema. TTE shows biventricular dilation with an apical aneurysm and EF 28%. She also reports 20 years of progressive constipation and early satiety. What is the most likely diagnosis?

✓ Correct: B — Chronic Chagas disease.

The combination of dilated cardiomyopathy + GI mega-syndrome (megacolon, megaesophagus) in a South/Central American patient is classic. Treatment is benznidazole or nifurtimox, most effective in acute/early chronic disease; chronic cardiomyopathy is largely supportive.

TYU 3

A 62-year-old splenectomized man from Cape Cod develops fevers, malaise, and dark urine 3 weeks after finding a tick on his groin. Labs: Hb 8.1, platelets 62K, AST/ALT 180/210, creatinine 2.4. Peripheral smear shows intra-erythrocytic ring forms and tetrad formations. What is the first-line therapy for this severe case?

✓ Correct: D — Severe babesiosis in an asplenic patient. Quinine + clindamycin with consideration of RBC exchange transfusion for high parasitemia or end-organ damage.

Atovaquone + azithromycin (C) is fine for mild/moderate disease but is inadequate for severe presentations. Maltese-cross tetrads + New England tick exposure + asplenia → Babesia.

Three Routes of Acquisition

Nematodes (roundworms) are acquired by three dominant routes. Remembering the route immediately narrows diagnosis and therapy.

RouteOrganismsSignature Clinical Clue

Strongyloides · Ancylostoma · Schistosoma (trematode, also skin-penetrating) · Hookworm (Necator). These all enter through bare feet or skin contact with contaminated water/soil.

▼2. Ascaris lumbricoides — Giant Intestinal Roundworm

Most prevalent helminth worldwide Loeffler syndrome Bowel obstruction in children Knobby mammillated eggs Albendazole

Organism & Life Cycle

  • Pathogen: Ascaris lumbricoides; adult worms up to 30 cm long.
  • Transmission: fecal–oral ingestion of embryonated eggs from contaminated soil/produce.
  • Life cycle: eggs hatch in small intestine → larvae penetrate gut wall → portal vein → liver → lungs (Loeffler pneumonitis) → coughed up and swallowed → mature into adult worms in jejunum.

Epidemiology / Exposure

  • Most prevalent helminth worldwide — >1 billion infected.
  • Endemic in tropics with poor sanitation; often coinfection with Trichuris & hookworm.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Pulmonary phase (larval migration): Loeffler syndrome — cough, wheeze, transient pulmonary infiltrates with eosinophilia.
  • Intestinal phase: abdominal pain, malnutrition.
  • Heavy burden: bowel obstruction (classic pediatric vignette), biliary/pancreatic duct obstruction, cholangitis, pancreatitis.

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis: stool O&P — characteristic knobby/mammillated eggs.
  • Treatment: albendazole or mebendazole (single dose).

▼3. Trichuris trichiura — Whipworm

Rectal prolapse in children Cecum/colon attachment Bipolar-plug eggs Bloody diarrhea

Organism & Life Cycle

  • Pathogen: Trichuris trichiura.
  • Transmission: fecal–oral ingestion of embryonated eggs. No tissue/lung migration.
  • Life cycle: adults embed into the cecum/colon mucosa.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Light infection: usually asymptomatic.
  • Heavy pediatric burden: chronic bloody diarrhea, anemia, and rectal prolapse (classic board clue).

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis: football-shaped eggs with bipolar plugs on stool O&P.
  • Treatment: albendazole or mebendazole × 3 days.

▼4. Hookworm — Ancylostoma duodenale / Necator americanus

Skin penetration (barefoot) Ground itch Iron-deficiency anemia Filariform larva Albendazole + iron

Organism & Life Cycle

  • Pathogens: Ancylostoma duodenale and Necator americanus.
  • Transmission: filariform larva penetrates intact skin (often bare foot) on warm, moist soil.
  • Life cycle: skin → blood → lungs → coughed up & swallowed → adult worms attach to small-intestinal mucosa and suck blood.

Clinical Manifestations

  • “Ground itch” at site of skin entry.
  • Chronic blood loss → iron-deficiency microcytic anemia (cardinal feature); in pregnancy/children may cause failure to thrive.
HIGH-YIELD:
  • Pediatric IDA + barefoot rural exposure → think hookworm. Treat empirically with albendazole and iron replacement.

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis: stool O&P (ovoid, thin-shelled eggs).
  • Treatment: albendazole or mebendazole; iron replacement.

▼5. Enterobius vermicularis — Pinworm

Nocturnal perianal itching Scotch-tape test Most common US helminth Pyrantel or albendazole

Organism & Life Cycle

  • Pathogen: Enterobius vermicularis.
  • Transmission: fecal–oral via ingested eggs; autoinfection from scratching.
  • Life cycle: gravid female migrates to perianal skin at night to lay eggs → intense pruritus ani → scratching spreads eggs.

Epidemiology / Exposure

  • Most common helminthic infection in the United States.
  • Especially common in school-age children; spreads rapidly within households.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Nocturnal perianal itching is the hallmark; may be asymptomatic.
  • Occasional vulvovaginitis or secondary bacterial infection from scratching.
⚠️ EXAM TRAP — Pinworm diagnosis ▼
Pinworm eggs are rarely seen in stool. If the vignette describes a child with night-time perianal itching and “stool O&P was negative,” the next step is the cellophane (Scotch) tape test applied to the perianal skin in the early morning — not another stool sample.

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis: Scotch-tape test → flattened, D-shaped eggs.
  • Treatment: albendazole or pyrantel pamoate; repeat in 2 weeks; treat entire household.

▼6. Strongyloides stercoralis — Autoinfection & Hyperinfection

Autoinfection Larva currens Hyperinfection in steroids / transplant Gram-negative bacteremia Ivermectin

Organism & Life Cycle

  • Pathogen: Strongyloides stercoralis.
  • Transmission: filariform larva penetrates skin → lungs → gut.
  • Unique feature — autoinfection: larvae can transform into the infectious filariform form within the host and re-penetrate the gut wall or perianal skin, sustaining infection for decades.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Chronic infection: often subclinical; intermittent abdominal pain, diarrhea, urticaria, and larva currens (serpiginous migratory rash).
  • Hyperinfection / disseminated disease: in immunocompromised hosts (corticosteroids, organ transplant, HTLV-1). Presents with gram-negative bacteremia/meningitis (from bowel translocation), ARDS, and high mortality.
⚠️ EXAM TRAP — Steroids + Strongyloides = Catastrophe ▼
A classic vignette: an immigrant given high-dose steroids for presumed asthma/COPD flare develops E. coli bacteremia and ARDS. This is Strongyloides hyperinfection — screen (serology) and treat with ivermectin before starting immunosuppression.

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis: stool agar plate culture or serology — O&P is insensitive (intermittent shedding). Suspect in any immigrant/refugee with unexplained eosinophilia.
  • Treatment: ivermectin (first line). Screen before chronic steroids or transplant.

▼7. Toxocara canis / cati — Visceral & Ocular Larva Migrans

Dog/cat feces Pica / sandbox exposure Eosinophilia + hepatosplenomegaly Unilateral retinal granuloma

Organism & Life Cycle

  • Pathogens: Toxocara canis and T. cati.
  • Transmission: ingestion of eggs from dog/cat feces (pica — toddlers eating dirt or sandbox exposure).
  • Life cycle: larvae migrate systemically; humans are a dead-end host (no adult worms).

Clinical Manifestations

  • Visceral larva migrans: hepatosplenomegaly, fever, eosinophilia, pulmonary infiltrates.
  • Ocular larva migrans: unilateral retinal granuloma mistaken for retinoblastoma → vision loss.

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis: serology (ELISA).
  • Treatment: albendazole ± corticosteroids for ocular disease.

▼8. Trichinella spiralis

Undercooked pork / bear / wild game Periorbital edema Myositis Eosinophilia Elevated CK

Organism & Life Cycle

  • Pathogen: Trichinella spiralis.
  • Transmission: ingestion of encysted larvae in undercooked pork, bear, or wild game.
  • Life cycle: larvae released in gut → enter circulation → encyst in striated muscle.

Clinical Manifestations

  • GI prodrome (diarrhea) in the first week.
  • Classic triad: periorbital edema + myositis (muscle pain) + eosinophilia, often with fever.
  • Severe cases: myocarditis, encephalitis.

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis: serology and/or muscle biopsy showing encysted larvae; elevated CK.
  • Treatment: albendazole; steroids for severe myositis or CNS involvement.
HIGH-YIELD — Nematode first-line therapy:
  • Albendazole / mebendazole: Ascaris, Trichuris, hookworm, Enterobius, Toxocara, Trichinella.
  • Ivermectin: Strongyloides (first line); also onchocerciasis.
  • Pyrantel pamoate: alternative for Enterobius (safe in pregnancy).

TYU 1

A 4-year-old from rural Kentucky presents with a microcytic anemia and mild abdominal pain. He is often barefoot outdoors. Stool O&P shows thin-shelled, ovoid eggs. What is the most likely organism and best therapy?

Answer: Hookworm (Necator americanus). Filariform larvae penetrated the skin; adult worms attach to intestinal mucosa and cause chronic blood loss → iron-deficiency anemia. Treat with albendazole + iron replacement.

TYU 2

A 62-year-old immigrant from the Caribbean is admitted for an asthma exacerbation and receives high-dose IV methylprednisolone. Two days later he develops fever, ARDS, and E. coli bacteremia. Eosinophilia is noted on admission labs. What is the most likely organism and treatment?

Answer: Strongyloides stercoralis hyperinfection syndrome. Treat with ivermectin; in retrospect this patient should have been screened with Strongyloides serology before steroids.

TYU 3

▼1. Cestode Framework — Definitive vs. Intermediate Host

Cysticercus Definitive vs. intermediate host Praziquantel Albendazole (tissue phase)

Route Overview

Cestodes (tapeworms) are acquired by ingesting larval cysts (cysticerci / metacestodes) in undercooked meat or fish, or by accidentally ingesting tapeworm eggs from contaminated food/water/dog feces. The clinical picture depends on whether the human serves as the definitive host (adult worm in gut) or the intermediate host (larval cyst in tissue).

▼2. Taenia solium — Pork Tapeworm & Neurocysticercosis

Neurocysticercosis New-onset seizures (adult) Calcified scolex Albendazole + steroids

Organism & Life Cycle

  • Pathogen: Taenia solium.
  • Two very different pictures:
    1. Taeniasis: ingest larval cyst (cysticercus) in undercooked pork → adult worm in intestine → usually mild GI symptoms.
    2. Cysticercosis / neurocysticercosis: ingest eggs (fecal–oral from a human carrier) → larvae migrate to muscle, eye, or brain → form cysts.

Epidemiology / Exposure

  • Endemic in Latin America, sub-Saharan Africa, Asia.
  • Seen in immigrants and travelers to endemic regions.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Intestinal taeniasis: mild abdominal symptoms; proglottids in stool.
  • Neurocysticercosis: new-onset seizures in adults from endemic regions — signature exam clue.
  • Imaging: multiple small cystic lesions with calcified scolex on CT/MRI.
⚠️ EXAM TRAP — New-onset seizure vignette ▼
An adult immigrant from Mexico/Central America with new-onset seizures and ring-enhancing or calcified brain lesions on CT = neurocysticercosis, not primary brain tumor or toxoplasmosis. Treat with albendazole + steroids.

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis: stool O&P for intestinal disease; CT/MRI + serology for cysticercosis.
  • Treatment:
    • Intestinal taeniasis: praziquantel.
    • Neurocysticercosis: albendazole + corticosteroids (blunt inflammation from dying cysts) + anti-epileptics. Avoid treating purely inactive / calcified lesions.

▼3. Taenia saginata — Beef Tapeworm

Undercooked beef Motile proglottids No cysticercosis

Organism & Life Cycle

  • Pathogen: Taenia saginata.
  • Transmission: ingestion of cysticerci in undercooked beef.
  • Humans are only the definitive host — cysticercosis does not occur with T. saginata.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Mild abdominal discomfort.
  • Patients may notice motile proglottids in stool or underwear.

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis: proglottids / eggs on stool O&P.
  • Treatment: praziquantel.

▼4. Diphyllobothrium latum — Fish Tapeworm

Raw freshwater fish B12 deficiency Megaloblastic anemia Operculated eggs

Organism & Life Cycle

  • Pathogen: Diphyllobothrium latum; adult worms can exceed 10 meters.
  • Transmission: ingestion of larvae in raw/undercooked freshwater fish (sushi, sashimi, ceviche, smoked fish).

Clinical Manifestations

  • Often asymptomatic; mild abdominal symptoms.
  • Worm competitively absorbs vitamin B12 → megaloblastic anemia and occasional neurologic symptoms in chronic infection.
HIGH-YIELD:
  • Patient with B12 deficiency and megaloblastic anemia who eats raw freshwater fish → think D. latum.
  • This is the only helminth that classically causes B12 deficiency.

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis: operculated eggs on stool O&P.
  • Treatment: praziquantel + B12 replacement.

▼5. Echinococcus granulosus — Hydatid Disease

Dog-sheep cycle Hepatic hydatid cyst Eggshell calcification Anaphylaxis if ruptured PAIR procedure

Organism & Life Cycle

  • Pathogen: Echinococcus granulosus.
  • Life cycle: dogs are definitive hosts; sheep are usual intermediate hosts. Humans accidentally ingest eggs from dog feces.
  • Larvae migrate to the liver and form slow-growing hydatid cysts.

Epidemiology / Exposure

  • Sheep-herding regions: Middle East, Mediterranean, South America, Central Asia.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Large hepatic cyst with daughter cysts (“eggshell calcification” on CT).
  • Rupture → release of antigenic fluid → anaphylaxis.
  • May also involve lung, bone, brain.

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis: imaging (CT/US) + serology.
  • Treatment: albendazole pre/post-procedure + PAIR (Puncture, Aspiration, Injection of scolicidal agent, Re-aspiration) or surgical cystectomy. Do not simply aspirate without scolicide — spillage causes dissemination and anaphylaxis.

▼6. Schistosoma (Trematodes / Flukes)

Freshwater snail + skin penetration Pipe-stem fibrosis Hematuria Bladder SCC Praziquantel

Organism & Life Cycle

  • Pathogens: S. mansoni, S. japonicum, S. haematobium.
  • Life cycle: freshwater snail intermediate host releases cercariae → penetrate human skin during wading/swimming → mature into adult worms in venous plexuses (mesenteric or vesical).

Epidemiology & Chronic Syndromes

SpeciesGeographyVenous PlexusChronic Disease
S. mansoniSub-Saharan Africa, South America, CaribbeanMesentericIntestinal fibrosis, periportal (“pipe-stem”) fibrosis → portal hypertension, varices
S. japonicumEast AsiaMesentericSimilar to mansoni; more severe hepatic disease
S. haematobiumAfrica, Middle EastVesical (bladder)Hematuria, bladder fibrosis, squamous cell carcinoma of the bladder

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis: eggs on stool O&P (mansoni/japonicum) or urine (haematobium). S. mansoni has a lateral spine; S. haematobium has a terminal spine.
  • Treatment: praziquantel for all species.
HIGH-YIELD — Praziquantel is the antiplatyhelminth:
  • Praziquantel treats virtually all flatworms: Taenia, D. latum, Schistosoma, Clonorchis, Paragonimus.
  • Albendazole is preferred for tissue-phase disease: neurocysticercosis, hydatid disease, nematodes.

TYU 1

A 34-year-old man who immigrated from Mexico 5 years ago presents after a witnessed seizure. MRI of the brain shows multiple small cystic lesions, one with a calcified center. What is the most likely diagnosis and appropriate therapy?

Answer: Neurocysticercosis from Taenia solium egg ingestion. The calcified scolex within a cyst is classic. Treat with albendazole plus corticosteroids (steroids blunt the inflammatory response from dying larvae), plus anti-epileptics for seizure control.

TYU 2

A 45-year-old avid consumer of homemade gravlax and smoked freshwater trout presents with fatigue and pallor. Labs show macrocytic anemia with hypersegmented neutrophils and low B12. What is the most likely cause?

Answer: Diphyllobothrium latum (fish tapeworm). It competitively absorbs B12 in the intestine. Diagnose with stool O&P (operculated eggs). Treat with praziquantel and replace B12.

TYU 3

A 27-year-old returns from 3 months in rural Egypt where he swam regularly in the Nile. He now has painless gross hematuria. Urinalysis shows ova with a terminal spine. What is the causative organism, and what long-term complication must be screened for?

Answer: Schistosoma haematobium (terminal-spine egg, bladder venous plexus). Chronic infection causes bladder fibrosis and predisposes to squamous cell carcinoma of the bladder. Treat with praziquantel.

Q1

A 24-year-old man returns from a month of hiking in Colorado where he drank untreated stream water. He now has 10 days of foul-smelling, fatty, non-bloody diarrhea with bloating and belching. Stool O&P shows pear-shaped, flagellated, binucleate trophozoites. What is the first-line therapy?

A. Metronidazole

B. Praziquantel

C. Nitazoxanide

D. Albendazole

E. Ivermectin

Answer: A. Metronidazole. This is classic Giardia lamblia (pear-shaped binucleate trophozoite with two "eyes," flagella; stream/beaver water). Tinidazole is an acceptable alternative. Nitazoxanide (C) is first line for Cryptosporidium, not Giardia.

Q2

A 32-year-old man with AIDS (CD4 count 40) has 4 weeks of profuse, watery, non-bloody diarrhea and 10-lb weight loss. Modified acid-fast stain of stool reveals small (4–6 μm) oocysts. What is the best treatment?

A. Metronidazole

B. TMP–SMX

C. Nitazoxanide plus initiation of antiretroviral therapy

D. Paromomycin

E. Praziquantel

Answer: C. Nitazoxanide plus ART. Cryptosporidium (small modified acid-fast oocyst) in an AIDS patient. The most effective therapy is immune reconstitution with ART; nitazoxanide has modest activity. TMP–SMX (B) is for Cystoisospora and Cyclospora, which have larger oocysts.

Q3

A 40-year-old man returns from a 2-week trip to rural Mexico with fever, RUQ pain, and tender hepatomegaly. Ultrasound shows a 6-cm subcapsular liver abscess. Serology for Entamoeba histolytica is positive; stool O&P is negative. What is the best treatment?

A. Metronidazole alone

B. Metronidazole followed by paromomycin

C. Percutaneous drainage alone

D. Praziquantel

E. Albendazole

Answer: B. Metronidazole followed by paromomycin (or other luminal agent such as iodoquinol). Metronidazole kills invasive trophozoites in tissue; a luminal agent is required afterward to eradicate cysts in the colon and prevent relapse. Drainage is reserved for large or impending-rupture abscesses.

Q4

A 28-year-old refugee from Ethiopia presents with 2 weeks of fevers spiking every 48 hours, malaise, and jaundice. Giemsa-stained thick and thin smears show ring forms and Schüffner stippling in enlarged red cells. Which drug(s) are required?

A. Chloroquine only

B. Chloroquine plus primaquine (after G6PD testing)

C. Artemether–lumefantrine only

D. Mefloquine only

E. IV artesunate

Answer: B. Chloroquine plus primaquine after G6PD testing. Schüffner stippling + 48-hour (tertian) fever = P. vivax or P. ovale. These species form hypnozoites in the liver that require primaquine (or tafenoquine) for radical cure. Always screen for G6PD deficiency before giving primaquine (hemolysis risk).

Q5

A 50-year-old man traveled to Ghana and returns with fever, confusion, and renal failure. A thin blood smear shows banana-shaped gametocytes and heavy parasitemia (8%). What is the best initial therapy?

A. Oral chloroquine

B. Oral atovaquone–proguanil

C. IV artesunate

D. Primaquine

E. Doxycycline

Answer: C. IV artesunate. Banana-shaped gametocytes and high parasitemia (≥5%) with end-organ dysfunction define severe P. falciparum. Severe malaria requires parenteral artesunate. P. falciparum does NOT form hypnozoites — no primaquine needed for radical cure.

Q6

A 58-year-old man on Nantucket in August develops fever, chills, and fatigue. He is asplenic from prior trauma. CBC shows hemolytic anemia. Blood smear reveals ring forms and a tetrad ("Maltese cross") in red cells. What is the most likely organism?

A. P. falciparum

B. Babesia microti

C. Trypanosoma cruzi

D. Leishmania donovani

E. Ehrlichia chaffeensis

Answer: B. Babesia microti. Northeast US / Ixodes tick exposure, asplenia (severe disease), and the tetrad (Maltese cross) on smear are diagnostic. Treat with atovaquone + azithromycin (mild–moderate) or clindamycin + quinine (severe). No hypnozoites — Babesia is a piroplasm, not a Plasmodium.

Q7

A 35-year-old man from rural Bolivia now lives in the US and is being evaluated for cardiomyopathy. Echo shows dilated cardiomyopathy and an apical aneurysm. Serology for Trypanosoma cruzi is positive. Which other clinical feature is most likely?

A. Megaesophagus/megacolon

B. Splenomegaly with pancytopenia

C. Cutaneous nodule with satellite lesions

D. Hepatic hydatid cyst

E. Periorbital edema with myositis

Answer: A. Megaesophagus/megacolon. Chronic Chagas disease destroys autonomic ganglia → cardiomyopathy plus megaorgan syndromes (achalasia-like dysphagia, chronic constipation/megacolon). Vector: reduviid "kissing bug" (Latin America). Treat chronic disease with benznidazole or nifurtimox.

Q8

A 10-year-old boy from rural Kentucky has microcytic anemia, pallor, and a history of walking barefoot. Stool O&P shows ovoid, thin-shelled eggs. What is the best therapy?

A. Metronidazole

B. Albendazole plus iron

C. Praziquantel

D. Ivermectin

E. TMP–SMX

Answer: B. Albendazole plus iron. Hookworm (Necator/Ancylostoma) — skin penetration by filariform larvae, chronic blood loss, iron-deficiency anemia.

Q9

A 62-year-old man from Puerto Rico starts high-dose prednisone for an asthma exacerbation. Three days later he develops fever, ARDS, and gram-negative bacteremia. Admission labs had shown eosinophilia. Which condition was missed?

A. Ascaris lumbricoides

B. Trichuris trichiura

C. Strongyloides stercoralis (hyperinfection)

D. Taenia solium

E. Giardia lamblia

Answer: C. Strongyloides stercoralis hyperinfection. Autoinfection + steroid immunosuppression → massive larval dissemination with gram-negative bacteremia (gut translocation) and pulmonary involvement. Screen (serology) and treat with ivermectin before immunosuppression.

Q10

A 6-year-old girl has 3 weeks of nighttime perianal itching. Two siblings are affected. Stool O&P is negative. What is the next best step?

A. Repeat stool O&P

B. Scotch-tape test

C. Serology for Toxocara

D. Colonoscopy

E. Empiric metronidazole

Answer: B. Scotch-tape test. Pinworm eggs are laid on perianal skin; stool O&P is insensitive. Treat the child and household with albendazole or pyrantel pamoate; repeat in 2 weeks.

Q11

A 34-year-old man from Mexico presents after a first-time generalized tonic-clonic seizure. MRI reveals multiple small brain cysts, one with a central calcification. What is the best therapy?

A. Praziquantel alone

B. Albendazole plus corticosteroids

Q12
