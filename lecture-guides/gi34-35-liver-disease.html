▼Six Major Histologic Injury Patterns

⭐ The liver has a limited vocabulary for responding to injury — nearly every insult produces one of six recognizable histologic patterns, and recognizing the pattern narrows the differential immediately.

🔥 Pattern 1 — Hepatitic (Inflammatory)

Hepatocyte injury with lobular inflammation. Dominant lesion is hepatocyte apoptosis (Councilman bodies — shrunken, eosinophilic apoptotic hepatocytes) and ballooning degeneration (swollen, pale hepatocytes — failing cells). Portal tracts contain lymphocytes; if inflammation spills across the limiting plate into the lobule, call it interface hepatitis (chronic).

Think: viral hepatitis, autoimmune hepatitis, drug-induced hepatitis (DILI), ischemia.

Labs: AST/ALT >> Alk phos (hepatocellular pattern).

🟢 Pattern 2 — Cholestatic

Impaired bile flow. Histology shows bile plugs in canaliculi (brown/green pigment between hepatocytes), hepatocyte"feathery degeneration" from bile toxicity, and bile duct proliferation in chronic cases.

Think: bile duct obstruction (stone, stricture, tumor), primary biliary cholangitis, primary sclerosing cholangitis, drug-induced cholestasis.

Labs: Alk phos & GGT >> AST/ALT; conjugated hyperbilirubinemia.

🧈 Pattern 3 — Steatotic (Fatty)

Accumulation of lipid droplets within hepatocytes. Two morphologies:

  • Macrovesicular — a single large fat droplet pushes the nucleus to the side (most common; alcohol, obesity, diabetes, MASLD).
  • Microvesicular — many small droplets; nucleus stays central. More dangerous; suggests mitochondrial injury (Reye syndrome, acute fatty liver of pregnancy, valproate toxicity, tetracyclines).

When steatosis is accompanied by inflammation and hepatocyte ballooning, it becomes steatohepatitis (MASH/NASH or alcoholic hepatitis).

🩸 Pattern 4 — Vascular

Injury to sinusoids or central veins. Classic finding is centrilobular (zone 3) congestion and hemorrhagic necrosis — red blood cells pool in sinusoids around central veins, with loss of hepatocytes.

Think: right heart failure ("nutmeg liver"), Budd-Chiari syndrome, sinusoidal obstruction syndrome (SOS/VOD), shock liver.

🌳 Pattern 5 — Biliary

Targeting of bile ductules and bile ducts. Expect bile duct destruction or proliferation, portal fibrosis, and sometimes granulomas (PBC). Can overlap clinically with cholestatic pattern but histologically focuses on duct epithelium.

Think: PBC (florid duct lesion), PSC (onion-skin periductal fibrosis), biliary atresia (infants).

🎯 Pattern 6 — Mass Lesion

Focal lesion: benign (hemangioma, focal nodular hyperplasia, hepatic adenoma) or malignant (hepatocellular carcinoma, cholangiocarcinoma, metastasis). Evaluate with imaging first; biopsy only when imaging is indeterminate because of bleeding risk.

⭐ High-Yield:
  • Councilman body = apoptotic hepatocyte (viral/AIH classic).
  • Ballooning = dying hepatocyte (steatohepatitis, DILI).
  • Interface hepatitis = chronic hepatitis hallmark (AIH, chronic HCV).
  • Macrovesicular steatosis = alcohol/obesity; microvesicular = mitochondrial toxicity.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Micro- vs. Macrovesicular Fat
  • Students lump all"fatty liver" together. On Step 1, microvesicular steatosis is the scary one — it points to mitochondrial β-oxidation failure. Classic vignettes: child with viral illness given aspirin (Reye syndrome), third-trimester pregnancy with vomiting and elevated ammonia (acute fatty liver of pregnancy), or a patient on valproate/tetracycline with sudden liver failure.
  • Macrovesicular is the chronic, reversible pattern of alcohol/MASLD.

▼Liver Function Tests & Interpretation

⭐"LFTs" is a misnomer — AST, ALT, alk phos, and GGT measure hepatocyte injury, not function. True function tests are albumin, bilirubin, and PT/INR.

🧪 Markers of Injury (Hepatocellular vs. Cholestatic)

MarkerWhat it reflectsKey points
ALT (alanine aminotransferase)Hepatocyte injury — cytoplasmic enzymeMore liver-specific than AST. ↑↑ in viral hepatitis, DILI.
AST (aspartate aminotransferase)Hepatocyte injury — cytoplasm + mitochondriaAlso in muscle, RBCs, heart. AST > ALT (2:1) suggests alcoholic liver disease.
Alk phos (ALP)Biliary epithelium / obstructionAlso in bone, placenta. Elevated in cholestasis, mets, PBC, PSC.
GGTBiliary — confirms liver source of ↑ALPAlso ↑ in heavy alcohol use (enzyme induction).
⚡ R-Ratio Logic:
  • Hepatocellular pattern: ALT (& AST) markedly elevated, ALP near normal. Ratio ALT/ALP (as fold-elevation) > 5.
  • Cholestatic pattern: ALP markedly elevated, ALT mildly elevated. Ratio < 2.
  • Mixed: ratio 2–5.

🎯 True Liver Function Tests

TestWhat it measuresAbnormality implies
AlbuminSynthetic function (half-life ~20 days)Low = chronic hepatic dysfunction or malnutrition/nephrotic syndrome. Slow to change.
PT / INRSynthetic function — factors II, VII, IX, X (vit-K-dependent)Prolonged = acute OR chronic liver dysfunction. Factor VII has ~6-hr half-life → earliest marker of acute failure.
Bilirubin (total & direct)Uptake, conjugation, excretion↑ Indirect: hemolysis, Gilbert, Crigler-Najjar. ↑ Direct: obstruction, intrahepatic cholestasis, Dubin-Johnson, Rotor.

🧠 Mnemonic:"ABC PT" = True Liver Function

A — Albumin · B — Bilirubin · C — Coagulation (PT/INR)

  • These three tell you whether the liver is working.
  • AST/ALT/ALP only tell you whether it is injured.
  • A hepatocyte can be badly damaged with normal"LFTs" (burned-out cirrhosis) — synthetic labs always win.
⚠️ Exam Trap: AST/ALT in Alcohol

AST > ALT in a 2:1 ratio is almost pathognomonic for alcoholic liver disease in the real world. Mechanism: alcohol depletes pyridoxine (B6), which ALT needs more than AST does; mitochondrial AST also leaks with alcohol injury. Nearly every other hepatitis has ALT > AST.

▼Jaundice & Bilirubin Metabolism

⭐ Yellow skin/sclerae appears when total bilirubin exceeds ~2.5 mg/dL. The step at which bilirubin metabolism breaks tells you the cause.

🔬 The Normal Pathway (memorize cold)

  1. RBC breakdown → heme → biliverdin → unconjugated (indirect) bilirubin — water-insoluble, bound to albumin, transported to liver.
  2. Hepatocyte uptake via OATP.
  3. Conjugation with glucuronic acid by UGT1A1 → conjugated (direct) bilirubin — water-soluble.
  4. Excretion into bile canaliculi via MRP2.
  5. Gut bacteria convert to urobilinogen → most excreted in stool (stercobilin, brown color); some reabsorbed and excreted in urine (urobilin, yellow color).

📊 Classification of Hyperbilirubinemia

TypeMechanismClassic causesUrine bilirubin
Unconjugated (indirect) — pre-hepaticOverproduction or impaired uptake/conjugationHemolysis, neonatal physiologic, Gilbert (stress/fasting), Crigler-NajjarAbsent (unconjugated is albumin-bound → cannot filter)
Conjugated (direct) — hepatic/post-hepaticDefective excretion or obstructionDubin-Johnson, Rotor, hepatitis, stone/stricture/tumor in biliary treePresent (water-soluble, dark urine)

🧬 Inherited Hyperbilirubinemias (Step 1 classics)

Gilbert syndrome

Mild, intermittent unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia triggered by fasting, stress, illness, or exercise. UGT1A1 activity reduced to ~30%. Benign, extremely common (~5% of population). Normal liver on biopsy.

Crigler-Najjar type I

Complete absence of UGT1A1. Severe unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia in newborns → kernicterus (bilirubin deposition in basal ganglia → permanent neurologic damage or death). Treatment: phototherapy ± liver transplant.

Dubin-Johnson & Rotor

Defective hepatic excretion of conjugated bilirubin. Mild, benign, direct hyperbilirubinemia. Dubin-Johnson = black liver (MRP2 defect, grossly pigmented on biopsy). Rotor = no pigmentation, milder (OATP defect).

⚠️ Exam Trap: Urine Color & Bilirubin
  • Dark urine with pale stools = conjugated hyperbilirubinemia from obstructive/post-hepatic cause (bile cannot reach the gut). Classic for pancreatic head tumor, common bile duct stone.
  • Dark urine with normal stools = intrahepatic conjugated hyperbilirubinemia (hepatitis) — some bile still reaches gut.
  • No bilirubin in urine despite jaundice = unconjugated (hemolysis, Gilbert).

▼Cholestasis

⭐ Cholestasis = impaired bile flow, either from obstruction (extrahepatic) or failure of hepatocyte/canalicular transport (intrahepatic). The clinical consequences flow from bile failing to reach the duodenum.

📋 Intrahepatic vs. Extrahepatic

IntrahepaticExtrahepatic (obstructive)
MechanismDefective hepatocyte/canalicular secretionMechanical blockage of ducts
CausesPBC, PSC (intra-duct), pregnancy cholestasis, DILI (estrogens, erythromycin, anabolic steroids), sepsis, TPN, genetic (PFIC)CBD stone, pancreatic head cancer, cholangiocarcinoma, PSC (extra-duct), biliary atresia
ImagingNon-dilated ductsDilated ducts on US/MRCP

🧑‍⚕️ Clinical Features

  • Jaundice with conjugated hyperbilirubinemia.
  • Pruritus — retained bile salts deposit in skin; severe, classic for PBC and pregnancy cholestasis.
  • Pale, fatty stools (steatorrhea) — no bile salts in gut → malabsorption of fats and fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K).
  • Dark urine — conjugated bilirubin filtered and excreted.
  • Hypercholesterolemia → xanthomas (especially periocular xanthelasmas in PBC).
  • Prolonged PT from vitamin K malabsorption — corrects with IV/IM vitamin K (unlike PT prolongation from synthetic failure).
  • Labs: ↑↑ ALP, ↑ GGT, ↑ direct bilirubin, modest ↑ AST/ALT.
⚡ High-Yield:
  • Charcot triad (ascending cholangitis): fever, RUQ pain, jaundice.
  • Reynolds pentad: add hypotension + altered mental status = suppurative cholangitis → emergency ERCP.
  • PT correction with vitamin K differentiates cholestasis (corrects) from hepatocellular failure (doesn't).
⚠️ Exam Trap: PBC vs. PSC
  • PBC (primary biliary cholangitis): middle-aged woman, pruritus, xanthomas, anti-mitochondrial Ab (AMA), florid duct lesion, associated with other autoimmune disease. Intrahepatic ducts only.
  • PSC (primary sclerosing cholangitis): young man with ulcerative colitis, p-ANCA,"onion-skin" periductal fibrosis,"beaded" intra- AND extra-hepatic ducts on MRCP. High risk of cholangiocarcinoma.

▼Acute Hepatitis

⭐ Acute hepatitis = new-onset hepatocellular inflammation lasting < 6 months. Classic prodrome (malaise, nausea, RUQ discomfort) precedes jaundice; most cases self-limited.

Major Causes

  • Viral — Hep A, B, C, D, E (covered in detail in LO tab 4).
  • Drug-induced (DILI) — acetaminophen (most common cause of ALF in the US), INH, amiodarone, halothane, mushrooms (Amanita phalloides).
  • Ischemic — shock liver; AST & ALT in thousands, rapid fall.
  • Autoimmune hepatitis — covered in LO tab 4.

🔬 Histology

Lobular disarray with apoptotic hepatocytes (Councilman bodies), ballooning degeneration, focal spotty necrosis, and lymphocytic portal & lobular inflammation. Most lobular inflammation concentrates around the central vein (zone 3).

🧪 Labs & Clinical

  • ALT/AST elevated into hundreds to thousands; ALT usually > AST (except alcohol).
  • Bilirubin rises late (mixed pattern).
  • PT/INR normal unless progressing toward acute liver failure.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Acetaminophen Zones

Acetaminophen produces centrilobular (zone 3) necrosis because zone 3 hepatocytes have the highest CYP2E1 activity → most NAPQI produced there. Antidote: N-acetylcysteine (replenishes glutathione). Ischemic injury also hits zone 3 (furthest from arterial supply).

▼Acute & Chronic Liver Failure

⭐ Liver failure = loss of synthetic function (jaundice + coagulopathy + encephalopathy). Acute = < 26 weeks in previously normal liver; chronic = cirrhosis-based (covered in Cirrhosis tab).

⚡ Acute Liver Failure (ALF) — Definition

Triad:

  1. Coagulopathy — INR > 1.5
  2. Hepatic encephalopathy — any grade
  3. No prior liver disease, illness < 26 weeks

Sub-classified as hyperacute (< 7 d — best prognosis, e.g., acetaminophen), acute (1–4 wk), or subacute (4–26 wk — worst prognosis).

Major Causes (know the top 3)

  1. Acetaminophen (~ 46% of US cases)
  2. Idiosyncratic DILI
  3. Viral hepatitis (HAV, HBV; HEV in pregnancy)

Others: autoimmune, Wilson disease (acute presentation), Budd-Chiari, ischemic hepatitis, Amanita mushroom.

💥 Complications of ALF

  • Hepatic encephalopathy & cerebral edema — ammonia-driven; leading cause of death. Treat with lactulose, rifaximin, head elevation, hypertonic saline for ICP.
  • Coagulopathy & bleeding — reduced clotting factors.
  • Hypoglycemia — failed gluconeogenesis/glycogenolysis.
  • Lactic acidosis — impaired lactate clearance.
  • Renal failure (hepatorenal syndrome), infection (SBP), multi-organ failure.

🧠 Hepatic Encephalopathy — Mechanism

Gut-derived ammonia normally converted to urea by the liver. When the liver fails (or blood is shunted past it via portal-systemic collaterals), ammonia crosses the blood-brain barrier, is taken up by astrocytes, combines with glutamate to form glutamine → osmotic swelling → cerebral edema and altered mentation.

Precipitants (memorize): GI bleed, infection (SBP!), constipation, hypokalemia/alkalosis, dehydration, TIPS, sedatives, high-protein meal.

Signs: asterixis ("liver flap"), day/night reversal, confusion, coma. Stage I–IV.

Treatment: lactulose (traps NH₃ as NH₄⁺ in the colon and purges it) + rifaximin (reduces urease-producing gut bacteria).

⚡ High-Yield — King's College Criteria (Acetaminophen ALF):
  • Arterial pH < 7.3, OR
  • INR > 6.5 + creatinine > 3.4 mg/dL + grade III/IV encephalopathy
  • → meets criteria for emergency liver transplant.
🧠 Mnemonic:"HEPATIC" (ALF complications)
  • H — Hypoglycemia
  • E — Encephalopathy / cerebral Edema
  • P — Platelet + clotting dysfunction (coagulopathy)
  • A — Acidosis (lactic)
  • T — Toxins build up (ammonia)
  • I — Infection (spontaneous bacterial peritonitis)
  • C — Cardiorenal failure (hepatorenal syndrome)
⚠️ Exam Trap: Lactulose Mechanism

Students say"lactulose is a laxative." Not the point. Lactulose is metabolized by colonic bacteria to lactic acid → acidifies colon lumen → NH₃ + H⁺ → NH₄⁺ (ionized, nonabsorbable) → trapped and excreted. The laxative effect is a bonus, not the mechanism.

▼Portal Hypertension

⭐ Portal HTN = sustained elevation of portal venous pressure (HVPG > 5 mmHg; clinically significant > 10 mmHg). Its complications — varices, ascites, splenomegaly, encephalopathy — cause most cirrhosis deaths.

💥 Six Consequences — Memorize

  1. Varices — porto-systemic collaterals: esophageal (L gastric → azygos), umbilical/caput medusae (paraumbilical veins), anorectal (superior rectal → inferior rectal; not the same as hemorrhoids!), retroperitoneal (rare). Variceal hemorrhage = leading cause of death in portal HTN.
  2. Ascites — hydrostatic pressure + hypoalbuminemia + nitric-oxide–driven splanchnic vasodilation → renal Na⁺/water retention. SAAG ≥ 1.1 g/dL.
  3. Congestive splenomegaly → thrombocytopenia (hypersplenism).
  4. Hepatic encephalopathy — systemic shunting of NH₃-rich portal blood past the liver.
  5. Hepatopulmonary syndrome — intrapulmonary vascular dilations → platypnea, orthodeoxia.
  6. Hepatorenal syndrome — splanchnic vasodilation → effective arterial underfilling → renal vasoconstriction → AKI.

🩸 Variceal Bleeding — Management

  • Primary prevention: nonselective β-blockers (propranolol, nadolol) — β₂ blockade causes splanchnic vasoconstriction → ↓ portal flow.
  • Active bleed: IV octreotide (somatostatin analog → splanchnic vasoconstriction) + endoscopic band ligation + ceftriaxone prophylaxis.
  • Refractory: TIPS (transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt) — decompresses but precipitates encephalopathy.
⚡ High-Yield — SAAG:
  • SAAG = serum albumin − ascitic albumin.
  • ≥ 1.1 g/dL: portal HTN (cirrhosis, CHF, Budd-Chiari).
  • < 1.1 g/dL: NOT portal HTN — peritoneal carcinomatosis, TB peritonitis, nephrotic, pancreatitis.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Hemorrhoids vs. Anorectal Varices

Students assume hemorrhoids = portal HTN. Wrong. Hemorrhoids are dilated internal pudendal vein tributaries and occur in people with normal portal pressure. Anorectal varices (true porto-systemic collaterals) form between the superior rectal vein (portal system) and the middle/inferior rectal veins (systemic) — these indicate portal HTN.

Testing Your Understanding — LO 1, Question 1

A third-year medical student reviews a liver biopsy from a 16-year-old with a recent viral illness who was given aspirin for fever. The pathologist notes numerous hepatocytes containing many tiny lipid droplets with a centrally-placed nucleus. Name the injury pattern, the specific diagnosis you suspect, and the molecular mechanism.

Consider: micro- vs. macrovesicular steatosis, aspirin & mitochondria, Reye syndrome

✓ Answer: Microvesicular steatosis → Reye syndrome

Aspirin metabolites (salicylate) inhibit mitochondrial β-oxidation enzymes, preventing hepatocytes from oxidizing fatty acids. Fatty acids accumulate as many small cytoplasmic droplets around an intact central nucleus = microvesicular steatosis. Clinically: vomiting, hepatomegaly, hypoglycemia, elevated ammonia, and rapidly progressive encephalopathy in a child recovering from a viral illness. Never give aspirin to children with viral illness.

Testing Your Understanding — LO 2, Question 2

A 62-year-old man with a 30-year history of heavy alcohol use has AST 180, ALT 70, bilirubin 2.5, albumin 2.8, INR 1.6. Which labs reflect injury, which reflect function, and which finding specifically suggests alcohol as the driver?

Consider: AST/ALT ratio, synthetic markers, half-life of factor VII

✓ Answer: AST/ALT > 2 suggests alcohol; low albumin & high INR indicate synthetic failure

AST and ALT are injury markers (hepatocyte leak). The AST:ALT ratio of 2.6 is classic for alcoholic liver disease — alcohol depletes B6, which ALT requires more than AST; mitochondrial AST is also released. True function is reflected by albumin (2.8 — chronically low) and INR (1.6 — both acute & chronic coagulopathy). Elevated direct bilirubin reflects excretion failure. Albumin's long half-life (~20 d) shows this is chronic, while INR can rise quickly because factor VII has a 6-hour half-life.

Testing Your Understanding — LO 3, Question 3

A 55-year-old with alcoholic cirrhosis develops hematemesis. Endoscopy shows bleeding esophageal varices. Explain the vascular anatomy that creates these varices, why the patient also has splenomegaly and thrombocytopenia, and why propranolol (not metoprolol) is used for prevention.

Consider: porto-systemic collaterals, splanchnic β₂ receptors, hypersplenism

✓ Answer: Left gastric → azygos collaterals; β₂-mediated splanchnic vasoconstriction

Cirrhosis raises portal pressure → blood seeks alternative routes to the systemic circulation. At the gastroesophageal junction, the left gastric (coronary) vein (portal) anastomoses with esophageal branches of the azygos (systemic) — these submucosal collaterals enlarge into varices and rupture. Splenomegaly reflects congestion of the splenic vein (portal tributary) → hypersplenism → sequestration of platelets → thrombocytopenia. Nonselective β-blockers like propranolol block β₂ receptors in splanchnic arterioles → unopposed α-mediated splanchnic vasoconstriction → ↓ portal inflow → ↓ portal pressure. Selective β₁ blockers (metoprolol) lack this splanchnic effect and won't work.

▼Definition & Pathogenesis of Cirrhosis

★ Cirrhosis is the end-stage histologic pattern shared by nearly every chronic liver disease. It is defined by diffuse bridging fibrosis + regenerative nodule formation that destroys normal lobular architecture. Once established, it is the substrate for portal hypertension, hepatocellular dysfunction, and hepatocellular carcinoma.

The Core Sequence

Chronic injury → persistent inflammation → hepatic stellate cell (Ito cell) activation → transformation into myofibroblasts → deposition of type I and III collagen in the space of Disse → capillarization of sinusoids (loss of fenestrae) → impaired hepatocyte–sinusoid exchange and increased intrahepatic resistance.

HY — Stellate Cells: Normally store vitamin A in the space of Disse. When activated by TGF-β (from Kupffer cells), they become the principal fibrogenic cell of the liver.

Reversibility

Early fibrosis is reversible if the inciting injury is removed (e.g., sustained virologic response to HCV, alcohol cessation, weight loss in MASLD). Once crosslinked collagen and regenerative nodules are established, reversal is limited — cirrhosis is generally considered irreversible in routine clinical practice.

⚠️ Exam Trap

The"most common preventable cause of hepatic fibrosis" answer depends on population: globally = chronic viral hepatitis; US/Europe = MASLD/alcohol.

▼Complications of Decompensation

★ These six complications define decompensated cirrhosis on Step 1:

1. Ascites & Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP)

Ascites is the most common first sign of decompensation. Driven by portal HTN + splanchnic vasodilation + hypoalbuminemia. SAAG ≥ 1.1 g/dL.

SBP dx: Ascites PMN > 250/mm³. Most common organism: E. coli. Treatment: IV cefotaxime.

⚠️ Exam Trap

Cirrhotic presenting with new confusion, fever, or abdominal pain → paracentesis before antibiotics. A PMN > 250 confirms SBP even if culture negative.

2. Esophageal Varices

Dilated submucosal veins of the distal esophagus from portal → azygos collateral flow. Screen all new cirrhotics by EGD. Primary prophylaxis: nonselective β-blocker (propranolol, nadolol) or endoscopic variceal ligation. Acute bleed: octreotide + banding + ceftriaxone prophylaxis for SBP.

3. Hepatic Encephalopathy (HE)

Neuropsychiatric syndrome driven by accumulation of ammonia (and other neurotoxins) that bypass the cirrhotic liver via portosystemic shunts. Asterixis is the classic finding. Precipitants ="HEPATIC" (GI bleed, infection, electrolytes, constipation, dehydration, drugs, dietary protein load).

Treatment: Lactulose (traps NH4+ in gut lumen) ± rifaximin (decreases urease-producing gut flora).

4. Hepatorenal Syndrome (HRS)

Functional renal failure in advanced cirrhosis driven by splanchnic vasodilation → intense renal vasoconstriction. Bland urine, FeNa < 1%, no improvement with volume. Type 1 = rapid (weeks); Type 2 = gradual. Definitive therapy = liver transplant.

5. Hepatopulmonary Syndrome

Triad: liver disease + hypoxemia + intrapulmonary vascular dilatation. Classic finding: platypnea-orthodeoxia (dyspnea/hypoxia worse sitting up).

6. Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC)

Cirrhosis of ANY cause is the dominant risk factor for HCC. Highest risk: HBV (even without cirrhosis), HCV, hemochromatosis, aflatoxin-B1 exposure (p53 mutation). Screening: q6mo U/S ± AFP in all cirrhotics.

HY: AFP > 400 ng/mL + a hepatic mass in a cirrhotic = HCC (biopsy usually not required).

▼Acetaldehyde, ROS, & Malignancy

★ Acetaldehyde is a Group 1 carcinogen (IARC). It forms DNA adducts and interferes with DNA repair.

Mechanisms of Tissue Injury

  • Acetaldehyde-protein adducts: trigger innate immunity + autoantibodies → hepatic inflammation.
  • CYP2E1 induction: generates reactive oxygen species → lipid peroxidation, mitochondrial damage.
  • Gut permeability: ethanol loosens tight junctions → portal LPS → Kupffer cell TLR4 activation → TNFα.
  • Nutritional deficits: thiamine (Wernicke–Korsakoff), folate, B6, zinc.

Alcohol-Associated Cancers

Ethanol (via acetaldehyde) increases risk for cancers of the oropharynx, larynx, esophagus (squamous cell), liver (HCC), breast, and colon. Risk is synergistic with tobacco for upper aerodigestive cancers.

HY: A heavy drinker + smoker presenting with dysphagia and weight loss → esophageal squamous cell carcinoma (upper/middle esophagus).

AST:ALT Ratio

AST > ALT with a ratio > 2 is highly suggestive of alcoholic injury. Two reasons:

  1. Alcohol depletes pyridoxal-5-phosphate (B6), a cofactor more important for ALT than AST.

Maddrey discriminant function (DF ≥ 32) or MELD ≥ 20 identifies severe alcoholic hepatitis. Treatment: cessation, nutrition (thiamine, folate), and selective use of prednisolone in severe disease without active infection.

▼Chronic Passive Congestion —"Nutmeg Liver"

★ Elevated central venous pressure (any cause of right-sided heart failure) is transmitted back through the IVC and hepatic veins → stagnant blood in the central vein and zone 3 sinusoids.

Gross Appearance

Histology

  • Centrilobular hepatocyte atrophy / necrosis — zone 3 is furthest from oxygenated portal blood.
  • With prolonged congestion: centrilobular (zone 3) fibrosis → ultimately cardiac cirrhosis.
HY —"Reverse Lobulation": In advanced passive congestion, central veins become the center of fibrotic bridges — the portal tracts paradoxically sit on the edges of pseudo-lobules.

▼Budd-Chiari Syndrome

★ Occlusion of the large hepatic veins and/or hepatic segment of the IVC, most often thrombotic.

Triad

Abdominal pain + hepatomegaly + ascites. Caudate lobe is often spared (it drains directly into the IVC) and becomes hypertrophied on imaging — a classic Step 1 clue.

Risk Factors —"Hypercoagulable Liver"

  • Polycythemia vera and other JAK2+ myeloproliferative neoplasms (most common identified cause).
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome.
  • Paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria.
  • Pregnancy, oral contraceptives.
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma invading the hepatic vein.

Diagnosis & Treatment

Doppler U/S is first-line (absent or reversed flow in hepatic veins). Confirm with MR/CT venography. Treat with anticoagulation; TIPS or transplant for fulminant disease.

⚠️ Exam Trap:"Painful Hepatomegaly + Ascites in a Young Woman on OCPs"

Think Budd-Chiari, not simple hepatic steatosis or viral hepatitis. Always probe for MPN, APLS, or prothrombotic states.

▼Sinusoidal Obstruction Syndrome (SOS / VOD)

★ Non-thrombotic obstruction of small centrilobular venules and sinusoids by sloughed endothelial/sinusoidal debris and subintimal collagen.

Classic Setting

  • First 3 weeks after hematopoietic stem-cell transplant conditioning (cyclophosphamide, busulfan).
  • Ingestion of pyrrolizidine alkaloids (bush teas containing Senecio, Crotalaria, comfrey).
  • Oxaliplatin chemotherapy (for metastatic colorectal cancer).

Presentation

Rapid onset of tender hepatomegaly, weight gain, ascites, and jaundice. Doppler: patent hepatic veins (distinguishes from Budd-Chiari).

HY: In a post-HSCT patient with new painful hepatomegaly, jaundice, and ascites + patent hepatic veins on Doppler → SOS/VOD. Defibrotide is the specific treatment.

Testing Your Understanding — LOs 9–10, Question 1

An autopsy of a 76-year-old man with long-standing severe tricuspid regurgitation shows a mottled red-and-tan liver on cut section with congested central veins surrounded by pale periportal hepatocytes. Name the gross appearance, the acinar zone most affected, and why.

Consider: direction of venous drainage, which zone is farthest from oxygenated portal blood, the red-and-tan "nutmeg" gross

✓ Answer: Zone 3 (centrilobular)

The gross is a "nutmeg liver" of chronic passive congestion. Congestion is transmitted back through the central vein into zone 3 sinusoids, producing red, blood-filled zones around central veins (dark stripes) alternating with paler periportal zone 1 (light stripes). Zone 3 is also the most vulnerable to hypoxic injury because it sits farthest from the oxygenated portal blood supply — hence why ischemic and toxic injuries center there.

Testing Your Understanding — LOs 9–10, Question 2

A 32-year-old woman with known polycythemia vera presents with 4 weeks of worsening abdominal pain, massive hepatomegaly, and tense ascites. Doppler ultrasound shows absent flow in the hepatic veins; the caudate lobe is markedly enlarged. Name the syndrome and explain the caudate lobe sign.

Consider: JAK2+ polycythemia vera, caudate lobe drainage directly to IVC, Doppler flow in hepatic veins

✓ Answer: Budd-Chiari syndrome

Budd-Chiari syndrome — thrombotic occlusion of the hepatic veins. JAK2-positive myeloproliferative neoplasms are a leading predisposing condition. The caudate lobe is characteristically spared and enlarges because it drains directly into the IVC via separate emissary veins rather than through the main hepatic veins. Distinguishes from SOS/VOD (patent hepatic veins on Doppler) and from portal vein thrombosis (patent hepatic veins; presinusoidal picture).

Testing Your Understanding — LOs 9–10, Question 3

A 45-year-old man develops painful hepatomegaly, 6 kg weight gain, and jaundice 10 days after allogeneic HSCT for AML conditioned with busulfan and cyclophosphamide. Doppler U/S shows patent hepatic veins and a patent portal vein with reversed flow. Name the syndrome and the best specific therapy.

Consider: sinusoidal endothelial injury post-HSCT, why hepatic veins remain patent, FDA-approved endothelial-protective therapy

✓ Answer: Defibrotide

Sinusoidal Obstruction Syndrome (SOS / veno-occlusive disease) — classic presentation 1–3 weeks after myeloablative HSCT. Conditioning chemotherapy damages sinusoidal endothelial cells, causing occlusion at the sinusoidal level (hepatic veins remain patent — this differentiates it from Budd-Chiari). Specific therapy is defibrotide, which has pro-fibrinolytic and endothelial-protective actions and is FDA-approved. Supportive care includes fluid management and avoidance of hepatotoxins.

LO 1 — Liver Injury Patterns (Q1–Q2)

Q1. A 37-year-old man is found unresponsive 6 hours after ingesting an unknown quantity of acetaminophen. Admission labs: AST 7400, ALT 8200, INR 3.1, total bilirubin 5.1. Liver biopsy shows confluent necrosis extending from central veins. Which zone of the hepatic acinus is preferentially injured?

A. Zone 1 (periportal)

B. Zone 2 (midzonal)

C. Zone 3 (centrilobular)

D. Zones 1 and 2 simultaneously

✓ Correct: C — Acetaminophen toxicity produces centrilobular (zone 3) necrosis because CYP2E1 (which generates NAPQI) is concentrated in zone 3.

Q2. A 52-year-old woman has been taking isoniazid for latent TB. She presents with jaundice, tender hepatomegaly, and nausea. AST 1100, ALT 1450, total bilirubin 6.2, ALP 140. Her biopsy shows panlobular lymphocytic infiltrate with scattered apoptotic hepatocytes. This histologic pattern is best classified as:

A. Chronic cholestatic hepatitis

B. Steatohepatitis

C. Acute hepatitic injury pattern

D. Granulomatous hepatitis

✓ Correct: C — Drug-induced (INH) acute hepatitic pattern: ALT > AST in the thousands, panlobular lymphocytes, apoptotic (Councilman) bodies.

LO 2 — Liver Function Tests (Q3–Q4)

Q3. A 66-year-old woman with advanced cirrhosis is evaluated for transplant listing. Which combination of labs best reflects synthetic liver function?

A. AST, ALT, GGT

B. Albumin, PT/INR, total bilirubin

C. Alkaline phosphatase and GGT

D. Ammonia and LDH

✓ Correct: B — True synthetic/functional tests: albumin, coagulation (PT/INR), bilirubin ("ABC PT"). Transaminases are markers of hepatocyte injury, not synthetic capacity.

Q4. A 30-year-old woman has asymptomatic isolated ALP elevation at 340 U/L; GGT is also elevated. All other LFTs are normal. Which single test best confirms that the ALP is of hepatic origin?

A. Serum γ-glutamyl transferase (GGT)

B. Serum calcium

C. Ferritin

D. Uric acid

✓ Correct: A — GGT is elevated only in hepatobiliary disease; it is the test used to confirm that an elevated ALP is hepatic rather than bony or placental in origin.

LO 3 — Jaundice & Cholestasis (Q5–Q8)

Q5. A 19-year-old medical student notices mild scleral icterus after an all-nighter before exams. Total bilirubin 3.1 (indirect 2.8), AST/ALT normal, ALP normal, CBC normal, reticulocyte count normal. Hemolysis labs are negative. Most likely diagnosis?

A. Dubin-Johnson syndrome

B. Rotor syndrome

C. Gilbert syndrome

D. Crigler-Najjar type II

✓ Correct: C — Mild, fasting/stress-related unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia without hemolysis and normal LFTs = Gilbert syndrome (mildly reduced UGT1A1 activity).

Q6. A 45-year-old woman presents with pruritus and fatigue. Labs: ALP 620, GGT elevated, AST/ALT mildly elevated, total bilirubin 2.0 with direct fraction predominant. AMA is positive at 1:320. Which diagnosis is most likely?

A. Primary sclerosing cholangitis

B. Primary biliary cholangitis

C. Autoimmune hepatitis

D. Acute viral hepatitis

✓ Correct: B — Middle-aged woman, pruritus, cholestatic LFT pattern, + AMA → PBC. Biopsy shows florid duct lesion with granulomas.

Q7. A 28-year-old man with ulcerative colitis develops cholestatic LFTs. MRCP shows multifocal intra- and extra-hepatic bile duct strictures producing a"beaded" appearance. AMA is negative, p-ANCA is positive. Which complication is he at highest lifetime risk for?

A. Hepatocellular carcinoma

B. Cholangiocarcinoma

C. Pancreatic adenocarcinoma

D. Gastric MALT lymphoma

✓ Correct: B — Classic PSC. Highest lifetime risk is cholangiocarcinoma (~10–15%); colon cancer risk is also increased in PSC+UC.

Q8. A 24-year-old man presents with painless jaundice; labs show total bilirubin 4.5 with elevated direct fraction, normal ALT/AST/ALP. Liver biopsy shows a grossly black liver. Which transporter is defective?

A. UGT1A1

B. MRP2 (ABCC2) canalicular transporter

C. OATP sinusoidal uptake

D. Bile salt export pump (BSEP)

✓ Correct: B — Dubin-Johnson syndrome: defective canalicular MRP2 export of conjugated bilirubin → black-pigmented liver on gross exam.

LO 4 — Cirrhosis & Decompensation (Q9–Q11)

Q9. A 58-year-old man with compensated cirrhosis from HCV is admitted with fever 38.6°C, diffuse abdominal pain, and confusion. Paracentesis: PMN 520/mm³, SAAG 1.6, total protein 0.8 g/dL. Gram stain negative. Best next step?

A. Repeat paracentesis in 48 hours

B. Start IV cefotaxime now

C. Await culture before antibiotics

D. Start oral norfloxacin only

✓ Correct: B — PMN > 250/mm³ defines SBP regardless of Gram stain or culture. Empiric IV 3rd-gen cephalosporin (cefotaxime) is started immediately.

Q10. A 60-year-old cirrhotic man develops a massive upper GI bleed from esophageal varices. After endoscopic banding and octreotide, which of the following reduces his infectious mortality?

A. Proton pump inhibitor

B. Non-selective β-blocker

C. Prophylactic ceftriaxone

D. Fresh frozen plasma

✓ Correct: C — Variceal bleeding in cirrhosis carries a high risk of SBP; 7 days of prophylactic ceftriaxone reduces mortality.

Q11. A 55-year-old man with known cirrhosis develops progressive confusion, lethargy, and asterixis over 3 days. He has been constipated. Exam: no fever, no focal findings. Best initial therapy for his encephalopathy?

A. IV thiamine

B. Lactulose

C. Haloperidol

D. High-protein feeding

✓ Correct: B — Lactulose traps NH4+ in the gut lumen and lowers colonic pH; rifaximin is added for refractory disease. Constipation is a common precipitant.

LO 5 — Alcohol-Related Liver Disease (Q12–Q15)

Q12. A 41-year-old man drinks a pint of vodka daily. Fasting labs show glucose 48 mg/dL, lactate elevated, β-hydroxybutyrate elevated. Which NADH-driven step best explains his hypoglycemia?

A. Pyruvate → lactate

B. Oxaloacetate → malate

C. Acetyl-CoA → ketones

D. Dihydroxyacetone-P → glycerol-3-P

✓ Correct: B — Elevated NADH drives OAA → malate, depleting OAA for gluconeogenesis. Pyruvate → lactate contributes to acidosis; DHAP → glycerol-3-P contributes to steatosis.

Q13. A patient with a 20-year drinking history is admitted with fever, jaundice, and tender hepatomegaly. AST 280, ALT 95, GGT 1100, INR 2.0. Biopsy shows ballooned hepatocytes with eosinophilic intracytoplasmic inclusions surrounded by neutrophils and perivenular fibrosis. Which statement about the eosinophilic inclusions is correct?

A. They are pathognomonic for alcoholic hepatitis

B. They are composed of damaged keratin 8/18 intermediate filaments

C. They stain positive for PAS and are diastase-resistant

D. They represent iron deposition

✓ Correct: B — Mallory-Denk bodies are aggregated keratin 8/18 filaments. They are highly suggestive of ALD but also occur in MASH, Wilson, and PBC.

Q14. Chronic ethanol abuse up-regulates CYP2E1 (MEOS). A direct consequence of this induction is:

A. Decreased acetaminophen toxicity

B. Increased conversion of acetaminophen to NAPQI

C. Decreased warfarin clearance

D. Decreased tolerance to alcohol

✓ Correct: B — CYP2E1 produces NAPQI from acetaminophen. Chronic alcohol use lowers the acetaminophen dose needed to cause fulminant hepatic necrosis.

Q15. A 50-year-old man with alcoholic hepatitis has a Maddrey discriminant function of 38 and no active infection. Which therapy is most likely to reduce short-term mortality?

A. Pentoxifylline alone

B. Oral prednisolone

C. N-acetylcysteine alone

D. Spironolactone

✓ Correct: B — In severe alcoholic hepatitis (DF ≥ 32 or MELD > 20) without infection, prednisolone improves 28-day survival.

LO 6 — Viral Hepatitis Types (Q16–Q17)

Q16. A 24-year-old woman in her third trimester returns from rural South Asia with fever, jaundice, and malaise. Total bilirubin 9, AST 1200, ALT 1400, INR 2.6. Which virus is most concerning given the clinical context?

A. HAV

B. HBV

C. HCV

D. HEV

✓ Correct: D — HEV is fecal-oral, endemic in Asia, and carries ~20% mortality in pregnancy from fulminant liver failure.

Q17. An IV drug user with known chronic HBV develops a sudden, severe deterioration with ALT 3100 and INR 3.0. Which superimposed infection is most likely?

A. Hepatitis A superinfection

B. Hepatitis D superinfection

C. Hepatitis C coinfection

D. Hepatitis E

✓ Correct: B — HDV requires HBsAg to replicate. Superinfection of a chronic HBV carrier with HDV produces severe accelerated liver injury and a high risk of fulminant failure.

LO 7 — Acute vs Chronic Viral Hepatitis (Q18–Q19)

Q18. A 45-year-old man with chronic HCV is found to have a serum cryoprecipitate and new membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis on biopsy. The direct mechanism of the renal lesion is:

A. Anti-GBM antibody deposition

B. Immune complexes containing HCV antigens + IgM rheumatoid factor depositing in glomeruli

C. Direct HCV infection of podocytes

D. Complement deficiency

✓ Correct: B — Mixed cryoglobulinemia: HCV-containing immune complexes plus monoclonal IgM rheumatoid factor precipitate in cold and deposit in glomeruli → MPGN.

Q19. A liver biopsy from a patient with chronic HBV shows portal-based lymphocytic infiltrates that spill across the limiting plate into the lobule, along with"ground-glass" hepatocytes on H&E. The lymphocytic activity described is termed:

A. Bridging necrosis

B. Interface hepatitis (piecemeal necrosis)

C. Councilman body formation

D. Granulomatous hepatitis

✓ Correct: B — Interface hepatitis (piecemeal necrosis) is the histologic hallmark of chronic active hepatitis, seen in chronic HBV, HCV, and AIH.

LO 8 — Autoimmune Hepatitis (Q20–Q21)

Q20. A 10-year-old girl is evaluated for fatigue and transaminase elevation. ALT 540, AST 380, γ-globulin elevated. ANA negative, ASMA negative, AMA negative, but anti-LKM1 positive. Which diagnosis fits best?

A. Autoimmune hepatitis type 1

B. Autoimmune hepatitis type 2

C. Primary biliary cholangitis

D. Wilson disease

✓ Correct: B — Anti-LKM1 defines type 2 AIH, which preferentially affects children/adolescents and tends to run a more aggressive course than type 1.

Q21. Which histologic feature most favors autoimmune hepatitis over chronic viral hepatitis on biopsy?

A. Ground-glass cytoplasm

B. Lymphoid aggregates in portal tracts

C. Plasma-cell rich interface hepatitis with hepatocyte rosettes

D. Steatosis

✓ Correct: C — Plasma-cell rich interface hepatitis with rosetted hepatocytes is the classic AIH pattern. Ground-glass cytoplasm is HBV; lymphoid aggregates/steatosis suggest HCV.

LO 9 — Chronic Passive Congestion (Q22–Q23)

Q22. A 70-year-old woman with severe tricuspid regurgitation has a cut section of liver at autopsy showing red-purple centrilobular areas surrounded by tan-yellow peripheral hepatocytes. This gross appearance is best described as:

A. Micronodular cirrhosis

B. Nutmeg liver

C. Steatosis

D. Cardiac cirrhosis

✓ Correct: B — Nutmeg liver describes the mottled gross appearance of chronic passive congestion. Cardiac cirrhosis is the advanced/fibrotic end-stage.

Q23. Ascites from chronic right-sided heart failure and cardiac cirrhosis is typically:

A. SAAG < 1.1, high protein

B. SAAG ≥ 1.1, low protein (< 2.5 g/dL)

C. SAAG ≥ 1.1, high protein (≥ 2.5 g/dL)

D. SAAG < 1.1, low protein

✓ Correct: C — Cardiac ascites: high-SAAG (portal HTN physiology) but high-protein because the sinusoids are not damaged and remain permeable to protein. Cirrhotic ascites is high-SAAG but low-protein.

LO 10 — Hepatic Venous Outflow Obstruction (Q24–Q25)

Q24. A 34-year-old woman on OCPs is admitted with 2 weeks of painful hepatomegaly and new ascites. Doppler U/S shows absent flow in all three hepatic veins and marked caudate lobe enlargement. JAK2 V617F is positive. Treatment strategy should include:

A. Observation only

B. Immediate anticoagulation and evaluation for TIPS

C. Defibrotide

D. Ursodeoxycholic acid

✓ Correct: B — Budd-Chiari with underlying MPN. Anticoagulation is immediate; TIPS or transplant is used for refractory/fulminant disease.

Q25. A 48-year-old man develops painful hepatomegaly, 7 kg weight gain, and jaundice 14 days after HSCT conditioning with cyclophosphamide and busulfan. Doppler U/S: hepatic veins patent, no thrombus. Which of the following best differentiates his condition from Budd-Chiari on imaging?

A. Caudate lobe hypertrophy

B. Patent hepatic veins

C. Reversed portal flow

D. Presence of ascites

✓ Correct: B — SOS/VOD is non-thrombotic obstruction at the level of the sinusoids; hepatic veins remain patent, unlike Budd-Chiari.
