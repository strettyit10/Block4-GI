USMLE Step 1

Chronic Pancreatitis — Complete Case Tutor

Question 1

Pancreatitis & Abnormal Stools

What is pancreatitis, what caused it, and why are the stools bulky, oily, and malodorous?

What is pancreatitis?

Pancreatitis is autodigestion of the pancreas — the gland's own proteolytic enzymes activate prematurely inside the acinar cells and begin destroying pancreatic tissue instead of waiting to be activated in the duodenum.

Normally: Enzymes are secreted as inactive zymogens (trypsinogen, chymotrypsinogen, proelastase, prolipase) → travel to duodenum → enterokinase on brush border activates trypsinogen → trypsin activates all others.

In pancreatitis: Zymogens activate inside the gland → autodigestion → inflammation → necrosis → fibrosis → exocrine insufficiency.

Why did alcohol cause this?
  • 1 Sphincter spasm: Alcohol causes spasm of the sphincter of Oddi → backed-up pancreatic secretions → intrapancreatic pressure rises.
  • 2 Direct acinar toxicity: Alcohol and its metabolite acetaldehyde directly injure acinar cells → promotes premature zymogen activation inside cells.
  • 3 Protein plugs: Alcohol increases protein concentration in pancreatic juice → plugs form in small ducts → obstruction → upstream pressure buildup.
  • 4 Repeated injury → fibrosis: Each acute episode destroys more functional tissue, replaced by fibrosis → progressive exocrine (and endocrine) failure.
⟳
End Result
Years of alcohol-induced pancreatitis → widespread fibrosis → pancreas can no longer produce adequate lipase, proteases, or amylase → Exocrine Pancreatic Insufficiency (EPI).
Explaining the abnormal stools

The stool findings — bulky, malodorous, oily, with fat droplets and undigested meat fibers — map directly onto the loss of specific pancreatic enzymes.

Lipase absent
Steatorrhea

Dietary triglycerides are not hydrolyzed. Fat passes intact into colon → bacterial fermentation → oily, bulky, foul-smelling stools. Fat droplets visible on microscopy. Fat malabsorption is the dominant feature because there is no backup enzyme for lipase.

Proteases absent
Azotorrhea

Without trypsin, chymotrypsin, elastase → dietary proteins not cleaved → pass undigested. Undigested meat fibers seen on microscopy. Caloric deficit contributes to progressive weight loss despite large food intake.

Amylase reduced
Carbs (relatively spared)

Salivary α-amylase + brush-border disaccharidases (maltase, sucrase, lactase) provide significant backup. Carbohydrate malabsorption is milder — though undigested starch reaching the colon causes gas and bloating.

Key exam point: Fat malabsorption is the most clinically severe feature of EPI because fat digestion is almost entirely dependent on pancreatic lipase — there is no meaningful alternative pathway. This is why fat restriction is central to treatment.

Question 2

Secretin & Pancreatic Secretion

What should secretin do — and what does this patient's response reveal?

Secretin is a peptide hormone released from S cells in the duodenal mucosa in response to luminal acidification (pH < 4.5). It acts on pancreatic ductal (centroacinar) cells to produce a high-volume, bicarbonate-rich secretion. This serves to neutralize gastric acid and create the alkaline pH needed for pancreatic enzymes to work.

Critical distinction: Secretin drives ductal cells (water + HCO₃⁻). CCK drives acinar cells (digestive enzymes). These are two separate populations responding to two separate signals.

Both volume and HCO₃⁻ are catastrophically reduced. The pancreas has essentially no functional ductal tissue remaining to respond to secretin. This is diagnostic of severe exocrine pancreatic insufficiency.

Consequences of absent HCO₃⁻
  • 1 Acid not neutralized: Gastric acid enters duodenum unchecked. Duodenal pH remains very low throughout digestion.
  • 2 Lipase denatures: Pancreatic lipase has an optimal pH of 7–8 and rapidly denatures below pH 4. Even supplemental lipase is destroyed in this environment.
  • 3 Bile salt precipitation: Bile salts precipitate at low pH → impaired micelle formation → worsens fat malabsorption beyond enzyme deficiency alone.
  • 4 Mucosal injury: Sustained low duodenal pH → duodenal erosions, impaired brush-border enzyme activity.

Clinical use of the secretin test: IV secretin + collection of duodenal fluid over 60 min is the gold standard for diagnosing exocrine pancreatic insufficiency. It directly measures functional pancreatic reserve.

Question 3

Carb & Protein Digestion — and the Glucose Puzzle

Normal digestion pathways, and why this patient has high blood glucose despite adequate absorption.

Normal carbohydrate digestion & absorption
  • 1 Mouth / Stomach: Salivary α-amylase cleaves starch at α-1,4 bonds → maltose, maltotriose, α-limit dextrins. Continues in stomach until gastric acid denatures it (~pH 3).
  • 2 Duodenum / Jejunum: Pancreatic α-amylase (secreted by acinar cells in response to CCK) continues starch hydrolysis → oligosaccharides.
  • 3 Brush border enzymes: Maltase (cleaves maltose → 2 glucose), Sucrase (sucrose → glucose + fructose), Lactase (lactose → glucose + galactose), Isomaltase (α-1,6 bonds in dextrins).
  • 4 Absorption: Glucose + galactose → SGLT-1 (sodium-coupled cotransport, apical membrane). Fructose → GLUT-5 (facilitated diffusion). All exit via GLUT-2 (basolateral) into portal blood.
Normal protein digestion & absorption
  • 1 Stomach: HCl denatures proteins + activates pepsinogen → pepsin. Pepsin cleaves peptide bonds (especially aromatic AAs) → large polypeptides.
  • 2 Pancreatic endopeptidases: Trypsin, chymotrypsin, elastase → cleave internal peptide bonds → shorter oligopeptides. (Trypsin is activated by enterokinase; it then activates all others — master activator.)
  • 3 Pancreatic exopeptidases: Carboxypeptidase A & B → cleave from the C-terminus → releases individual amino acids from the ends.
  • 4 Brush border: Aminopeptidases, dipeptidases → cleave remaining di/tripeptides into free amino acids.
  • 5 Absorption: Free AAs → Na⁺-coupled transporters (apical). Di/tripeptides → PepT1 (H⁺-coupled cotransport). All exit via basolateral transporters into portal blood.
Does glucose absorption need the pancreas?

No. Monosaccharide absorption via SGLT-1/GLUT-2 is entirely independent of the pancreas. Salivary amylase + brush-border disaccharidases can compensate substantially for absent pancreatic amylase.

This is why carbohydrate malabsorption is far milder than fat/protein malabsorption in EPI. The patient's glucose rises to 200 mg% after the oral load — confirming that glucose absorption is intact.

The glucose rises fine — but doesn't come back down properly. That's the real problem. The question is what's wrong with glucose disposal.

Why is his blood glucose elevated?

Key insight for exams
The glucose load test tells you about insulin secretory capacity, not absorption. In chronic pancreatitis, both exocrine AND endocrine function are progressively destroyed. Always think of the pancreas as having two compartments that can both fail.
Question 4

The Hormone & Three Enzymes Used for Treatment

Replacing what the destroyed pancreas can no longer produce — one hormone and three enzymes.

Rx
Treatment logic
The treatment strategy is replacement of what the fibrosed pancreas can no longer make. The endocrine deficit (insulin) is treated with the hormone. The exocrine deficit is treated with pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy (PERT).
The Hormone
Insulin

Why needed: Chronic fibrosis destroys islet β cells → absolute insulin deficiency → pancreatogenic diabetes (Type 3c). This is distinct from Type 1 (autoimmune) and Type 2 (peripheral resistance), though the end result — insulin deficiency — is the same.

Mechanism: Insulin binds receptor tyrosine kinase (insulin receptor) → autophosphorylation → signaling cascade → GLUT-4 translocation to cell membrane in muscle and adipose → glucose uptake. Also: promotes glycogen synthesis, inhibits gluconeogenesis, activates lipogenesis.

Type 3c diabetes (pancreatogenic): secondary to pancreatic disease. Often harder to manage than Type 2 because α cells (glucagon) are also destroyed — so there is no counterregulatory response to hypoglycemia. Patients are prone to severe hypoglycemia on insulin.

His fasting glucose was already 130 mg% (diabetic range) and his post-load glucose peaked at 200 — confirming insulin deficiency, not just impaired absorption.

1
Enzyme 1
Lipase

Hydrolyzes triglycerides at the sn-1 and sn-3 positions → 2-monoglyceride + 2 free fatty acids. Requires colipase (also in pancreatic juice) to anchor to fat droplet surface and displace bile salt coating. Optimal pH 7–8.

Without it: Steatorrhea. With replacement: Fat absorbed → weight gain, stool volume drops, oiliness resolves.

2
Enzyme 2
Protease (Trypsin)

Trypsinogen → activated by enterokinase (enteropeptidase) on the duodenal brush border → trypsin. Trypsin is the master activator: it activates all other zymogens (chymotrypsinogen, proelastase, procarboxypeptidases) and autocatalyzes more trypsinogen.

Without it: Azotorrhea, undigested meat fibers. With replacement: Protein digestion restored, weight gain resumes.

3
Enzyme 3
Amylase

Cleaves α-1,4 glycosidic bonds in starch → maltose, maltotriose, α-limit dextrins. Though salivary amylase provides backup, pancreatic amylase is the dominant starch-digesting enzyme post-stomach. Carbohydrate malabsorption is milder than fat/protein.

Without it: Gas, bloating, osmotic diarrhea from undigested starch reaching colon. With replacement: Improved stool consistency and reduced frequency.

How PERT is delivered clinically

Pancreatic enzymes are given as enteric-coated microspheres (e.g., pancrelipase / Creon) taken with every meal. The enteric coating prevents destruction by gastric acid and releases enzymes in the duodenum. However, the acid environment in this patient (absent HCO₃⁻) still partially inactivates even enteric-coated lipase — which is a key reason why fat restriction remains essential alongside PERT.

Clinical result in this patient: He stopped losing weight and reduced the volume and frequency of his stools — confirming successful exocrine replacement and restoration of fat and protein absorption.

Question 5

Why Restrict Dietary Fat?

Even with enzyme replacement, fat absorption is never fully normalized. Here's why restriction is essential.

Five reasons fat restriction is necessary
  • 1 Enzyme dose ceiling: No pill can replicate the full output of a healthy pancreas. A normal pancreas secretes lipase in massive excess; PERT pills provide a fraction of that. High fat intake simply overwhelms the supplemental dose — steatorrhea persists if fat load is too high.
  • 2 Acid environment destroys lipase: This patient has virtually no HCO₃⁻ secretion (proven by the secretin test). The duodenum stays acidic. Pancreatic lipase denatures below pH 4. Even enteric-coated PERT releases enzymes into a suboptimal acid environment, reducing efficacy.
  • 3 Bile salt depletion vicious cycle: Unabsorbed fatty acids in the colon bind calcium and bile salts (saponification). Bile salt loss → depleted bile salt pool → worse micelle formation → even more fat malabsorption. Restricting fat intake limits this cycle.
  • 4 Symptom burden: Large amounts of undigested fat reaching the colon → osmotic diarrhea (fat draws water) + bacterial fermentation (produces gas, odor). Restriction directly reduces stool frequency, volume, and malodor — improving quality of life.
  • 5 Reduces pancreatic stimulation: Dietary fat is the most potent stimulus for CCK release from I cells of the duodenum. CCK stimulates the damaged acinar cells → can precipitate pain, inflammation, and further damage. Restricting fat reduces this pathological stimulus.
MCT oil — the workaround
Long-chain fats (LCT)
Require lipase + micelles. Cannot be absorbed without functioning fat digestion machinery.
vs
MCT oil (C6–C12)
Do NOT need lipase or micelles. Absorbed directly across enterocyte membrane → portal vein.

Clinical use: MCT oil provides high-calorie supplementation without taxing the impaired fat digestion machinery. Essential for preventing malnutrition in EPI. Drawback: lacks essential fatty acids (linoleic, linolenic) which must still be supplied by small amounts of LCT.

Fat-soluble vitamin deficiency

Fat malabsorption → vitamins A, D, E, K are not absorbed (they require micelles for uptake into enterocytes).

ADEK supplementation is a required part of EPI management, in addition to fat restriction and PERT.
