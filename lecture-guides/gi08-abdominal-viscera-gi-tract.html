▼ Serous Sac Anatomy

⭐ The peritoneum is the serous sac of the abdomen — think "fist in a balloon." The parietal layer lines the abdominal wall; the visceral layer wraps around organs.

🎈 The Fist-in-Balloon Analogy

  • Balloon wall = parietal peritoneum — outer layer lining the abdominal/pelvic walls and the inferior surface of the diaphragm.
  • Fist = visceral peritoneum — inner layer in direct contact with the organs.
  • Inside the balloon = peritoneal cavity — a potential space normally containing only a small amount of serous fluid (for lubrication during organ movement).
  • The peritoneal cavity is normally a closed, sealed space — no air, only serous fluid.
🎯 Step 1: Free Air in the Peritoneal Cavity = Perforation
  • Air should only be found within organs, not in the peritoneal cavity.
  • Free intraperitoneal air on upright CXR (pneumoperitoneum) = organ perforation (e.g., perforated peptic ulcer, perforated diverticulitis, bowel perforation).
  • In an upright patient: air rises (found superiorly — seen as free air under the diaphragm on CXR); fluid sinks and collects in the dependent pouches.
  • Fluid settles in the rectovaginal pouch (of Douglas) in females or rectovesical pouch in males — the most dependent part of the peritoneal cavity.

▼ Intraperitoneal vs Retroperitoneal Organs

⭐ Knowing which organs are intraperitoneal vs retroperitoneal is MUST-KNOW — it determines mobility (surgical access) and vulnerability to injury.

📍 Definitions

  • Intraperitoneal = organ is "within" the peritoneum → surrounded by visceral peritoneum and connected to the posterior abdominal wall by mesentery (a double layer of peritoneum containing the NVB). These organs are mobile.
  • Retroperitoneal = organ is "behind" the peritoneum → fixed to the posterior abdominal wall, no mesentery, cannot be moved. Their NVB still originates on the posterior wall but is not in mesentery.

⚠️ TRAP: The Pancreas Exception ▼

Most of the pancreas (head, neck, body) is retroperitoneal. But the tail of the pancreas is intraperitoneal — it sits in the splenorenal ligament and is the only part that is mobile. This is surgically relevant: when the spleen is removed (splenectomy), the tail of the pancreas can be inadvertently damaged.

▼ Greater & Lesser Sacs

⭐ The peritoneal cavity is divided into two communicating spaces: the greater sac (most of the peritoneal cavity) and the lesser sac / omental bursa (posterior to stomach and liver).

🔗 Communication via the Omental Foramen

  • Greater sac = the main peritoneal cavity; entered when you penetrate the parietal peritoneum.
  • Lesser sac (omental bursa) = a smaller potential space posterior to the stomach and liver. Clinically important as a site where fluid (blood, pancreatic enzymes) can collect.
  • The two sacs communicate through the omental foramen (foramen of Winslow / epiploic foramen).
  • Borders of the omental foramen: anterior = hepatoduodenal ligament (containing the portal triad); posterior = IVC; superior = caudate lobe of liver; inferior = 1st part of duodenum.
🎯 Step 1: Lesser Sac Fluid Collections
  • Acute pancreatitis → pancreatic enzymes leak → collect in the lesser sac (omental bursa) → epigastric pain radiating to the back; fluid collection on CT posterior to stomach.
  • Pseudocyst of the pancreas → walled-off collection of pancreatic fluid in the lesser sac — complication of acute/chronic pancreatitis.
  • Draining the lesser sac surgically requires passing through the omental foramen or the lesser omentum.

▼ Greater & Lesser Omenta

⭐ The omenta are peritoneal folds (double layers of peritoneum) connecting the stomach to adjacent structures.

🟡 Greater Omentum

  • Attaches to the greater curvature of the stomach and drapes inferiorly into the abdomen, then reflects back on itself — looks like an apron (4 layers of peritoneum total).
  • Contains a small amount of fat between its layers.
  • Contains the left and right gastroomental (gastroepiploic) arteries.
  • Nickname: "The Policeman of the Abdomen" — it migrates to sites of inflammation and wraps around the organ to wall off infection and prevent spread. Clinically relevant in appendicitis (omentum tries to contain the infection).

🔵 Lesser Omentum

  • Attaches from the lesser curvature of the stomach to the liver. Divided into two ligamentous parts:
LigamentConnectsContentsNotes
Hepatoduodenal ligament Liver → 1st part of duodenum Portal triad: hepatic artery proper, portal vein, common bile duct Thicker; forms the free edge of the lesser omentum = anterior border of omental foramen
Hepatogastric ligament Liver → stomach Left and right gastric arteries; no major NVB Thinner; window into lesser sac if cut
⭐ HY: Pringle Maneuver
  • During liver surgery, the surgeon can compress the hepatoduodenal ligament (= free edge of lesser omentum / anterior border of omental foramen) between thumb and index finger to temporarily stop blood flow to the liver.
  • This is the Pringle maneuver — it occludes the portal vein and hepatic artery to control hemorrhage.

▼ Esophagus

⭐ The esophagus descends posterior to the trachea (the posterior tracheal wall has no cartilage to accommodate esophageal dilation). Blood supply follows a cervical → thoracic → abdominal pattern.

🩸 Blood Supply

RegionArterial SupplyVenous Drainage
CervicalInferior thyroid arteryInferior thyroid vein
ThoracicMultiple branches off descending aortaAzygous vein system
AbdominalInferior phrenic arteries + esophageal branch of left gastric arteryInferior phrenic veins, left gastric vein, short gastric vein
⭐ HY: Esophageal Varices in Portal Hypertension
  • The abdominal esophageal veins (left gastric, short gastric) drain into the portal system.
  • In portal hypertension (e.g., cirrhosis), portal blood is diverted through these veins into the azygous system (systemic) — a portosystemic anastomosis.
  • The dilated submucosal veins = esophageal varices — risk of rupture → massive upper GI bleed (hematemesis), potentially fatal.
  • Other portosystemic shunts: caput medusae (umbilical), anorectal varices (hemorrhoids).

🏥 Clinical Correlations

  • Achalasia: failure of the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) to relax → progressive dysphagia to BOTH solids AND liquids (key: starts equally for both). Barium swallow: "bird's beak sign" (dilated esophagus with distal tapered stenosis). Complication of Chagas disease (Trypanosoma cruzi → destroys myenteric plexus neurons). Treated with balloon dilation, Heller myotomy, or CCB/nitrates.
  • Apple core sign (eaten apple sign): contrast imaging shows a ring-like filling defect resembling a core of an eaten apple → suggests advanced achalasia OR an esophageal/colonic tumor causing a fixed narrowing.
⚠️ TRAP: Achalasia vs GERD Dysphagia ▼

Achalasia = dysphagia to both solids AND liquids from the onset (LES can't open at all). GERD/esophageal stricture = dysphagia initially to solids only, then progresses to liquids as narrowing worsens. Esophageal carcinoma = dysphagia to solids first → liquids later (progressive mechanical obstruction).

▼ Stomach

⭐ Knowing the labeled anatomy of the stomach is HY HY HY per Dr. Uren. Key regions, curvatures, and their associated ligaments and vessels must be memorized.

🗺️ Stomach Anatomy Key Points

  • Regions: Cardia → Fundus → Body → Pylorus (pyloric antrum → pyloric canal → pyloric sphincter)
  • Lesser curvature: concave, faces right; receives left & right gastric arteries (within lesser omentum)
  • Greater curvature: convex, faces left; receives left & right gastroomental (gastroepiploic) arteries (within greater omentum)
  • Lesser omentum = hepatogastric + hepatoduodenal ligaments (connects stomach to liver)
  • Greater omentum = "policeman of the abdomen" (attaches to greater curvature)
  • Hepatoduodenal ligament (part of lesser omentum) contains the portal triad: hepatic artery proper + portal vein + common bile duct

▼ Small Intestine

⭐ Three parts: duodenum → jejunum → ileum (mnemonic: "DJ Ileum"). Despite its name, the small intestine is the longest segment of the GI tract (~6–7 m).

🔵 Duodenum (4 Parts)

PartPeritoneal StatusKey Relations

⭐ HY: Ligament of Treitz
  • The suspensory ligament of Treitz (at the duodenojejunal junction, 4th part of duodenum) is the surgical landmark dividing upper GI (proximal to Treitz) from lower GI (distal to Treitz).
  • Upper GI bleeds: hematemesis, melena; lower GI bleeds: hematochezia.
  • Nasogastric tube, endoscopy, and small bowel follow-through all use Treitz as reference point.

  • After cystectomy (bladder removal), a segment of ileum (not jejunum) is used as a urostomy conduit for urine drainage — the ileum is preferred due to its better vascularity and functional characteristics.
  • Being able to distinguish jejunum from ileum is surgically critical for choosing the right segment.

▼ Large Intestine (Colon)

⭐ Function: converts fluid waste to semi-solid stool. The colon alternates between intraperitoneal and retroperitoneal segments.

🗺️ Colon Anatomy

  • Order: Cecum → Appendix → Ascending colon → Transverse colon → Descending colon → Sigmoid colon → Rectum → Anal canal
  • Taeniae coli: 3 longitudinal muscle bands running the length of the large intestine; they constrict to form haustra.
  • Haustra: sacculations/pouches of the colon; increase surface area for absorption.
  • Omental appendages: small fat-filled outpouchings hanging from the colon.
  • Hepatic flexure (right colic flexure): bend between ascending and transverse colon, near the liver.
  • Splenic flexure (left colic flexure): bend between transverse and descending colon, near the spleen — the most mobile and vulnerable part of the colon; watershed area between SMA and IMA territories.

⚠️ TRAP: Splenic Flexure Watershed Ischemia ▼

The splenic flexure is a watershed area — the most distal territory of the SMA anastomoses with the most proximal territory of the IMA here. During hypotension or aortic surgery, this is the area most vulnerable to ischemic colitis because it has the least collateral supply. Classic board question: elderly patient after AAA repair develops bloody diarrhea → ischemic colitis at splenic flexure.

▼ Foregut / Midgut / Hindgut Divisions

⭐ The GI tract's blood supply is organized by embryological origin — this is why blood supply maps so cleanly to specific segments. "Embryology makes anatomy make sense."

DivisionExtentArteryAortic Level
Foregut Distal esophagus → 2nd part of duodenum (+ liver, gallbladder, spleen, pancreas) Celiac trunk T12/L1
Midgut 3rd part of duodenum → proximal 2/3 of transverse colon (+ jejunum, ileum, cecum, appendix, ascending colon) SMA L1
Hindgut Distal 1/3 of transverse colon → superior rectum (+ descending colon, sigmoid colon) IMA L3
⭐ Key Rule: Branches named for what they SUPPLY, not where they originate
  • Due to enormous anatomical variation between patients, arterial branches are named based on their destination (what they supply), NOT their origin. This is why the same vessel may arise from different parent arteries in different people.
  • Know the names and destinations — the exact origin is variable and less testable.

▼ Celiac Trunk (Foregut Supply)

⭐ The celiac trunk branches at T12/L1 into three main arteries: left gastric, splenic, common hepatic.

🧠 Mnemonic: Celiac Trunk — "Left Surgeon's Common Habit" ▼

Left gastric · Splenic · Common hepatic

(Or: "The celiac has 3 daughters: Left, Splenic, Common")

🔴 Left Gastric Artery

  • Supplies the left lesser curvature of the stomach
  • Gives off esophageal branches → supplies the distal esophagus
  • Anastomoses with the right gastric artery along the lesser curvature

🟣 Splenic Artery

  • Supplies the spleen
  • Gives off short gastric arteries → supply the fundus of the stomach
  • Gives off left gastroomental artery (left gastroepiploic) → supplies the left greater curvature

🟠 Common Hepatic Artery

  • Gives off right gastric artery → supplies right lesser curvature, anastomoses with left gastric artery
  • Gives off gastroduodenal artery (GDA):
    • → Right gastroomental artery (right gastroepiploic) → right greater curvature (anastomoses with left gastroomental)
    • → Superior pancreaticoduodenal arteries → pancreas and duodenum (anastomose with inferior pancreaticoduodenal arteries from SMA)
  • Gives off hepatic artery proper:
    • → Right hepatic artery → right lobe of liver; gives off cystic artery → gallbladder
    • → Left hepatic artery → left lobe of liver
⭐ HY: Gastroduodenal Artery in Posterior Duodenal Ulcer
  • The GDA runs posterior to the 1st part of the duodenum.
  • A posterior duodenal ulcer that perforates posteriorly can erode into the GDA → massive arterial hemorrhage. This is the most common cause of life-threatening upper GI bleed from a peptic ulcer.
  • Anterior duodenal ulcer → perforates into peritoneum → peritonitis + free air under diaphragm.

⚠️ TRAP: SMA Syndrome ▼

The SMA crosses anterior to the 3rd part of the duodenum. In SMA syndrome, the angle between the SMA and aorta narrows (e.g., in severe weight loss, prolonged supine positioning) → compresses the 3rd part of the duodenum → gastric outlet obstruction-like symptoms (vomiting, abdominal pain after meals). Treated with weight gain, prone positioning, or surgery.

⭐ HY: IMA in AAA Repair
  • During abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) repair, the IMA is often sacrificed (ligated/oversewn).
  • If collateral supply (SMA-IMA anastomosis at splenic flexure) is poor → ischemic colitis of the left/sigmoid colon post-op.
  • Classic presentation: bloody diarrhea 1–3 days after aortic surgery.

▼ Hepatic Portal System

⭐ All venous blood from the GI tract passes through the liver first (first-pass effect/metabolism) before entering the systemic circulation. The hepatic portal vein is formed by the union of three tributaries.

🔀 Portal Vein Formation

Portal vein = Splenic vein + Superior mesenteric vein (SMV) + Inferior mesenteric vein (IMV)

  • SMV: drains midgut (jejunum, ileum, ascending + transverse colon)
  • Splenic vein: drains spleen; joined by IMV (drains hindgut) before joining SMV
  • The portal vein enters the hepatoduodenal ligament (posterior to hepatic artery and bile duct)
🎯 Step 1: Portal Hypertension — Portosystemic Anastomoses
  • In portal hypertension (e.g., cirrhosis), portal pressure rises → blood is diverted into systemic circulation via portosystemic anastomoses:
  • Esophageal varices: left gastric vein ↔ azygous system → hematemesis (most dangerous)
  • Caput medusae: paraumbilical veins ↔ superficial abdominal wall veins → radiating veins around umbilicus
  • Anorectal varices: superior rectal vein (portal) ↔ inferior/middle rectal veins (systemic) → appear as hemorrhoids
  • Other: splenomegaly, ascites, hepatic encephalopathy (↑ NH₃ bypasses liver)

▼ Lymphatic Drainage

⭐ GI lymphatic drainage follows the arterial supply in reverse. Intestinal lymph (chyle) is unique because it transports absorbed dietary fat (chylomicrons).

  • Lymph from the GI tract drains into terminal aortic lymph nodes → intestinal trunk.
  • The intestinal trunk drains all GI tract lymph into the cisterna chyli (at L1, anterior to the aorta).
  • The cisterna chyli is the origin of the thoracic duct, which ascends through the thorax and empties into the left subclavian/internal jugular venous angle.
  • Chyle: lymphatic fluid that contains fat (looks milky white) — chylomicrons absorbed from jejunum enter lacteals → intestinal lymphatics → cisterna chyli → thoracic duct → venous blood.
⭐ HY: Chylous Ascites & Virchow's Node
  • Chylous ascites: milky/white peritoneal fluid caused by disruption of the cisterna chyli or mesenteric lymphatics (trauma, malignancy, surgery) → fat-laden chyle leaks into the peritoneal cavity.
  • Virchow's node (Troisier's sign): enlarged left supraclavicular lymph node — classic sign of intra-abdominal malignancy (esp. gastric cancer) spreading via the thoracic duct to the left subclavian node.
  • GI cancers spread to regional lymph nodes that follow the arterial supply: foregut → celiac nodes; midgut → SMA nodes; hindgut → IMA nodes.

▼ Enteric Nervous System (ENS)

⭐ The ENS is the "second brain" — an intrinsic plexus of neurons that extends from esophagus to anus, controlling GI motility and secretions independent of the central nervous system.

🧠 Two Key Plexuses

PlexusLocationFunctionMnemonic
Myenteric plexus (Auerbach's) Between the circular and longitudinal muscle layers Controls smooth muscle contraction (motility/peristalsis) Myenteric = Muscle
Submucosal plexus (Meissner's) In the submucosa Controls glandular secretions & mucosal blood flow Submucosal = Secretions
⭐ HY: Hirschsprung Disease — Absent ENS
  • Hirschsprung disease = congenital absence of ganglion cells (both myenteric and submucosal plexuses) in the distal colon and rectum → failure of peristalsis in the aganglionic segment → functional obstruction.
  • Presents: failure to pass meconium in newborns >48 hours; chronic constipation; "ribbon-like" stools.
  • Barium enema: transition zone between narrow aganglionic segment and proximal dilated colon (megacolon).
  • Biopsy: absence of ganglion cells on rectal biopsy (diagnostic).
  • Caused by failure of neural crest cell migration → RET proto-oncogene mutation (also associated with MEN2A).

⚖️ Extrinsic Modulation: PSNS vs SNS

  • The ENS operates semi-autonomously but is modulated by extrinsic autonomic input.
  • PSNS stimulates the ENS → ↑ motility, ↑ secretions ("rest and digest")
  • SNS inhibits the ENS → ↓ motility, ↑ sphincter tone ("fight or flight" = stop digesting)

▼ Autonomic Innervation Table (Know This!)

⭐ Dr. Uren specifically called out this table — it must be memorized. Note: splanchnic nerves ≠ SNS. Pelvic splanchnic nerves are PSNS!

GI DivisionSNS NervePSNS Nerve
Foregut Greater splanchnic nerves (T5–T9) Vagus nerve (CN X)
Midgut Lesser splanchnic nerves (T10–T11) Vagus nerve (CN X)
Hindgut Lumbar splanchnic nerves (L1–L2) Pelvic splanchnic nerves (S2–S4)
⚠️ TRAP: "All splanchnic nerves = SNS" ▼

FALSE. Dr. Uren specifically emphasized this. The pelvic splanchnic nerves (S2–S4) are parasympathetic — they supply the hindgut with PSNS innervation. Greater, lesser, and lumbar splanchnic nerves ARE sympathetic. But "splanchnic" alone doesn't tell you the type. The pelvic splanchnic nerves are the only PSNS nerves not from the vagus to the GI tract.

🎯 Step 1: Vagotomy & Gut Motility
  • Vagotomy (surgical or pharmacological) → removes PSNS drive to foregut and midgut → ↓ gastric acid secretion (historically used for peptic ulcer disease) + ↓ GI motility → gastroparesis.
  • Vagal tone is mediated by CN X (cranial nerve 10) — travels in the esophageal plexus and enters the abdomen with the esophagus.
  • Hirschsprung affects the intrinsic ENS (absent ganglion cells), NOT extrinsic autonomic input.

▼ Referred Pain

⭐ Visceral pain is diffuse and difficult to localize because visceral afferents converge on the same dorsal horn neurons as somatic afferents — the brain misattributes the pain to a body wall region (dermatome).

🧩 Mechanism: "Nerve Hijacking"

  • Visceral afferent fibers (from internal organs) and somatic afferent fibers (from skin/muscles) both converge on dorsal horn neurons in the spinal cord.
  • The brain is more practiced at interpreting somatic pain → attributes the visceral signal to the corresponding dermatome or myotome → referred pain.
  • Key: referred pain is felt at the surface (dermatome), not at the organ itself.

📍 Abdominal Referred Pain Patterns

Pain LocationGI OriginSpinal LevelsSNS Nerve
Epigastric Foregut (stomach, duodenum, liver, gallbladder, pancreas) T5–T9 Greater splanchnic
Periumbilical Midgut (small intestine, appendix, right colon) T10–T11 Lesser splanchnic
Hypogastric / suprapubic Hindgut (left colon, sigmoid, rectum) L1–L2 Lumbar splanchnic
🎯 Step 1: Classic Referred Pain Clinical Applications
  • Appendicitis: begins as periumbilical pain (appendix = midgut → T10–T11) → migrates to RLQ (McBurney's point) as parietal peritoneum becomes inflamed (somatic, well-localized).
  • Acute cholecystitis: starts as epigastric or RUQ pain (gallbladder = foregut) → may radiate to right shoulder/scapula (diaphragmatic irritation → phrenic nerve → C3–C5 dermatome).
  • Acute pancreatitis: epigastric pain radiating to the back (pancreas = retroperitoneal foregut structure; pain also from lesser sac irritation).
  • Myocardial infarction: can refer to left arm/jaw — note that cardiac pain can overlap with epigastric pain (heart and foregut share T5–T9 levels).

▼ Meckel's Diverticulum — Rule of 2's

⭐ Meckel's diverticulum is the most common congenital anomaly of the GI tract and a classic Step 1 high-yield topic.

📌 Pathophysiology

  • True diverticulum (all 3 bowel wall layers) — persistence of the vitelline (omphalomesenteric) duct that normally obliterates by week 7 of development.
  • May contain ectopic gastric mucosa (most common) or pancreatic tissue → these can secrete acid → ulceration → bleeding.
  • Location: ileum (midgut origin)
🧠 Mnemonic: Meckel's Rule of 2's ▼
  • 2 times more likely in males
  • 2 inches long
  • 2 feet from the ileocecal valve (in the ileum)
  • 2% of the population
  • Commonly presents in first 2 years of life
  • May have 2 types of ectopic epithelium (gastric AND pancreatic)

🏥 Clinical Presentation & Diagnosis

  • Presentations: painless rectal bleeding / hematochezia (most common in children), melena (dark stool), RLQ pain mimicking appendicitis, intussusception, volvulus, bowel obstruction near the diverticulum.
  • Diagnosis: Meckel's scan (⁹⁹ᵐTc-pertechnetate scan) — the radiotracer is taken up by ectopic gastric mucosa → hot spot in RLQ.
  • Treatment: surgical resection if symptomatic.
⚠️ TRAP: Meckel's vs Appendicitis ▼

Both Meckel's diverticulum and appendicitis can cause RLQ pain in a young patient (both midgut → periumbilical then RLQ pain). Key differentiators: Meckel's is more likely to cause painless rectal bleeding (from ectopic gastric mucosa secreting acid → ulceration). Appendicitis causes fever, rebound tenderness at McBurney's point, elevated WBC. If a Meckel's scan is positive (hot spot in RLQ), that confirms Meckel's.

LO 1 — Peritoneum & Omenta (Q1–Q3)

Q1. A 45-year-old man undergoes elective laparoscopic cholecystectomy. The surgeon places a clip on the hepatoduodenal ligament to control bleeding — a maneuver known as the Pringle maneuver. Which of the following structures runs within the hepatoduodenal ligament?

A. Portal vein, hepatic artery, common bile duct

B. Portal vein, inferior vena cava, common bile duct

C. Hepatic veins, hepatic artery, cystic duct

D. Superior mesenteric artery, portal vein, cystic duct

E. Hepatic artery, celiac trunk, common bile duct

✓ Correct: A — Portal vein, hepatic artery, common bile duct

The hepatoduodenal ligament (the free edge of the lesser omentum / right border of the lesser omentum) contains the portal triad: portal vein (posterior), hepatic artery proper (left), and common bile duct (right) — remembered as "VHD" or "Bile Duct is Right." The Pringle maneuver compresses this ligament between the thumb (in the omental foramen/epiploic foramen of Winslow) and index finger to temporarily occlude hepatic inflow.

Q2. A surgical resident is asked to identify retroperitoneal organs before performing a Whipple procedure. Which of the following correctly lists only retroperitoneal structures?

A. Stomach, duodenum (D1), sigmoid colon, rectum

B. Suprarenal glands, aorta, duodenum (D2–D4), pancreas (body/tail)

C. Suprarenal glands, kidneys, duodenum (D2–D4), ascending colon

D. Liver, gallbladder, pancreas head, cecum

E. Spleen, transverse colon, sigmoid colon, appendix

✓ Correct: C — Suprarenal glands, kidneys, duodenum (D2–D4), ascending colon

Use the mnemonic SAD PUCKER: Suprarenal glands, Aorta/IVC, Duodenum (D2–D4), Pancreas (head, body — tail is actually intraperitoneal), Ureters, Colon (ascending & descending), Kidneys, Esophagus (thoracic), Rectum (lower 2/3). The stomach, spleen, transverse colon, sigmoid colon, liver, and gallbladder are intraperitoneal. Note: pancreas body/tail is retroperitoneal, not IP — choice B incorrectly lists body/tail as retroperitoneal but that is correct; however B also includes aorta (not a visceral organ) making C the better clinical answer for "structures."

Q3. During a trauma exploratory laparotomy, the surgeon enters the lesser sac (omental bursa). Through which opening did the surgeon's hand most likely pass to access this space?

A. Through the greater omentum directly

B. Via the epiploic foramen (foramen of Winslow), posterior to the hepatoduodenal ligament

C. Through the left gastroepiploic vessels in the gastrosplenic ligament

D. Via the falciform ligament, between the right and left lobes of the liver

E. Through the transverse mesocolon inferiorly

✓ Correct: B — Via the epiploic foramen (foramen of Winslow), posterior to the hepatoduodenal ligament

The lesser sac (omental bursa) is a potential space posterior to the stomach and lesser omentum. Its only natural communication with the greater peritoneal cavity is the epiploic foramen (foramen of Winslow), bounded anteriorly by the hepatoduodenal ligament, posteriorly by the IVC, superiorly by the caudate lobe, and inferiorly by the first part of the duodenum. Surgeons can also enter the lesser sac by incising through the greater omentum or the gastrocolic ligament, but the natural anatomic opening is the epiploic foramen.

LO 2 — GI Tract Anatomy (Q4–Q7)

Q4. A 35-year-old woman with long-standing GERD presents with progressive dysphagia. Endoscopy shows a dilated esophagus with a bird-beak narrowing at the gastroesophageal junction. Manometry confirms failure of the lower esophageal sphincter to relax. Which cell type is lost in this condition?

A. Pacemaker cells of Cajal (ICC)

B. Myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus neurons — inhibitory nitrergic neurons

C. Submucosal (Meissner's) plexus secretomotor neurons

D. Chief cells of the gastric fundus

E. Parietal cells of the gastric body

✓ Correct: B — Myenteric plexus neurons (inhibitory nitrergic neurons)

This is achalasia — loss of inhibitory neurons (NO and VIP-secreting) in the myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus of the lower esophagus leads to failure of LES relaxation and absent peristalsis. The LES stays tonically contracted → progressive dysphagia for both solids and liquids. Classic imaging: bird-beak appearance on barium swallow. Chagas disease (T. cruzi) can destroy the myenteric plexus and produce secondary achalasia.

Q5. A 28-year-old man with a history of peptic ulcer disease presents with acute onset of sharp epigastric pain radiating to the back. CT shows air around the retroperitoneal duodenum and the head of the pancreas. Which part of the duodenum has most likely perforated?

A. First part (D1) — superior portion

B. Second part (D2) — descending portion

C. Third part (D3) — horizontal portion

D. Fourth part (D4) — ascending portion

E. Duodenojejunal flexure (ligament of Treitz)

✓ Correct: B — Second part (D2) — descending portion

D2 is entirely retroperitoneal and receives the common bile duct and pancreatic duct at the ampulla of Vater on its posteromedial wall. Peptic ulcer perforation of D2 leaks air into the retroperitoneal space around the duodenum and pancreatic head — a classic CT finding. D1 (the bulb) is the most common site of peptic ulcer disease, but perforation there would be intraperitoneal (D1 proximal portion is intraperitoneal). D2 perforation is specifically associated with retroperitoneal air.

Q6. A pathologist examines a small bowel biopsy and notes tall, closely packed circular folds (plicae circulares), long villi, and numerous goblet cells. These features are consistent with which segment?

A. Duodenum (D1)

B. Jejunum

C. Ileum

D. Cecum

E. Ascending colon

✓ Correct: B — Jejunum

The jejunum has tall, prominent plicae circulares (valves of Kerckring), long villi, and a thick wall — optimized for absorption. The ileum has fewer, shorter plicae circulares, shorter villi, more goblet cells, Peyer's patches (aggregated lymphoid follicles in the submucosa), and contains the terminal ileum's vitamin B12/intrinsic factor receptors and bile acid transporters. The ileum wall is thinner. A thick-walled segment with prominent circular folds = jejunum.

Q7. A 72-year-old woman with atherosclerosis and atrial fibrillation develops sudden severe abdominal pain, abdominal distension, and bloody diarrhea. Colonoscopy shows ischemic changes at the splenic flexure of the colon. Which of the following best explains the vulnerability of this location?

A. The splenic flexure is suspended by a long mesentery, making it prone to volvulus

B. The splenic flexure is a watershed area between the SMA and IMA territories with limited collateral supply

C. The splenic flexure receives blood exclusively from the IMA, which has no collaterals

D. The splenic flexure is retroperitoneal and at risk due to compression from the left kidney

E. The splenic flexure is the site of transition between taeniae coli and continuous longitudinal muscle

✓ Correct: B — Watershed area between SMA and IMA with limited collateral supply

The splenic flexure and the rectosigmoid junction are the two classic watershed zones of the colon. The splenic flexure sits at the border between the SMA territory (transverse colon via middle colic artery) and the IMA territory (descending colon via left colic artery). The marginal artery of Drummond connects these, but is often tenuous at the splenic flexure — making this region most vulnerable to ischemia during low-flow states, embolism, or systemic hypotension.

LO 3 — Vasculature & Lymphatics (Q8–Q11)

Q8. A 58-year-old man with a history of hypertension and smoking presents with post-prandial abdominal pain, weight loss, and a bruit on auscultation. Angiography reveals atherosclerotic narrowing at the origin of the celiac trunk. Which of the following would NOT be directly affected by celiac trunk occlusion?

A. Blood supply to the liver

B. Blood supply to the spleen

C. Blood supply to the stomach (lesser curvature)

D. Blood supply to the transverse colon

E. Blood supply to the gallbladder

✓ Correct: D — Blood supply to the transverse colon

The celiac trunk (foregut artery, T12–L1) gives off the left gastric, splenic, and common hepatic arteries — supplying the liver (hepatic), spleen (splenic), gallbladder (cystic from right hepatic), and stomach (left & right gastric, gastroepiploic arteries). The transverse colon is midgut and supplied by the middle colic artery, a branch of the SMA — not the celiac trunk.

Q9. A 55-year-old man with known cirrhosis and portal hypertension vomits bright red blood. Endoscopy reveals large varices at the gastroesophageal junction. These varices arise from dilation of which portosystemic anastomosis?

A. Superior rectal vein ↔ middle/inferior rectal veins

B. Paraumbilical veins ↔ superficial epigastric veins

C. Left gastric (coronary) vein ↔ azygos/esophageal veins

D. Splenic vein ↔ left renal vein (splenorenal shunt)

E. SMV ↔ inferior epigastric veins

✓ Correct: C — Left gastric (coronary) vein ↔ azygos/esophageal veins

Esophageal varices form at the gastroesophageal junction where the portal system (left gastric/coronary vein) meets the systemic venous drainage (azygos and hemiazygos veins → esophageal tributaries). Portal hypertension forces blood retrograde through the left gastric vein into the esophageal plexus → varices. These are the most dangerous portosystemic anastomosis site due to risk of rupture and massive hemorrhage. Rectal varices (anorectal junction) form from superior rectal ↔ middle/inferior rectal anastomosis.

Q10. Following abdominoperineal resection for rectal cancer, the surgeon ligates the inferior mesenteric artery (IMA) at its origin. Which vessel is most responsible for maintaining blood supply to the remaining sigmoid colon?

A. Right colic artery (from SMA)

B. Middle colic artery (from SMA) via marginal artery of Drummond

C. Ileocolic artery (from SMA)

D. Superior rectal artery (from IMA)

E. Left gastroepiploic artery (from splenic)

✓ Correct: B — Middle colic artery via marginal artery of Drummond

When the IMA is ligated, collateral flow to the descending and sigmoid colon can be maintained through the marginal artery of Drummond (an arterial arcade running along the inner border of the colon from ileocolic to sigmoid) connecting SMA and IMA territories. The key collateral is from the middle colic artery (SMA branch) through the marginal artery. However, this collateral is often tenuous at the splenic flexure — hence why the splenic flexure is a watershed zone at risk after IMA ligation.

Q11. A 45-year-old woman with metastatic gastric cancer is found to have a hard, non-tender, left supraclavicular lymph node on exam. This finding is most likely due to spread of tumor cells via which pathway?

A. Direct hematogenous spread to the subclavian vein

B. Lymphatic spread via the thoracic duct to the left venous angle

C. Trans-coelomic spread through the peritoneal cavity

D. Retrograde spread from the hepatic lymph nodes via the portal vein

E. Direct extension through the diaphragm to the thoracic lymph nodes

✓ Correct: B — Lymphatic spread via the thoracic duct to the left venous angle

This is Virchow's node (sentinel node) — a left supraclavicular lymph node enlarged by metastatic intra-abdominal malignancy (classically gastric, but also pancreatic, colon, ovarian). The pathway: gut lymphatics → cisterna chyli (at L1, formed by right and left lumbar trunks + intestinal trunk) → thoracic duct → empties at the left venous angle (junction of left subclavian and left internal jugular veins) → retrograde flow or tumor cell trapping in the left supraclavicular node.

LO 4 — Innervation & Referred Pain (Q12–Q15)

Q12. A 3-day-old male infant has not passed meconium since birth. Abdominal X-ray shows a massively dilated colon proximal to a narrow segment in the rectosigmoid. Rectal biopsy confirms absence of ganglion cells in the submucosa and myenteric plexus. Which of the following best describes the underlying pathophysiology?

A. Failure of neural crest cell migration to the distal colon resulting in a tonically constricted (aganglionic) segment

B. Hypertrophy of the myenteric plexus causing excessive peristaltic contractions

C. Loss of pacemaker cells (ICC) in the sigmoid colon leading to absent slow waves

D. Overactivation of submucosal (Meissner's) plexus causing hypersecretion and obstipation

E. Congenital absence of the internal anal sphincter due to failure of smooth muscle differentiation

✓ Correct: A — Failure of neural crest cell migration to the distal colon

Hirschsprung disease (congenital aganglionic megacolon): neural crest cells fail to migrate to the distal colon during development → aganglionic segment lacks both myenteric (Auerbach's) and submucosal (Meissner's) plexus neurons. Without inhibitory innervation, the affected segment remains tonically contracted → functional obstruction → proximal normal colon dilates (megacolon). The transition zone is at the rectosigmoid in 75% of cases. Biopsy confirmation: absence of ganglion cells + hypertrophied acetylcholinesterase-positive nerve fibers.

Q13. A 2nd-year medical student asks her attending: "Why does early appendicitis cause periumbilical pain that only later migrates to the RLQ?" Which of the following best explains the initial periumbilical localization?

A. The appendix is physically located near the umbilicus in early life

B. Visceral afferents from the midgut (T10) converge with somatic afferents from the T10 dermatome (periumbilical)

C. Somatic afferents from the RLQ are suppressed by sympathetic outflow until inflammation breaches the peritoneum

D. The appendix shares a lymphatic drainage route with the umbilical region

E. Early inflammation activates the submucosal plexus, which projects to the T10 spinal level via the vagus nerve

✓ Correct: B — Visceral afferents from the midgut (T10) converge with somatic afferents from the T10 dermatome (periumbilical)

Referred pain mechanism: visceral pain from the appendix (midgut) travels via sympathetic afferents (not vagal!) through the thoracic splanchnic nerves to spinal cord level T10. The brain misinterprets these signals as coming from the T10 somatic dermatome → periumbilical pain. Once inflammation involves the parietal peritoneum (rich in somatic innervation), the pain becomes somatic, well-localized, and shifts to the RLQ (McBurney's point). Key: visceral = vague, midline/referred; parietal = sharp, localized, rebound tenderness.

Q14. A surgeon is discussing the autonomic innervation of the GI tract with residents. She states that "cutting the thoracic splanchnic nerves would increase GI motility." A student says this is wrong. Who is correct, and why?

A. The surgeon is correct — sympathetic stimulation via thoracic splanchnics inhibits motility, so cutting them would remove inhibition and increase motility

B. The student is correct — thoracic splanchnic nerves carry parasympathetic fibers that stimulate motility, so cutting them would decrease motility

C. The student is correct — splanchnic nerves carry mixed sympathetic and parasympathetic fibers, so the net effect of cutting is unpredictable

D. The surgeon is correct — the splanchnic nerves primarily carry sensory afferents, and cutting them eliminates pain-mediated ileus

E. Neither is correct — thoracic splanchnic nerves have no effect on GI motility

✓ Correct: A — The surgeon is correct

The thoracic splanchnic nerves (greater = T5–9, lesser = T10–11, least = T12) carry preganglionic sympathetic fibers to the celiac, superior mesenteric, and aorticorenal ganglia. Sympathetic activation decreases GI motility and secretion (contracts sphincters, reduces peristalsis — "fight or flight"). Cutting/blocking these nerves removes sympathetic inhibition → increased GI motility. The surgeon is correct. Common Step 1 trap: students confuse "splanchnic" with "parasympathetic" — splanchnic nerves are sympathetic (except pelvic splanchnics, S2–S4, which are parasympathetic).

Q15. A 30-year-old man presents with painless rectal bleeding. Colonoscopy is performed and a 2 cm outpouching is found 2 feet proximal to the ileocecal valve containing both gastric and pancreatic tissue on biopsy. A Meckel's scan (⁹⁹ᵐTc-pertechnetate) would show a hot spot in which quadrant?

A. Right upper quadrant (RUQ)

B. Left upper quadrant (LUQ)

C. Left lower quadrant (LLQ)

D. Right lower quadrant (RLQ)

E. Periumbilical region

✓ Correct: D — Right lower quadrant (RLQ)

This is a classic Meckel's diverticulum presentation (Rule of 2's: 2%, 2 inches, 2 feet from ileocecal valve, 2 types of ectopic tissue, presents in first 2 years). The ⁹⁹ᵐTc-pertechnetate scan is taken up by ectopic gastric mucosa (not pancreatic) → hot spot appears in the RLQ where the terminal ileum resides, 2 feet proximal to the ileocecal valve. Painless rectal bleeding in a child = Meckel's until proven otherwise. The gastric mucosa secretes acid → ulceration of adjacent ileal mucosa → bleeding.
