▼ Parietal Cell Physiology & Pharmacologic Targets

⭐ The parietal cell produces HCl via the H⁺/K⁺-ATPase (proton pump) — the rate-limiting step in acid secretion. Three inputs stimulate it: histamine, acetylcholine, and gastrin.

🔬 Three Stimulatory Inputs to the Parietal Cell

  • Histamine — released by ECL cells → binds H₂ receptor (Gs-coupled) → ↑cAMP → PKA → activates H⁺/K⁺-ATPase
  • Acetylcholine (ACh) — parasympathetic input → binds M₃ receptor → ↑intracellular Ca²⁺ → activates proton pump. Also binds M₁ on ECL cells to release histamine.
  • Gastrin — from G cells in antrum → binds CCK-B receptor → ↑Ca²⁺ → activates proton pump
⭐ Step 1 HY: The H⁺/K⁺-ATPase is the final common pathway — all three signals converge on it. PPIs (which directly block the pump) are therefore the most powerful acid suppressors.

⚠️ Exam Trap

Antacids neutralize acid but do not reduce acid production — they have no effect on parietal cell secretion. They are short-acting and symptomatic only. For peptic ulcers, they are adjuncts, not primary therapy.

▼ Anticholinergics

⭐ Anticholinergics block muscarinic receptors on parietal cells (M₃) and ECL cells (M₁), reducing ACh-stimulated acid and histamine release.

  • Example: Atropine (muscarinic cholinergic blocker)
  • Rarely used clinically — side effects are prohibitive: tachycardia, constipation, urinary retention, sexual dysfunction, dry mouth, blurred vision
  • Understand the target conceptually; not a first-line PUD drug
Step 1 Note: Anticholinergics are "understand-the-mechanism" drugs. You won't be expected to prescribe them for PUD, but you need to know why they could theoretically reduce acid and why they aren't used.

Cimetidine is the classic CYP450 inhibitor in pharmacology. It increases the half-life of warfarin (bleeding risk!), benzodiazepines, phenytoin, and decreases activation of clopidogrel (a prodrug requiring CYP2C19). Famotidine does NOT have these interactions to a clinically significant degree.

⭐ Key Limitation: Tolerance develops with long-term H₂ blocker use — upregulation of H₂ receptors over time reduces efficacy. PPIs are preferred for chronic acid suppression.

▼ Proton Pump Inhibitors ("-prazoles")

⭐ PPIs irreversibly inhibit the H⁺/K⁺-ATPase — the rate-limiting step in acid production. They are the most effective acid-suppressing drugs available, reducing secretion 60–80%.

IrreversibleProdrugAcid-activatedSulfhydryl bindingNo tolerance

🔬 Mechanism of Action

  1. Oral dose taken as inactive, delayed-release (enteric-coated) prodrug
  2. Lipophilic — crosses into parietal cell from the blood
  3. Acid environment of the secretory canaliculus protonates the PPI → becomes active sulfenamide
  4. Active form binds irreversibly to cysteine sulfhydryl groups on H⁺/K⁺-ATPase → pump permanently inactivated
  5. Acid suppression lasts ~48 hours; takes several days to fully suppress all pumps
  6. Tolerance does NOT occur — unlike H₂ blockers
⚠️ Clinical Pearl — Timing

PPIs must be taken 30–60 minutes before a meal (except P-CABs — see below). The meal stimulates proton pumps to be active, which allows the acid environment to activate the PPI. Taking them on an empty stomach is less effective.

  • Bone fractures — especially in women with long-term use (impaired Ca²⁺ absorption)
  • ↑ C. difficile risk — reduced acid allows bacterial overgrowth
  • Hypomagnesemia

Side effects: Indigestion, stomach tenderness; can cause acute tubular nephritis; bloody diarrhea (rare); fundic gland polyps with long-term use (benign, resolve on stopping).

  • ↑ Opportunistic infections — especially C. difficile (gastric acid is a protective barrier)
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency — B12 release from food requires pepsin activation by acid
  • Impaired calcium absorption → bone density loss, fractures
  • Hypomagnesemia

▼ How H. pylori Causes Ulcers

⭐ Most non-NSAID peptic ulcers are caused by H. pylori. The bacteria survive in the mucosal layer by secreting urease.

  • H. pylori secretes urease → converts urea → NH₃ + CO₂ → NH₃ buffers local acid → bacteria can survive in gastric mucosa
  • Bacteria secrete virulence factors + stimulate pro-inflammatory cytokines → disrupt mucosal barrier
  • Acid + inflammation → tissue damage → ulceration
  • Untreated: can progress to chronic gastritis, peptic ulcer disease, or gastric adenocarcinoma / MALT lymphoma
⭐ Step 1 HY: H. pylori is gram-negative, spiral-shaped, urease-positive. The urea breath test exploits urease activity (radiolabeled urea → detectable ¹³CO₂ in exhaled air).

▼ Diagnosis of H. pylori

  • Urea breath test — patient ingests radiolabeled urea; if H. pylori is present, urease breaks it down → radiolabeled CO₂ detected in breath. Non-invasive, highly accurate.
  • Stool antigen test — detects H. pylori antigens in feces. Non-invasive.
  • Endoscopy + biopsy — urease test (CLO test) or histology; used if endoscopy is indicated
  • Serology (IgG) — less useful (stays positive after eradication)

▼ Antibiotics Used Against H. pylori

⭐ H. pylori is gram-negative, so moderate-spectrum antibiotics active against gram-negative bacteria are used.

  • Amoxicillin — β-lactam, cell wall synthesis inhibitor
  • Clarithromycin — macrolide, 50S ribosome inhibitor; resistance increasing
  • Metronidazole (Flagyl) — nitroimidazole; effective in anaerobic/microaerophilic environments
  • Tetracycline — 30S ribosome inhibitor
  • Levofloxacin — fluoroquinolone; used in resistant or second-line regimens

▼ Treatment Regimens

⭐ Combination therapy is required because monotherapy leads to rapid resistance. The current recommended regimen is quadruple therapy.

💊 Quadruple Therapy (Recommended by Medical Letter)

Quadruple Therapy = 2 Antibiotics + PPI + Bismuth
  • Metronidazole (Flagyl)
  • Tetracycline
  • PPI (any; reduces acid to help antibiotics work)
  • Bismuth subsalicylate (Pepto-Bismol) — antimicrobial, forms protective coating, treats GI symptoms

Duration: 7–14 days of antibiotics; PPI + bismuth continued until ulcer resolves

💊 Triple Therapy (Older; Falling Out of Favor)

  • PPI + Amoxicillin + Clarithromycin
  • Falling out of favor due to increasing clarithromycin resistance (>15% in many areas)
⚠️ Exam Point — Why Combination?

Monotherapy fails due to rapid resistance. The PPI serves double duty: suppresses acid (helps ulcer heal) AND raises gastric pH to improve antibiotic efficacy (many antibiotics are acid-labile). Bismuth has intrinsic antimicrobial activity against H. pylori.

▼ Post-Treatment Monitoring

  • Retest for H. pylori 4 weeks after completing antibiotic therapy
  • Use urea breath test or stool antigen (NOT serology — IgG remains positive after eradication)
  • If still infected → use a different antibiotic regimen (e.g., switch to levofloxacin-based therapy)
⭐ Step 1 High-Yield Summary — LO 2
  • Quadruple therapy = Metronidazole + Tetracycline + PPI + Bismuth subsalicylate (Pepto-Bismol)
  • PPI serves dual purpose: heals ulcer + improves antibiotic efficacy
  • Test for eradication 4 weeks post-treatment using urea breath test or stool antigen
  • H. pylori → urease → NH₃ → survives gastric acid; urease is the key virulence factor
  • Untreated H. pylori → risk of MALT lymphoma and gastric adenocarcinoma

▼ Physiological Basis of GERD

⭐ GERD results from retrograde movement of gastric acid into the esophagus, causing mucosal inflammation. The primary defense is the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) — when it fails, acid refluxes.

Normal defense mechanisms:

  • LES maintains high resting pressure (~20 mmHg) to prevent reflux
  • Esophageal peristalsis clears refluxed material
  • Salivary bicarbonate neutralizes esophageal acid

GERD develops when:

  • LES pressure is reduced → allows reflux (see factors below)
  • Increased volume of gastric contents
  • Increased caustic quality (low pH, bile salts)
  • Increased abdominal pressure (pregnancy, obesity)
  • Impaired esophageal acid clearance (scleroderma)

Complications: Esophagitis → Barrett's esophagus → esophageal adenocarcinoma (with chronic untreated GERD)

▼ Factors That Reduce LES Pressure

CategoryExamples
DietaryFatty foods, caffeine, chocolate, alcohol, citrus
Drugsβ-agonists, α-adrenergic antagonists, calcium channel blockers, anticholinergics, morphine, diazepam, barbiturates, progesterone
Systemic diseaseScleroderma (impairs esophageal motility and acid clearance)
PhysiologicPregnancy (progesterone ↓ LES tone + increased abdominal pressure)
⚠️ Step 1 Trap — Drugs That Worsen GERD

Many common drugs reduce LES pressure: calcium channel blockers, anticholinergics, nitrates, morphine, diazepam. Recognizing that a patient's GERD is drug-induced is a common clinical reasoning question.

▼ Antacid Rationale in PUD & GERD

⭐ The "all roads lead to reducing acid" principle: regardless of the etiology of ulcer or GERD, acid is always damaging the tissue — neutralizing or suppressing it relieves symptoms and promotes healing.

Antacid rationale:

  • Goal: raise gastric pH from ~1 to ~4 (1000-fold reduction in H⁺ concentration)
  • At pH 4, pepsin becomes inactive → mucosal protection increases
  • Antacids are short-acting (hours), best for immediate/intermittent symptom relief
  • For chronic conditions (PUD, chronic GERD), H₂ blockers or PPIs are preferred for sustained acid suppression

Common antacid formulations: Maalox/Mylanta (Mg/Al combo), Tums (CaCO₃), Rolaids (Mg/Ca)

▼ Stepwise Treatment of GERD

Step 1 — Behavioral Modification + Antacids (mild/intermittent)
  • Antacids (Maalox, Tums, Mylanta)
  • Elevate head of bed
  • Avoid late meals, overeating, meals before naps
  • Quit smoking
  • Avoid medications that reduce LES pressure
Step 2 — Add Pharmacotherapy (if GERD worsens)
  • Add H₂ receptor antagonists (famotidine)
  • Add metoclopramide (D2 antagonist) → ↑antral contractions → ↑gastric emptying → reduces reflux volume
Step 3 — Escalate (if still uncontrolled)
  • Replace H₂ blockers with PPI (e.g., omeprazole)
  • If PPI fails → try P-CAB (vonoprazan)
  • If medically uncontrolled → surgery (fundoplication) — last resort
⭐ Step 1 High-Yield Summary — LO 3
  • GERD = reflux of acid through incompetent LES → esophageal injury
  • CCBs, anticholinergics, nitrates, morphine, diazepam → reduce LES pressure → worsen GERD
  • Pregnancy worsens GERD — progesterone ↓ LES + increased abdominal pressure
  • Antacids raise pH 1→4 (1000-fold ↓ in H⁺); short-acting symptomatic relief
  • Metoclopramide increases gastric emptying (prokinetic) — reduces reflux volume
  • Chronic GERD → Barrett's esophagus → esophageal adenocarcinoma

▼ Concept of Cytoprotection

⭐ Rather than suppressing acid, cytoprotective drugs strengthen the mucosal barrier — they help the tissue defend itself against acid that is already present.

Normal cytoprotective mechanisms in the stomach:

  • Mucus layer (bicarbonate-rich) — traps HCO₃⁻ near epithelium
  • Epithelial tight junctions — physical barrier
  • Prostaglandins (PGE₂, PGI₂) — stimulate mucus/bicarbonate secretion, inhibit acid production, promote mucosal blood flow

NSAIDs deplete prostaglandins (COX inhibition) → remove this cytoprotective layer → NSAID-induced ulcers. This is why misoprostol (a PGE1 analog) is specifically effective in NSAID users.

▼ Sucralfate

⭐ Sucralfate is a sucrose-sulfate-alumina compound that binds to exposed tissue proteins at the ulcer base, forming a protective barrier against acid.

  • Mechanism: In acid environment → polymerizes → binds to positively charged tissue proteins at the ulcer base → forms a physical acid-resistant coat (lots of negative charges bind to positive charges in ulcerated tissue)
  • High affinity for defective (ulcerated) mucosa — selectively coats ulcers, not normal mucosa
  • Absorption is minimal — absorbed drug is excreted unmetabolized in urine
  • Best for non-NSAID ulcers — not well understood why it is less effective for NSAID-induced ulcers
  • Also used for healing esophageal erosions from GER
  • Side effects: Constipation (aluminum content); may bind to other drugs and reduce their absorption (take separately)
⚠️ Exam Note — Sucralfate Limitations

Sucralfate is not effective for NSAID-induced ulcers — the mechanism is not fully understood, but it likely doesn't compensate for the loss of prostaglandin-mediated protection. For NSAID users, misoprostol or PPIs are preferred.

▼ Misoprostol (PGE1 Analog)

⭐ Misoprostol is a synthetic PGE1 analog that mimics the normal cytoprotective effects of prostaglandins — particularly useful in NSAID users where natural PGs are depleted.

  • Mechanism: Binds PG receptors (Gi-coupled) → inhibits H₂-stimulated cAMP in parietal cells → ↓acid secretion; also stimulates mucus and bicarbonate secretion
  • Especially effective in NSAID users — replaces the prostaglandin protection that NSAIDs destroy via COX inhibition
  • Side effects: Abdominal pain, diarrhea (very common — not well tolerated)
  • ⚠️ Abortifacient — causes uterine contractions; absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy; used in combination with mifepristone for medical abortion
  • Not widely used clinically due to poor tolerability, but conceptually important
⚠️ High-Yield — Misoprostol in Pregnancy

Misoprostol is contraindicated in pregnancy — it causes uterine contractions (abortifacient). This is a classic Step 1 point: do NOT prescribe misoprostol to prevent NSAID-induced ulcers in a pregnant patient. Use a PPI instead.

▼ Vomiting Center Overview

Vomiting (emesis) is coordinated by the Emetic Center (Vomiting Center) in the medullary reticular formation. It receives convergent input from multiple peripheral and central sources and orchestrates the motor act of vomiting via cranial nerves and the phrenic nerve.

⚠️ Exam Trap
Motion sickness uses H1 and M1 receptors — NOT D2 or 5-HT3. This is why ondansetron (5-HT3 blocker) and metoclopramide (D2 blocker) are ineffective for motion sickness, while scopolamine and antihistamines work.

▼ Chemoreceptor Trigger Zone (CTZ)

The CTZ is located in the area postrema of the medulla, at the floor of the 4th ventricle. This region is outside the blood-brain barrier, allowing it to sense blood-borne toxins, drugs, and metabolic products directly.

CTZ Key Facts

  • Location: Area postrema — outside BBB → directly samples blood
  • Triggers: Opioids, dopamine agonists (levodopa), digoxin, chemotherapy drugs, uremia, radiation metabolites
  • Key receptors: D2 (dopamine), 5-HT3 (serotonin), NK1 (substance P), μ-opioid
  • Drugs targeting CTZ: Metoclopramide (D2 antagonist), ondansetron (5-HT3 antagonist), aprepitant (NK1 antagonist)
High-Yield
The CTZ is the primary target for antiemetics used in chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV). D2 and 5-HT3 are the two most pharmacologically important CTZ receptors for Step 1.

▼ Nucleus Tractus Solitarius (NTS)

The NTS relays visceral afferent signals from the GI tract (via the vagus nerve) to the emetic center. It is particularly important in chemotherapy- and radiation-induced nausea.

NTS Pathway

  • Chemotherapy / radiation → enterochromaffin cells in GI mucosa release serotonin (5-HT)
  • 5-HT binds 5-HT3 receptors on vagal afferent neurons
  • Vagal afferents → NTS → Emetic Center → vomiting
  • Also receives input from GI distension, pharyngeal irritation, and toxic ingestion

Ondansetron (5-HT3 antagonist) works peripherally at vagal nerve endings (NTS pathway) and centrally at the CTZ — making it effective against both GI-triggered and blood-borne nausea.

▼ 5-HT3 Antagonists — Ondansetron

Mechanism

Ondansetron (Zofran) selectively blocks 5-HT3 receptors — both at the CTZ (area postrema) and peripherally on vagal afferents in the GI tract. This dual site of action makes it the most effective drug for chemotherapy-induced and post-operative nausea.

Key Pharmacology

  • Class: 5-HT3 serotonin receptor antagonist
  • Prototype: Ondansetron (Zofran); also granisetron, dolasetron (all "-setron" suffix)
  • Indications: CINV (chemotherapy-induced nausea/vomiting), post-op nausea, radiation-induced nausea
  • NOT effective for: Motion sickness (vestibular H1/M1 pathway), opioid-induced vomiting alone
  • Route: Oral or IV
  • Side effects: Headache, constipation, QT prolongation (at high doses)
High-Yield
Ondansetron = first-line for CINV. It works peripherally (blocks 5-HT released from enterochromaffin cells during chemo) AND centrally (CTZ block). "-setron" suffix = 5-HT3 antagonist family.

▼ D2 Antagonists — Metoclopramide

Mechanism

Metoclopramide (Reglan) blocks D2 dopamine receptors at the CTZ. It also has prokinetic effects — it stimulates GI motility by enhancing acetylcholine release and blocking dopamine-mediated inhibition of smooth muscle, making it useful for gastroparesis.

Key Pharmacology

  • Class: D2 antagonist + prokinetic
  • Indications: CINV (adjunct), gastroparesis, diabetic gastric stasis, post-op nausea, GERD (limited use)
  • NOT effective for: Motion sickness
  • Route: Oral or IV

Side Effects — Critical for Step 1

  • Extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS): Akathisia, dystonia, parkinsonism — due to D2 block in striatum
  • Tardive dyskinesia with long-term use (FDA black box warning)
  • Hyperprolactinemia: D2 block in tuberoinfundibular pathway → ↑prolactin → galactorrhea, amenorrhea, gynecomastia
  • Contraindicated in Parkinson's disease (worsens dopamine deficiency)
⚠️ Exam Trap
Metoclopramide in Parkinson's patient → NEVER do this. D2 blockade worsens parkinsonism. Also: metoclopramide crosses the BBB (unlike domperidone) → central D2 effects → EPS.

▼ Motion Sickness Drugs

Motion sickness requires drugs targeting the vestibular pathway (H1 and M1 receptors), not CTZ/NTS drugs.

Scopolamine (Anticholinergic)

  • Class: Muscarinic M1 antagonist
  • Route: Transdermal patch (behind the ear) — preferred for sustained release
  • Indications: Motion sickness prophylaxis, post-op nausea
  • Side effects: Classic anticholinergic — dry mouth, blurred vision, urinary retention, sedation, confusion (especially elderly)
  • Caution: Narrow-angle glaucoma, BPH, elderly patients

Diphenhydramine / Dimenhydrinate (Dramamine)

  • Class: H1 antihistamine (first generation — crosses BBB)
  • Route: Oral
  • Indications: Motion sickness, mild nausea, pregnancy nausea (safe)
  • Side effects: Sedation, anticholinergic effects (dry mouth, urinary retention)
  • Note: Dimenhydrinate = diphenhydramine + 8-chlorotheophylline (brand: Dramamine)
💡 Mnemonic
Scopolamine = "Scope out the Sea" — it's the patch you wear on a cruise ship. Best for sustained prophylaxis of motion sickness.

▼ Oral Rehydration Therapy (ORS)

ORS is the cornerstone of diarrhea management — especially for acute infectious diarrhea and cholera. It exploits the sodium-glucose cotransporter (SGLT1) in intestinal epithelium to drive water absorption even when active transport of sodium alone is impaired.

ORS Mechanism

  • SGLT1 cotransports Na+ + glucose together into enterocytes
  • This cotransport remains intact even during secretory diarrhea (e.g., cholera) — which only impairs electrogenic Na channels, not SGLT1
  • Na+ absorption drives water to follow osmotically → net rehydration
  • WHO ORS formula: glucose + NaCl + KCl + sodium bicarbonate/citrate in water
High-Yield
ORS works in cholera because SGLT1 is unaffected by cholera toxin. Cholera toxin activates Gs → ↑cAMP → ↑Cl- secretion and ↓Na+ absorption via Na+ channels — but glucose-linked Na cotransport (SGLT1) is spared.
⚠️ Exam Trap
Plain water does NOT work as well as ORS — without glucose, sodium is not absorbed efficiently by the gut. Do NOT give plain water only in severe diarrhea.

▼ Opioid-Based Antidiarrheals

Opioid receptor agonists reduce intestinal motility and secretion — useful for non-infectious diarrhea. Two key drugs are designed to work peripherally (in the gut) with minimal CNS entry.

Loperamide (Imodium)

  • Mechanism: μ-opioid receptor agonist in the GI wall → ↓peristalsis, ↑sphincter tone, ↓intestinal secretion
  • CNS penetration: Minimal — does NOT cross BBB (P-glycoprotein efflux pump extrudes it) → no euphoria or addiction at normal doses
  • Indications: Traveler's diarrhea, IBS-D, acute non-infectious diarrhea
  • Contraindications: Bloody diarrhea, fever (may worsen invasive bacterial infection by retaining toxins); C. difficile colitis
  • Side effects: Constipation, abdominal cramps; toxic megacolon if used with severe colitis

Diphenoxylate + Atropine (Lomotil)

  • Mechanism: Diphenoxylate = synthetic opioid → μ-receptor agonist → ↓peristalsis. Atropine added at sub-therapeutic dose to discourage abuse (causes unpleasant anticholinergic effects if large doses taken)
  • CNS penetration: More than loperamide — Schedule V controlled substance
  • Indications: Non-infectious diarrhea (2nd-line after loperamide)
  • Side effects: Anticholinergic effects from atropine (dry mouth, blurred vision, urinary retention), sedation
  • Contraindications: Infectious diarrhea, C. diff, children under 2
💡 Mnemonic
Lomotil = Lo-mo-til = "Lo Motility" — diphenoxylate + atropine; the atropine is there to make you feel terrible if you abuse it (anticholinergic misery = abuse deterrent).

▼ Bismuth Subsalicylate (Pepto-Bismol)

  • Mechanism: Anti-secretory (via salicylate component inhibiting prostaglandin synthesis); antimicrobial against H. pylori and enteric pathogens; coats GI mucosa
  • Indications: Traveler's diarrhea prophylaxis and treatment; H. pylori therapy (quadruple regimen); indigestion, heartburn
  • Side effects: Black tongue and black stool (harmless — bismuth sulfide); tinnitus at high doses (salicylate toxicity)
  • Contraindications: Children with viral illness (salicylate component → risk of Reye syndrome); aspirin allergy
⚠️ Exam Trap
Black stool from bismuth subsalicylate ≠ GI bleeding. Distinguish by history (taking Pepto-Bismol?). GI bleed → melena is tarry and malodorous; bismuth-black stool is not tarry. Also: bismuth is part of the quadruple H. pylori regimen.
⭐ Step 1 High-Yield Summary — LO 7
  • ORS = SGLT1-mediated Na + glucose cotransport → drives water absorption; works even in cholera (SGLT1 intact)
  • Loperamide = μ-opioid agonist; minimal BBB penetration; first-line antidiarrheal; avoid in infectious diarrhea/colitis
  • Diphenoxylate + atropine (Lomotil) = synthetic opioid; Schedule V; atropine discourages abuse; avoid in children <2
  • Bismuth subsalicylate = anti-secretory + antimicrobial; causes black stool/tongue (benign); salicylate component → avoid in children with viral illness (Reye syndrome)
  • Never use loperamide/opioid antidiarrheals in C. diff or bloody diarrhea — retaining toxins worsens outcome

▼ Bulk-Forming Agents

Bulk-forming agents are indigestible dietary fibers that absorb water, swell in the colon, and increase stool bulk — mechanically stimulating peristalsis. They mimic the effect of dietary fiber.

Key Drugs

  • Psyllium (Metamucil) — natural husk fiber; soluble fiber; also lowers LDL cholesterol
  • Methylcellulose (Citrucel) — semi-synthetic fiber; less gas-forming than psyllium
  • Polyethylene glycol (PEG / Miralax) — technically osmotic but often grouped here; non-absorbed polymer that retains water in stool

Pharmacology

  • Onset: 12–72 hours (slow — need fiber bulk to build up)
  • Must be taken with adequate water — insufficient water → fiber can cause obstruction
  • Safest for long-term use; preferred in pregnancy, elderly, IBS
  • Side effects: Bloating, flatulence, abdominal cramping initially; rare esophageal obstruction if not enough fluid
High-Yield
PEG (Miralax) is actually an osmotic agent — it draws water into the colon lumen. However, because it works gently and is non-absorbed, it's widely used as a "gentle bulk/osmotic" agent. PEG is also used for bowel prep before colonoscopy (at higher doses).

▼ Osmotic Laxatives

Osmotic laxatives draw water into the intestinal lumen by osmotic pressure, softening stool and stimulating peristalsis. They are non-absorbed (or poorly absorbed) solutes.

▼ Stool Softeners (Emollients)

Docusate (Colace)

  • Mechanism: Anionic surfactant — lowers surface tension of stool, allowing water and fat to penetrate the stool mass → softens stool
  • Onset: 1–3 days
  • Indications: Post-surgical constipation, post-MI (avoid Valsalva), hemorrhoids, anorectal surgery — situations where straining is dangerous
  • Side effects: Minimal; may cause mild abdominal cramping; generally very well tolerated
  • Not effective for severe constipation — purely stool softening, no peristaltic stimulation
💡 Mnemonic
"Docusate = Don't Strain" — used when straining is medically dangerous (post-MI, hemorrhoids, post-op). It's a softener, not a stimulant — it won't make you go, just make it easier when you do.

  • Stimulants (senna, bisacodyl) = fastest for acute use; NOT for long-term (cathartic colon, dependence)
  • Docusate (Colace) = stool softener only; used when straining is dangerous (post-MI, hemorrhoids); no stimulant effect
  • Lactulose in hepatic encephalopathy: acidifies colon → NH3 → NH4+ → trapped → lower serum ammonia

▼ 5-ASA Agents (Aminosalicylates)

5-aminosalicylic acid (5-ASA / mesalazine / mesalamine) is the first-line drug for mild-to-moderate UC. It works topically in the colonic mucosa to reduce inflammation.

Mechanism

  • Inhibits COX and LOX → ↓prostaglandins and leukotrienes in colonic mucosa
  • Scavenges reactive oxygen species; inhibits NF-κB → anti-inflammatory
  • Acts topically — must reach the colon intact

Formulations

  • Sulfasalazine = 5-ASA + sulfapyridine (carrier); bacteria cleave in colon → releases 5-ASA; sulfapyridine absorbed → side effects
  • Mesalamine (Asacol, Pentasa, Lialda) = 5-ASA alone in delayed-release coating; fewer systemic side effects than sulfasalazine
  • Rectal formulations: Suppositories and enemas for proctitis and left-sided UC

Key Side Effects

  • Sulfasalazine: Nausea, headache, hemolytic anemia, oligospermia (reversible), Stevens-Johnson syndrome (sulfapyridine component)
  • Mesalamine: Generally well tolerated; rare nephrotoxicity (monitor creatinine)
⚠️ Exam Trap
5-ASA agents are effective for UC but have limited efficacy in Crohn's disease (Crohn's is transmural and often involves small bowel where 5-ASA does not reach at therapeutic concentrations).

▼ Corticosteroids

Corticosteroids (prednisone, budesonide, IV hydrocortisone/methylprednisolone) are used for acute flares of both Crohn's and UC. They are not used for maintenance due to serious long-term side effects.

Key Points

  • Induction of remission only — bridge therapy, not maintenance
  • Budesonide (Entocort) — high first-pass metabolism → acts locally in ileum/right colon; fewer systemic SE than prednisone; preferred for ileal Crohn's
  • IV methylprednisolone/hydrocortisone — for severe/hospitalized IBD flares

Side Effects (systemic steroids)

  • HPA axis suppression; adrenal insufficiency on abrupt withdrawal
  • Cushing's syndrome, hyperglycemia, osteoporosis, avascular necrosis
  • Immunosuppression → opportunistic infections
  • Cataracts, glaucoma, mood changes, peptic ulcers

▼ Fecal Microbiota Transplantation (FMT)

FMT involves transferring stool from a healthy screened donor into the GI tract of a recipient to restore normal gut microbiome composition (eubiosis).

Key Points

  • Primary indication (FDA approved): Recurrent Clostridioides difficile (C. diff) infection — highly effective (>90% cure rate for recurrent C. diff)
  • Emerging research: IBD (UC especially), IBS, metabolic disease, antibiotic-associated dysbiosis
  • Routes: Colonoscopy, nasojejunal tube, capsule (oral — frozen stool capsules)
  • Rationale: Restore competitive exclusion of pathogens; rebuild diverse microbiome; modulate immune responses at mucosa
  • Screening required: Donor screened for infectious agents (HIV, hepatitis, multi-drug resistant organisms)
  • Risks: Transmission of infection from donor (rare but serious); GI side effects (cramping, bloating, transient diarrhea)
High-Yield
FMT is FDA-cleared for recurrent C. diff. It is NOT standard of care for IBD yet, but research is ongoing. The gut microbiome plays a central role in IBD pathogenesis — dysbiosis (loss of diversity, overgrowth of pro-inflammatory bacteria) is a feature of both Crohn's and UC.

Q1 (LO 1). A patient taking omeprazole daily for 6 months is found to have a serum Mg²⁺ of 1.1 mEq/L. Which mechanism explains this finding?
Long-term PPI use → elevated gastric pH → impairs acid-dependent activation of TRPM6/7 magnesium transporters in the small bowel → hypomagnesemia. This is a well-described complication of long-term PPI use and requires PPI discontinuation or magnesium supplementation.
Q2 (LO 1). A 45-year-old man with GERD is started on famotidine. Which statement best describes famotidine's advantage over cimetidine?
Cimetidine is notorious for CYP450 inhibition (warfarin, phenytoin, theophylline interactions) and antiandrogenic effects (gynecomastia, impotence). Famotidine and ranitidine lack these properties. All H2 blockers are competitive reversible antagonists.
Q3 (LO 1). A patient starting a PPI is told the drug must be taken 30–60 minutes before a meal. Why?
PPIs are prodrugs activated in the acidic canaliculi of actively secreting proton pumps. The H⁺/K⁺-ATPase must be in its active (secreting) state for the PPI to enter the canaliculus, become protonated, and covalently bind the pump. Meal-stimulated acid secretion maximizes active pumps available — so PPIs are most effective taken 30–60 min before the first meal of the day.
Q4 (LO 2). A 52-year-old man tests positive for H. pylori via urea breath test. He is started on bismuth quadruple therapy. Which four-drug combination is this?
Bismuth quadruple therapy = Bismuth subsalicylate + Metronidazole + Tetracycline + PPI. This regimen is used when clarithromycin resistance is suspected or as a salvage regimen. Triple therapy = PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin (or metronidazole).
Q5 (LO 3). A patient with GERD reports that alcohol, chocolate, and peppermint worsen her symptoms. Which mechanism explains why these substances exacerbate GERD?
Alcohol, chocolate, peppermint, caffeine, fatty foods, and nicotine all reduce LES pressure → allow gastric contents to reflux into the esophagus. GERD is fundamentally a disorder of LES incompetence.
Q6 (LO 4). A 68-year-old woman taking daily ibuprofen for arthritis develops a peptic ulcer. Which agent would be MOST appropriate to both treat the current ulcer and prevent recurrence while she continues NSAID therapy?
Misoprostol (PGE1 analog) directly replaces the prostaglandins that NSAIDs deplete. NSAIDs inhibit COX → ↓prostaglandins → ↓mucus, ↓bicarbonate, ↑acid vulnerability. Misoprostol restores these cytoprotective functions. Sucralfate is NOT effective for NSAID-induced ulcers for poorly understood reasons. Note: if patient were pregnant, use PPI instead (misoprostol is contraindicated).
Q7 (LO 5). A patient undergoing cisplatin chemotherapy develops severe nausea. The oncologist explains that the drug triggers nausea by causing enterochromaffin cells in the GI mucosa to release serotonin. Which pathway does this activate?
Chemotherapy → enterochromaffin cells release 5-HT → activates 5-HT3 receptors on vagal afferents → NTS → emetic center. This is the peripheral pathway. The CTZ (D2, NK1) is also activated by blood-borne cisplatin metabolites. Ondansetron works by blocking 5-HT3 at both sites.
Q8 (LO 6). A 24-year-old medical student gets nauseated on a ferry. His colleague gives him ondansetron. Despite taking it, he continues to vomit. Why is ondansetron ineffective here?
Motion sickness signals travel from the inner ear vestibular system → cerebellum → emetic center, mediated by H1 and M1 receptors. Ondansetron is a 5-HT3 antagonist — it has no activity at H1 or M1 receptors. The correct drugs for motion sickness are diphenhydramine (H1 blocker) or scopolamine (M1 blocker).
Q9 (LO 6). A 55-year-old man with Parkinson's disease develops gastroparesis. His neurologist is asked to recommend an antiemetic/prokinetic. Which drug is CONTRAINDICATED?
Metoclopramide crosses the BBB and blocks D2 receptors centrally → worsens dopamine deficiency in Parkinson's → accelerates parkinsonian symptoms. Domperidone is a D2 antagonist that does NOT cross the BBB (stays peripheral) and is safer in Parkinson's, though not available in the US. Ondansetron is safe.
Q10 (LO 7). During a cholera outbreak in a refugee camp, oral rehydration solution (ORS) is distributed. A medic asks why plain water is insufficient. Which is the best explanation?
SGLT1 cotransports Na⁺ and glucose together into enterocytes — water follows osmotically. Cholera toxin disables Na-Cl co-transport but SPARES SGLT1. Plain water lacks glucose, so SGLT1 cannot be activated and Na (and thus water) cannot be absorbed efficiently. The glucose in ORS is the critical driver of Na — and thus water — absorption even in active cholera.
Q11 (LO 7). A parent brings her 5-year-old child to the ED after he ingested a large quantity of Pepto-Bismol during a stomach flu. The parent notes his tongue and stool are black. What is the most important concern regarding this child?
Bismuth subsalicylate contains a salicylate component. Salicylates given to children with viral illness can cause Reye syndrome (acute hepatic failure + encephalopathy). The black stool/tongue is from bismuth sulfide — it is BENIGN and not GI bleeding. The key danger here is the salicylate in a child with viral gastroenteritis.
Q12 (LO 8). A patient with hepatic encephalopathy is started on lactulose. His family is confused — "how does a laxative help his brain?" Which mechanism explains lactulose's benefit?
Colonic bacteria metabolize lactulose → lactic acid + acetic acid → lowers luminal pH. This acidification converts NH3 (membrane-permeable, absorbed → reaches brain) into NH4⁺ (ionized, membrane-impermeable, trapped in colon lumen) → excreted in stool → lower serum and CNS ammonia → improved hepatic encephalopathy.
Q13 (LO 8). A patient is started on opioid pain management after hip surgery. The surgical team also starts docusate sodium (Colace). Which statement BEST describes the rationale?
Docusate is a stool softener (anionic surfactant) that lowers stool surface tension, allowing water and fat to penetrate and soften the stool mass. It does NOT stimulate peristalsis. After hip surgery, straining (Valsalva) is dangerous — docusate prevents painful hard stools. For opioid-induced constipation, a stimulant laxative (senna) is actually more appropriate, which is why "senna + docusate" is a common combo order for opioid patients.
Q14 (LO 9). A 29-year-old man with Crohn's disease is started on infliximab. Before initiating therapy, which screening step is MOST critical?
Anti-TNF-α agents (infliximab, adalimumab) cause significant immunosuppression. TNF-α is critical for granuloma maintenance — blocking it can reactivate latent TB (granulomas dissolve → bacilli released → active TB). ALL patients must be screened with PPD or IGRA (QuantiFERON) + CXR before starting anti-TNF therapy. TPMT genotyping is required before azathioprine/6-MP, not infliximab.
Q15 (LO 9). Which of the following BEST distinguishes adalimumab from infliximab?
Both target TNF-α, but: Infliximab = CHIMERIC (mouse + human Fc region) → given by IV infusion → more immunogenic (anti-drug antibodies, infusion reactions). Adalimumab = FULLY HUMAN → given by SQ self-injection → less immunogenic → preferred for outpatient maintenance. Both are approved for Crohn's and UC. Neither requires TPMT testing (that's for thiopurines).
