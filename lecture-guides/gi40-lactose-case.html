Why does low lactase cause these specific symptoms?
  • 1Lactose not cleaved: Without lactase, lactose (glucose + galactose, β-1,4 bond) passes intact from the small intestine into the colon. No other brush border enzyme can cleave β-1,4 bonds.
  • 2Osmotic diarrhea: Unabsorbed lactose is an osmotically active solute. It draws water into the colonic lumen → watery diarrhea. This is the direct osmotic mechanism.
  • 3Bacterial fermentation: Colonic bacteria ferment lactose → short-chain fatty acids (butyrate, propionate, acetate) + CO₂ + H₂ gas. Gas production → bloating, cramping, gurgling (borborygmi).
  • 4Acidic stool: Fermentation products lower colonic pH → can cause perianal irritation. Stool pH <5.5 is a clinical clue to carbohydrate malabsorption.
  • 5Positive hydrogen breath test: H₂ produced by bacterial fermentation is absorbed into blood and exhaled → elevated breath H₂ after lactose challenge is the standard non-invasive diagnostic test.
Ruling out alternative diagnoses
  • 1Celiac disease excluded: Normal small bowel biopsy — celiac causes villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, and intraepithelial lymphocytosis on biopsy. All brush border enzymes would be reduced (diffuse mucosal damage), not just lactase.
  • 2Crohn's disease excluded: No history of abdominal surgery, no pathologic changes on biopsy. Crohn's causes transmural inflammation, skip lesions, granulomas.
  • 3IBS re-evaluated: The prior IBS diagnosis was likely incorrect — or at minimum incomplete. IBS is a functional diagnosis (no structural or enzymatic cause), but her enzyme data reveals a clear biochemical explanation. Dairy-specific symptom triggers and the hydrogen breath test pattern are not typical of IBS.
  • 4Sucrase-isomaltase deficiency excluded: Sucrase 77 and isomaltase 83 are both within normal range. This condition would cause symptoms from starch and sucrose ingestion, not specifically dairy.

How Glucose and Galactose Move from Lumen to Blood

The complete transport pathway — apical uptake, intracellular handling, basolateral exit, and portal delivery.

Lactase-phlorizin hydrolase is a single integral membrane protein anchored in the brush border with two catalytic sites: one cleaves lactose (β-galactosidase activity), the other cleaves phlorizin and other glycolipids (phlorizin hydrolase activity). The enzyme is expressed exclusively in the small intestinal brush border. The patient's lactase activity of 2 (vs. normal 44) means this step essentially doesn't happen — lactose passes to the colon intact.

Step 2 — Apical membrane uptake via SGLT1

SGLT1 (Sodium-Glucose Linked Transporter 1, SLC5A1) is the sole apical transporter for both glucose and galactose. This is a secondary active transporter:

  • 1Cotransport stoichiometry: 2 Na⁺ ions are cotransported with every 1 glucose or galactose molecule. Both Na⁺ and the sugar must bind before the transporter can cycle.
  • 2Driving force: The low intracellular Na⁺ maintained by basolateral Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase creates the electrochemical gradient that drives Na⁺ (and therefore glucose/galactose) into the cell — even against the glucose concentration gradient.
  • 3Energy source: ATP is consumed indirectly — by Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase to maintain the Na⁺ gradient. SGLT1 itself uses no ATP but is ultimately dependent on this gradient. Hence "secondary" active transport.
  • 4Location: Expressed throughout the small intestine, with highest density in the proximal jejunum. Expression in the duodenum is lower. Also expressed in kidney proximal tubule (renal glucose reabsorption — the target of SGLT2 inhibitor drugs).
Why galactose uses SGLT1 too: SGLT1 has structural affinity for both D-glucose and D-galactose (stereoisomers differing only at the C-4 position). Fructose, by contrast, cannot use SGLT1 — it uses the separate GLUT5 transporter. This is why glucose-galactose malabsorption (rare congenital SGLT1 mutation) spares fructose absorption.
Clinical relevance — ORT: SGLT1's sodium cotransport is the basis of oral rehydration therapy. Adding glucose to electrolyte solution activates SGLT1 → drives Na⁺ absorption → water follows osmotically. Even maximal CFTR-mediated secretion (cholera) cannot overcome SGLT1-mediated absorption with adequate glucose present.
Glucose-galactose malabsorption: Rare autosomal recessive mutation in SGLT1 → neither glucose nor galactose absorbed → life-threatening osmotic diarrhea from birth that stops completely with a fructose-only diet. Dramatic illustration of SGLT1's indispensability.
Steps 3 & 4 — Intracellular transit and basolateral exit via GLUT2

Once inside the enterocyte, glucose and galactose move toward the basolateral membrane. Neither undergoes significant metabolism within the enterocyte (unlike fructose, some of which is converted to glucose).

GLUT2 (SLC2A2) on the basolateral membrane mediates exit:

  • 1Facilitated diffusion: GLUT2 requires no energy and no Na⁺ — it moves monosaccharides down their concentration gradient. As long as intracellular glucose/galactose exceeds portal blood concentration, exit is passive and continuous.
  • 2Low affinity, high capacity: GLUT2 has low affinity (high Km ~17 mM) but very high maximum transport rate. It is designed to handle large post-meal glucose surges without saturating.
  • 3Also transports fructose: GLUT2 is non-selective — it transports glucose, galactose, and fructose equally. This is why all three monosaccharides use the same exit route despite entering via different apical transporters.
GLUT2 apical recruitment: At very high luminal glucose concentrations, GLUT2 can be rapidly inserted into the apical membrane as a high-capacity overflow absorption route alongside SGLT1. This may account for significant additional glucose absorption after large carbohydrate meals.
Galactose — hepatic fate: After GLUT2 exit into portal blood, galactose travels to the liver where it is almost completely extracted on first pass. Hepatic galactokinase phosphorylates it to galactose-1-phosphate → UDP-galactose → incorporated into glycogen or glycoproteins. Very little galactose reaches systemic circulation normally. This is why galactosemia (deficiency of galactose-1-phosphate uridylyltransferase) causes hepatic accumulation and liver damage.

Question 3

Primary Carbohydrate Sources & Their Digesting Enzymes

What carbohydrates are in the diet, where they come from, and which brush border oligosaccharidases handle each one.

The three major dietary carbohydrate sources
Starch (~60% of carbohydrate intake)
The dominant dietary carbohydrate. A glucose polymer found in grains (wheat, rice, corn), legumes, root vegetables (potatoes). Two forms: amylose (linear, α-1,4 bonds only, ~20%) and amylopectin (branched, α-1,4 backbone + α-1,6 at branch points every 24–30 glucose units, ~80%). The α-1,6 branch points require specific enzymatic action.
Lactose (dairy — the problem here)
A disaccharide of glucose + galactose linked by a β-1,4 bond. Found exclusively in mammalian milk and dairy products (milk, yogurt, cheese, ice cream, butter). The β-1,4 bond is unusual — no amylase can cleave it. It requires the specific β-galactosidase activity of lactase-phlorizin hydrolase. This enzyme is the only brush border carbohydrase that handles β linkages.
Sucrose (table sugar)
A disaccharide of glucose + fructose linked by an α-1,2 bond (between the anomeric carbons of both sugars). Found in cane sugar, beet sugar, fruits, and processed foods. The α-1,2 bond is specifically cleaved by the sucrase domain of the sucrase-isomaltase complex. Sucrase activity is normal (77 vs. 87), so sucrose is handled without difficulty.
The brush border oligosaccharidases — locations, substrates, products

After luminal digestion by salivary and pancreatic amylases reduces starch to oligosaccharides, the brush border enzymes complete the final hydrolysis to monosaccharides. These are integral membrane proteins with their active sites facing the intestinal lumen, expressed throughout the duodenum and jejunum (highest density in proximal jejunum):

Starch digestion pathway — from food to monosaccharides
  • 1Salivary α-amylase (mouth/stomach): Cleaves internal α-1,4 bonds → maltose, maltotriose, α-limit dextrins. Cannot cleave α-1,6 branch points or terminal bonds. Active until denatured by gastric acid (~pH 3).
  • 2Pancreatic α-amylase (duodenum): Identical specificity to salivary amylase but secreted in far greater quantity. Dominates starch digestion. Activated by HCO₃⁻ environment. Reduces starch to a mixture of maltose, maltotriose, and α-limit dextrins.
  • 3Maltase (brush border): Cleaves maltose → 2 glucose; maltotriose → glucose + maltose. Also processively cleaves longer linear chains.
  • 4Isomaltase (brush border): Specifically cleaves the α-1,6 branch point bonds in α-limit dextrins, releasing the branched glucose units that amylase cannot access.
  • 5Final product: 100% free glucose, absorbed via SGLT1. Starch digestion requires the sequential action of luminal amylases (endo-) followed by brush border exoglycosidases. No single enzyme accomplishes complete digestion.
Question 4

Is the Time of Onset Significant?

Why primary lactase non-persistence characteristically develops in late childhood to early adulthood — and what this tells us about the underlying mechanism.

YES
The onset timing is highly significant — it is a diagnostic clue
The patient is 18 years old with symptoms worsening over the past year. This gradual onset in late adolescence is entirely characteristic of primary lactase non-persistence. The timing reflects the developmental biology of LCT gene regulation. If symptoms had been present from birth, congenital lactase deficiency would be suspected. If they developed acutely after a GI illness, secondary lactase deficiency would be suspected.
The developmental biology of lactase expression
  • 1Fetal/neonatal life: Lactase expression is high from ~26 weeks gestation onward. This is essential — breast milk is the sole caloric source for infants, and lactose is its primary carbohydrate (~7g/100mL). Every mammal (including all humans) is born with high lactase activity.
  • 2After weaning — the divergence: In most mammals and in ~65% of the global human population, lactase expression begins declining after weaning. This decline is genetically programmed — controlled by regulatory variants upstream of the LCT gene. It is the ancestral, normal mammalian condition. Lactase persistence is the derived (mutant) state.
  • 3Timeline of decline: In people with lactase non-persistence, the decline is gradual. It typically begins in childhood (ages 2–5) and continues through adolescence. By the late teens to early twenties, lactase activity has fallen to its permanently low adult level. At 18, this is exactly the right age for these symptoms to be becoming clinically apparent.
  • 4Gradual symptom onset: Because the decline in lactase activity is slow, symptoms develop gradually over years rather than acutely. This also explains why the prior IBS diagnosis was plausible — the symptoms resembled functional bowel disorder and worsened gradually without an obvious precipitating event.
The genetics of lactase persistence

Lactase persistence (continued high lactase expression into adulthood) is caused by single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) in a regulatory region ~14 kb upstream of the LCT gene — not in the coding sequence itself, but in an enhancer element that controls transcription.

Key SNPs:
C/T-13910 (rs4988235) — European populations. The T allele maintains lactase expression. This mutation arose ~10,000 years ago in Northern Europe, coinciding with the emergence of cattle domestication and dairying.

G/A-13915, C/G-13907, T/G-13915 — Independent mutations in African and Middle Eastern pastoralist populations. Convergent evolution — lactase persistence evolved independently multiple times in dairying cultures.
Evolutionary significance: Lactase persistence is one of the strongest signals of positive selection in the human genome — in populations that domesticated cattle, individuals who could digest milk as adults had a significant caloric advantage, especially during famines. This selective pressure drove lactase persistence alleles to high frequency (~90% in Northern Europeans, ~80% in Scandinavians) over ~400 generations.
Global prevalence of lactase non-persistence: ~65% of adult humans worldwide cannot fully digest lactose. Highest rates: East Asian (~90%), West African (~75%), Southern European (~50%). Lowest rates: Northern European (~5-15%), nomadic East African pastoralists (~15%). The patient's ethnicity is not specified, but primary lactase non-persistence is the most common carbohydrate enzyme deficiency globally.

Question 5

What Did the Physician Recommend?

The full clinical management toolkit for primary lactase non-persistence — from avoidance to supplementation to tolerance strategies.

Rx
The physician gave recommendations on "what to do when she wants to eat dairy"
The key phrase in the case is that the physician provided options for when she wants to eat dairy — not simply "avoid all dairy forever." This reflects the modern management approach: total dairy avoidance is not necessary for most patients, and several evidence-based strategies allow lactose-containing foods to be tolerated. The goal is symptom control while maintaining calcium and vitamin D intake.
Strategy 1 — Exogenous lactase supplements

Lactase enzyme supplements (e.g., Lactaid) contain β-galactosidase from Aspergillus oryzae or Kluyveromyces lactis (fungal/yeast sources). Taken immediately before or with dairy-containing meals.

Mechanism: Exogenous β-galactosidase hydrolyzes lactose in the food (pre-digestion if added to milk) or in the small intestinal lumen (if taken as a pill with the meal) → glucose + galactose → absorbed normally via SGLT1. Bypasses the absent endogenous lactase entirely.

Limitations: Efficacy is dose-dependent and variable between individuals. May not completely eliminate symptoms with very large lactose loads. Does not work if forgotten or taken late. Available as drops, chewable tablets, or capsules.

Strategy 2 — Reduced-lactose and lactose-free products

Lactose-free milk is produced by adding exogenous lactase to milk and allowing pre-hydrolysis of lactose before packaging. The resulting product contains glucose and galactose (already hydrolyzed) instead of lactose — tastes slightly sweeter but is nutritionally equivalent.

Key fact: Hard aged cheeses (cheddar, parmesan, Swiss) have very low residual lactose content because the fermentation process during cheese-making converts most lactose. A patient who cannot tolerate a glass of milk may be perfectly comfortable eating aged cheese.

Yogurt with live active cultures is often better tolerated than milk because the bacterial cultures (Lactobacillus, Streptococcus thermophilus) produce their own β-galactosidase that aids lactose digestion in the gut.

Strategy 3 — Dose and context modifications
  • 1Small doses with meals: Taking dairy with solid food slows gastric emptying → lactose delivered to the small intestine more slowly → residual lactase (even the tiny 2 units) has more time per unit of lactose → better tolerance than drinking milk on an empty stomach.
  • 2Distribute intake: Spreading dairy intake throughout the day (small amounts with each meal) rather than one large serving allows better symptom management within residual enzyme capacity.
  • 3Gradual colonic adaptation: Regular small exposures to lactose promote colonic bacterial adaptation — the bacterial community gradually shifts toward more β-galactosidase-producing bacteria that can ferment lactose more efficiently, producing less gas and fewer symptoms over weeks.
Strategy 4 — Calcium and vitamin D maintenance

This is the most medically important nutritional consideration. Dairy is the primary dietary source of calcium and vitamin D in most Western diets. Eliminating dairy without replacement creates real risk:

Risk at this patient's age (18 yo): Peak bone mass is achieved between ages 18–25. This is the critical window for building skeletal calcium stores that protect against osteoporosis decades later. Calcium restriction during this window has permanent consequences for bone density.
Alternatives the physician likely recommended: Calcium-fortified non-dairy milks (soy, almond, oat — check that they're fortified), canned salmon/sardines with bones, leafy greens (kale, bok choy — spinach contains oxalates that reduce calcium absorption), calcium supplements (calcium citrate is better absorbed than calcium carbonate, especially without food). Vitamin D supplementation separately since most non-dairy foods don't contain it.

The hydrogen breath test is the standard non-invasive diagnostic test for lactase deficiency. Method: patient ingests a standard lactose dose (usually 25-50g); exhaled H₂ is measured at baseline and every 30 minutes for 3 hours.
