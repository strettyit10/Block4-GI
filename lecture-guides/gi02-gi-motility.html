▼ 1. Slow Waves & Basic Electrical Rhythm (BER)

⭐ Slow waves (= BER) are rhythmic oscillations in resting membrane potential that are always present in GI smooth muscle and set the maximal possible contraction rate — but they do NOT by themselves cause contractions.

🔬 BER Origin & Regional Rates

BER originates from Interstitial Cells of Cajal (ICC) — "pacemaker cells" of the GI tract — located in the myenteric plexus region of the muscularis externa.

RegionBER FrequencyClinical Significance
Stomach (corpus)~3/minMax gastric peristalsis = 3 contractions/min
Duodenum~11–12/minHighest rate in small intestine — sets "pacemaker"
Ileum~8–9/minFrequency gradient → aborally decreasing
ColonVariable (slow baseline; fast during mass movements)Haustration occurs at slow rate; mass movements are discrete events
⚡ High-Yield:
  • BER sets the ceiling rate of contractions — contractions cannot occur faster than the BER frequency for that segment.
  • Slow waves alone do NOT contract the muscle; they only set the timing window for action potentials to fire.
  • ICC = pacemaker; their loss → disrupted motility (e.g., gastroparesis in some forms).

🔬 Slow Wave Characteristics

  • Resting membrane potential of GI smooth muscle: approximately −65 to −55 mV
  • Slow waves oscillate rhythmically between this resting potential and a less-negative peak (~−40 mV)
  • Generated spontaneously by ICC; conducted to adjacent smooth muscle cells via gap junctions
  • Propagate aborally (toward the colon) along the length of the GI tract
  • NOT action potentials — they are subthreshold oscillations
⚠️ TRAP: Slow Waves ≠ Contractions ▼
  • Many students confuse slow waves with action potentials. Slow waves are subthreshold oscillations — the muscle only contracts when APs fire on top of slow wave peaks.
  • Slow waves are always present whether or not the gut is contracting.
  • A segment can have its BER active but produce no contractions at all if membrane potential never reaches the AP threshold of −40 mV.

▼ 2. Action Potentials on Slow Waves

⭐ When the membrane potential at the peak of a slow wave reaches −40 mV (the action potential threshold), voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open and an AP fires — producing a contraction.

🔬 AP Mechanism: Smooth Muscle vs. Cardiac/Skeletal

PhaseMechanismKey Ion(s)
DepolarizationVoltage-gated Ca²⁺ and Na⁺ channels openCa²⁺, Na⁺ (inward)
PlateauContinued Ca²⁺ entry (no fast inactivation)Ca²⁺ (sustained)
RepolarizationDelayed K⁺ conductance increases; K⁺ exitsK⁺ (outward)

Unique feature: GI smooth muscle APs use Ca²⁺/Na⁺ for the upstroke (not just Na⁺ like skeletal muscle). The plateau is driven by Ca²⁺ — the same Ca²⁺ that triggers cross-bridge cycling and contraction.

⚠️ TRAP: AP Threshold is −40 mV, Not −55 mV ▼
  • The resting membrane potential is around −65 mV. APs fire only when the peak of the slow wave reaches −40 mV.
  • Depolarizing inputs (stretch, ACh) shift the slow wave peaks toward −40 mV, increasing the likelihood that each slow wave generates an AP.
  • Hyperpolarizing inputs (NE, VIP) shift peaks away from −40 mV, reducing AP likelihood.

▼ 3. Depolarizers & Hyperpolarizers

⭐ The rate and strength of GI contractions are modulated by inputs that either depolarize the membrane (bringing it closer to AP threshold) or hyperpolarize it (moving it away from threshold).

🔬 Modulators Table

AgentEffectSource / Context
Stretch (distension)Depolarize ↑Mechanical; food in gut; reflex initiation
ACh (Muscarinic)Depolarize ↑PSNS / ENS; ↑motility + ↑secretion
GastrinDepolarize ↑G cells (antrum); stimulated by food/protein

🧠 Mnemonic: SAGES vs. NVN (Depolarizers vs. Hyperpolarizers)
▼

Depolarize (↑ motility): Stretch, ACh, Gastrin, Enterochromaffin/5-HT, Substance P = SAGES

Hyperpolarize (↓ motility): NE (norepinephrine), VIP, NO = NVN

⚠️ TRAP: VIP & NO Are Inhibitory — They Relax Smooth Muscle ▼
  • VIP and NO are released by inhibitory ENS neurons. They hyperpolarize smooth muscle → relaxation.
  • VIP mediates: receptive relaxation (stomach), descending inhibition in peristalsis, LES relaxation.
  • Loss of VIP/NO neurons → LES cannot relax → Achalasia.

▼ 4. Contraction Strength Regulation

⭐ The number of action potentials fired per slow wave peak determines contraction strength — more APs = more Ca²⁺ entry = more cross-bridge cycling = stronger, more sustained contraction.

🔬 AP Frequency → Contraction Strength

  • 0 APs on a slow wave = no contraction (muscle relaxed)
  • 1–2 APs = weak twitch
  • Multiple APs per slow wave = tetanic-like sustained contraction
  • Frequency limited by BER rate (e.g., stomach cannot contract faster than 3×/min)

Ca²⁺ enters via voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels during APs → binds calmodulin → activates myosin light-chain kinase (MLCK) → phosphorylates myosin → cross-bridge cycling → contraction.

⚠️ TRAP: BER Sets the Rate Ceiling, Not the Strength ▼
  • BER controls when contractions can occur (sets the timing). The strength of each contraction is determined by how many APs fire on each slow wave peak.
  • This is why increasing vagal (ACh) tone increases both frequency and strength of contractions — it depolarizes the membrane, making APs more likely and more numerous per cycle.

🧪 Testing Your Understanding

TYU 1.

A patient is given a drug that hyperpolarizes GI smooth muscle cells by opening K⁺ channels. How would this affect BER slow waves, action potential generation, and the strength of peristaltic contractions? What class of drug might produce this effect?

The drug would keep resting membrane potential more negative (hyperpolarized), making it harder for slow wave peaks to reach −40 mV AP threshold. Result: BER frequency is unchanged (slow waves are always present and set by ICC), but fewer or no APs fire on each slow wave peak → fewer/no Ca²⁺ influx events → weaker or absent contractions. This mimics the effect of sympathetic (NE) activation. Examples: opioids act partly this way (↓GI motility via K⁺ channel opening in ENS neurons); alpha-2 adrenergic agonists similarly inhibit GI motility.

Consider: Why does an opioid like morphine cause constipation? (Hint: think about the ENS, BER, and what it means when fewer APs fire.)

TYU 2.

Explain why the maximum peristaltic rate differs between the stomach (~3/min), duodenum (~12/min), and ileum (~9/min). What structure generates the BER, and what is its anatomical location?

The BER (slow wave) frequency is intrinsic to each GI segment and is set by Interstitial Cells of Cajal (ICC) in the myenteric plexus. ICCs act as pacemakers. The duodenum has the highest ICC firing rate (~11–12/min), which progressively decreases along the small intestine (frequency gradient). The stomach ICC fires at only ~3/min. This gradient ensures net aborally-directed propulsion — the proximal segment is always "cycling faster" than distal. Contractions cannot exceed the BER rate for each segment, so the stomach is limited to ~3 peristaltic waves/minute, no matter how strongly ACh stimulates it.

Consider: If ICC are destroyed by inflammation, what would happen to coordinated peristalsis? (Think: gastroparesis, as in diabetic autonomic neuropathy.)

▼ 1. Stages of Swallowing

⭐ Swallowing consists of 3 stages: the oral (voluntary) stage, and the pharyngeal + esophageal stages (both involuntary, coordinated by the medullary swallowing center).

🔬 Three Stages of Swallowing

⚡ High-Yield:
  • LES normally relaxes via VIP and NO released by ENS inhibitory neurons — the same neurons lost in achalasia.
  • Secondary peristalsis is entirely ENS-driven — persists even after vagotomy.
⚠️ TRAP: Secondary Peristalsis Does NOT Require Swallowing ▼
  • Secondary peristalsis is triggered by distension of the esophagus, NOT by swallowing. It is entirely intrinsic (ENS).
  • After vagotomy, primary esophageal peristalsis is lost but secondary peristalsis persists — the esophagus can still clear itself of retained food via the ENS.

▼ 2. Achalasia — Clinical Correlate

⭐ Achalasia = failure of the LES to relax due to loss of inhibitory VIP/NO ENS neurons → food backs up → esophageal dilation → GERD.

🔬 Pathophysiology & Features

FeatureDetail
MechanismLoss of ENS inhibitory neurons that release VIP and NO → LES cannot relax during swallowing
Effect on peristalsisBoth primary and secondary esophageal peristalsis impaired (same neurons involved)
ConsequencesFood accumulates above LES → progressive esophageal dilation → GERD + regurgitation + dysphagia
PresentationDysphagia to both solids AND liquids (vs. mechanical obstruction = solids only); regurgitation of undigested food; heartburn
TreatmentPneumatic (balloon) dilation; Heller myotomy (surgical LES cut); botulinum toxin injection (temporary — inhibits ACh at LES)
⚠️ TRAP: Achalasia Dysphagia Is Solids AND Liquids ▼
  • Mechanical obstruction (e.g., esophageal carcinoma, stricture) causes dysphagia to solids first, then liquids.
  • Achalasia = dysphagia to BOTH solids and liquids from the start — because the problem is motor (LES won't relax) rather than structural narrowing.
  • Botulinum toxin works by blocking ACh release at the LES → relieves tonically elevated LES pressure. Effect is temporary because the neurons regenerate.

▼ 3. Stomach Motility

⭐ The stomach has 3 muscle layers (oblique + circular + longitudinal) for grinding; the corpus ICC sets BER at ~3/min. Two key concepts: receptive relaxation (accommodation) and regulation of gastric emptying.

🔬 Receptive Relaxation (Gastric Accommodation)

FeatureDetail
DefinitionStomach wall relaxes as food enters, accommodating a large volume without a large increase in intragastric pressure
MediatorVIP (vasoactive intestinal peptide) — released by ENS inhibitory neurons
Neural pathwayVagus nerve → ENS inhibitory neurons → VIP/NO release → smooth muscle relaxation
Normal capacity~50 mL empty → 10–20× expansion (up to 1–1.5 L) with minimal pressure rise
Lost afterVagotomy; gastric bypass surgery
Clinical consequenceLoss of receptive relaxation → rapid pressure rise with small meals → early satiety; principle behind bariatric surgery for weight loss
⚡ High-Yield:
  • Receptive relaxation = vagally-mediated via VIP; lost after vagotomy.
  • Bariatric surgery (e.g., sleeve gastrectomy) removes most of the stomach fundus, which is responsible for accommodation → early satiety → reduced food intake.

🔬 Gastric Emptying — Rate by Food Type

Food TypeEmptying RateReason
Isotonic salineFastestNo enterogastric inhibition; no CCK or secretin release
CarbohydratesFastMild osmoreceptor stimulation; minimal fat/protein signals
ProteinsIntermediateCCK released by I cells in duodenum → slows emptying
Fats / OleateSlowestStrongest CCK + secretin release; enterogastric reflex (SNS) inhibits antral contractions

Clinical: Fatty/protein meals keep you feeling full longer (delayed gastric emptying). Gastroparesis (delayed emptying) → impaired fat digestion + early satiety paradoxically.

⚠️ TRAP: Fat Delays Gastric Emptying via CCK — Not Just Mechanical ▼
  • Fat → CCK release from duodenal I cells → acts directly on stomach (↓antral contractions, ↑pyloric tone) AND stimulates vagal afferents → powerful brake on gastric emptying.
  • Enterogastric reflex: duodenal distension, acid, fat → SNS afferents → inhibit gastric motility (this is why you don't feel hungry soon after a fatty meal).

🧪 Testing Your Understanding

TYU 1.

A patient presents with dysphagia to both solids and liquids, regurgitation of undigested food, and recurrent aspiration events at night. Esophageal manometry reveals elevated resting LES pressure that fails to relax with swallowing, and absent primary peristalsis. What neurotransmitters are lost and what treatment options exist?

This is achalasia. The underlying pathology is loss of inhibitory ENS neurons that release VIP and NO. These neurons normally cause LES relaxation ahead of the peristaltic wave. Without them, the LES remains tonically contracted. Both primary (vagal) and secondary (ENS) peristalsis are impaired. Treatments: (1) Pneumatic balloon dilation — mechanically disrupts LES muscle fibers; (2) Heller myotomy — surgical incision of LES; (3) Botulinum toxin injection — blocks ACh release at LES → reduces LES pressure temporarily.

Consider: Why does botulinum toxin only provide temporary relief? (Hint: think about what happens to neuromuscular junctions over time.)

TYU 2.

After a surgical vagotomy for peptic ulcer disease, a patient complains of early satiety after small meals. Explain the physiological mechanism and identify what is lost after vagotomy that contributes to this symptom.

Vagotomy destroys the efferent vagal pathway required for receptive relaxation (gastric accommodation). Normally, when food enters the stomach, vagal efferent fibers activate ENS inhibitory neurons that release VIP → smooth muscle of the fundus and corpus relaxes → stomach expands without a large pressure rise. After vagotomy, this reflex is abolished. The stomach cannot accommodate food normally, so intragastric pressure rises rapidly with small volumes → mechanoreceptors signal fullness → early satiety. Additionally, vagotomy impairs primary esophageal peristalsis and reduces acid secretion (therapeutic goal), but the accommodation loss is a significant side effect.

Consider: Sleeve gastrectomy removes the fundus. How does this differ mechanistically from vagotomy-induced early satiety?

▼ 1. Peristaltic Reflex — Law of the Intestine

⭐ The Law of the Intestine: a bolus in the gut lumen simultaneously triggers ascending excitation (contraction proximal) and descending inhibition (relaxation distal) — producing net aboral propulsion.

🔬 Neural Basis of the Peristaltic Reflex

ComponentDirectionNeurotransmittersEffect
Ascending excitationOral (proximal) to bolusACh, Substance PCircular muscle contracts; bolus propelled forward
Descending inhibitionAboral (distal) to bolusVIP, NOCircular muscle relaxes; lumen opens ahead of bolus
Initiating signalAt bolus locationSerotonin (5-HT) from enterochromaffin cells → interneuronsStretch → 5-HT release → ENS interneurons → coordinate reflex

Direction: Normally aboral (toward the colon). During vomiting and during voluntary suppression of defecation in the rectum, retrograde peristalsis occurs.

⚡ High-Yield:
  • Ascending = ACh + Substance P (tachykinin) = excitatory
  • Descending = VIP + NO = inhibitory
  • Serotonin (5-HT3/4 receptors) initiates the reflex — this is why 5-HT3 antagonists (ondansetron) are antiemetic and reduce GI motility as a side effect
⚠️ TRAP: Retrograde Peristalsis Is NOT the Same as Normal Peristalsis Going Backward ▼
  • Retrograde peristalsis is an active, coordinated process — it's the same peristaltic reflex but oriented orally instead of aborally.
  • It occurs physiologically: (1) in the colon/rectum when you voluntarily suppress defecation — feces pushed back; (2) in vomiting — retrograde peristalsis of small intestine + stomach to expel contents.

▼ 2. Segmentation

⭐ Segmentation = alternating propulsive and receiving pockets of intestine; serves dual purpose of mixing chyme with digestive enzymes AND slowly propelling it aborally. More intrinsic/ENS-driven than peristalsis.

🔬 Peristalsis vs. Segmentation — Side-by-Side

FeaturePeristalsisSegmentation
PatternCoordinated wave moving aborallyAlternating propulsive + receiving segments
Primary functionPropulsionMixing AND propulsion
Neural controlENS + extrinsic (vagal/spinal)Primarily ENS (intrinsic)
RateLimited by BER frequencyContractions at BER rate
CoverageShort, discrete segmentsMay cover longer areas simultaneously
Net directionStrictly aboral (normally)Net aboral but mixing predominates
Clinical relevanceImpaired in: achalasia (esoph), neuropathyImportant for nutrient/enzyme mixing, absorption
⚠️ TRAP: Segmentation Propels AND Mixes — Not Just Mixing ▼
  • Students often think segmentation is only for mixing. It also contributes to net aboral propulsion because the propulsive and receiving segment pattern gradually moves content forward.
  • The net propulsive effect of segmentation is slower than peristalsis — this is appropriate for the small intestine where time is needed for digestion and absorption.

▼ 3. Migrating Myoelectric Complex (MMC)

⭐ The MMC = the interdigestive "housekeeper" — sweeps undigested material and bacteria from stomach to terminal ileum every 75–120 min when you haven't eaten. Phase III is the only propulsive phase and requires motilin.

🔬 MMC Phases

PhaseDurationActivityPropulsive?
Phase I~40–60 minQuiescence — no contractionsNo
Phase II~20–30 minIrregular contractions; increasing electrical activityNo
Phase III~5–10 minIntense, regular, coordinated peristaltic contractions sweeping from stomach to terminal ileumYes — only propulsive phase
Phase IV~5 minTransition back to quiescence (Phase I)No
⚡ High-Yield:
  • MMC begins 3–4 hours after the last meal (during interdigestive period)
  • Phase III = initiated by motilin (released by M cells = enterochromaffin-like cells of the small intestine)
  • MMC is independent of vagal stimulation — driven by ENS + motilin
  • Eating STOPS the MMC immediately — digestive motility (peristalsis/segmentation) takes over
  • If you eat continuously (grazing) → no MMC → bacteria accumulate in small intestine → potential SIBO (small intestinal bacterial overgrowth)

Clinical relevance of motilin: Erythromycin (antibiotic) is a motilin receptor agonist — used as a prokinetic agent in gastroparesis to stimulate Phase III-like contractions and enhance gastric emptying.

⚠️ TRAP: MMC Requires Fasting — Eating Stops It Immediately ▼
  • The MMC is an interdigestive pattern — it only occurs when the gut is empty. As soon as food is ingested, the MMC is replaced by the normal fed-state motility patterns (peristalsis + segmentation).
  • "Grazing" (eating small amounts continuously) suppresses MMC → impaired bacterial clearance. This is the physiological basis for recommending structured meal timing in patients prone to SIBO.

▼ 4. Gallbladder & Bile Secretion

⭐ Fat in the duodenum triggers CCK → gallbladder contraction + sphincter of Oddi relaxation → bile into duodenum for fat emulsification. Between meals, bile is stored in the gallbladder and recycled via enterohepatic circulation.

🔬 Gallbladder Contraction Regulation

SignalSourceEffect on GallbladderEffect on Sphincter of Oddi
CCK (cholecystokinin)I cells (duodenum) — triggered by fats + proteinsContracts (↑bile ejection)Relaxes (opens to duodenum)
Vagus (PSNS/ACh)CNS (cephalic phase; vagal tone)Contracts (↑bile ejection)Relaxes
Interdigestive (between meals)—Relaxed (stores bile)Contracted (closed)

Enterohepatic Circulation: Bile acids secreted into duodenum → emulsify fats → absorbed in terminal ileum → returned to liver via portal vein → re-secreted into bile. ~95% of bile acids are recycled this way.

⚠️ TRAP: CCK Contracts the Gallbladder AND Relaxes the Sphincter of Oddi — Both Are Required ▼
  • For bile to enter the duodenum, BOTH must happen simultaneously: gallbladder contracts AND sphincter of Oddi relaxes. CCK does both.
  • Biliary colic in gallstones: CCK triggers gallbladder contraction against a stone occluding the cystic duct → pain.

🧪 Testing Your Understanding

TYU 1.

A patient is found to have small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) and reports snacking continuously throughout the day rather than eating structured meals. What physiological mechanism connects their eating pattern to bacterial overgrowth? What hormone initiates the relevant housekeeping process, and from which cells is it released?

Continuous snacking suppresses the Migrating Myoelectric Complex (MMC). The MMC is an interdigestive motility pattern that sweeps bacteria and undigested material from the stomach through the terminal ileum to the colon during fasting states. When eating is constant, the gut never enters the interdigestive state, so the MMC never initiates. Without periodic Phase III sweeping contractions, bacteria accumulate in the small intestine → SIBO. The MMC Phase III is triggered by motilin, released by M cells (enterochromaffin-like cells) of the small intestinal mucosa. Clinical note: Erythromycin is a motilin receptor agonist used as a prokinetic in gastroparesis.

Consider: What class of drugs causes SIBO as a side effect by suppressing MMC? (Hint: think about what happens to GI motility with chronic opioid use.)

TYU 2.

Compare and contrast peristalsis and segmentation in the small intestine in terms of neural control, function, and directionality. Which pattern predominates immediately after eating, and why?

Peristalsis: Coordinated aboral wave; Law of the Intestine (ascending ACh/Substance P = contraction; descending VIP/NO = relaxation); both ENS + extrinsic (vagal) control; primarily propulsive. Segmentation: Alternating propulsive/receiving pockets; more intrinsic (ENS) control; dual function of mixing AND propulsion; contractions at BER rate; net aboral movement but slow. After eating, both patterns are active — segmentation predominates in the early postprandial period because efficient mixing with digestive enzymes and bile is needed for digestion and absorption. As the meal progresses distally and absorption occurs, peristalsis becomes more important to propel remaining chyme toward the colon.

Consider: In a patient with diabetic autonomic neuropathy affecting extrinsic GI innervation, which motility pattern would be more preserved — peristalsis or segmentation? Why?

▼ 1. Haustration (Segmental Colonic Motility)

⭐ Haustration = segmental contraction pattern of the large intestine; creates haustra (sacculations) by simultaneous contraction of circular muscle + taenia coli; moves content slowly to maximize water and Na⁺ reabsorption (colonic salvage).

🔬 Haustration Characteristics

FeatureDetail
MechanismCircular muscle + 3 bands of longitudinal muscle (taenia coli) contract simultaneously → haustra (sacs) form
SpeedVery SLOW (appropriate for water absorption)
FunctionColonic salvage: Na⁺ and water reabsorption from feces
Structural noteColon has NO villi — absorption is by Na⁺/water reabsorption through colonocyte pumps
BER regulationContractions at slow colonic BER (variable; baseline is slow)
⚡ High-Yield:
  • Too slow through colon = too much water reabsorbed = constipation
  • Too fast through colon = too little water reabsorbed = diarrhea
  • The colon's absorptive capacity (~1.5 L/day) can be overwhelmed when small intestinal output is too high → secretory diarrhea
⚠️ TRAP: Haustration Moves Content SLOWLY — It Is Not Propulsive in the Usual Sense ▼
  • Haustration moves content very slowly because the goal is maximum water reabsorption. The actual propulsion of feces to the rectum is accomplished by mass movements (discrete propulsive events).

▼ 2. Mass Movements & Gastrocolic Reflex

⭐ Mass movements = discrete, powerful propulsive contractions of the colon that move feces from the colon to the rectum; occur 3–4×/day; strongly stimulated by food entering the stomach via the gastrocolic reflex.

🔬 Mass Movements

FeatureDetail
Frequency3–4 times/day (especially after meals)
MechanismHaustra smooth out → colon becomes a tube → powerful contraction propels feces to rectum
Initiating stimulusGastrocolic reflex (food entering stomach)
Neural mediatorsPNS (vagal) + gastrin + CCK → colonic mass movements

🔬 Gastrocolic Reflex

Trigger: Food/chyme entering the stomach → distension → vagal reflex AND hormone release (gastrin, CCK) → stimulate colonic mass movements → urge to defecate after eating.

Clinical correlation: This explains why many people feel the urge to defecate shortly after eating breakfast (especially in the morning when the colon is most sensitive).

IBD/IBS: Exaggerated gastrocolic reflex contributes to post-meal urgency in IBD and IBS.

⚠️ TRAP: The Gastrocolic Reflex Is BOTH Neural AND Hormonal ▼
  • The gastrocolic reflex involves both the vagus (neural fast pathway) AND hormones (gastrin, CCK — slower). The hormone component explains why the urge may come 20–30 minutes after eating, not just immediately.

▼ 3. Defecation Reflex (Rectosphincteric Reflex)

⭐ Feces enter rectum → stretch receptors → IAS (smooth muscle, involuntary) automatically relaxes → urge to defecate. Actual defecation = voluntary EAS relaxation + Valsalva. Voluntary suppression → retrograde peristalsis returns feces.

🔬 IAS vs. EAS

🔬 Voluntary Control of Defecation

Defecation sequence:

  1. Mass movement propels feces into rectum
  2. Stretch receptors in rectal wall → signal via ENS → IAS reflexively relaxes → awareness of urge
  3. If socially acceptable: EAS voluntarily relaxed + Valsalva maneuver (↑intra-abdominal pressure) → defecation
  4. If suppression desired: EAS voluntarily contracts (puborectalis) → retrograde peristalsis pushes feces back into sigmoid colon → urge subsides temporarily
⚠️ TRAP: IAS Relaxes Automatically — EAS Is the Voluntary Sphincter ▼
  • The IAS relaxation is the involuntary reflex — you cannot prevent it. It creates the urge.
  • The EAS is under voluntary control — you can contract it to temporarily suppress defecation. But if you repeatedly ignore the urge, the rectum can adapt (accommodation), reducing the signal over time → contributes to chronic constipation.
  • In Hirschsprung's disease, the IAS CANNOT relax (due to absent ENS) → the reflex is absent → feces cannot exit → megacolon.

⚠️ TRAP: ENS Is Truly Independent — 500 Million Neurons, as Many as the Spinal Cord ▼
  • The ENS can coordinate complete peristalsis and segmentation without any input from the brain or spinal cord. CNS input (via vagus and splanchnic nerves) modulates the ENS but is not required for basic GI motility.
  • This is why isolated intestinal segments placed in a dish still exhibit peristalsis when distended.

During intense exercise, the sympathetic nervous system (SNS) is dominant (fight-or-flight). LO 1–2: Basic Electrical Rhythm & Smooth Muscle Contraction

Q1.

A researcher records electrical activity from GI smooth muscle cells and notes rhythmic oscillations in membrane potential occurring at a constant frequency even in the absence of neural input. These oscillations do not reach action potential threshold. Which cells are responsible for generating these oscillations, and where are they located?

  • (A) Enterochromaffin cells in the mucosal epithelium
  • (B) Interstitial cells of Cajal in the myenteric plexus
  • (C) Smooth muscle cells of the circular muscle layer
  • (D) ENS motor neurons in the submucosal plexus
  • (E) Goblet cells of the intestinal mucosa
Correct Answer: B — Interstitial cells of Cajal (ICC)
ICC are the pacemaker cells of the GI tract, located in the myenteric plexus region between the circular and longitudinal muscle layers. They generate the Basic Electrical Rhythm (BER) — subthreshold slow wave oscillations that set the maximal contraction frequency for each GI segment. These oscillations are always present and do NOT themselves produce contractions; action potentials (and thus contractions) only occur when slow wave peaks reach −40 mV.

Q2.

The BER frequency in the duodenum is approximately 11–12 per minute, while in the stomach it is approximately 3 per minute. Which of the following best explains the physiological significance of this frequency gradient along the GI tract?

  • (A) It allows the stomach to absorb more nutrients than the duodenum
  • (B) It ensures the proximal segment contracts faster, driving net aboral propulsion
  • (C) It prevents action potentials from propagating between GI segments
  • (D) It causes the ileum to empty into the colon faster than the duodenum fills
  • (E) It produces stronger contractions in the stomach than in the small intestine
Correct Answer: B
The decreasing BER frequency gradient from duodenum (~12/min) to ileum (~8–9/min) ensures the proximal segment can contract at a higher maximal rate than the distal segment. This means content is propelled aborally because the proximal segment "cycles" faster than the distal, maintaining net aboral flow. The stomach's slower rate (3/min) ensures that chyme exits into the duodenum at a controlled rate — at most 3 times per minute — giving the duodenum time to process each bolus.

Q3.

During recording of GI smooth muscle action potentials, it is noted that the upstroke and plateau phase are abolished by a drug that blocks L-type voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels. This finding is most consistent with which of the following?

  • (A) GI smooth muscle APs rely primarily on Na⁺ for depolarization, not Ca²⁺
  • (B) Ca²⁺ entry through voltage-gated channels is required for both the AP upstroke and contraction in GI smooth muscle
  • (C) The BER slow waves are generated by Ca²⁺ channels in ICC
  • (D) Blocking Ca²⁺ channels hyperpolarizes the membrane below resting potential
  • (E) Ca²⁺ channel blockade only affects the plateau phase, not contraction
Correct Answer: B
Unlike skeletal and cardiac muscle (which use Na⁺ for the fast upstroke), GI smooth muscle APs are unique: both depolarization (upstroke) AND the plateau phase are driven by Ca²⁺ and Na⁺ entry through voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels. This entering Ca²⁺ is also the trigger for cross-bridge cycling and contraction. Therefore, L-type Ca²⁺ channel blockers (like verapamil or nifedipine) both abolish the AP and prevent contraction — used clinically in some GI spasm conditions.

Q4.

A patient is administered intravenous cholera toxin, which causes massive hypersecretion of Cl⁻ and water into the intestinal lumen, creating significant intestinal distension. Based on the mechanism of GI smooth muscle modulation, which of the following effects would be expected on GI smooth muscle membrane potential and contraction frequency?

  • (A) Hyperpolarization; decreased contraction frequency
  • (B) Depolarization; increased contraction frequency up to the BER maximum
  • (C) No change in membrane potential; unchanged contraction frequency
  • (D) Depolarization; contraction frequency exceeds the BER maximum
  • (E) Hyperpolarization; increased BER frequency
Correct Answer: B
Massive intestinal distension is a potent depolarizing stimulus — stretch directly depolarizes GI smooth muscle cells and activates ENS mechanoreceptors (triggering stretch-sensitive 5-HT release from enterochromaffin cells). Depolarization shifts membrane potential closer to the −40 mV AP threshold → more APs fire per slow wave peak → increased contraction frequency. However, contractions cannot exceed the BER-set maximum (slow wave frequency) for each segment — the BER is not changed by stretch, only the likelihood of APs occurring. This explains the intense cramps/contractions seen in diarrheal illnesses.

LO 3: Swallowing & Esophageal Peristalsis

Q5.

A 45-year-old man undergoes surgical vagotomy for a bleeding peptic ulcer. Postoperatively, he develops dysphagia. Which type of esophageal peristalsis is most likely to be impaired, and which will be preserved?

  • (A) Primary impaired; secondary preserved
  • (B) Both primary and secondary impaired
  • (C) Secondary impaired; primary preserved
  • (D) Neither type is affected by vagotomy
  • (E) Primary preserved; secondary impaired only at the LES
Correct Answer: A — Primary impaired; secondary preserved
Primary esophageal peristalsis is triggered by swallowing and is entirely vagus-nerve mediated. After vagotomy, primary peristalsis is lost. Secondary esophageal peristalsis, however, is triggered by esophageal distension (food remaining in the esophagus) and is coordinated entirely by the ENS (intrinsic). Since the ENS is not cut during vagotomy, secondary peristalsis persists. This means patients with vagotomy may still clear retained food from the esophagus, though swallowing-triggered peristalsis is impaired.

Q6.

A 38-year-old woman presents with progressive dysphagia to both solids and liquids, regurgitation of undigested food, and weight loss over 8 months. Barium swallow shows a "bird-beak" narrowing at the gastroesophageal junction with proximal esophageal dilation. Esophageal manometry shows elevated LES pressure that fails to relax with swallowing. Which neurotransmitters are deficient at the LES?

  • (B) Gastrin and CCK
  • (C) VIP and NO
  • (D) Serotonin and motilin
  • (E) NE and VIP
Correct Answer: C — VIP and NO
This is achalasia. The LES normally relaxes during swallowing via inhibitory ENS neurons that release VIP and NO. In achalasia, these inhibitory neurons are selectively destroyed (etiology: likely autoimmune; associated with Chagas disease in Latin America). Without VIP/NO, the LES cannot relax → tonically elevated LES pressure → dysphagia to both solids AND liquids (characteristic of motor dysphagia vs. mechanical obstruction which causes solids-first). The "bird-beak" appearance on barium swallow is classic. Treatment targets the LES mechanically (dilation, myotomy) or pharmacologically (botulinum toxin).

Q7.

During esophageal manometry, a patient is asked to swallow. A peristaltic pressure wave is recorded traveling down the esophagus from proximal to distal, and the LES pressure drops to near zero seconds before the wave arrives. Which mechanism is responsible for the LES relaxation preceding peristalsis?

  • (B) Descending inhibition via ENS inhibitory neurons releasing VIP and NO

LES relaxation ahead of the peristaltic wave is identical in mechanism to descending inhibition in the peristaltic reflex throughout the gut: ENS inhibitory neurons release VIP and NO, which hyperpolarize the LES smooth muscle → relaxation. This is a key concept because these are the same neurons lost in achalasia. ACh (choice A) actually contracts the LES — it is an excitatory neurotransmitter at smooth muscle. Q8.

The swallowing center is located in which region of the brain, and which stage of swallowing does it directly control?

  • (A) Cerebral cortex; oral stage
  • (B) Medulla oblongata; pharyngeal and esophageal stages
  • (C) Cerebellum; esophageal peristalsis only
  • (D) Hypothalamus; pharyngeal stage only
  • (E) Pons; oral and pharyngeal stages
Correct Answer: B — Medulla oblongata; pharyngeal and esophageal stages
The swallowing center (deglutition center) is located in the medulla oblongata. It coordinates the involuntary pharyngeal and esophageal stages of swallowing via cranial nerves V, VII, IX, X, and XII. The oral (preparatory) stage is voluntary and controlled by the cerebral cortex. Once the tongue voluntarily initiates the swallow reflex by pushing the bolus into the pharynx, the medullary swallowing center takes over and the pharyngeal/esophageal stages proceed involuntarily (and cannot be voluntarily stopped).

LO 4–5: Receptive Relaxation & Gastric Emptying

Q9.

Following Roux-en-Y gastric bypass surgery, a patient reports feeling full after eating only a few bites of food. Which of the following physiological mechanisms best explains this early satiety?

  • (A) Loss of vagal innervation to the stomach reduces acid secretion
  • (B) Reduced stomach volume and impaired gastric accommodation → rapid pressure rise with small meals
  • (C) CCK release from the bypassed duodenum is eliminated
  • (D) The gastric BER frequency increases after surgery, causing faster emptying
  • (E) Gastrin secretion is abolished, eliminating the cephalic phase
Correct Answer: B
Gastric bypass significantly reduces the functional stomach volume. Normally, receptive relaxation (mediated by VIP via the vagus) allows the stomach to expand up to 10–20× its empty volume (~50 mL → 1–1.5 L) without a significant pressure increase. After gastric bypass, the remaining stomach pouch is very small AND the normal accommodation reflex may be impaired. Therefore, a small amount of food rapidly increases intragastric pressure → mechanoreceptors signal distension → satiety. This is the intended therapeutic mechanism for weight loss. Sleeve gastrectomy (not bypass) removes the fundus, which is the primary accommodation reservoir.

Q10.

A researcher infuses different nutrients into the duodenum of a study subject and measures gastric emptying rates using scintigraphy. Which of the following meal types would be expected to empty most slowly from the stomach?

  • (A) Isotonic saline solution
  • (B) Glucose solution (10%)
  • (C) Protein shake (pure whey protein)
  • (D) Lipid emulsion (70% fat)
  • (E) Mixed carbohydrate-protein meal
Correct Answer: D — Lipid emulsion (fat)
Fats are the most potent stimulus for slowing gastric emptying. Fat in the duodenum triggers: (1) CCK release from I cells → directly inhibits gastric antral contractions + increases pyloric tone; (2) Secretin release; (3) Enterogastric reflex (SNS afferents) → inhibits gastric motility. The order from fastest to slowest: saline > glucose (carbs) > protein > fat. This is why a fatty meal keeps you feeling full for hours. Clinically, patients with dumping syndrome (post-gastrectomy) benefit from high-fat, low-carbohydrate diets because fat slows gastric emptying and reduces the osmotic surge to the duodenum.

Q11.

Receptive relaxation of the stomach is mediated by which of the following pathways?

  • (A) Gastrin → G-protein coupled receptor → gastric smooth muscle relaxation
  • (B) Vagus → ENS inhibitory neurons → VIP release → smooth muscle relaxation
  • (C) CCK → I cell activation → ENS inhibition → stomach wall relaxation
  • (D) Stretch → ICC → BER frequency increase → slower gastric emptying
  • (E) SNS activation → NE → alpha-2 receptors → stomach wall relaxation
Correct Answer: B — Vagus → ENS inhibitory neurons → VIP → smooth muscle relaxation
Receptive relaxation is a vagovagal reflex: food entering the stomach (or esophagus) triggers vagal afferents → medulla → vagal efferents → activate ENS inhibitory interneurons in the gastric wall → release VIP (and NO) → hyperpolarize gastric fundus smooth muscle → wall relaxes → accommodation. This reflex is abolished by vagotomy (choice A is wrong — gastrin contracts stomach; choice E SNS causes vasoconstriction but does not mediate the accommodation reflex).

Q12.

A 52-year-old patient with long-standing type 2 diabetes presents with nausea, vomiting, and abdominal bloating after meals. A gastric emptying scan shows markedly delayed gastric emptying (gastroparesis). Which of the following prokinetic drugs works by mimicking motilin to stimulate Phase III-like contractions?

  • (A) Metoclopramide (D2 blocker)
  • (B) Ondansetron (5-HT3 antagonist)
  • (C) Erythromycin (motilin receptor agonist)
  • (D) Domperidone (D2 blocker, peripherally acting)
  • (E) Neostigmine (acetylcholinesterase inhibitor)
Correct Answer: C — Erythromycin
Erythromycin, while primarily an antibiotic (macrolide), is a potent motilin receptor agonist. Motilin is the hormone released by M cells (enterochromaffin-like cells of the small intestine) that triggers Phase III of the MMC during fasting. When used as a prokinetic in gastroparesis, erythromycin mimics motilin to stimulate Phase III-like intense propulsive contractions of the stomach → empties retained food. Metoclopramide (D2 blocker + 5-HT4 agonist) also works but risks extrapyramidal side effects. Note: Ondansetron (5-HT3 antagonist) reduces nausea but decreases motility — it is not prokinetic.

LO 6–7: Peristalsis & Segmentation

Q13.

A bolus of food in the small intestine causes contraction of circular muscle proximal to the bolus (ascending excitation) and relaxation of circular muscle distal to the bolus (descending inhibition). What are the neurotransmitters responsible for each arm of this reflex?

  • (A) Ascending: VIP, NO; Descending: ACh, Substance P
  • (B) Ascending: ACh, Substance P; Descending: VIP, NO
  • (C) Ascending: NE, ATP; Descending: Serotonin, gastrin
  • (D) Ascending: ACh, Substance P; Descending: CCK, secretin
  • (E) Ascending: Serotonin; Descending: ACh
Correct Answer: B — Ascending: ACh + Substance P; Descending: VIP + NO
This is the Law of the Intestine (peristaltic reflex). The ascending (oral-directed) limb uses excitatory neurons releasing ACh and Substance P → contraction proximal to bolus. The descending (aboral-directed) limb uses inhibitory neurons releasing VIP and NO → relaxation distal to bolus. This coordinated pattern propels the bolus aborally. The initiating signal is serotonin (5-HT) released by enterochromaffin cells in response to luminal distension → activates ENS interneurons that coordinate both limbs.

  • (C) Segmentation requires vagal input; peristalsis is independent of the vagus

  • (E) Peristalsis is controlled entirely by the vagus; segmentation by the splanchnic nerves
Correct Answer: B
Peristalsis is coordinated by both the ENS (intrinsic) and extrinsic inputs (vagal efferents modulate strength and frequency). Segmentation is predominantly ENS-controlled (intrinsic) — it continues even after complete extrinsic denervation and is less affected by vagotomy. This distinction is why segmentation is more robust in patients with autonomic neuropathy. Q15.

During vomiting, the GI contents must be propelled in a retrograde direction (from small intestine toward the stomach and out). Which of the following best describes the mechanism?

  • (B) Retrograde peristalsis — ascending excitation and descending inhibition are reversed, with oral direction as the active phase

  • (D) ICC fire in reverse sequence, producing retrograde slow waves
  • (E) Mass movement from colon creates back-pressure that reverses SI flow
Correct Answer: B
Retrograde peristalsis is an active, coordinated process using the same peristaltic reflex machinery but oriented in the oral direction. The vomiting center (area postrema, medulla) triggers ENS circuits that reverse the polarity: now ascending excitation (ACh/Substance P) occurs aborally, and descending inhibition (VIP/NO) occurs orally → content is propelled from the small intestine back toward the stomach, where it is then expelled by gastric contraction + relaxation of the LES + esophageal retrograde peristalsis + Valsalva. Q16.

A patient with a spinal cord injury at T10 (above the celiac and superior mesenteric ganglia outflow) develops chronic diarrhea. Despite the loss of sympathetic modulation of the GI tract, peristalsis continues. Which of the following best explains this?

The ENS ("second brain") is capable of coordinating complete peristalsis, segmentation, and sphincter function entirely independently of CNS input — 500 million neurons, comparable to the spinal cord in neuron count. LO 8: MMC

Q17.

A 35-year-old woman undergoes hydrogen breath testing, which reveals elevated hydrogen after ingestion of lactulose, consistent with small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO). She reports eating 8–10 small meals per day ("grazing"). Which physiological mechanism best explains her predisposition to SIBO?

  • (A) Continuous eating increases gastric acid secretion, which acidifies the duodenum and promotes bacterial growth
  • (B) Constant feeding suppresses the MMC, eliminating interdigestive sweeping of bacteria from the small intestine
  • (C) Multiple small meals increase CCK release, which inhibits ileocecal valve function and allows colonic bacteria to reflux
  • (D) Frequent eating decreases motilin secretion, reducing absorptive capacity of the ileum
  • (E) Continuous peristalsis from frequent eating overwhelms the IgA defense mechanism of the SI mucosa
Correct Answer: B
The MMC (Migrating Myoelectric Complex) serves a critical housekeeping function: during interdigestive (fasting) periods, Phase III MMC sweeps undigested material and bacteria from the stomach through the terminal ileum into the colon every 75–120 min. Eating stops the MMC immediately. Grazing (continuous small meals) prevents the gut from ever entering the fasting state → no MMC → bacteria that would normally be cleared accumulate in the small intestine → SIBO. Phase III is triggered by motilin from M cells — erythromycin (motilin agonist) can help SIBO patients by artificially inducing Phase III contractions.

Q18.

The Migrating Myoelectric Complex (MMC) consists of 4 phases. Phase III is the only propulsive phase and is sometimes called the "intestinal housekeeper." Which hormone initiates Phase III, and from which cells is it released?

  • (A) Gastrin; released from G cells of the gastric antrum
  • (B) CCK; released from I cells of the duodenum
  • (C) Motilin; released from M cells (enterochromaffin-like cells) of the small intestine
  • (D) Secretin; released from S cells of the duodenum
  • (E) VIP; released from ENS neurons of the myenteric plexus
Correct Answer: C — Motilin; M cells of the small intestine
Phase III MMC is exclusively triggered by motilin, a peptide hormone released by M cells (enterochromaffin-like cells) of the small intestinal mucosa during fasting. Motilin is secreted in a cyclical pattern (~every 75–120 min) during the interdigestive state. It binds to motilin receptors on GI smooth muscle and ENS neurons → intense propulsive contractions. Clinically: erythromycin acts as a motilin receptor agonist and is used as a prokinetic for gastroparesis. The MMC is independent of vagal stimulation (works after vagotomy).

Q19.

A patient undergoes truncal vagotomy as part of an ulcer repair. Which of the following effects on the MMC would be expected?

  • (A) MMC is abolished because it requires vagal initiation
  • (B) MMC Phase III frequency decreases by 50% because the vagus provides 50% of the motilin signal
  • (C) MMC continues unchanged because it is driven by ENS + motilin, independent of the vagus
  • (D) MMC becomes continuous (no longer cycles) because vagal inhibition is lost
  • (E) MMC Phase I is abolished but Phase III persists
Correct Answer: C
The MMC is explicitly independent of vagal stimulation. It is generated and coordinated by the ENS and initiated by motilin (a hormone, not a neural signal). Vagotomy does not significantly alter the MMC pattern. This is actually one reason vagotomy was historically performed — it reduces acid secretion (parietal cell vagal stimulation) without abolishing basic GI motility patterns like the MMC. In contrast, primary esophageal peristalsis IS vagus-dependent and is lost after vagotomy.

Q20.

A patient treated with long-term opioid therapy develops intractable nausea and bloating. Abdominal X-ray shows a large amount of retained food in the stomach 6 hours after the last meal. Opioids contribute to this gastroparesis by which mechanism?

  • (E) Opioids compete with motilin for M-cell receptors → impair Phase I of MMC

Opioids activate μ-opioid receptors on ENS neurons throughout the GI tract. LO 9–10: Large Intestine, Gastrocolic Reflex & Defecation

Q21.

  • (A) Villi of the colonic mucosa; to increase surface area for fat absorption
  • (B) Circular muscle + taenia coli contracting simultaneously; to facilitate slow movement and maximize Na⁺/water reabsorption
  • (C) Longitudinal muscle contracting alone; to propel feces quickly to the rectum
  • (D) Colonic valves formed by mucosal folds; to prevent retrograde fecal movement
  • (E) Submucosal edema from lymph drainage; no functional significance
Correct Answer: B
Haustra are formed by simultaneous contraction of circular smooth muscle and the 3 bands of longitudinal muscle (taenia coli), which create sacculations (sacs = haustra). This segmental pattern moves colonic contents very slowly — the key function is colonic salvage: maximizing reabsorption of Na⁺ and water from the increasingly dehydrated fecal material. Important note: the colon has NO villi (absorption is entirely by Na⁺/water pumps in colonocytes, not villi-based absorption like the small intestine). Mass movements (not haustration) are responsible for propelling feces to the rectum.

Q22.

  • (A) Food in stomach → SNS afferents → colonic mass movements
  • (B) Food in stomach → PSNS (vagal) afferents + gastrin/CCK release → colonic mass movements
  • (C) Food in stomach → motilin release → Phase III MMC → colonic mass movements
  • (D) Food in duodenum only → CCK → direct colonic smooth muscle contraction
  • (E) Feces in rectum → gastrin release → gastric relaxation + colonic contraction
Correct Answer: B
The gastrocolic reflex is both neural and hormonal: (1) Food distending the stomach → vagal afferents → medulla → vagal efferents to colon (fast, neural component); (2) Food in stomach + duodenum → gastrin (G cells, antrum) and CCK (I cells, duodenum) release → circulating hormones stimulate colonic smooth muscle (slower, hormonal component). The combined effect = colonic mass movements → feces propelled to rectum → urge to defecate. The hormonal component explains why the urge sometimes peaks 20–30 min after eating. This reflex is exaggerated in IBD and IBS-D.

Q23.

  • (E) Inflammatory infiltrate in mucosa; absent ileogastric reflex

This is Hirschsprung's disease. The diagnostic biopsy finding is absence of ganglion cells (aganglionosis) in both the submucosal plexus (Meissner's) and myenteric plexus (Auerbach's) of the aganglionic segment. Without ENS ganglia, VIP/NO cannot be released → IAS (internal anal sphincter) remains in tonic contraction → the rectosphincteric (defecation) reflex is absent. On anorectal manometry, the normal reflex (rectal distension → IAS pressure drop) is absent in Hirschsprung's. Diagnosis confirmed by: absent ganglion cells on biopsy + absent rectosphincteric reflex on manometry.

Q24.

A 68-year-old woman with chronic constipation reports that she can feel the urge to defecate but consistently delays going to the bathroom for hours. Repeated voluntary suppression of defecation involves: EAS contracts (voluntary) + retrograde peristalsis pushes feces back into sigmoid colon. LO 11: Autonomic & ENS Regulation

Q25.

This is the brain-gut axis at work. Q26.

Atropine blocks muscarinic (M2/M3) receptors → blocks the effects of ACh released by both PSNS postganglionic fibers and ENS excitatory motor neurons. Q27.

Ondansetron is a 5-HT3 receptor antagonist used for nausea and vomiting. A known side effect is constipation. Based on the role of serotonin in GI motility, which mechanism best explains this side effect?

  • (B) Blocking 5-HT3 on ENS sensory neurons → impairs initiation of peristaltic reflex → ↓peristalsis

Serotonin (5-HT) released by enterochromaffin cells in response to mucosal distension initiates the peristaltic reflex by activating 5-HT3 (and 5-HT4) receptors on ENS sensory neurons (intrinsic primary afferent neurons, IPANs). Q28.

A patient with type 1 diabetes develops both gastroparesis and orthostatic hypotension. These findings together are most consistent with:

Correct Answer: B — Diabetic autonomic neuropathy
Long-standing diabetes causes demyelination and axonal damage of small autonomic nerve fibers. Vagal (PSNS) neuropathy → ↓gastric motility → gastroparesis (delayed emptying, nausea, vomiting, bloating). Sympathetic neuropathy → inability to increase vascular resistance on standing → orthostatic hypotension. Both can occur together in diabetic autonomic neuropathy. Q30.

Q31.

Parkinson's disease is increasingly recognized as starting in the ENS (Braak hypothesis) — Lewy bodies (α-synuclein aggregates) appear first in the ENS and dorsal vagal nucleus, THEN propagate to the brain via the vagus. Q32.

  • (B) Serotonin and motilin
  • (C) VIP and NO
  • (D) Gastrin and CCK
  • (E) NE and dopamine

Descending inhibition in the peristaltic reflex (Law of the Intestine): ENS inhibitory motor neurons release VIP (vasoactive intestinal peptide) and NO (nitric oxide) → these hyperpolarize circular smooth muscle distal to the bolus → relaxation → lumen opens ahead of the bolus to allow passage. These are the same inhibitory neurotransmitters lost in achalasia (LES) and Hirschsprung's (IAS). ACh + Substance P = ascending excitation (contraction proximal to bolus).

Q33.

Q34.

Post-operative ileus is multifactorial: (1) Surgical manipulation of the bowel → local inflammation → prostaglandins and cytokines inhibit smooth muscle; (2) Opioid analgesia (μ-opioid receptors on ENS → ↓ACh → ↓motility); (3) Reflex ↑SNS (pain, stress) → ↓GI motility. Q35.

Which of the following is the MOST appropriate description of the relationship between BER (slow waves) and GI smooth muscle contraction?

  • (A) Each slow wave automatically produces one contraction of equal strength
  • (B) Slow waves must reach −40 mV for APs to fire; APs cause Ca²⁺-mediated contraction; more APs per wave = stronger contraction
  • (C) Slow waves produce contractions only when VIP is co-released by ENS neurons

This is the core BER concept: slow waves are always present (subthreshold) and set the timing. APs fire only when slow wave peaks reach −40 mV.   • (B) Gallbladder contraction + sphincter of Oddi relaxation

  • (B) The ENS is capable of coordinating peristalsis entirely autonomously, without CNS or extrinsic neural input

  • (E) Peristalsis is a purely myogenic phenomenon requiring no neural coordination

This classic experiment demonstrates the autonomy of the ENS — the "second brain." The ENS contains ~500 million neurons organized into sensory, inter-, and motor neurons. Q38.

In healthy individuals, what prevents bacteria that colonize the colon from migrating proximally into the small intestine under normal conditions? (Choose the BEST answer.)

Q39.

Which of the following best describes why patients on long-term opioids develop constipation?

Q41.

A child undergoes a surgical pull-through procedure for Hirschsprung's disease. The aganglionic segment is resected and normal ganglionic colon is anastomosed to the anus. Which of the following would be expected AFTER successful surgery?

  • (A) The rectosphincteric reflex immediately normalizes because the ENS is reconnected
  • (B) Normal bowel function returns because the obstruction (aganglionic segment) is removed, even if the IAS initially remains partially contracted
  • (C) Permanent constipation because the surgical anastomosis prevents peristalsis
  • (D) Diarrhea because haustration is permanently disrupted
  • (E) The ENS cannot regenerate, so constipation recurs unless colostomy is maintained
Correct Answer: B
Pull-through surgery removes the aganglionic (obstructing) segment and anastomoses normal ganglionic bowel to the anus. After surgery, feces can now traverse the entire bowel to the rectum without encountering the obstruction. The IAS may take some time to adapt, and some children require post-operative management (dilations, laxatives) while the anastomosis matures. Long-term outcomes are generally good. True functional recovery depends on the length of aganglionic segment removed and whether adequate ganglionic bowel was preserved. Total colonic Hirschsprung's has worse outcomes than short-segment disease.

Q42.

Which of the following correctly describes the role of the ileocecal valve in GI physiology?

  • (A) It contracts in response to CCK to prevent bile reflux from the duodenum
  • (B) It opens in response to the gastroileal reflex (food in stomach → ileocecal valve relaxes → ileal emptying into colon) and prevents colonic bacterial reflux into the SI
  • (C) It is controlled entirely by the myenteric plexus without any ANS modulation
  • (D) It contracts during MMC Phase III to prevent ileal contents from entering the colon too quickly
  • (E) It is a passive mechanical valve with no neural or hormonal regulation
Correct Answer: B
The ileocecal valve: (1) Receives neural regulation — PSNS (vagus) relaxes it; SNS contracts it; (2) Responds to the gastroileal reflex: food in stomach → gastrin/vagal signals → ileocecal valve relaxes → terminal ileum empties into cecum; (3) Prevents cecoileal reflux — stops colonic bacteria from migrating backward into the terminal ileum (one mechanism protecting against SIBO). When the ileocecal valve is surgically resected (e.g., in Crohn's ileocolitis), patients are at significantly higher risk for SIBO and require prolonged antibiotic courses.

Q43.

A 22-year-old female reports abdominal pain, bloating, and alternating constipation and diarrhea for 2 years. Colonoscopy and labs are normal. She is diagnosed with IBS-mixed type. Which of the following mechanisms is most consistent with the pathophysiology of IBS?

  • (A) Absence of ENS ganglia in the rectosigmoid colon
  • (B) Loss of ICC → absent BER → disorganized motility
  • (C) Altered gut-brain axis: visceral hypersensitivity + dysregulated ENS motor responses + exaggerated gastrocolic reflex
  • (D) Structural narrowing of the sigmoid colon causing intermittent obstruction
  • (E) Overactivation of PSNS → continuous MMC-like contractions
Correct Answer: C
IBS is a functional disorder (no structural pathology on colonoscopy or imaging). Current understanding: (1) Visceral hypersensitivity — enhanced ENS/spinal cord processing of GI stimuli → pain at normal distension levels; (2) Dysregulated ENS motor activity — altered serotonin signaling in the gut (↑5-HT in IBS-D, ↓5-HT in IBS-C) → dyscoordinated peristalsis/segmentation; (3) Exaggerated gastrocolic reflex → post-meal urgency; (4) Brain-gut axis: stress via CRH → mast cell activation → ↑GI hypersensitivity. Treatments target these mechanisms: antispasmodics, low-FODMAP diet, 5-HT3 antagonists (IBS-D), fiber, antidepressants (alter gut-brain signaling).

Which of the following correctly matches the GI reflex with its trigger and effect?

  • (A) Gastrocolic reflex: feces in rectum → colonic mass movements
  • (B) Rectosphincteric reflex: food in stomach → IAS relaxation
  • (C) Gastroileal reflex: food in stomach → ileocecal valve relaxes → ileal emptying into colon
  • (D) Ileogastric reflex: distension of cecum → slows ileal emptying
  • (E) Enteroenteric reflex: distension of colon → stimulates SI motility
