▼1. Case 1 — 24-year-old man with abdominal pain + anemia

⭐ A young adult with 6 months of abdominal pain, fatigue, RLQ tenderness, microcytic anemia, and occult GI bleeding who has a proximal-colon lesion on colonoscopy is the classic Crohn disease vignette.

Young adult + RLQ pain Microcytic anemia (Fe-def) Occult blood Skip lesions / proximal colon Terminal ileum involvement

📋 Clinical Vignette

  • 24-year-old man: 6 months of abdominal pain and fatigue
  • Afebrile, pale, mild RLQ tenderness, active bowel sounds, no palpable mass
  • Labs: Hgb 8.9 g/dL, Hct 26.7%, MCV 74 (microcytic anemia from chronic GI blood loss/malabsorption), Plt 255k, WBC 7.78k
  • Stool: occult blood positive
  • Colonoscopy: lesion seen in proximal colon (skip distribution suggests Crohn)

🧠 Differential Diagnosis & Best Answer

Crohn disease✅ BestYoung adult, chronic RLQ pain (ileocecal), iron-deficiency anemia, occult bleeding, proximal/skip colon involvement
Acute appendicitis❌Acute (hours-days), localized McBurney tenderness, fever, leukocytosis — not 6 months of pain with anemia

Lightning High-Yield:
  • Answer: A — Crohn disease
  • Key trigger words: young adult + RLQ chronic pain + microcytic anemia + occult blood + proximal colon lesion

▼2. Crohn Disease — Clinical, Pathology, Natural History

⭐ Crohn = transmural, segmental ("skip") inflammation that can involve ANY site from mouth to anus, with the terminal ileum most commonly affected; the pathognomonic histologic feature is the non-caseating granuloma.

👥 Epidemiology & Presentation

  • Bimodal peak: 15–35 and 50–70; slight female predominance in adults
  • Risk factors: Ashkenazi Jewish ancestry, NOD2/CARD15 mutations, Western diet, smoking (worsens Crohn, protects UC)
  • Classic symptoms: chronic RLQ/periumbilical pain, non-bloody or occult-blood-positive diarrhea, weight loss, fatigue, fever, oral aphthous ulcers
  • Complications drive the clinical picture: strictures (fibrostenotic), fistulas (enteroenteric, enterovesical, perianal), abscesses, malabsorption, B12 deficiency (terminal ileum)

🔍 Gross Pathology

  • Skip lesions: discontinuous inflammation with intervening normal mucosa
  • Cobblestone mucosa: linear fissured ulcers between edematous mucosal islands
  • Creeping fat: mesenteric fat wraps around the bowel serosa (transmural inflammation pulls in adipose)
  • "String sign" on barium imaging: narrow, rigid, fibrotic terminal ileum
  • Strictures, fissures, fistulas, bowel-wall thickening (transmural → "hosepipe")
  • Site: terminal ileum + right colon most common; can involve ANY segment of GI tract (mouth to anus); rectal sparing common

🔬 Histology

  • Non-caseating granulomas — pathognomonic (but present in only ~35% of biopsies); clusters of epithelioid macrophages ± multinucleated giant cells; NO central necrosis (distinguishes from TB)
  • Transmural inflammation: all 4 layers — mucosa, submucosa, muscularis, serosa
  • Lymphoid aggregates (often 4+ per section), deep fissuring ulcers
  • Chronicity markers: crypt architectural distortion (branching, shortening), pyloric metaplasia, Paneth cell metaplasia in left colon
  • Neutrophilic crypt abscesses are seen but are NOT specific to Crohn
🧠 Mnemonic: "CHRISTMAS" for Crohn
▼
  • C — Cobblestone mucosa
  • H — High temperature (low-grade fever)
  • R — Reduced lumen (strictures, string sign)
  • I — Intestinal fistulas
  • S — Skip lesions
  • T — Transmural inflammation
  • M — Malabsorption + B12 deficiency
  • A — Abdominal pain (RLQ), non-caseating granulomAs
  • S — Submucosal fibrosis, Smoking worsens

💧 Pathogenesis

  • Genetic susceptibility (NOD2/CARD15, ATG16L1, IL23R) + environmental trigger (microbiome, smoking) → dysregulated TH1/TH17 response to commensal gut flora
  • Chronic transmural inflammation → granulomas, fibrosis (→ strictures), deep penetration (→ fistulas/abscesses)

📆 Natural History

  • At diagnosis, ~70% have inflammatory (non-stricturing, non-penetrating) disease
  • Over 10+ years, disease evolves toward fibrostenotic (strictures) and penetrating (fistulas/abscesses) phenotypes
  • >50% require surgery within 10 years; recurrence at anastomosis is common
  • Perianal fistulas/abscesses occur in 25–30% — a Crohn-defining complication; UC does NOT cause perianal fistulas

👨‍⚕️ Extraintestinal Manifestations (IBD Overall)

  • Skin: erythema nodosum (tender shin nodules), pyoderma gangrenosum (ulcer with violaceous border)
  • Eyes: uveitis, episcleritis
  • Joints: peripheral arthritis, sacroiliitis, ankylosing spondylitis (HLA-B27)
  • Hepatobiliary: primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC, much more common in UC), gallstones (Crohn — bile-acid malabsorption)
  • Renal: oxalate kidney stones (Crohn — fat malabsorption → calcium binds fat → free oxalate absorbed)
⚠️ TRAP: Granulomas in Crohn vs TB vs Sarcoid ▼
  • Crohn = NON-caseating, loose granulomas on a background of chronic transmural inflammation and crypt distortion.
  • TB = CASEATING granulomas with central necrosis, acid-fast bacilli on stain — ileocecal TB mimics Crohn grossly but histology and AFB stain differentiate.
  • Sarcoid = non-caseating too, but systemic (lung/lymph nodes predominate) and lacks Crohn's transmural distribution + crypt distortion.
  • If a USMLE stem says "caseating granuloma of terminal ileum" → think intestinal TB (Mycobacterium bovis or tuberculosis), NOT Crohn.

▼3. Case 2 — 39-year-old woman with recurrent bloody diarrhea

⭐ Recurrent relapsing-remitting episodes of bloody diarrhea, cramping, and occult blood — with stool negative for ova/parasites — pointing to ulcerative colitis.

Relapsing bloody diarrhea 12y recurrent episodes No O&P Continuous from rectum Pseudopolyps

📋 Clinical Vignette

  • 39y woman: 1 month of abdominal cramps + diarrhea (6 stools/day)
  • History of similar self-limited episodes over 12 years, each lasting ~2 weeks
  • Stool: occult blood positive, no ova/parasites
  • Colonoscopy (terminal ileum → colon): colonic mucosal inflammation consistent with UC

🧠 Complication Risk & Best Answer

ComplicationFit?Why
Diverticulitis❌Diverticular disease is unrelated to UC
Adenocarcinoma✅ BestLong-standing UC (>8–10y) → dysplasia → colorectal adenocarcinoma; risk rises steeply with extent and duration
Perirectal fistula❌Perianal fistulas are Crohn, NOT UC (mucosal-only, no transmural tracking)
Fat malabsorption❌UC spares the small bowel — no fat/B12 malabsorption
Primary biliary cirrhosis❌UC is linked to PSC, NOT PBC (distractor)
Lightning High-Yield:
  • Answer: B — adenocarcinoma. UC patients with pancolitis have up to 18× the colorectal cancer risk of the general population.

🔍 Histology — Chronicity Marker on Biopsy

  • Question 4 answer: Crypt architectural disarray/distortion (E) — branching, shortening, and loss of parallel crypt orientation indicate chronic colitis (UC or Crohn)
  • Crypt abscesses (D) = acute inflammation (neutrophils in crypt lumina), seen in both infectious colitis and active IBD; do NOT by themselves distinguish acute from chronic
  • Superficial mucosal hemorrhage (B) and lymphocytic infiltrate (A) — nonspecific
  • Brunner gland hyperplasia (C) — duodenal finding, irrelevant
Lightning High-Yield:
  • Biopsy chronic markers: crypt distortion, basal plasmacytosis, Paneth cell metaplasia in left colon, pyloric metaplasia, atrophy
  • Biopsy acute markers: neutrophilic cryptitis, crypt abscesses, mucosal erosions/hemorrhage
  • Chronic + active = acute-on-chronic flare (most IBD biopsies look like this)

▼4. Ulcerative Colitis — Clinical, Pathology, Complications

⭐ UC = continuous mucosal/submucosal inflammation that ALWAYS starts in the rectum and extends proximally in a single contiguous swath; limited to the colon; classic symptom is bloody diarrhea with mucus.

👥 Epidemiology & Clinical Presentation

  • Peak onset 15–35 years; second smaller peak 50–70
  • Smoking is protective (opposite of Crohn); appendectomy also protective
  • Classic presentation: bloody diarrhea, mucus in stool, lower abdominal pain/cramps, fecal urgency, tenesmus
  • Relapsing-remitting course; flares triggered by infection, NSAIDs, stress

🔍 Gross Pathology

  • Continuous, confluent inflammation from rectum proximally (NO skip lesions)
  • Rectum ALWAYS involved; extent categorized as proctitis → left-sided colitis → pancolitis
  • Friable, erythematous, granular mucosa with superficial ulcers; loss of haustra ("lead-pipe" colon on imaging in advanced disease)
  • Pseudopolyps: residual islands of regenerating mucosa between ulcers
  • Backwash ileitis: mild terminal ileum inflammation adjacent to an inflamed cecum in pancolitis (does NOT equal Crohn)
  • Disease is mucosal + submucosal only — does NOT go transmural except in toxic megacolon

🔬 Histology

  • Crypt distortion + crypt abscesses in the colon
  • Confluent mucosal inflammation; lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate concentrated in the lamina propria
  • Goblet cell depletion, Paneth cell metaplasia in left colon
  • NO granulomas, no transmural inflammation (except toxic megacolon)

🚨 Severe Complications

  • Toxic megacolon: severe dilation (>6 cm transverse colon) with systemic toxicity (fever, tachycardia, leukocytosis); high perforation risk — AVOID anti-diarrheals and opiates which precipitate it
  • Perforation, massive hemorrhage
  • Colorectal cancer (next section)
  • PSC: ~5% of UC patients; "onion-skin fibrosis" of bile ducts; beaded strictures on MRCP/ERCP; p-ANCA positive; ↑ risk of cholangiocarcinoma
⚠️ TRAP: "Pseudopolyps" are not neoplastic ▼
  • Pseudopolyps in UC are regenerative mucosa between ulcers, NOT adenomas. They do NOT carry cancer risk themselves.
  • True neoplastic risk in UC comes from flat dysplasia arising in inflamed mucosa — which is why surveillance colonoscopies must take extensive random biopsies (and chromoendoscopy enhances detection).

▼6. IBD-Associated Colorectal Cancer & Surveillance

⭐ Colorectal cancer accounts for 10–15% of IBD deaths; IBD patients are 6× more likely to develop CRC than the general population and develop it at a younger mean age (40–50 vs 60).

📈 Risk Factors

  • Extent of colitis: pancolitis >> left-sided > proctitis (standardized incidence ratio ~14× for pancolitis)
  • Duration: cumulative CRC risk rises after 7–8 years; ~2% at 10y, ~8% at 20y, ~18% at 30y
  • PSC: dramatically increases CRC risk — colonoscopy annually from diagnosis
  • Family history of CRC, young age at IBD diagnosis, chronic inflammation severity

🧬 Molecular Mechanism — Inflammation-Dysplasia-Carcinoma

IBD-CRC arises through a different sequence than sporadic CRC:

FeatureSporadic CRCIBD-CRC
Initiating eventAPC loss (early)p53 mutation (early) from chronic inflammation DNA damage
IntermediateK-ras activationK-ras activation
Late eventp53 lossAPC loss (late)
PrecursorAdenomatous polypFlat dysplasia in inflamed mucosa
Mean age~60y40–50y (younger)
MorphologyUsually single, polypoidMultifocal, flat, often mucinous/signet-ring; synchronous CRCs more common
Site (UC-CRC)Any colon segmentRectum + sigmoid most common in UC

🔍 Surveillance Guidelines

  • Begin surveillance colonoscopy after 7–8 years of colitis (both UC and Crohn-colitis)
  • Decrease interval as disease duration increases (usually every 1–3 years)
  • PSC + UC: annual colonoscopy from diagnosis
  • Traditional approach: random 4-quadrant biopsies every 10 cm ("at least 33 biopsies") — samples only 0.05–0.1% of colonic surface
  • Chromoendoscopy (spray dye onto mucosa) enhances detection of flat neoplasia invisible on white light — now preferred
⚠️ TRAP: Sporadic vs IBD-CRC molecular sequence ▼
  • Sporadic sequence: APC → K-ras → p53 (APC first; think Adenoma-first)
  • IBD-CRC sequence: p53 → K-ras → APC (p53 first from chronic inflammatory DNA damage; APC is a late event)
  • USMLE favorite: "What is the EARLIEST molecular event in a 45y UC patient's colon cancer?" → p53 mutation, not APC.

🧪 Testing Your Understanding

TYU 1

A 28-year-old man with a 5-year history of Crohn disease presents with fever, foul-smelling vaginal discharge in his female partner who has not been sexually active with anyone else, and air in his bladder on CT (pneumaturia). What complication is he most likely experiencing and what pathologic feature explains it?

✓ Answer: Enterovesical (and possibly enteroenteric/enterovaginal) fistula from transmural inflammation. Crohn disease penetrates all four layers of the bowel wall, allowing sinus tracts and fistulas to form between bowel and adjacent structures — bladder (pneumaturia, recurrent polymicrobial UTIs), vagina (stool passing through vaginal discharge), skin (enterocutaneous), or other bowel loops. This is Crohn-defining and does NOT occur in UC (mucosal-only disease).

TYU 2

A 46-year-old woman with 15 years of pancolitis (UC) undergoes surveillance colonoscopy. Random biopsies show flat dysplasia without a polypoid mass. She has no family history of cancer. What is the underlying pathway driving her cancer risk, and what is the next step?

✓ Answer: Inflammation-dysplasia-carcinoma sequence driven by chronic mucosal DNA damage. In IBD-CRC the molecular sequence is p53 (early) → K-ras → APC (late), opposite to the sporadic APC→K-ras→p53 sequence. Flat dysplasia in long-standing pancolitis is a high-risk lesion; guideline-based management is total proctocolectomy (curative and eliminates ongoing CRC risk). Chromoendoscopy may be used adjunctively for mapping before surgery.

TYU 3

A 32-year-old Ashkenazi Jewish smoker presents with chronic diarrhea, RLQ pain, and a perianal abscess. Biopsy of the terminal ileum shows non-caseating granulomas and transmural lymphoid aggregates. Name three counseling points about modifiable risk factors or complications you would discuss.

✓ Answer:
  • Smoking cessation — smoking worsens Crohn activity and increases surgical recurrence (opposite of UC, where smoking is protective).
  • B12 monitoring and supplementation — the terminal ileum absorbs B12; chronic inflammation or surgical resection causes deficiency and megaloblastic anemia.
  • Kidney stone risk — fat malabsorption from ileal disease → intraluminal calcium binds fat → free oxalate absorbed → calcium oxalate stones. Encourage hydration, low-oxalate diet, and consider oral calcium supplementation.
  • (Bonus: perianal fistulas/abscesses suggest aggressive phenotype; early biologic therapy — e.g., anti-TNF — may be warranted.)

▼1. Case 3 — 65-year-old hospitalized woman with abdominal distention

Vignette: A 65-year-old woman is hospitalized for pneumonia complicated by septicemia. She has required multiple antibiotics and was intubated and mechanically ventilated. On day 20 of hospitalization, she has abdominal distention, absent bowel sounds, and an abdominal radiograph showing dilated loops of small bowel (ileus). Labs: leukocytosis and hypoalbuminemia. Colonoscopy reveals yellow-white plaques (pseudomembranes).

What is the most likely diagnosis? (Q5)

Answer: A. Pseudomembranous enterocolitis — essentially always caused by C. difficile in this setting.

Classic USMLE buzzword triad: recent antibiotics + hospitalized patient + yellow-white pseudomembranes on colonoscopy.

Why not the other choices?

  • Gas gangrene / myonecrosis — Clostridium perfringens soft-tissue infection, not colitis.
  • Ulcerative proctitis — distal UC; would show chronic bloody diarrhea, not acute ileus after antibiotics.
  • Ischemic bowel — sudden pain out of proportion, older vasculopath; watershed areas (splenic flexure, rectosigmoid); hematochezia not pseudomembranes.
  • Toxic megacolon — a complication of severe C. diff or UC (colon >6 cm, systemic toxicity); not the primary diagnosis here.
▼Exam Trap: Ileus does not rule out C. diff
Students often expect profuse watery diarrhea. In severe C. diff, the colon becomes atonic → ileus → no diarrhea at all. Abdominal distention + recent antibiotics + leukocytosis should still trigger C. diff workup (and toxic megacolon concern).

▼2. C. difficile — Microbiology & Pathogenesis

Organism

  • Gram-positive, spore-forming, obligate anaerobe.
  • Spores resist alcohol hand sanitizer and most disinfectants → wash hands with soap & water; clean rooms with bleach.
  • Transmitted fecal-oral route (Q6 answer = E).

Toxins A and B (the real weapons)

  • Toxin A (TcdA) — enterotoxin → fluid secretion + inflammation.
  • Toxin B (TcdB) — cytotoxin → disrupts cytoskeleton.
  • Both glucosylate and inactivate Rho/Rac GTPases → actin depolymerization → tight-junction breakdown → apoptosis & neutrophil-rich inflammation → pseudomembrane.
  • Only toxigenic strains cause disease. Nontoxigenic carriage is possible and usually asymptomatic.
▼Exam Trap: Q6 distractors
  • A. Gram-negative — false; it is gram-positive.
  • B. Invasive — false; disease is toxin-mediated, not invasive.
  • C. Antifungals — false; antibacterials (clindamycin, cephalosporins, fluoroquinolones) are the classic culprits.
  • D. 30% of outpatients colonized — false; ~3% outpatients, ~20–30% of hospitalized patients.
  • E. Fecal-oral spread — CORRECT.

▼3. Clinical Spectrum & Risk Factors

Spectrum of disease

  • Asymptomatic carrier
  • Mild-moderate diarrhea (watery, non-bloody, foul-smelling)
  • Cramping lower abdominal pain, low-grade fever, malaise, anorexia
  • Leukocytosis (can be dramatic — WBC >15,000)
  • Hypoalbuminemia (protein-losing)
  • Pseudomembranous colitis
  • Ileus / toxic megacolon / perforation (severe, complicated CDI)
▼Mnemonic: “4 Cs” — high-risk antibiotics
Clindamycin, Cephalosporins, Ciprofloxacin (fluoroquinolones), Carbapenems. Essentially, broad-spectrum antibiotics that wipe out normal flora → C. diff overgrowth.

Risk factors

  • Antibiotic exposure (within 3 months, especially within 1 month)
  • Hospitalization or long-term care facility residence
  • Age >65
  • Proton pump inhibitors (weaker but real)
  • Immunosuppression, IBD, recent GI surgery

CDI definition

Acute onset of diarrhea PLUS documented toxigenic C. difficile (or its toxin) AND no other documented cause for diarrhea.

▼4. Gross & Histologic Features

Gross (endoscopy)

  • Yellow-white plaques (pseudomembranes), 2–10 mm, scattered on an erythematous base.
  • Plaques can coalesce in severe disease to form a confluent “membrane.”
  • Most prominent in the rectosigmoid but can be pancolonic.

Microscopic (the “volcano” lesion)

  • Volcano / mushroom-shaped eruption of fibrin + neutrophils + necrotic epithelial debris + mucin emerging from a damaged crypt.
  • Superficial mucosal necrosis; lamina propria filled with neutrophils.
  • Adjacent mucosa may look near-normal (patchy distribution).
▼Exam Trap: Pseudomembrane vs true membrane
The “pseudo” is histologic: it is not a layered connective-tissue structure — it is a plug of inflammatory exudate sitting on ulcerated mucosa. Contrast with diphtheritic true membrane (pharynx) or fibropurulent exudate.

▼5. Diagnostic Testing

Comparison of available tests

Test Sensitivity Specificity Role
NAAT (PCR)High (85–100%)High (94–100%)Preferred in acute disease; detects toxin genes. Risk: false-positive in colonized patients.
Toxin A/B EIA (ELISA)LowHighConfirms active toxin production; often paired with NAAT or GDH.
GDH antigenHighLowGood screening test (rule out); doesn’t distinguish toxigenic vs nontoxigenic.

Practical testing algorithm

  • Only test patients with ≥3 unformed stools in 24h (do not test asymptomatic carriers).
  • Common two-step: GDH + toxin EIA; discordant results resolved with NAAT.
  • Do not re-test “cure” — patients can shed toxin genes for weeks after clinical resolution.

▼6. Treatment & Recurrence

Initial episode

  • Stop the offending antibiotic if possible.
  • Oral vancomycin or fidaxomicin × 10 days (first-line per current IDSA guidelines).
  • Metronidazole is a second-line alternative when vancomycin/fidaxomicin are unavailable.
  • Fulminant CDI (ileus, megacolon, shock): high-dose oral vancomycin + IV metronidazole ± rectal vancomycin; urgent surgical consult.

Recurrent CDI

  • Recurrence rate ~20% after first episode, >40% after second.
  • Options: tapered-pulsed vancomycin, fidaxomicin, bezlotoxumab (anti-toxin B monoclonal).
  • Fecal microbiota transplant (FMT) for multiply-recurrent disease — restores normal flora.
High-yield: Do not use antimotility agents (loperamide) in active CDI — retention of toxin worsens colitis and risk of toxic megacolon.

Testing Your Understanding

TYU 1

A 72-year-old man completed a course of ceftriaxone for pneumonia 2 weeks ago. He now has 8 watery stools daily and a WBC of 22,000. Stool NAAT is positive for C. difficile toxin gene but toxin EIA is negative. How should this be interpreted, and what is the best next step?

Discordant NAAT(+)/EIA(−) can mean either active toxin production below the EIA detection limit or colonization. In a symptomatic patient with high WBC and recent antibiotic use, treat as CDI (clinical diagnosis trumps EIA). Start oral vancomycin or fidaxomicin for 10 days and stop any unnecessary antibiotics. Do not rely on the negative EIA alone to withhold therapy.

TYU 2

Why are alcohol-based hand rubs inadequate for infection control in CDI, and what is the correct approach?

C. difficile forms spores that are highly resistant to alcohol. Effective control requires soap-and-water hand washing (mechanical removal), contact precautions (gown + gloves), and environmental cleaning with a sporicidal agent such as bleach. Private rooms and dedicated equipment minimize fecal-oral spread.

TYU 3

A patient with severe CDI develops abdominal distention, absent bowel sounds, and colonic diameter of 7 cm on imaging. They have not had diarrhea for 24 hours. Why is the absence of diarrhea dangerous rather than reassuring?

Cessation of diarrhea in severe CDI often reflects ileus or toxic megacolon, not improvement. Toxin and organisms are retained in a dilated atonic colon, raising risk of perforation. Management includes stopping antimotility agents, aggressive combined IV metronidazole + high-dose oral (or per-NG) vancomycin ± rectal vancomycin, and urgent surgical evaluation (possible subtotal colectomy).

LO 3 · Diverticular Disease

Covers LOs: 1 (signs/symptoms & differential), 2 (pathogenesis & gross/histologic features)

  • 2. Anatomy & pathophysiology of a diverticulum

▼1. Case 4 — 65-year-old woman with hematochezia

Vignette: A 65-year-old woman has hematochezia. Stool is positive for occult blood. CT of the abdomen shows numerous air-filled, 1-cm outpouchings of the sigmoid and descending colon. Colonoscopy shows small-mouthed diverticula.

Although rare, which long-term complication is most likely? (Q7)

Answer: D. Pericolic abscess.

20% of patients with diverticulosis eventually develop diverticulitis; of these, perforation or micro-perforation can seal locally → pericolic (or pelvic) abscess. Adenocarcinoma, malabsorption, UC, and toxic megacolon are not typical complications of diverticular disease.

Pathophysiology question (Q8)

Answer: E. Nerves, vasa recta, and connective-tissue sheaths penetrate the muscle coat.

Diverticula are false diverticula (mucosa + submucosa only) that herniate through the muscularis propria at points where the vasa recta penetrate the circular muscle layer — a naturally weak spot.

▼Exam Trap: Q8 distractors
  • A. Lack of haustral folds — false; haustra are present. Excess segmentation (high intraluminal pressure) drives the defect.
  • B. High-fiber diet alleviates acute pressure — false; high fiber is for prevention. Acute diverticulitis → bowel rest.
  • C. Most common in thin-walled cecum — false; most common in sigmoid in Western populations.
  • D. Crypt abscesses — false; that is an IBD histology, not diverticular.

▼2. Anatomy & Pathophysiology of a Diverticulum

True vs false diverticulum

  • True diverticulum — all 3 layers (mucosa, submucosa, muscularis). Example: Meckel.
  • False (pseudo) diverticulum — only mucosa + submucosa herniating through muscular wall. Colonic diverticula are false.

The weak point — where vasa recta pierce the wall

  • The circular muscle layer is penetrated by nerves, vasa recta, and connective-tissue sheaths on the mesenteric side of the antimesenteric taeniae.
  • These penetration points are inherently weak → mucosa/submucosa herniate outward when intraluminal pressure rises.

Why the sigmoid?

  • Narrowest lumen of the colon → highest pressure per Laplace’s law (T = P × r; narrow r generates highest wall stress for a given pressure).
  • Exaggerated segmentation, chronic constipation, low-fiber (low-bulk) stool all raise pressure.
▼Mnemonic: “Sigmoid is where the Scary Small Sacs Squeeze”
Sigmoid = Small radius = Sky-high pressure. Also: in Western diets 95% of diverticula are in the sigmoid; in Asian populations, right-sided diverticula predominate.

▼3. Clinical Spectrum — Diverticulosis to Diverticulitis

Diverticulosis (the anatomic finding)

  • Prevalence rises sharply with age; ~50% of adults >60 in the US.
  • Most patients are asymptomatic.
  • Risk factors: age, low-fiber Western diet, obesity, NSAIDs, smoking, connective tissue disorders (Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos).

Diverticulitis (the complication)

  • Occurs in ~20% of diverticulosis patients.
  • Mechanism: fecalith obstructs the diverticular neck → stasis → micro-perforation → inflammation.
  • Presentation: LLQ pain (“left-sided appendicitis”), fever, leukocytosis, sometimes palpable mass.
  • CT is the diagnostic test of choice — pericolic fat stranding, wall thickening, abscess if present.
▼Exam Trap: Do NOT colonoscope acute diverticulitis
Acute inflammation raises perforation risk. Colonoscopy is reserved for 6–8 weeks after resolution to rule out concurrent malignancy (and should be done at least once in every first-time diverticulitis patient).

▼4. Diverticular Bleeding

Why it bleeds

  • The same vasa recta that create the weak point get stretched over the diverticular dome.
  • Chronic injury → asymmetric rupture of an arterial vasa recta into the lumen.
  • Painless, sudden, large-volume hematochezia — a classic USMLE stem.

Key clinical pattern

  • Most common cause of painless lower GI bleeding in adults >60.
  • Most bleeds (75–80%) stop spontaneously.
  • Paradoxically, bleeding diverticula are often right-sided even though diverticulitis is sigmoid-predominant — right-sided diverticula have wider necks and thinner overlying mucosa.
  • Hematochezia most common from sigmoid; melena possible from right colon.

▼5. Complications

Hinchey classification of diverticulitis

  • I — pericolic abscess (small, confined)
  • II — pelvic / distant intra-abdominal abscess
  • III — purulent peritonitis
  • IV — feculent peritonitis (free perforation)

Other complications

  • Fistula — most commonly colovesical (men more than women because women’s uterus is between colon and bladder); presents with pneumaturia or fecaluria.
  • Stricture / obstruction from scarring after recurrent attacks.
  • Free perforation → peritonitis, sepsis.
  • Diverticular bleeding (see above).
High-yield: New colovesical fistula in an older adult? Think diverticular disease (most common), then Crohn disease or malignancy.

▼6. Management & Prevention

Uncomplicated diverticulitis

  • Outpatient: bowel rest + oral antibiotics (e.g., ciprofloxacin + metronidazole or amoxicillin-clavulanate) in selected patients.
  • Inpatient: NPO, IV fluids, IV antibiotics covering enteric gram-negatives and anaerobes.

Complicated diverticulitis

  • Abscess >3 cm → percutaneous drainage + antibiotics.
  • Peritonitis, free perforation, failure of medical therapy → surgery (Hartmann procedure or primary anastomosis).

Prevention / long-term

  • High-fiber diet, regular exercise, weight loss. Nuts and seeds are no longer believed to cause diverticulitis (older teaching; modern data do not support restriction).
  • Elective sigmoidectomy considered after recurrent attacks or in immunocompromised patients.

Testing Your Understanding

TYU 1

A 68-year-old man presents with 2 days of LLQ pain, fever, and leukocytosis. CT reveals sigmoid wall thickening, pericolic fat stranding, and a 4.5-cm fluid collection with air. What is the appropriate management?

This is complicated diverticulitis with pericolic abscess (Hinchey I–II). For abscesses >3–4 cm, the preferred approach is CT-guided percutaneous drainage plus broad-spectrum IV antibiotics (covering enteric gram-negatives and anaerobes). Elective sigmoid resection is considered 6–8 weeks later. Colonoscopy is performed after recovery to exclude underlying malignancy.

TYU 2

A 74-year-old woman with known diverticulosis suddenly passes a large volume of bright red blood per rectum without abdominal pain. Hemodynamically she is stable. What is the likely source, and what is the most appropriate diagnostic next step?

Classic painless diverticular hemorrhage from an arterial vasa recta ruptured into the diverticular lumen. After resuscitation, the next step is typically colonoscopy (diagnostic and therapeutic — clips/epinephrine injection). If bleeding is too brisk for endoscopy, CT angiography can localize active extravasation; if unstable, angiography with embolization is used.

TYU 3

A 70-year-old man with a history of recurrent diverticulitis now complains of pneumaturia and recurrent UTIs. Imaging shows air in the bladder. What is the likely diagnosis and why?

Colovesical fistula, most commonly a complication of diverticular disease in older men (women’s uterus typically shields the bladder). Recurrent sigmoid inflammation erodes into the adjacent bladder dome → bacteria and air track into the urinary tract. Workup includes CT with oral contrast, cystoscopy, and often colonoscopy to exclude malignancy. Definitive management is surgical resection of the fistula and affected colon.

Classify colonic polyps, recognize which have malignant potential, and trace the molecular adenoma–carcinoma sequence that drives sporadic colorectal cancer.

▼1. Case 5 — 70y M with obstructing descending colon mass

Vignette: A 70-year-old man has a screening colonoscopy that reveals a large villous adenoma. Assuming it is not removed, 5 years later he has constipation, microcytic anemia, and a 5-kg weight loss. At surgery there is a 7-cm mass encircling the descending colon (apple-core lesion).

Which neoplasm is he now most likely to have? (Q9)

Answer: C. Adenocarcinoma.

A villous adenoma that is left in place can progress via the adenoma–carcinoma sequence to invasive adenocarcinoma. Left-sided tumors tend to be annular/obstructing (“apple-core” lesion) and present with changes in bowel habits, obstruction, and hematochezia.

Which lesion has the highest risk of malignant transformation? (Q10)

Answer: D. Villous adenoma.

Villous adenomas have approximately 10× the risk of becoming colorectal cancer compared with tubular adenomas. Hyperplastic polyps (<1 cm), fundic gland polyps, and leiomyomas have very low or essentially no malignant potential.

▼Exam Trap: “Villous” = “Villainous”
The higher the villous component and the larger the size (>4 cm), the higher the risk of carcinoma. High-grade dysplasia at any size is also high-risk. Tubulovillous is intermediate risk. Size, villous histology, and high-grade dysplasia are the three adenoma features that drive surveillance intervals.

▼2. Classification of Colonic Polyps

By malignant potential

  • Neoplastic (precancerous)
    • Tubular adenoma (most common, <25% villous)
    • Tubulovillous adenoma (25–75% villous)
    • Villous adenoma (>75% villous) — highest risk
    • Sessile serrated adenoma / polyp (SSA/P)
    • Traditional serrated adenoma (TSA) — rare
  • Non-neoplastic (low/no risk)
    • Hyperplastic polyp (small left-sided, usually <5 mm)
    • Juvenile (hamartomatous — sporadic, usually benign)
    • Peutz-Jeghers hamartomatous polyp
    • Inflammatory polyp (pseudopolyp from IBD)

By gross appearance

  • Pedunculated — on a stalk, usually tubular adenomas; easier to resect endoscopically.
  • Sessile — broad-based; villous and sessile serrated polyps tend to be sessile; harder to resect.
High-yield: Only adenomas (tubular, tubulovillous, villous) and sessile serrated lesions are truly precancerous. Small distal hyperplastic polyps are not.

▼3. Adenomas (Neoplastic Polyps)

Tubular adenoma

  • Most common adenoma; typically pedunculated, small.
  • Histology: tightly packed, tubular glands with low-grade dysplasia (elongated, hyperchromatic nuclei, pseudostratification).
  • Low malignant potential when small (<1 cm).

Villous adenoma

  • Often sessile, “cauliflower-like” gross appearance.
  • Histology: long, finger-like villi with dysplastic epithelium.
  • ~10× higher malignancy risk than tubular.
  • Large villous adenomas in the rectum can rarely cause secretory diarrhea with hypokalemia (McKittrick-Wheelock syndrome).

Risk features driving malignant potential

  • Size >1 cm (especially >4 cm — risk approaches 40%)
  • Villous histology
  • High-grade dysplasia
  • Sessile base (incomplete resection risk)
▼Mnemonic: “Big, Villous, Bad”
Three adenoma features that raise cancer risk: Big (>1 cm), Villous architecture, Bad (high-grade) dysplasia.

▼4. Serrated Polyps & the Serrated Pathway

Hyperplastic polyp

  • Small (<5 mm), usually distal colon/rectum.
  • Histology: serrated (“saw-toothed”) crypts in the upper third; no dysplasia.
  • No malignant potential when typical.

Sessile serrated adenoma/polyp (SSA/P)

  • Usually right-sided, flat, often covered by mucus cap — easy to miss.
  • Serrations extend to the crypt base with basal dilation and lateral branching (“L”- and “T”-shaped crypts).
  • Molecular: BRAF V600E mutation → CpG island methylator phenotype (CIMP) → MLH1 silencing → microsatellite instability (MSI).
  • Precancerous — drives ~20–30% of colorectal cancers (the “serrated pathway”).
▼Exam Trap: Hyperplastic vs sessile serrated
Small distal (rectosigmoid) hyperplastic polyps = benign. Large or right-sided “hyperplastic-looking” polyps should be treated as sessile serrated until proven otherwise — they are precancerous and require surveillance.

▼5. Molecular Pathways of Colorectal Cancer

Chromosomal instability (CIN) / Classic adenoma–carcinoma sequence (~80% of sporadic CRC)

Normal epithelium → APC loss → hyperproliferative epithelium / early adenoma → KRAS activation → late adenoma → p53 loss → invasive carcinoma.

  • APC (5q): first hit, Wnt/β-catenin dysregulation.
  • KRAS (12p): drives growth of adenomas.
  • p53 (17p) / SMAD4 / DCC: final transformation to invasive cancer.
  • Familial Adenomatous Polyposis (FAP): germline APC mutation → hundreds of adenomas, near-100% cancer risk by age 40 → prophylactic colectomy.

Microsatellite instability (MSI) / Mismatch repair pathway (~15% of sporadic CRC)

  • Sporadic: arises from the serrated pathway — BRAF mutation + CIMP → MLH1 promoter hypermethylation → MSI.
  • Lynch syndrome (HNPCC): germline mismatch repair gene mutation (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2) → right-sided CRC, young age, associated endometrial/ovarian/gastric/urothelial cancers.
  • MSI tumors tend to be right-sided, mucinous, with prominent lymphocytic infiltrate (“Crohn-like” reaction) and better prognosis; they respond to immune checkpoint inhibitors.
High-yield sequence: APC → KRAS → p53 (CIN, left-sided) vs BRAF → CIMP → MLH1 silencing → MSI (serrated, right-sided).
▼Exam Trap: IBD-associated CRC is different
In IBD-associated CRC the molecular sequence is p53 first, APC later (opposite of sporadic). That is why flat dysplasia arises in chronically inflamed mucosa before any adenoma forms — random biopsy surveillance is needed.

▼6. Colon Cancer — Clinical, Gross, Histology, Staging

Right-sided vs left-sided presentation

Feature Right colon (cecum/ascending) Left colon (descending/sigmoid)
Gross morphologyExophytic / polypoid (wide lumen)Annular / “apple-core” (narrow lumen)
Classic presentationOccult bleed → iron-deficiency anemia, weight loss, fatigueObstruction, change in bowel habits, hematochezia
MolecularMSI / serrated pathway more commonClassic APC→KRAS→p53 CIN
Prognosis (stage-adjusted)Generally comparable (MSI subset may do better)—

Histology

  • Most are adenocarcinoma, graded well / moderately / poorly differentiated.
  • Mucinous adenocarcinoma (>50% extracellular mucin) — worse prognosis; more common in MSI.
  • Signet-ring cell carcinoma — aggressive, poor prognosis.

Staging and prognosis

  • TNM / AJCC staging (depth of invasion T, regional LN N, distant M) — preferred system.
  • Dukes classification (historic): A = mucosa/submucosa, B = through muscularis, C = LN, D = distant.
  • Liver is the most common distant metastasis (portal venous drainage); rectum can metastasize to lung via systemic veins.
  • CEA is used for surveillance after treatment, not screening.
  • Overall 5-year survival ~65% (varies by stage: >90% stage I, <15% stage IV).

Screening (brief)

  • Average-risk screening now typically begins at age 45.
  • Options: colonoscopy every 10 y (gold standard), FIT annually, stool DNA (Cologuard) every 3 y, CT colonography every 5 y.
  • High-risk groups (Lynch, FAP, IBD, family history) need earlier and more frequent surveillance.

Testing Your Understanding

TYU 1

A 68-year-old man presents with fatigue. Labs show microcytic anemia (Hgb 9.1, MCV 72). He denies overt bleeding. Colonoscopy reveals a large polypoid mass in the cecum. What type of lesion is most likely and what molecular pathway is most often responsible?

Right-sided colon cancer classically presents with occult GI bleeding → iron-deficiency anemia and fatigue (wide lumen + cecal stool is liquid, so no obstruction). Right-sided tumors more often arise via the serrated pathway: BRAF mutation → CIMP → MLH1 silencing → microsatellite instability (MSI). These tumors tend to be mucinous, have a lymphocytic infiltrate, and respond to checkpoint inhibitor therapy.

TYU 2

A 42-year-old woman has hundreds of adenomatous polyps throughout her colon on colonoscopy. Her father died of colon cancer at 38. What is the diagnosis, the underlying genetic defect, and the definitive management?

Familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP) — autosomal dominant, caused by germline mutation of the APC tumor-suppressor gene on chromosome 5q21. Cancer risk approaches 100% by age 40 if untreated, so definitive management is prophylactic total colectomy (typically in the second decade of life). Variants: Gardner syndrome (FAP + desmoid tumors, osteomas, dental abnormalities), Turcot syndrome (FAP + CNS tumors, typically medulloblastoma).

TYU 3

A 36-year-old man has right-sided colon cancer. Family history: mother with endometrial cancer at 45, maternal uncle with colon cancer at 48. Tumor testing shows loss of MSH2 expression and high microsatellite instability. What is the diagnosis and what additional cancers should he be screened for?

Lynch syndrome (HNPCC) — autosomal dominant germline mutation of a mismatch repair gene (most commonly MLH1 or MSH2; also MSH6, PMS2). Diagnosis driven by young age, right-sided colon cancer, MSI-high tumor, and family history (Amsterdam / Bethesda criteria). Screen for associated cancers: endometrial (most common extracolonic), ovarian, gastric, small bowel, urothelial (ureter/renal pelvis), hepatobiliary, and skin (Muir-Torre variant — sebaceous tumors). Start colonoscopy at age 20–25 (every 1–2 years), plus gynecologic surveillance.

Acute appendicitis would present acutely with RLQ pain and fever. Angiodysplasia and diverticulosis cause painless bleeding, typically in older adults. Meckel diverticulum more often presents in children with painless bleeding.

Q2

Which pathologic feature of Crohn disease can be seen on biopsies?

A. Eosinophilic mucosal infiltrate

B. Crypt abscess

C. Brunner gland hyperplasia

D. Noncaseating granuloma

E. Thickened apical subepithelial collagen layer

Answer: D. Noncaseating granuloma. Granulomas are present in ~35% of Crohn biopsies and are highly specific (not sensitive). Crypt abscesses occur in both UC and Crohn but are more prominent in UC. Eosinophilic mucosa suggests eosinophilic gastroenteritis. Brunner gland hyperplasia is duodenal (peptic injury). Thickened subepithelial collagen is collagenous colitis.

Q3

A 39-year-old woman has had recurrent bloody diarrhea and cramping for 12 years, consistent with ulcerative colitis. She is at greatest risk for developing which of the following complications?

A. Diverticulitis

B. Adenocarcinoma

C. Perirectal fistula formation

D. Fat malabsorption

E. Primary biliary cirrhosis

Answer: B. Adenocarcinoma. After ~10 years of UC, cumulative CRC risk climbs dramatically (especially with pancolitis or PSC). Perirectal fistulas are Crohn. Fat malabsorption is an ileal/small-bowel problem (Crohn, not UC). UC is associated with primary sclerosing cholangitis, not primary biliary cirrhosis/cholangitis.

Q4

What changes on colon biopsies indicate chronicity versus an acute event in UC?

A. Lymphocytic infiltrate

B. Superficial mucosal hemorrhage

C. Brunner gland hyperplasia

D. Crypt abscess

E. Crypt architectural disarray

Answer: E. Crypt architectural disarray (branching, distortion, dropout, basal plasmacytosis) is the histologic hallmark of chronicity. Crypt abscesses can occur acutely. Lymphocytic infiltrate is nonspecific. Brunner gland hyperplasia is duodenal.

Q5

A 65-year-old hospitalized woman, treated with multiple antibiotics and recently ventilated, develops abdominal distention with ileus on day 20. Labs show leukocytosis and hypoalbuminemia. Colonoscopy shows yellow-white plaques on erythematous mucosa. What is the most likely diagnosis?

A. Pseudomembranous enterocolitis

B. Gas gangrene with myonecrosis

C. Ulcerative proctitis

D. Ischemic bowel disease

Answer: A. Pseudomembranous enterocolitis. The classic combo of recent antibiotics, hospitalization, and yellow-white pseudomembranes is diagnostic of C. difficile colitis. Toxic megacolon is a complication but not the primary diagnosis here.

Q6

What is true about C. difficile infection?

B. It is invasive, producing toxins A and B

Answer: E. Fecal-oral route. C. difficile is a gram-positive spore-forming anaerobe; disease is toxin-mediated (not invasive); antibacterials (not antifungals) drive overgrowth; ~20–30% of hospitalized (not outpatient) adults are colonized.

Q7

A 65-year-old woman with sigmoid diverticulosis has hematochezia. CT shows 1-cm air-filled sigmoid outpouchings. Although rare, which long-term complication is most likely?

Answer: D. Pericolic abscess. About 20% of diverticulosis patients develop diverticulitis; localized microperforation leads to pericolic or pelvic abscess. Diverticular disease is not a precursor to CRC, UC, malabsorption, or toxic megacolon.

Q8

What is true about the pathophysiology of diverticular disease?

A. Risk increased from lack of haustral folds

B. In acute settings, a high-fiber diet can alleviate pressure

C. The most common site is in the thin-walled cecum

E. Nerves, vasa recta, and connective tissue sheaths penetrate the muscle coat

In Western populations, the sigmoid (not cecum) is the most common site. Acute diverticulitis is managed with bowel rest (not high fiber); high fiber is for prevention. Crypt abscesses are IBD histology.

Q9

A 70-year-old man has a villous adenoma seen on screening colonoscopy that is not removed. Five years later he has constipation, microcytic anemia, and 5-kg weight loss. At surgery there is a 7-cm mass encircling the descending colon. Which neoplasm is he now most likely to have?

A. Villous adenoma

B. Carcinoid tumor

C. Adenocarcinoma

D. Mucinous cystadenoma

E. Leiomyosarcoma

Answer: C. Adenocarcinoma. Untreated villous adenomas progress via APC → KRAS → p53 to invasive adenocarcinoma. Left-sided tumors present as annular “apple-core” lesions with obstruction, change in bowel habits, and hematochezia.

Q10

Which of the following lesions found on colonoscopy has the highest risk of transforming into a malignancy?

A. A pedunculated tubular adenoma

B. An 8-mm hyperplastic polyp

C. A fundic gland polyp

D. A villous adenoma

E. An 8-mm leiomyoma

Answer: D. Villous adenoma — ~10× the malignant potential of a tubular adenoma. Small hyperplastic polyps, fundic gland polyps, and leiomyomas are essentially benign.

Q11

A 28-year-old man with a 4-year history of bloody diarrhea is found on colonoscopy to have continuous inflammation extending from the rectum to the splenic flexure, with loss of haustra on imaging (“lead-pipe colon”). A. Erythema nodosum

B. Ankylosing spondylitis

Q12

A 30-year-old woman with Crohn disease involving the terminal ileum develops steatorrhea and is found to have megaloblastic anemia. Which of the following best explains her findings?

A. Lactose intolerance

B. Bacterial overgrowth producing folate

C. Loss of bile-salt reabsorption and vitamin B12 absorption

D. Pancreatic insufficiency

E. Gluten sensitivity

Answer: C. The terminal ileum is the site of bile-salt reabsorption (bile-salt loss → fat malabsorption / steatorrhea) and B12–intrinsic factor absorption (loss → megaloblastic anemia). This explains why Crohn disease involving the ileum — or surgical resection of the ileum — causes this dual pattern.

Q13

A 35-year-old man with long-standing UC undergoes surveillance colonoscopy. Random biopsies show flat high-grade dysplasia without a visible mass. What is the recommended management?

A. Repeat colonoscopy in 6 months

B. Endoscopic mucosal resection

C. Topical 5-ASA

D. Total proctocolectomy

E. Azathioprine escalation

Answer: D. Total proctocolectomy. Flat, invisible high-grade dysplasia in UC has a high concurrent / subsequent cancer risk and cannot be reliably resected endoscopically. Current guidelines support colectomy. Visible, well-demarcated dysplastic lesions may be amenable to endoscopic resection with close surveillance, but flat HGD typically warrants surgery.

Q14

A 68-year-old man hospitalized for pneumonia develops profuse watery diarrhea after 5 days of ceftriaxone. Stool GDH is positive; toxin EIA is positive. He is clinically mild (WBC 11K, no renal dysfunction). What is first-line therapy?

A. Oral metronidazole

B. Oral vancomycin

C. IV vancomycin

D. Loperamide

E. Fecal microbiota transplant

Answer: B. Oral vancomycin (or fidaxomicin) × 10 days is first-line for any CDI under current IDSA guidance. Metronidazole is second-line only. IV vancomycin does not reach the colon lumen and is ineffective. Loperamide is contraindicated. FMT is reserved for recurrent disease.

Q15

A 73-year-old man presents with a single episode of painless hematochezia. He is stable. Colonoscopy shows multiple right-sided diverticula, one with a visible vessel. What vessel is responsible for this bleeding?

A. Superior mesenteric vein

B. Inferior hemorrhoidal plexus

C. Vasa recta

D. Marginal artery of Drummond

E. Inferior epigastric artery

Answer: C. Vasa recta. Diverticula herniate through the colonic wall exactly where the vasa recta pierce the muscularis. Chronic injury can cause asymmetric arterial rupture of a vasa recta into the diverticular lumen, producing painless, brisk lower GI bleeding. Right-sided diverticula bleed disproportionately because they are wider-mouthed with thinner overlying mucosa.

Q16

A 24-year-old man is referred after a screening colonoscopy showed >100 tubular adenomas throughout the colon. Genetic testing confirms a germline APC mutation. Which of the following is he also at elevated risk for?

A. Pheochromocytoma

B. Medullary thyroid carcinoma

C. Desmoid tumors and osteomas of the jaw

D. Hemangioblastomas

E. Schwannomas

Gardner also includes dental abnormalities, epidermoid cysts, and congenital hypertrophy of the retinal pigment epithelium. Turcot = FAP + medulloblastoma. MEN2, VHL, and NF2 syndromes explain the other choices.

Q17

A 52-year-old man has a 1.5-cm right-sided polyp removed at screening colonoscopy. Histology shows serrated crypts extending to the base with lateral L-shaped extensions. Molecular testing reveals BRAF V600E mutation and MLH1 promoter hypermethylation. What lesion is this, and what future CRC subtype does it predispose to?

A. Hyperplastic polyp — no increased risk

B. Tubular adenoma — CIN pathway CRC

C. Sessile serrated lesion — MSI-high CRC via serrated pathway

D. Juvenile polyp — no increased risk

E. Peutz-Jeghers polyp — small-bowel adenocarcinoma

Answer: C. Basal crypt serration and L-/T-shaped extensions with BRAF mutation + MLH1 hypermethylation define a sessile serrated lesion. These progress along the serrated pathway (BRAF → CIMP → MLH1 silencing) to MSI-high, right-sided colorectal cancers — often mucinous with a prominent lymphocytic infiltrate and responsive to immune checkpoint inhibitors.

Q18

A 66-year-old man with metastatic colon cancer has tumor testing showing deficient mismatch repair / MSI-high status. Which treatment is most likely to benefit him?

A. Imatinib

B. Cetuximab

C. Bevacizumab

Answer: D. Pembrolizumab (anti-PD-1). MSI-high / dMMR tumors produce neoantigens and respond strongly to immune checkpoint inhibitors — now a standard first-line option in metastatic MSI-high CRC. Cetuximab requires wild-type RAS/BRAF; bevacizumab is anti-VEGF; imatinib targets GIST (c-KIT); trastuzumab targets HER2-amplified tumors.
