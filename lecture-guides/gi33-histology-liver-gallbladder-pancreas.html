▼1. Liver Functions — The Big Picture

⭐ Think of the liver as the body's customs office + chemical factory. Everything absorbed from the gut goes through it first. It runs two jobs at once: an exocrine job (makes bile, ships it to the duodenum) and a giant metabolic / processing job (synthesizes, stores, detoxifies). Every histology feature you'll learn maps back to one of those two jobs.

📈 The 7 Headline Jobs

  • 1. Filters blood (dual supply). Two pipes in, one pipe out. ~80% from the portal vein (nutrient-rich, O₂-poor, fresh from the gut) + ~20% from the hepatic artery (O₂-rich, from the celiac trunk). They mix in the sinusoids.
  • 2. Makes bile. Hepatocytes secrete bile salts, cholesterol, phospholipids, and bilirubin glucuronides → emulsify and absorb dietary fat in the duodenum.
  • 3. Makes plasma proteins. Albumin, fibrinogen, transferrin, ceruloplasmin, apolipoproteins, and almost all clotting factors. One famous exception: Factor VIII (made by endothelium — see trap below).
  • 4. Runs sugar metabolism. Glycogen storage (fed) and gluconeogenesis (fasting) → the liver is the body's blood-sugar buffer.
  • 5. Detoxifies. Drugs, alcohol, and ammonia (urea cycle clears nitrogen from amino-acid breakdown).
  • 6. Recycles old red cells. Kupffer cells (resident sinusoidal macrophages) phagocytose senescent RBCs and stray gut bacteria.
  • 7. Stores stuff. Vitamin A (in Ito / stellate cells), vitamins D/E/K, iron (as ferritin), and B₁₂.
⚡ Why the dual blood supply matters — 3 quick consequences:
  • "First-pass" metabolism. Anything absorbed by the gut hits the liver before systemic circulation → oral bioavailability is often much lower than IV.
  • Detox shield. Toxins get neutralized before they reach the heart and brain — until the liver fails (e.g., cirrhosis → hepatic encephalopathy from unfiltered ammonia).
  • Vessel-loss patterns. Lose the hepatic artery → bile-duct ischemia (the biliary tree is supplied only by the artery). Lose the portal vein → hepatocyte hypoxia.
⚠️ EXAM TRAP: Factor VIII ▼
  • The rule: the liver makes every clotting factor except Factor VIII. Factor VIII is made by vascular endothelium (including liver sinusoidal endothelium and Weibel-Palade bodies).
  • Why it matters: in advanced liver failure, Factor VIII can be normal or even high while everything else (PT, INR) is wrecked.
  • Step 1 distinction: Liver failure → low II, V, VII, IX, X → Factor VIII preserved. DIC → everything consumed, including Factor VIII low. Use Factor VIII to tell them apart.

▼2. The Classic Hepatic Lobule

⭐ Picture a honeycomb cell. The classic lobule is the textbook way to identify liver on a slide: a hexagon with 1 central vein in the middle and a portal triad at each of 6 corners. Hepatocyte plates radiate outward like spokes on a wheel.

🔍 What You See on H&E (in order of obviousness)

  • Central vein (a.k.a. centrilobular venule) → the bullseye. Thin-walled, endothelium only, no smooth muscle, often looks "empty."
  • 6 portal triads at the corners → each a fibrous-looking pocket holding 3 tubes: hepatic arteriole + portal venule + bile ductule (+ lymphatics).
  • Hepatocyte plates → 1 cell thick, radiating from the central vein out to the corners, separated by sinusoids.
  • Sinusoids → dilated, irregular capillaries running alongside the plates. Blood flows from corners IN to the bullseye.
  • Reticulin scaffold (type III collagen) holds the plates together — invisible on H&E; show up on silver (Gomori) stain.

📰 "Lost on a slide?" — 3-Step ID Checklist

Hand yourself an unknown liver section. Ask in this order:

  1. Where's the bullseye? → The single thin-walled venule at the hub of radiating plates is the central vein.
  2. Where are the corners? → Look for fibrous pockets containing 3 tubes together — that's a portal triad.
  3. Which plates are which zone? → Zone 1 = touching the corner (triad). Zone 3 = touching the bullseye (central vein).
🧠 Mnemonic: "1 in the middle, 6 on the edge"
▼

The classic lobule is a hexagon: 1 central vein + 6 portal triads. Picture a soccer-ball pattern — the central vein is the dot at the center of the panel, the triads are the seams.

Bonus image: imagine a wagon wheel. Hub = central vein. Spokes = hepatocyte plates. Rim = the ring of 6 triads.

⚠️ EXAM TRAP: Central vein vs portal venule ▼
  • The problem: both are thin-walled venules. They look almost identical at first glance.
  • The fix: look at what's next door. A portal venule sits in a triad — surrounded by an artery and a bile duct. The central vein sits alone, with hepatocyte plates radiating around it.
  • One-liner: "Alone = central. With company = portal."

▼3. The Portal Triad (Portal Tract)

⭐ Each corner of the hexagon is a "loading dock." The portal triad = hepatic arteriole + portal venule + bile ductule (plus lymphatics). It's where blood enters the lobule AND where bile leaves it — same address, traffic going both directions.

StructureWall / LiningContentDirection of Flow
Hepatic arteriole Thick smooth muscle wall (muscular artery); thick for its diameter O₂-rich blood from celiac trunk INTO the lobule → sinusoids
Portal venule Thin wall, largest lumen in the triad O₂-poor, nutrient-rich blood from GI tract, spleen, pancreas INTO the lobule → sinusoids
Bile ductule Single layer of cuboidal epithelium (cholangiocytes); 1–2 ductules per triad Bile OUT of the lobule → larger ducts → common hepatic duct
Lymphatic vessel(s) Single thin layer of fenestrated endothelium; often collapsed on slides Lymph (rich in plasma proteins escaped from sinusoids via Space of Disse) OUT of the lobule → thoracic duct
⚡ 5-second triad ID on histology:
  • Artery → small lumen, thick muscular wall ("thickest wall for its size").
  • Vein → biggest lumen, paper-thin wall.
  • Duct → clearly cuboidal epithelium (one neat layer of cube-shaped cells).
🧠 Mnemonic: Portal triad = "A-V-D"
▼

Artery · Vein · Duct → the "AVD" trio at every corner.

Naming nuance: if you count lymphatics as a 4th element, some books call it a portal tract instead of a triad. Both terms are correct — same structure.

⚠️ EXAM TRAP: Biliary ischemia ▼
  • The setup: hepatocytes get blood from both the artery AND the portal vein. The biliary tree gets blood from the hepatic artery ONLY.
  • The consequence: if the hepatic artery clots off, hepatocytes can survive on portal flow — but bile ducts die.
  • Classic vignette: Post–liver transplant hepatic artery thrombosis → ischemic cholangiopathy (bile-duct necrosis → strictures, biliary cast syndrome) even though portal flow is fine. This is THE go-to answer for "why is the biliary tree failing after transplant?"

▼4. Hepatocytes — The Functional Cell

⭐ The hepatocyte is the worker bee of the liver. Polygonal epithelial cell with a big round central nucleus (often two nuclei), pink cytoplasm, stacked into 1-cell-thick plates radiating from the central vein. Two faces — one talks to blood (sinusoid side), the other talks to bile (canaliculus side).

🔍 What Hepatocytes Look Like on H&E

  • Big round central nucleus with a prominent nucleolus → often binucleate (this is the buzzword!).
  • Pink (eosinophilic) cytoplasm → packed with smooth ER, mitochondria, and lysosomes for synthesis + detox.
  • Polygonal shape (6–8 sides) → stacked into 1-cell-thick plates.
  • Reticulin scaffold (type III collagen) holds plates in place → invisible on H&E; only shows on silver (Gomori) stain. Loss of this scaffold = hallmark of HCC.

🔄 The Two Faces of a Hepatocyte

Most epithelia are polarized top-to-bottom. Hepatocytes are polarized sideways — one side faces blood, the opposite side faces bile.

SurfaceFacesWhat it does
Basolateral (the "blood side") Sinusoid (across the space of Disse) Picks up nutrients, toxins, drugs · ships out plasma proteins, VLDL, urea into blood
Apical (the "bile side") Bile canaliculus (groove between two adjacent hepatocytes) Secretes bile salts, conjugated bilirubin, cholesterol, phospholipids

Key trick: the bile canaliculus has no lining cell of its own. Two neighbor hepatocytes carve out a groove between themselves, seal it with tight junctions + desmosomes, and become their own duct walls.

⚡ Reticulin Stain & HCC — the classic pathology bridge:
  • Normal liver: silver stain shows 1-cell-thick plates outlined by neat reticulin fibers.
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC): plates expand to ≥3 cells thick, reticulin scaffold disorganized or gone.
  • Why this matters: on biopsy, "loss of reticulin pattern" is one of the cleanest diagnostic clues for HCC.
⚠️ EXAM TRAP: Binucleate cells are NORMAL ▼
  • Don't panic: binucleation is a normal feature of hepatocytes — a sign of regenerative readiness (DNA replication ahead of cell division).
  • Real malignancy clues: nuclear atypia, loss of plate architecture, reticulin collapse — NOT simply "two nuclei."

▼5. Flow of BLOOD Through the Lobule

⭐ One-line rule: blood flows IN (corners → bullseye). Blood enters at the portal triad, drifts inward through sinusoids toward the central vein, then exits the lobule on its way to the IVC.

🌊 The Path, Step by Step

  1. Portal vein (from gut) → interlobar → interlobular → portal venule in triad
  2. Hepatic artery (from celiac trunk) → interlobar → interlobular → hepatic arteriole in triad
  3. Arteriolar + portal blood MIX as they dump into sinusoids at the lobule's edge
  4. Sinusoidal blood flows centripetally (inward) past hepatocyte plates → exchanges plasma across the Space of Disse
  5. Drains into central vein (= terminal hepatic venule)
  6. Central veins → sublobular veins → hepatic veins (L, R, middle) → IVC → right atrium

Fun fact: the sinusoids are the only place in the body where arterial and venous blood routinely mix before reaching the heart.

⚡ The two rules that are 90% of the testable content:
  • Blood → INWARD (triad → central vein) · centripetal
  • Bile → OUTWARD (central → triad) · centrifugal
  • Blood and bile move in OPPOSITE directions — this single point shows up over and over.

📊 Pressure Numbers Worth Knowing

  • Aortic pressure ~90 mmHg → drops to ~25–30 mmHg by the time arterial blood reaches the hepatic arteriole — low enough to mix safely with portal blood.
  • Normal portal pressure: 5–10 mmHg.
  • Portal HTN: > 10–12 mmHg, OR a hepatic venous pressure gradient (HVPG) > 5 mmHg defines clinical portal hypertension.
🧠 Mnemonic: Blood goes "CPU" → Central
▼

Compartment of triad → Passes through sinusoid → Unites at central vein.

Or even simpler: "corners deliver, center collects."

⚠️ EXAM TRAP: Budd-Chiari vs Right Heart Failure ▼

Both produce identical-looking biopsies: centrilobular (Zone 3) congestion = "nutmeg liver" gross appearance. Why? Outflow is blocked → blood backs up at the central vein → Zone 3 sits in pooled blood. Distinguish by the clinical setup:

  • Budd-Chiari = hepatic vein thrombosis. Triggers: hypercoagulable states, OCPs, polycythemia vera, pregnancy. Clinical: tender hepatomegaly + ascites + abdominal pain.
  • Right HF / tricuspid regurgitation = backup from the heart. Same Zone 3 congestion, but caused by elevated central venous pressure. Clinical: JVD, peripheral edema, pulsatile liver.

▼6. Flow of BILE Through the Lobule

⭐ Bile flows the OPPOSITE way blood does. Blood flows IN to the central vein; bile flows OUT to the portal triad. Same lobule, two highways, opposite directions.

💨 The Bile Pathway (memorize this exit route)

  1. Hepatocyte apical membrane — bile is dumped here, into a groove…
  2. Bile canaliculus — a sealed groove between two hepatocytes. No lining cell — just hepatocyte membranes held together by tight junctions and desmosomes.
  3. Canals of Hering — the handoff zone: half hepatocyte, half cholangiocyte. This is where stem cells (oval cells) live — the liver's regeneration HQ.
  4. Bile ductules — first true ducts; lined by cuboidal cholangiocytes; sit in the portal triad.
  5. Interlobular ducts → R + L hepatic ducts → common hepatic → common bile duct → duodenum (ampulla of Vater).

🛡️ Cholangiocytes — the duct-liners

  • Shape: cuboidal in small ducts → columnar in large ducts.
  • Job #1: pump HCO₃⁻ + water into bile (via CFTR), triggered by secretin → alkalinizes and dilutes bile. (This is the "ductular" component; hepatocytes make the "canalicular" component.)
  • Job #2: ship IgA into bile (express IgA receptor).
  • Their weakness: they depend ENTIRELY on the hepatic artery (no portal backup) → first to die in ischemia. Also targeted by autoimmunity → PBC and PSC.
⚡ CFTR & the liver: No CFTR → no bicarb → thick, sticky bile clogs small ductules → focal biliary cirrhosis. Classic hepatic complication of cystic fibrosis.
🧠 Mnemonic: Bile goes "CHIRP"
▼

Canaliculus → canals of Hering → ductules → Interlobular ducts → R + L hepatic ducts → common hepatic → Passage to duodenum (via CBD & ampulla of Vater).

⚠️ EXAM TRAP: Direction confusion ▼

One sentence to never forget: "Bile LEAVES the lobule. Blood COMES IN."

  • Bile → central → portal triad (centrifugal/outward).
  • Blood → portal triad → central vein (centripetal/inward).
  • Test writers love flipping these. Lock in the anchor sentence above.

▼7. Liver Acinus & Zones 1–3 (Rappaport)

⭐ The acinus is the functional lobule — a diamond drawn around blood supply (not the classic hexagon). Two portal triads sit at its wide corners, two central veins at its tips. Divided into 3 zones based on how fresh the blood is.

Memory hook: Think of an assembly line. Zone 1 = first in line (fresh oxygen + toxins). Zone 3 = last in line (low oxygen, exhausted blood, but hoarding all the detox enzymes).

📊 The Three Zones

ZonePositionO₂ & Nutrient SupplyMetabolic RoleFirst to Die From…
Zone 1 (periportal) Closest to portal triad Highest O₂, nutrients, toxins Oxidative metabolism, gluconeogenesis, β-oxidation, urea cycle, bile synthesis, plasma-protein synthesis Viral hepatitis; toxic ingestion of directly hepatotoxic substances; iron overload (hemochromatosis — iron reaches Zone 1 first)
Zone 2 (midzonal) Between Zones 1 & 3 Intermediate Mixed metabolism Yellow fever (classic association)
Zone 3 (centrilobular, pericentral) Closest to central vein Lowest O₂ — already extracted by zones 1&2 CYP450 detoxification, alcohol metabolism, glycolysis, lipogenesis Ischemic injury (shock liver); alcohol-related steatosis & cirrhosis; acetaminophen toxicity; passive venous congestion (nutmeg liver)
⚡ Zone 3 is the MOST TESTED zone in the lecture. Memorize these 4 bullets:
  • Ischemia → Zone 3 dies first (lowest O₂).
  • Alcoholic fatty liver → steatosis starts in Zone 3.
  • Acetaminophen toxicity → Zone 3 necrosis (CYP2E1 lives there).
  • Nutmeg liver (right heart failure / Budd-Chiari) → Zone 3 congestion.
🧠 Mnemonic: Zone 3 = the "SICK" Zone
▼

Steatosis · Ischemia · CYP450 damage (APAP) · Kold blood (congestion)

Zone 1 by contrast gets the "first pass" — fresh oxygen, fresh toxins, fresh iron. So:

  • Viral hepatitis hits Zone 1 first.
  • Hemochromatosis (iron overload) hits Zone 1 first.
⚠️ EXAM TRAP: Why acetaminophen kills Zone 3 (not Zone 1) ▼

The logic chain: APAP is harmless until CYP2E1 converts it into NAPQI, which is toxic. CYP2E1 is concentrated in Zone 3 → NAPQI is generated there → Zone 3 dies.

  • Counterintuitive because Zone 1 sounds "most metabolic" — but the toxic detox enzymes (CYPs) are packed into Zone 3.
  • Rule of thumb: damage = wherever the enzyme bioactivates the toxin. That's Zone 3 for APAP, CCl₄, and most xenobiotics.

▼8. Three Ways to Define "Lobule"

⭐ Same liver, three different lenses. Each model centers on a different structure and is useful for a different job.

ModelShapeCentered OnBest For
Classic lobule Hexagon Central vein What you see on H&E slides (anatomy)
Portal lobule Triangle Portal triad (bile duct) What the liver secretes — treats it like any other exocrine gland
Acinus (Rappaport) Diamond Vessel axis between 2 triads How the liver gets damaged — defines Zones 1–3 (pathology)
⚡ The one-line translator:
  • Anatomists draw classic lobules (hexagons).
  • Pathologists speak in acini & zones.
  • Geometrically: one classic lobule's central vein is surrounded by the Zone-3 tips of 6 different acini. One acinus bridges 2 neighboring lobules.
⚠️ EXAM TRAP: "Centrilobular" vs "periportal" ▼

Same region, different name. Don't let the vocabulary flip trip you up:

  • "Centrilobular" (around central vein) = Zone 3.
  • "Periportal" (around portal triad) = Zone 1.
  • So "centrilobular necrosis from APAP" = "Zone 3 necrosis from APAP." Identical answer.

🧠 Testing Your Understanding

TYU LO 1&2, Q1 — Directionality

Trace the path of (a) a red blood cell from its entry into the liver to the IVC, and (b) a bile salt from its synthesis to the common hepatic duct. Which direction does each travel within the lobule?

Hint: "bile LEAVES the lobule; blood COMES IN."

✓ Answer:

Blood (inward / centripetal): portal venule + hepatic arteriole in the triad → mix in sinusoids → central vein → sublobular vein → hepatic vein → IVC.

Bile (outward / centrifugal): hepatocyte apical membrane → canaliculus → canals of Hering → bile ductule in the triad → interlobular duct → R/L hepatic duct → common hepatic duct.

Bottom line: Opposite directions, same lobule. The portal triad is the blood entrance AND the bile exit.

TYU LO 1&2, Q2 — Zonal Vulnerability

A 45-year-old takes a massive acetaminophen overdose. Biopsy shows necrosis clustered around the central veins. Why isn't the damage at the portal triads — after all, that's where the drug enters?

Hint: damage appears where the drug is activated, not where it enters.

✓ Answer:

APAP itself is harmless. Its toxic metabolite NAPQI is what kills hepatocytes. NAPQI is made by CYP2E1, which lives in Zone 3 (centrilobular) — so the damage happens where the activation happens.

Zone 3 is also doubly vulnerable: (1) lowest O₂ → glutathione depletes fast, (2) highest CYP2E1 concentration. Two strikes → centrilobular necrosis.

Antidote: N-acetylcysteine (NAC) replenishes glutathione to neutralize NAPQI.

▼ 1. Sinusoid structure — the world's leakiest capillary

⭐ Think "Swiss cheese hose with no liner." Hepatic sinusoids are the leakiest capillaries in the body — big fenestrae (no diaphragm) and no basement membrane. Plasma oozes out to bathe hepatocyte microvilli directly.

🔍 5 defining features

  • Endothelium: thin, flat, with "sieve plates" of 100-nm fenestrae. No diaphragms.
  • Basement membrane: absent/discontinuous. This is THE feature.
  • Lumen: wide, irregular, usually a single file of RBCs on H&E.
  • Flow: portal triad → central vein (centripetal/inward).
  • Blood mix: 80% portal (nutrient-rich) + 20% hepatic artery (O₂-rich). Only place in the body where arterial and venous blood mix BEFORE reaching the heart.

📏 The 3 capillary types (know cold)

TypeEndotheliumBasement membraneFound in
ContinuousTight, no poresContinuousCNS, muscle, lung, skin
FenestratedSmall pores (60–80 nm), often WITH diaphragmContinuousGut villi, kidney peritubular, endocrine glands
Discontinuous (sinusoidal)Large gaps (100–200 nm), NO diaphragmAbsent / discontinuousLiver, Spleen, Bone marrow
⚡ Lightning HY:
  • Mnemonic for sinusoidal organs = "LSB": Liver, Spleen, Bone marrow. All three need to move big stuff (whole cells, proteins).
  • Kidney glomerulus = the opposite: fenestrated endothelium BUT thick GBM → proteins blocked. Liver = fenestrated + no BM → proteins pass.

🧠 Why no basement membrane? (the "so what")

Liver's job = filter plasma. A basement membrane would block albumin, clotting factors, and lipoproteins from reaching hepatocytes. So evolution just... deleted it. Plasma flows through fenestrae → space of Disse → bathes hepatocyte microvilli directly.

Side effect: the liver makes a ton of lymph — roughly 25–50% of thoracic duct lymph is born in the space of Disse. (This is why cirrhotics get massive ascites.)

⚠️ EXAM TRAP: "Fenestrated" isn't enough — say "discontinuous" ▼

Listen to the stem's wording:

  • "Fenestrae WITH diaphragm + continuous BM" → classic fenestrated (kidney peritubular, gut villi). NOT liver.
  • "Fenestrae WITHOUT diaphragm + discontinuous/absent BM" → sinusoid (Liver, Spleen, BM).
  • "Capillary that lets albumin and chylomicron remnants pass" → sinusoid.

▼ 2. Kupffer cells — the resident macrophages

⭐ Kupffer cells = the liver's bouncers. They're tissue-resident macrophages that live INSIDE the sinusoid lumen, clinging to the endothelium. They make up ~80–90% of all tissue macrophages in the body.

🔍 Where & how to spot them

  • Location: INSIDE sinusoid lumen, stuck to endothelium. (NOT in space of Disse — that's Ito.)
  • Look: large, irregular, pseudopodia reaching into lumen; cytoplasm full of junk they've eaten (brown hemosiderin, lipofuscin, bacteria).
  • Signature stain: trypan blue → they gulp it and turn deep blue.
  • Origin: yolk-sac progenitors; they self-renew locally (unlike monocyte-derived macrophages).

💎 Their 4 core jobs

  1. Eat old RBCs → Hb → heme → biliverdin → unconjugated (indirect) bilirubin. This is the main source of the body's bilirubin — hepatocytes then conjugate it.
  2. Trap gut bacteria & LPS. Portal vein delivers bacteria from gut → Kupffer cells strip them out before blood reaches the heart. When Kupffer cells fail (cirrhosis) → spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP).
  3. Present antigen & secrete cytokines — TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6 when activated.
  4. Recycle iron — store as ferritin/hemosiderin (stand-out finding in hemochromatosis).
⚡ Lightning HY — 3 board-favorite stories:
  • Jaundice from hemolysis: unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia → Kupffer & splenic macrophages are overwhelmed by dying RBCs.
  • Most bilirubin is made by Kupffer cells, then conjugated by hepatocytes (UGT1A1), then dumped into bile.
  • Alcoholic liver disease: leaky gut → portal LPS → Kupffer activation → TNF-α → hepatocyte apoptosis + Ito cell activation. Kupffer cells are the ignition switch.
🧠 Mnemonic: "KUPFFER KLEANS"
▼

Kupffer KLEANS:

  • K — Kills bacteria (gut LPS)
  • L — Lyses old RBCs
  • E — Endotoxin clearance
  • A — Antigen presentation
  • N — Necrotic debris
  • S — Stores iron
⚠️ EXAM TRAP: Don't put Kupffer cells in the space of Disse ▼

One-line anchor: "Kupffer = Kin to blood. Ito = In the space."

  • Kupffer cells → inside the sinusoid lumen (intravascular).
  • Ito cells → outside the endothelium, in the space of Disse (perivascular).
  • Swapping these is the #1 test trap on this LO.

▼ 3. Space of Disse — the perisinusoidal space

⭐ Think of it as the liver's mezzanine. The space of Disse is the gap between the sinusoid (blood) and the hepatocyte plate (cells). It's where plasma soaks hepatocyte microvilli, where lymph is born, and where cirrhosis starts.

🔍 Anatomy in 4 bullets

  • Location: between sinusoidal endothelium (top) and hepatocyte basolateral surface (bottom).
  • Contents: leaked plasma + reticulin (type III collagen) + a few Ito cells. No RBCs (fenestrae are too small for a 7 μm cell).
  • Surface area boost: hepatocyte microvilli stick into this space — maximizing exchange.
  • What DOES get in: albumin, chylomicron remnants, lipoproteins, drugs, bile acids returning from the gut.

💎 4 functions (memorize this list)

  1. Plasma ↔ hepatocyte exchange. Hepatocytes absorb nutrients/drugs/toxins here and dump out albumin, clotting factors, VLDL.
  2. Lymph factory. 25–50% of thoracic duct lymph is made here. Route: space of Disse → spaces of Mall (periportal) → lymphatics in the triad.
  3. Vitamin A storage (via Ito cells — see next section).
  4. Fibrosis HQ. Activated Ito cells deposit type I collagen here → capillarization → cirrhosis.

🩺 Clinical Correlates

  • Ascites in cirrhosis: fibrosis blocks outflow → sinusoid pressure ↑ → more plasma forced into Disse → lymphatics overwhelmed → lymph weeps off the liver capsule into the peritoneum. Hence SAAG ≥ 1.1 ("portal hypertensive" ascites).
  • Nutmeg liver (right heart failure): central venous pressure ↑ backs up → central veins + sinusoids dilate → Disse floods → classic gross pattern of red centrilobular spots + pale periportal tissue.
⚡ Lightning HY:
  • The liver is the major source of body lymph. "Gut villi make all the lymph" is a trap — the liver contributes comparably via the space of Disse.
⚠️ EXAM TRAP: "Space of Disse" vs. "Bile canaliculus" ▼

Opposite poles of the same hepatocyte — don't mix them up:

  • Space of Disse = basolateral side. Plasma IN ↔ hepatocyte products OUT to blood. Flows toward central vein.
  • Bile canaliculus = apical side. Bile OUT. Flows toward portal triad.
  • Quick check: plasma/proteins → Disse. Bile → canaliculus. Opposite directions, opposite sides.

▼ 4. Ito cells (hepatic stellate cells) — vitamin A storage & cirrhosis

⭐ The Ito cell has a Jekyll-and-Hyde life. Quiet → stores vitamin A. Angry → turns into a myofibroblast and drives cirrhosis. Aliases: hepatic stellate cell, HSC, fat-storing cell, lipocyte.

💋 Quiet (healthy) Ito cell

  • Location: space of Disse.
  • Shape: small, star-shaped (stellate), loaded with cytoplasmic lipid droplets. Hard to see on H&E; autofluoresces under UV (retinyl esters).
  • Main job: stores 50–80% of the body's vitamin A as retinyl esters. Also stashes other fat-soluble vitamins.
  • Side job: secretes small amounts of type III collagen (reticulin) — normal scaffolding.

🔥 Activated (angry) Ito cell — cirrhosis engine

How it gets activated: chronic hepatocyte injury (alcohol, HBV/HCV, NASH, drugs) → Kupffer cells release TGF-β + PDGF → Ito cell transforms into a myofibroblast.

What the activated cell does:

  • Dumps its vitamin A droplets; proliferates, migrates, contracts.
  • Secretes type I collagen into the space of Disse (replacing the delicate type III reticulin).
  • Drives capillarization: loss of fenestrae + new basement membrane.
  • End result: cirrhosis → portal hypertension → liver failure.
⚡ Lightning HY — 3 things to remember:
  • Ito cell = THE cell of hepatic fibrosis. Anti-fibrotic therapy targets it (e.g., experimental PPAR-γ agonists).
  • Hypervitaminosis A (bodybuilder abusing retinoids) → Ito cell overload → activation → fibrosis. Classic Step 1 vignette.
  • Marker: activated stellate cells stain positive for α-SMA (smooth muscle actin). Buzzword → pick stellate cell.
🧠 Mnemonic: "ITO stores OIL on the OUTSIDE"
▼

Ito = In The Outside (space of Disse, behind endothelium). Stores Oil (vitamin A lipid droplets).

Kupffer = Kaptured in the lumen (intravascular). Eats bacteria + RBCs.

→ If in doubt on a stem: stores fat? Ito. Eats stuff? Kupffer.

⚠️ EXAM TRAP: Cirrhosis isn't just hepatocytes ▼

Cirrhosis has TWO ingredients: (1) regenerative hepatocyte nodules + (2) bridging fibrosis from activated Ito cells. No Ito cell activation → no cirrhosis.

  • "Type I collagen in the perisinusoidal space" → Ito / stellate cell (NOT fibroblast, NOT Kupffer).
  • Don't confuse with portal fibroblasts — those live in the triad and drive biliary fibrosis.

▼ 6. Capillarization of sinusoids — the pathology bridge

⭐ Capillarization = the sinusoid becomes a boring capillary. It loses its fenestrae AND grows a brand-new basement membrane. Plasma can no longer soak hepatocyte microvilli → the liver loses the very feature that made it a liver.

🔄 The 6-step cascade

  1. Injury (alcohol, HBV/HCV, NASH) damages hepatocytes → DAMPs released.
  2. Kupffer cells activate → TGF-β, PDGF, TNF-α, IL-1.
  3. Ito cells transform into α-SMA⁺ myofibroblasts.
  4. Myofibroblasts dump type I collagen + fibronectin + laminin into the space of Disse → new BM forms.
  5. Fenestrae disappear ("defenestration").
  6. Sinusoid now = generic capillary. Plasma-hepatocyte contact is lost.

🚨 4 clinical consequences

  • Hepatocytes starve & stop working: ↓ albumin, ↓ clotting factors, ↓ drug/bilirubin clearance.
  • Portal hypertension: fibrotic sinusoids resist flow → backpressure → varices, splenomegaly, ascites.
  • Cirrhosis = regenerative nodules + bridging fibrosis.
  • HCC risk skyrockets. Cirrhosis is the #1 risk factor for hepatocellular carcinoma.
⚡ Lightning HY:
  • Capillarization is the unifying mechanism of decompensated cirrhosis. Lost fenestrae + new BM = failed exchange.
  • Biopsy signature: reticulin collapse (lost type III scaffold) + trichrome-positive bridging fibrosis = cirrhosis.

▼ 1. Gallbladder overview — a reservoir, not a producer

⭐ The gallbladder is a smart balloon, not a factory. Hepatocytes make bile. The gallbladder just STORES it, CONCENTRATES it (5–20×), and SQUEEZES it out when you eat.

🔍 Quick anatomy

  • Sits on: inferior surface of the right hepatic lobe, in the gallbladder fossa.
  • Parts: fundus → body → neck → cystic duct.
  • Capacity: 30–50 mL. Concentrates bile 5–20× by reabsorbing water/electrolytes.
  • Trigger hormone: CCK (cholecystokinin), made by duodenal I cells in response to fat/AA.

▼ 2. Mucosa — absorber & mucus secretor

⭐ The mucosa does ONE big job: sucks water out of bile to concentrate it. Simple columnar + microvilli + heavily folded (rugae that flatten when the bladder fills).

🔍 Epithelium at a glance

  • Single layer of tall columnar cells. Oval basal nuclei. Light pink cytoplasm.
  • Apical microvilli — give a pale pink striated edge on H&E. Shorter than small intestinal brush border.
  • Tight junctions apically — regulate paracellular water flow (dominant route in GB).
  • Basolateral Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase — the engine of bile concentration (details in Section 5).
  • Mucus-secreting cells (especially near the neck) — protect against concentrated bile.

💪 How to ID it on a slide

Unique fingerprint: simple columnar + deeply folded + NO goblet cells + NO muscularis mucosae + NO submucosa. If all 5 are present → gallbladder. Full stop.

  • Not small intestine: no villi, goblet cells, crypts, Paneth cells.
  • Not stomach: no pits, no glands.

💊 Rokitansky-Aschoff sinuses

Mucosa punches through into the muscularis — like hernia pouches in the wall. Common in chronic cholecystitis, and they trap stones + bacteria (keeping infection alive).

Buzzword: "mucosal outpouching extending into the muscular wall" → Rokitansky-Aschoff sinus.

⚠️ EXAM TRAP: Goblet cells DO NOT belong here ▼
  • Normal gallbladder → NO goblet cells. Mucus comes from the columnar cells themselves + mucous glands near the neck.
  • Goblet cells on a gallbladder biopsy = intestinal metaplasia (chronic irritation, potentially precancerous).

▼ 3. Muscularis — the three-direction smooth muscle

⭐ Unlike the gut, the gallbladder doesn't peristalsize — it just squeezes. So the muscle isn't layered; it's a jumbled mesh of longitudinal + circular + oblique fibers all at once.

🔍 Key features

  • Smooth muscle only. (No skeletal muscle anywhere in the gallbladder.)
  • No inner-circular/outer-longitudinal layers — 3-direction interweave instead. This is the hallmark.
  • Thin compared to gut muscularis externa — it just needs to squeeze, not push stuff along.
  • Perimuscular CT layer (vessels, lymphatics, nerves) sits OUTSIDE the muscularis. Don't mistake this for a submucosa — the gallbladder doesn't have one.

💎 Function — the bile ejection reflex

  • CCK (+ vagal input) → contraction → bile out via cystic duct → CBD → duodenum.
  • CCK simultaneously relaxes the sphincter of Oddi — squeeze AND open. Coordinated release.
  • Sympathetic input inhibits contraction (fight-or-flight → digestion on pause).

▼ 4. Outer layer — adventitia OR serosa, depending on where you are

⭐ Plot twist: the gallbladder has BOTH adventitia AND serosa — on different faces of the same organ. Exam writers love this.

  • Only serosa: stomach, small intestine, most of large intestine.
  • Both: gallbladder; rectum (upper = serosa, lower = adventitia).
⚠️ EXAM TRAP: Serosa is NOT just adventitia with a hat ▼
  • Serosa = mesothelium (simple squamous) + thin loose CT. The mesothelial layer is the defining feature.
  • Adventitia = dense irregular CT, no mesothelium.
  • They're architecturally different layers — not additive.

▼ 5. Bile concentration mechanism — iso-osmotic absorption

⭐ How the GB concentrates bile 5–20×: pump Na⁺ out of the lumen → Cl⁻ follows (charge) → water follows (osmosis). Salt water leaves; bile salts, cholesterol, and bilirubin stay behind.

🔄 4-step mechanism

  1. Basolateral Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase pumps Na⁺ out of the cell → creates low intracellular [Na⁺].
  2. Apical NHE + Na⁺-coupled transporters pull Na⁺ from the bile lumen into the cell (down gradient).
  3. Cl⁻ follows (paracellular); water follows (aquaporins + paracellular leak).
  4. Net: isotonic salt water reabsorbed → bile salts/cholesterol/lecithin/bilirubin left behind → 5–20× concentration.

⚠️ EXAM TRAP: GB does NOT secrete bile ▼

Who does what:

  • Hepatocytes → MAKE bile.
  • Cholangiocytes → ADD bicarb + water (via CFTR, in response to secretin).
  • Gallbladder → ABSORBS salt/water to CONCENTRATE bile. Never "secretes."

▼ 6. CCK — the master hormone of gallbladder emptying

⭐ CCK is the "fat is here, send digestion" hormone. Made by I cells (duodenum + jejunum) in response to fatty acids and amino acids. Master conductor of the postprandial orchestra.

💎 The 5 jobs of CCK

  1. Contracts the gallbladder → bile shoots out.
  2. Relaxes the sphincter of Oddi → bile enters duodenum. (Squeeze + open, simultaneously.)
  3. Kicks pancreatic acinar cells → zymogen/enzyme secretion.
  4. Slows gastric emptying → buys the intestine time to handle fat.
  5. Sends satiety signal to hypothalamus (via vagal afferents) → "you're full."
🧠 Mnemonic: "CCK = Contract, Constrict, Kick"
▼
  • Contract the gallbladder
  • Constrict (slow) gastric emptying
  • Kick out pancreatic enzymes
  • Bonus: relaxes sphincter of Oddi (the "O")
⚠️ EXAM TRAP: CCK vs. Secretin — don't swap them ▼

Two different hormones, two different triggers, two different jobs:

  • CCK (I cells) ← fat + AAs → gallbladder contract + pancreatic ENZYMES.
  • Secretin (S cells) ← acid (low pH) → pancreatic/biliary BICARB + water.
  • Partnership: secretin makes the fluid; CCK dumps the enzymes into it.

🔥 Acute cholecystitis — the pathway

Stone lodges in cystic duct → bile stasis → mucosal injury → inflammation → bacterial superinfection (E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterococcus).

  • Presentation: RUQ pain → right shoulder, fever, leukocytosis.
  • Murphy's sign: patient stops inhaling mid-breath on RUQ palpation.
  • Histology: edematous inflamed mucosa, neutrophils, fibrinous exudate on serosa.

👯 Porcelain gallbladder

  • Chronic cholecystitis → dystrophic calcification of the wall → hard, blue, crackled shell (literally like porcelain).
  • Precancerous — high risk of gallbladder adenocarcinoma → prophylactic cholecystectomy usually recommended.

▼ 2. Exocrine acini — the zymogen factory

⭐ Think: enzyme factory with a safety lock. The acinus is a cluster of pyramidal cells pointing their tips inward — they make digestive enzymes but store them as inactive zymogens so the pancreas doesn't digest itself.

🔍 How to spot an acinus on a slide

Look for the "two-tone cell" — basal purple + apical pink = signature pancreatic acinar cell.

  • Shape: oval/flask-like cluster, wrapped in thin reticular CT.
  • Cells: pyramidal (wide base, narrow tip) — tips all point toward a tiny central lumen.
  • Nucleus: round, single, parked basally.
  • Basal cytoplasm — PURPLE (basophilic): stuffed with rough ER → "protein-synthesis factory floor."
  • Apical cytoplasm — PINK (eosinophilic): stuffed with zymogen granules → the "packaged products ready to ship."
  • Central lumen: where enzymes get dumped. Tiny — easy to miss.

Memory hook: "Basal Buys protein, Apical Ships it."

Quick rule: Striated ducts → salivary. Centroacinar + islets → pancreas.

⭐ Duct job: dilute the enzymes + alkalinize them. From the acinus outward, 4 duct levels — starting with centroacinar cells (the "pale cells poking INTO the acinus" — found nowhere else in the body). Duct cells add HCO₃⁻ + H₂O under secretin.

▼ 5. Islets of Langerhans — the endocrine pancreas

⭐ "Pale islands in a purple sea." Islets of Langerhans = compact clusters of endocrine cells floating in exocrine tissue. ~1 million per pancreas. Concentrated in the tail (F cells mostly in head).

🔍 Spotting an islet on H&E

  • Appearance: lighter-staining than the dark purple acini around them → "pale islands."
  • Size/shape: round-ish, ~100–200 μm.
  • Border: thin reticular (type III collagen) capsule.
  • Cells: smaller, lighter than acinar cells; arranged in irregular cords with fenestrated capillaries running between them (endocrine = direct blood access).

💩 The insulo-acinar portal system — why blood flow matters

Blood flows through the islet in a specific order — this is a paracrine hack.

  1. Arteriole enters the islet.
  2. Hits β cells first (center) — insulin gets released into the capillary.
  3. Then hits α/δ/F cells (periphery) — insulin-rich blood brakes their glucagon output before leaving.
  4. Efferent blood exits → feeds the nearby exocrine acini → islet hormones tune acinar enzyme output ("insulo-acinar portal system").
⚡ Lightning High-Yield:
  • β → α/δ flow direction is why β cells in the center can brake α cells in the periphery.
  • Type 1 DM logic: destroy β cells → lose insulin's brake on α cells → unrestrained glucagon → DKA.

💊 β-cell insulin release — the 5-step cascade

Memorize this — it shows up on Step 1 constantly. High glucose → the following chain:

  1. Glucose in: enters β cell via GLUT2 (high-Km, NOT insulin-dependent — so β cells always sense blood glucose).
  2. Glucokinase = the glucose sensor → glycolysis → ATP generated.
  3. ↑ ATP/ADP → closes KATP channels → membrane depolarizes.
  4. Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open → Ca²⁺ rushes in.
  5. Insulin + C-peptide granules exocytose (1:1 ratio — C-peptide = proof the insulin is endogenous).
⚡ Lightning High-Yield:
  • Sulfonylureas (glipizide, glyburide) → close KATP directly → force insulin out. Side effects: hypoglycemia, weight gain.
  • Diazoxide → opens KATP → blocks insulin release. Used for insulinoma.
  • C-peptide = the detective's clue:
    • HIGH in T2DM, insulinoma, sulfonylurea abuse
    • LOW/ABSENT with exogenous insulin injection (factitious hypoglycemia) or T1DM
⚠️ TRAP: Which cells are central? ▼

β is Biggest and in the Belly (center). Everything else (α, δ, F) = peripheral.

  • Center: β → insulin
  • Periphery: α (glucagon), δ (somatostatin), F (PP)

Why it matters: blood flows center → periphery, so β-cell insulin can brake α-cell glucagon via paracrine action (insulo-acinar portal system).

▼ 8. Pathology — pancreatitis, diabetes, insulinoma, pancreatic cancer

⭐ The Big 4: pancreatitis + pancreatic cancer (exocrine), diabetes (endocrine), insulinoma (bridge). These show up constantly.

🚨 Acute pancreatitis — "the pancreas eats itself"

Core mechanism: trypsinogen activates inside the pancreas → trypsin activates everything else → auto-digestion → inflammation cascade.

  • Causes (I GET SMASHED): Idiopathic, Gallstones, Ethanol (top 2 causes), Trauma, Steroids, Mumps, Autoimmune, Scorpion sting, Hypercalcemia/Hypertriglyceridemia, ERCP, Drugs (sulfa, didanosine, azathioprine).
  • Histology: necrotic acinar cells, neutrophils, edema, fat necrosis (Ca²⁺ soaps with free fatty acids → chalky-white "saponification" foci), ± hemorrhage.
  • Labs: ↑ amylase (early, less specific) + ↑ lipase (more specific, stays up longer).
  • Complications:
    • Pseudocyst — granulation tissue wall (NO epithelial lining = that's what makes it "pseudo")
    • ARDS, hypocalcemia (Ca²⁺ consumed in soaps), abscess, chronic pancreatitis

💉 Diabetes — know the histology signatures

  • Type 1 DM (autoimmune, β-cell killed):
    • Early: insulitis = lymphocytes around islets
    • Late: β cells gone, α/δ/F intact → shrunken islets
    • Associations: HLA-DR3/DR4, anti-GAD65 antibodies
  • Type 2 DM (resistance + β-cell fatigue):
    • Islet amyloid (IAPP / amylin) = pink, amorphous, Congo-red-positive deposits inside islets
    • β-cell mass variably reduced
  • Chronic complications (both types): microangiopathy (retinopathy, nephropathy → Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules), macrovascular atherosclerosis, peripheral neuropathy.
⚡ Lightning High-Yield:
  • "Pink amorphous deposits in islets" + older adult + hyperglycemia → T2DM (islet amyloid).
  • "Lymphocytes around islets" + young patient + DKA → T1DM (insulitis).

🍌 Islet cell tumors — "a tumor of every cell"

Pattern: each islet cell can turn into a functional tumor — the clinical picture = an overdose of that cell's hormone.

  • Insulinoma (β): most common. Whipple's triad → (1) hypoglycemia symptoms, (2) low glucose, (3) relief with glucose. ↑ insulin, ↑ C-peptide, ↑ proinsulin.
  • Glucagonoma (α): hyperglycemia + weight loss + necrolytic migratory erythema (the classic rash).
  • Somatostatinoma (δ): inhibits everything → diabetes + steatorrhea + gallstones + achlorhydria.
  • Gastrinoma / Zollinger-Ellison: recurrent peptic ulcers + diarrhea. Often in the "gastrinoma triangle" (including pancreatic head).
  • VIPoma (Verner-Morrison): WDHA = Watery Diarrhea + Hypokalemia + Achlorhydria.
  • All can be part of MEN1 (3 P's: Parathyroid, Pituitary, Pancreas).
⚡ Lightning High-Yield — insulinoma vs. factitious hypoglycemia:
  • Insulinoma: ↑ insulin + ↑ C-peptide
  • Factitious (injection): ↑ insulin + ↓ C-peptide

🔥 Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma — the grim one

Origin: pancreatic duct cells (NOT acinar, NOT islet). ⚠️ Very easy to miss this point.

  • Location: 60–70% in head → blocks common bile duct → painless jaundice + Courvoisier sign (palpable, nontender gallbladder).
  • Risk factors: smoking (biggest modifiable), chronic pancreatitis, age > 50, African ancestry, family history, BRCA2, KRAS mutations in >90%.
  • Histology: moderately/poorly differentiated glands embedded in dense desmoplastic stroma (signature stromal reaction).
  • Classic clinical clues:
    • Trousseau syndrome (migratory thrombophlebitis)
    • Weight loss + epigastric pain radiating to the back
    • New-onset diabetes in an older adult → screen for pancreatic cancer
  • Tumor marker: CA 19-9.
  • Prognosis: ~10% 5-year survival. Usually presents late because the pancreas is retroperitoneal.

Q1.

A 68-year-old man with severe right heart failure develops hepatic congestion. His liver biopsy shows red, dilated central veins with surrounding hepatocyte necrosis, while the hepatocytes closest to the portal triads appear relatively preserved. Which hepatic lobule zone is most affected?

  • (A) Zone 1 (periportal)
  • (B) Zone 2 (intermediate)
  • (C) Zone 3 (centrilobular)
  • (D) Portal triad
  • (E) Space of Disse
Correct Answer: C — Zone 3 (centrilobular)
Zone 3 hepatocytes are closest to the central vein and farthest from the oxygen-delivering portal triad. They receive blood only after Zones 1 and 2 have already extracted oxygen, so they are chronically the lowest-PO₂ hepatocytes. When central venous pressure rises (right HF, Budd-Chiari), blood backs up into centrilobular sinusoids → Zone 3 ischemia + necrosis. Gross appearance: "nutmeg liver" (alternating red congested centrilobular and pale periportal zones). A: Zone 1 is best oxygenated, last to die from ischemia. B: Zone 2 is intermediate. D: The portal triad is an anatomic landmark, not a zone. E: Space of Disse is the perisinusoidal space, not a "zone."

Q2.

A researcher injects a fluorescent dye into the hepatic artery of a rat. Which structure will the dye reach last on its journey through the liver before returning to systemic circulation?

  • (A) Sinusoid
  • (B) Space of Disse
  • (C) Hepatic vein
  • (D) Central vein
  • (E) Inferior vena cava
Correct Answer: E — Inferior vena cava
Hepatic blood flow: hepatic artery + portal vein → sinusoid → (some plasma leaks into space of Disse) → central vein → hepatic veins → inferior vena cava → right atrium. The IVC is the last hepatic-related structure before the heart. A, B, C, D are all upstream of the IVC. Mnemonic: blood flow in the liver is centripetal — from the peripheral triads to the central vein.

Q3.

A pathologist stains a liver biopsy with silver (reticulin) and sees a delicate latticework of black fibers running along hepatocyte plates. Which type of collagen does this stain highlight, and what functional role does it serve?

  • (A) Type I collagen — tensile scar formation
  • (B) Type II collagen — cartilage-like cushioning
  • (C) Type III collagen — reticular support of hepatocyte plates
  • (D) Type IV collagen — basement membrane beneath sinusoidal endothelium
  • (E) Elastin — vessel wall recoil
Correct Answer: C — Type III collagen, reticular support of hepatocyte plates
Silver stains reticulin fibers, which are type III collagen. In the liver, reticulin forms a delicate scaffold that supports the 1-cell-thick hepatocyte plates. Loss of this scaffold is a diagnostic feature of hepatocellular carcinoma (plates expand to 3+ cells thick; reticulin collapses). A: Type I collagen is deposited by activated stellate cells in cirrhosis — not the normal reticular scaffold. D: Sinusoidal endothelium normally lacks a continuous basement membrane. E: Elastin isn't a major hepatic component.

Q4.

A medical student is memorizing the portal triad. She correctly identifies the portal venule by its thin wall and large lumen. Which of the following is also a constant component of a portal triad?

  • (A) Central vein
  • (B) Sinusoid
  • (C) Bile canaliculus
  • (D) Hepatic arteriole
  • (E) Space of Disse
Correct Answer: D — Hepatic arteriole
The portal triad contains: (1) hepatic arteriole, (2) portal venule, (3) bile ductule (lined by cholangiocytes — NOT the canaliculus), plus lymphatics + nerves. Mnemonic A-V-D = Artery, Vein, Duct. A: Central vein is in the middle of the lobule, opposite the triads. B: Sinusoids carry blood between triads and central veins, not within triads. C: Bile canaliculi are microscopic grooves between hepatocytes, not in the triad; they drain INTO the bile ductules OF the triad. E: Space of Disse is the perisinusoidal space.

Q5.

A 45-year-old with chronic alcohol use is found to have fatty liver disease. The histology shows macrovesicular steatosis most prominent around the central veins. Which zone of the hepatic acinus is predicted to be least affected?

  • (A) Zone 1
  • (B) Zone 2
  • (C) Zone 3
  • (D) All zones equally
  • (E) Bile canaliculi
Correct Answer: A — Zone 1
Alcohol metabolism (via ADH and CYP2E1) happens primarily in Zone 3 hepatocytes, which are also the lowest in O₂ — perfect conditions for fatty change. Zone 1 (periportal) has the highest oxygen and glycogen, is metabolically skewed toward gluconeogenesis and plasma protein synthesis, and is relatively spared early in alcoholic liver disease. B: Zone 2 is intermediate. C: Zone 3 is MOST affected. D: Alcoholic fatty liver shows a strong zonal distribution, not uniform. E: Bile canaliculi are intercellular channels, not zones.

Q6.

A 62-year-old develops cholangitis. Which epithelial lining cell secretes bicarbonate-rich fluid in response to secretin and lines the bile ducts?

  • (A) Hepatocyte
  • (B) Kupffer cell
  • (C) Cholangiocyte
  • (D) Ito cell
  • (E) Sinusoidal endothelial cell
Correct Answer: C — Cholangiocyte
Cholangiocytes are the cuboidal-to-columnar epithelial cells lining bile ductules and ducts. Under secretin control, they secrete HCO₃⁻ + water via apical CFTR and Cl⁻/HCO₃⁻ exchangers — this is why bile is alkaline. Loss of CFTR in cystic fibrosis leads to thick viscous bile and can cause CF-related liver disease. A: Hepatocytes make bile acids/bilirubin but don't line ducts. B: Kupffer cells are sinusoidal macrophages. D: Ito cells store vitamin A in the space of Disse. E: Sinusoidal endothelium is fenestrated, not duct lining.

LO 3: Sinusoids & Space of Disse

Q7.

A researcher compares hepatic sinusoids to continuous capillaries of the brain. Which feature is unique to hepatic sinusoids?

  • (A) Tight junctions between endothelial cells
  • (B) Pericytes around the outside
  • (C) Discontinuous fenestrations and absent basement membrane
  • (D) Active transport of glucose
  • (E) Release of nitric oxide
Correct Answer: C — Discontinuous fenestrations and absent basement membrane
The sinusoid's signature is its endothelium — large, unbridled fenestrae (no diaphragm) AND a missing or discontinuous basement membrane. This architecture permits plasma (including large proteins like albumin and chylomicron remnants) to bathe hepatocyte microvilli. Continuous capillaries (brain, muscle) have tight junctions, complete BM, and no fenestrae. A, E: Not unique. B: Pericytes are around continuous capillaries; stellate cells (NOT pericytes) reside in the space of Disse. D: Not a uniquely sinusoidal feature.

Q8.

A patient with hereditary spherocytosis undergoes a liver biopsy. Within the sinusoids, numerous large cells are identified phagocytosing fragmented red blood cells. What cell is this, and what is the most immediate biochemical product of the RBC phagocytosis?

  • (A) Ito cell — vitamin A release
  • (B) Kupffer cell — unconjugated (indirect) bilirubin
  • (C) Hepatocyte — conjugated (direct) bilirubin
  • (D) Cholangiocyte — bile acids
  • (E) Sinusoidal endothelial cell — albumin
Correct Answer: B — Kupffer cell; unconjugated bilirubin
Kupffer cells are the intrasinusoidal macrophages that phagocytose senescent RBCs → heme → biliverdin → unconjugated (indirect) bilirubin (lipid-soluble, bound to albumin). Hepatocytes then take up unconjugated bilirubin, conjugate it to glucuronic acid via UGT1A1, and secrete conjugated (direct) bilirubin into bile. Therefore, the immediate product of Kupffer phagocytosis is UNconjugated bilirubin — conjugation happens next, in hepatocytes. A: Ito cells store vitamin A; they don't phagocytose RBCs. C: Hepatocytes make conjugated bilirubin but don't do the initial phagocytosis. D, E: Wrong cell functions.

Q9.

A 58-year-old bodybuilder abusing vitamin A supplements presents with jaundice and ascites. Biopsy shows bridging fibrosis with type I collagen deposition in the perisinusoidal space. Which cell type is most directly responsible for the collagen deposition?

  • (A) Hepatocyte
  • (B) Kupffer cell
  • (C) Hepatic stellate (Ito) cell
  • (D) Portal fibroblast
  • (E) Sinusoidal endothelial cell
Correct Answer: C — Hepatic stellate (Ito) cell
Chronic hypervitaminosis A overloads Ito cells (they normally store retinoids in lipid droplets), eventually activating them. Activated stellate cells lose their lipid droplets, transdifferentiate into α-SMA⁺ myofibroblasts, and deposit type I collagen in the space of Disse → capillarization + cirrhosis. B: Kupffer cells release cytokines (TGF-β, PDGF) that activate stellate cells but don't themselves deposit collagen. D: Portal fibroblasts contribute to biliary fibrosis, not the perisinusoidal space. A, E: Not collagen-producing.

Q10.

A patient with cirrhosis develops high-SAAG ascites. Which mechanism best explains the fluid weeping from the liver capsule into the peritoneum?

  • (A) Loss of plasma oncotic pressure only
  • (B) Portal hypertension drives plasma into the space of Disse faster than hepatic lymphatics can drain it
  • (C) Direct leak from hepatic veins
  • (D) Kupffer cell rupture releases cytokines into peritoneum
  • (E) Autoimmune destruction of peritoneal mesothelium
Correct Answer: B — Portal hypertension drives plasma into the space of Disse faster than lymphatics can drain it
Cirrhotic fibrosis + portal HTN → high sinusoidal hydrostatic pressure forces plasma through remaining fenestrae into space of Disse → overwhelms spaces of Mall / hepatic lymphatics → protein-rich lymph weeps from the liver surface into the peritoneum. This explains the high-SAAG (serum-ascites albumin gradient ≥ 1.1) signature. A: Hypoalbuminemia contributes but isn't the primary driver. C: Hepatic veins don't leak directly. The absence of which feature is most characteristic of the gallbladder vs. the small intestine?

Q12.

A medical student is asked: "How does the gallbladder concentrate bile?" The best answer is:

  • (A) Adds bile salts
  • (B) Active secretion of water into the lumen
  • (C) Active absorption of Na⁺ (via basolateral Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase) drives passive absorption of Cl⁻ and water

A 42-year-old obese woman presents with RUQ pain 30 minutes after eating a fatty meal. Ultrasound shows gallstones. What hormone, released from which duodenal cell type, is responsible for the gallbladder contraction that precipitated her pain?

Fatty acids and amino acids in the duodenum stimulate I cells to release CCK, which (1) contracts the gallbladder, (2) relaxes the sphincter of Oddi, (3) stimulates pancreatic enzyme secretion, and (4) slows gastric emptying. If a stone is in the cystic duct when this contraction occurs, the elevated pressure causes biliary colic. A: Secretin drives pancreatic HCO₃⁻. B: Gastrin drives gastric acid. A 34-year-old man with Crohn's disease involving the terminal ileum develops gallstones. Which stone type is most likely, and why?

Q15.

On a pancreas histology slide, a student identifies pyramidal cells with intensely basophilic basal cytoplasm and eosinophilic apical granules surrounding a central lumen. Small, pale-staining cells with euchromatic nuclei are seen within the lumen, projecting inward. What are these small pale cells, and what is their function?

Q18.

A 30-year-old woman presents with recurrent episodes of dizziness, sweating, and confusion. Her blood glucose during an episode is 40 mg/dL; the symptoms resolve with IV dextrose. Labs during a fasting test: high insulin, high C-peptide, and high proinsulin. What is the most likely diagnosis and why is C-peptide the key discriminator?

  • (A) Factitious hypoglycemia — exogenous insulin gives high insulin but LOW C-peptide
  • (B) Insulinoma — autonomous β-cell tumor gives high insulin AND high C-peptide (both endogenous)
  • (C) Glucagonoma — would cause hyperglycemia, not hypoglycemia
  • (D) Somatostatinoma — would suppress insulin, not elevate it
  • (E) Addison's disease — would show low cortisol, not high insulin
Correct Answer: B — Insulinoma
Insulinoma is a β-cell tumor that autonomously secretes insulin, causing Whipple's triad (symptomatic hypoglycemia + documented low glucose + relief with glucose). Because endogenous β cells cleave proinsulin → insulin + C-peptide, C-peptide is elevated. This distinguishes insulinoma from factitious hypoglycemia (someone injecting exogenous insulin, in which C-peptide would be LOW because exogenous insulin bypasses β-cell synthesis). Answer A mis-labels the scenario — high C-peptide is NOT factitious. C, D, E describe other tumors/diseases that would produce different clinical pictures.
