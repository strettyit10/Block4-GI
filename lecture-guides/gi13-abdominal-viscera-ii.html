▼ The Liver — Anatomy & Location

Basic Facts

  • Largest internal organ — approximately 1,500 g in adults
  • Located in the right upper quadrant (RUQ), wedged beneath the diaphragm; extends to the 4th intercostal space in the midclavicular line
  • Covered by Glisson's capsule (fibrous connective tissue); visceral peritoneum covers most surfaces except the bare area

Surfaces & Relations

  • Diaphragmatic (superior/anterior) surface: convex; faces the diaphragm; crossed by falciform ligament
  • Visceral (inferior) surface: H-shaped fissures divide into lobes; right limb = gallbladder fossa + IVC groove; left limb = ligamentum teres (round lig.) fossa + ligamentum venosum fissure; crossbar = porta hepatis
  • Bare area: triangular region on diaphragmatic surface not covered by peritoneum; bounded by the coronary ligament (and its left/right triangular ligament extensions)

Key Ligaments

  • Falciform ligament: sickle-shaped; attaches anterior liver to anterior abdominal wall; contains the round ligament (ligamentum teres = obliterated umbilical vein) in its free edge
  • Coronary ligament: reflects diaphragmatic peritoneum onto liver; surrounds bare area
  • Left & right triangular ligaments: where the coronary ligament folds meet laterally
  • Lesser omentum (hepatoduodenal + hepatogastric ligaments): connects liver to stomach and duodenum; hepatoduodenal ligament contains the portal triad

Five Fluids In/Out of the Liver

DirectionVessel/DuctContent
INPortal veinNutrient-rich venous blood from GI tract (70% of hepatic blood flow)
INHepatic artery properOxygenated arterial blood (30% of blood flow, ~50% of O₂)

🎯 Step 1 High-Yield — Liver Blood Supply
  • The liver has a dual blood supply: portal vein (70%) + hepatic artery (30%)
  • Portal vein carries nutrients absorbed from the GI tract — hepatic first-pass metabolism occurs here
  • Hepatic veins drain into the IVC just below the diaphragm — important in Budd-Chiari syndrome (hepatic vein thrombosis → post-sinusoidal portal HTN)

▼ Hepatic Lobes & Couinaud Segments

Gross (Morphological) Lobes

  • Right lobe (largest) and Left lobe — divided by the falciform ligament on the anterior surface
  • Quadrate lobe: on the inferior surface, between the gallbladder fossa (right) and the ligamentum teres fissure (left); anterior to the porta hepatis
  • Caudate lobe: on the posterior surface; bounded by IVC (right), ligamentum venosum fissure (left), and porta hepatis (inferior); receives blood from both right and left branches of portal vein and hepatic artery — important because it drains directly into the IVC via multiple small veins, allowing it to remain relatively unaffected in Budd-Chiari syndrome

Couinaud Functional Segments

The functional (surgical) division is based on portal and hepatic vein distribution, not external landmarks. There are 8 segments (I–VIII), each with its own portal vein branch, hepatic artery branch, and bile duct — allowing individual segments to be resected independently.

  • Segment I: caudate lobe (posterior)
  • Segments II–IV: left lobe (II = superior left, III = inferior left, IV = quadrate = medial left)
  • Segments V–VIII: right lobe (V/VI inferior, VII/VIII superior)
  • The true functional midplane of the liver (Cantlie's line) runs from the gallbladder fossa to the IVC — not along the falciform ligament
⚠️ TRAP: Gross Lobes vs. Functional Segments ▼

The falciform ligament does NOT divide the liver into functional halves. The true surgical midplane (Cantlie's line) runs gallbladder fossa → IVC. The quadrate and caudate lobes are grossly part of the "right lobe" by external appearance, but functionally belong to the left lobe based on portal vein supply (Couinaud segments I and IV).

▼ Porta Hepatis & Hepatic Pedicle

Porta Hepatis

The porta hepatis ("gateway to the liver") is a transverse fissure on the inferior surface of the liver where the portal triad enters and bile ducts exit. Structures passing through (from anterior to posterior):

  • Right and left hepatic bile ducts (anterior)
  • Right and left branches of hepatic artery proper (middle)
  • Right and left branches of portal vein (posterior, largest)
  • Lymphatics and autonomic nerves

Mnemonic: "BD HAP" — Bile Duct, Hepatic Artery, Portal vein (anterior to posterior)

Hepatic Pedicle (Free Edge of Lesser Omentum)

The hepatoduodenal ligament (free edge of the lesser omentum) contains the portal triad as it runs from the duodenum to the porta hepatis. It forms the anterior boundary of the epiploic foramen of Winslow (entrance to lesser sac).

  • Pringle maneuver: compressing the hepatoduodenal ligament between thumb and index finger temporarily stops hepatic blood flow during surgery — used to control hemorrhage

Hepatic Arterial Supply

  • Celiac trunk → common hepatic artery → hepatic artery proper → right and left hepatic arteries
  • Cystic artery typically arises from the right hepatic artery within Calot's triangle (bounded by cystic duct, common hepatic duct, and inferior liver surface) — highly variable!
  • Variant: replaced right hepatic artery from SMA (~20% of people) — critical to identify pre-operatively
🎯 Step 1 High-Yield — Portal Triad Contents
  • Portal vein provides ~70% of hepatic blood flow but the portal blood is venous (low O₂, high nutrients)
  • Hepatic artery provides ~30% of blood flow but ~50% of O₂ supply
  • At the histological level, each portal tract contains a portal venule, hepatic arteriole, and bile ductule — surrounded by hepatocytes in hepatic lobules

▼ Portal-Systemic Anastomoses

When portal venous pressure rises (portal hypertension), blood is forced through pre-existing connections into the systemic venous system. These portosystemic anastomoses become dilated and clinically apparent.

🎯 Step 1 High-Yield — Portal Hypertension Manifestations
  • Causes of portal HTN: pre-hepatic (portal vein thrombosis), intra-hepatic (cirrhosis — most common), post-hepatic (Budd-Chiari, right heart failure)
  • TIPS (transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt) creates an artificial anastomosis between portal vein and hepatic vein within the liver parenchyma
  • Esophageal varices from the left gastric vein are the most dangerous — variceal bleeding is the most common cause of death in cirrhosis
  • Caput medusae flows away from the umbilicus (compare with IVC obstruction where flow is also upward but limited to lower abdomen)
⚠️ TRAP: Hemorrhoids in Portal Hypertension ▼

Not all hemorrhoids are from portal hypertension! Common hemorrhoids occur due to increased intra-abdominal pressure (constipation, pregnancy). Portal hypertension-related hemorrhoids involve the superior rectal vein draining to the IMV/portal system. External hemorrhoids (below dentate line) drain to the inferior rectal vein (systemic) — they are NOT portosystemic anastomoses.

▼ Biliary Ductal System

Biliary Tree Architecture

  • Bile canaliculi → bile ductules → interlobular bile ducts → right and left hepatic ducts
  • Right hepatic duct + Left hepatic duct → Common hepatic duct
  • Common hepatic duct + Cystic duct (from gallbladder) → Common bile duct (CBD)
  • CBD travels in the free edge of the lesser omentum → behind the 1st part of the duodenum → groove on posterior surface of pancreatic head → joins pancreatic duct at ampulla of Vater (hepatopancreatic ampulla) → opens at major duodenal papilla in the 2nd part of the duodenum

Clinical: ERCP

Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography (ERCP) uses an endoscope passed to the major duodenal papilla to inject contrast into the CBD and pancreatic duct, allowing visualization and therapeutic intervention (stone removal, stent placement). ERCP is used to treat choledocholithiasis (CBD stones) and biliary strictures.

Cholelithiasis & Choledocholithiasis

  • Cholelithiasis: gallstones in the gallbladder — most common in women, obese, multiparous, age >40 ("4 F's")
  • Choledocholithiasis: gallstone in the CBD → obstructs bile flow → jaundice, dark urine, pale stool
  • Gallstone at ampulla of Vater → blocks both CBD and pancreatic duct → obstructive jaundice + acute pancreatitis simultaneously
  • Charcot's triad of cholangitis: RUQ pain + fever/chills + jaundice

▼ The Gallbladder

Anatomy

  • Pear-shaped, ~50 mL capacity; lies in the gallbladder fossa on the inferior surface of the liver, between the right lobe and quadrate lobe
  • Parts: fundus (projects beyond liver edge, palpable at Murphy's point — right 9th costal cartilage at midclavicular line) → body → neck (Hartmann's pouch — a bulge where stones lodge) → cystic duct
  • Relations: contacts the 1st/2nd part of the duodenum and the transverse colon — explains cholecystoenteric fistula formation in chronic cholecystitis

Cystic Artery & Calot's Triangle

  • Cystic artery typically arises from the right hepatic artery inside Calot's triangle
  • Calot's triangle boundaries: cystic duct (inferior), common hepatic duct (medial), inferior surface of liver (superior)
  • The cystic artery is highly variable — may arise from left hepatic artery, hepatic artery proper, gastroduodenal artery, or celiac trunk; must be identified carefully during cholecystectomy to avoid injury to hepatic arteries

Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy Pitfalls

  • CBD injury — most common serious complication; misidentification of CBD as cystic duct
  • "Critical view of safety" technique: dissecting out Calot's triangle until only two structures enter the gallbladder (cystic duct + cystic artery) before clipping
  • Mirizzi syndrome: large stone in Hartmann's pouch/cystic duct externally compresses the CBD → obstructive jaundice without CBD stone
⚠️ TRAP: Murphy's Sign vs. Murphy's Point ▼

Murphy's point is the anatomical location of the gallbladder fundus (right 9th costal cartilage at midclavicular line). Murphy's sign is the clinical sign of inspiratory arrest during deep palpation at this point — positive in acute cholecystitis. A positive Murphy's sign is highly specific for acute cholecystitis, not just cholelithiasis.

▼ GIT Innervation & Pain Referral

Visceral pain from abdominal organs is referred to dermatomes corresponding to the spinal cord levels that supply those organs. This is based on the embryological gut division (foregut/midgut/hindgut):

Spinal LevelNerveGut SegmentOrgansReferred Pain Zone
T5–T9Greater splanchnicForegutStomach, liver, gallbladder, spleen, pancreas (head), duodenum (to ampulla)Epigastric
T10–T11Lesser splanchnicMidgutSmall intestine, appendix, cecum, ascending & proximal transverse colonPeriumbilical
L1–L2Lumbar splanchnicHindgutDistal transverse colon, descending & sigmoid colon, rectumSuprapubic/hypogastric

Specific Referral Patterns to Know

  • Gallbladder/biliary colic: epigastric → right shoulder tip (phrenic nerve irritation via diaphragm, C3-C5)
  • Acute pancreatitis: epigastric pain radiating to the back (pancreas lies retroperitoneal against vertebral bodies)
  • Appendicitis: begins periumbilical (T10 visceral) → shifts to RLQ (McBurney's point) as parietal peritoneum becomes inflamed (somatic pain = localizes precisely)
  • Splenic trauma: left shoulder tip pain (Kehr's sign) — blood irritates left diaphragm → phrenic nerve (C3-C5) referral

▼ Splenic Anatomy & Location

Basic Facts

  • Largest lymphoid organ in the body (~150 g in adults)
  • Located in the left upper quadrant (LUQ), deep to ribs 9–11 in the midaxillary line
  • Long axis lies along the 10th rib
  • Intraperitoneal organ — completely covered by visceral peritoneum (except at hilum)

Surfaces

  • Diaphragmatic surface: smooth convex surface; faces the diaphragm superolaterally
  • Visceral (hilar) surface: contains the splenic hilum where vessels and nerves enter; has characteristic notches on the superior border (diagnostic clue for splenomegaly on exam)
  • Visceral surface has impressions for: stomach (gastric impression), left kidney (renal impression), splenic flexure of colon (colic impression), tail of pancreas

Peritoneal Ligaments

  • Gastrosplenic (gastrolienal) ligament: connects greater curvature of stomach to splenic hilum; contains short gastric arteries and left gastroepiploic artery
  • Splenorenal (lienorenal) ligament: connects spleen to left kidney; contains the splenic artery and splenic vein (and the tail of the pancreas)
  • Both ligaments are derived from the dorsal mesogastrium

🎯 Step 1 High-Yield — Post-Splenectomy
  • After splenectomy: give vaccines against S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae type b, N. meningitidis ("SHiN" — the encapsulated organisms)
  • Blood smear post-splenectomy shows: Howell-Jolly bodies (nuclear remnants), target cells, thrombocytosis
  • OPSI (overwhelming post-splenectomy infection) — typically caused by S. pneumoniae; mortality up to 50–70%

▼ Splenic Vasculature

Arterial Supply

  • Splenic artery — largest branch of the celiac trunk; runs a tortuous course along the superior border of the pancreas
  • Enters the hilum via the splenorenal ligament
  • Branches before entering hilum: short gastric arteries (pass through gastrosplenic ligament to supply fundus of stomach) and left gastroepiploic artery (also in gastrosplenic ligament, supplies greater curvature)
  • Also gives off dorsal pancreatic and greater pancreatic (pancreatica magna) branches to supply the pancreatic body and tail

Venous Drainage

  • Splenic vein — runs posterior to the pancreatic body/tail (in a groove)
  • Joins the superior mesenteric vein (SMV) behind the neck of the pancreas to form the portal vein
  • The splenic vein also receives the inferior mesenteric vein (IMV) — either at the SMV-splenic junction or into the splenic vein directly
🎯 Step 1 High-Yield — Portal Vein Formation
  • Portal vein = SMV + Splenic vein (at the neck of the pancreas)
  • IMV drains hindgut → enters splenic vein or SMV-splenic junction
  • The portal vein runs posterior to the 1st part of the duodenum and enters the hepatoduodenal ligament ascending to the porta hepatis
  • Splenic vein thrombosis → left-sided (sinistral) portal hypertension → isolated gastric varices and splenomegaly, with normal hepatic portal pressure

▼ Clinical Correlates

Splenomegaly

  • Normal spleen is not palpable — must enlarge to 3× its normal size before it can be palpated below the left costal margin
  • When palpable: the notches on the superior border of the spleen are a diagnostic clue that the mass is the spleen (not a kidney or other mass)
  • Enlargement occurs in the direction of the right iliac fossa (following the 10th rib obliquely)
  • Causes: infections (EBV, malaria, schistosomiasis), hematologic (lymphoma, leukemia, hemolytic anemias, myelofibrosis), portal hypertension, storage diseases (Gaucher's)

Splenic Trauma

  • The spleen is the most commonly injured abdominal organ in blunt abdominal trauma
  • Ribs 9–11 fracture on the left → can directly lacerate the spleen
  • Stab wound between ribs 10–11 in the midaxillary line → enters the spleen
  • Kehr's sign: left shoulder tip pain in splenic rupture — blood tracks under the left diaphragm → irritates phrenic nerve (C3-C5) → referred pain to left shoulder
  • Management: observation (hemodynamically stable) vs. splenorrhaphy vs. splenectomy
⚠️ TRAP: Splenic vs. Renal Mass on Palpation ▼

On physical exam, a spleen and left kidney can both present as LUQ masses. Key distinguishing features: Spleen — notched superior border, moves inferomedially on inspiration, dull to percussion (no resonant bowel anterior), cannot get above it (doesn't descend below the costal margin until huge), not ballottable. Kidney — smooth, no notch, bimanually ballottable, band of resonance over it (bowel anterior), moves inferiorly on inspiration.

▼ Pancreatic Anatomy

Location & Peritoneal Relationship

  • Retroperitoneal organ (secondarily retroperitoneal) — lies posterior to the stomach in the retroperitoneal space at the level of L1–L2
  • Exception: the tail of the pancreas extends into the splenorenal ligament and is therefore intraperitoneal, reaching close to the splenic hilum
  • Extends from the C-loop of the duodenum (head) to the splenic hilum (tail) — crosses the midline at L1

Parts of the Pancreas

PartLocationKey Relations
HeadWithin C-loop of duodenum; L1–L2CBD runs in groove on posterior surface; SMA/SMV anterior; right kidney posteriorly
Uncinate processExtension of head that hooks behind the SMA/SMVSMA and SMV pass anterior to uncinate process — surgical danger zone
NeckAnterior to portal vein formation (SMV + splenic vein junction)Portal vein forms immediately posterior to neck of pancreas

TailExtends left toward splenic hilum in splenorenal ligamentOnly intraperitoneal part; splenic artery runs along superior border

Embryological Development

  • Ventral pancreatic bud: arises from the hepatic diverticulum; rotates clockwise (with the duodenum) to fuse with the dorsal bud → forms the head, uncinate process, and main pancreatic duct
  • Dorsal pancreatic bud: larger; forms the body, tail, and accessory duct
  • After rotation, the main pancreatic duct (of Wirsung) = fusion of ventral duct + distal dorsal duct; accessory duct (of Santorini) = proximal dorsal duct remnant
  • Annular pancreas: failure of ventral bud rotation → ring of pancreatic tissue encircles the duodenum → duodenal obstruction
  • Pancreas divisum: failure of ventral and dorsal ducts to fuse → most common anatomic variant; accessory duct drains most of the gland → predisposes to pancreatitis
🎯 Step 1 High-Yield — Key Pancreatic Relations
  • Portal vein forms behind the neck of the pancreas (SMV + splenic vein)
  • CBD runs in a groove on the posterior surface of the pancreatic head — why head of pancreas cancer causes obstructive jaundice
  • SMA runs behind the neck and anterior to the uncinate process — pancreatic tumors and pseudocysts can compress the SMA
  • The tail of pancreas is in the splenorenal ligament — at risk during splenectomy (inadvertent pancreatectomy)

▼ Pancreatic Duct System

Duct Architecture

  • Main pancreatic duct (duct of Wirsung): runs the length of the gland from tail to head; joins the CBD at the hepatopancreatic ampulla (ampulla of Vater) → opens at the major duodenal papilla in the 2nd part (descending) of the duodenum
  • Accessory pancreatic duct (duct of Santorini): drains the superior head; opens at the minor duodenal papilla (2 cm proximal to the major papilla)
  • The CBD travels within a groove on the posterior surface of the pancreatic head — the bile duct does not pierce the pancreatic tissue but runs in a groove along it before joining the pancreatic duct

Sphincter Complex at the Major Papilla

Three distinct sphincter components control flow into the duodenum:

  • (1) Sphincter of the bile duct: surrounds the CBD as it passes through the duodenal wall — controls bile flow independently
  • (2) Sphincter of the pancreatic duct: surrounds the pancreatic duct in the duodenal wall — controls pancreatic juice flow independently
  • (3) Hepatopancreatic sphincter (sphincter of Oddi): surrounds the hepatopancreatic ampulla within the wall of the duodenum — controls combined flow

KEY POINT: The hepatopancreatic ampulla is the narrowest point of the biliary tract — gallstones that pass the cystic duct and CBD often impact here, simultaneously obstructing both bile and pancreatic juice.

⚠️ TRAP: Where Gallstones Get Stuck ▼

Gallstones most commonly cause symptoms when they impact at: (1) the cystic duct/neck (→ biliary colic, acute cholecystitis); (2) the common bile duct (→ obstructive jaundice, cholangitis); (3) the ampulla of Vater (→ both jaundice AND pancreatitis). A stone at the ampulla blocks the shared channel for bile and pancreatic juice — this is why gallstones are one of the two most common causes of acute pancreatitis (along with alcohol).

▼ Pancreatic Vasculature

Arterial Supply

  • Head & uncinate process: supplied by both the celiac trunk (via gastroduodenal → superior pancreaticoduodenal artery) and the SMA (via inferior pancreaticoduodenal artery) — these form anterior and posterior pancreaticoduodenal arches
  • Body & tail: splenic artery branches — dorsal pancreatic artery, greater pancreatic artery (pancreatica magna), caudal pancreatic artery
  • The dual supply to the head (celiac + SMA) makes the pancreatic head relatively resistant to ischemia; also means interrupting one source may be insufficient to infarct the head

Venous Drainage

  • Pancreatic head → superior and inferior pancreaticoduodenal veins → portal vein and SMV respectively
  • Pancreatic body/tail → splenic vein (runs in groove on posterior surface of pancreas)
  • All pancreatic venous drainage ultimately reaches the portal vein

▼ Obstructive Jaundice: Anatomical Basis

Why the Pancreatic Head Causes Obstructive Jaundice

The CBD runs in a groove along the posterior surface of the pancreatic head. Any process that enlarges or distorts the pancreatic head can compress or obstruct the CBD, preventing bile from reaching the duodenum. This results in:

  • Jaundice (scleral icterus → skin yellowing) — bilirubin backed up into blood
  • Dark urine — conjugated (water-soluble) bilirubin excreted by kidneys
  • Pale/acholic stools — absence of bile/stercobilin in stool
  • Steatorrhea — if pancreatic duct also obstructed, loss of lipase → fat malabsorption
  • Pruritus — bile salt deposition in skin

Courvoisier's Law & Pancreatic Cancer

  • Courvoisier's law: painless obstructive jaundice + palpable (distended) gallbladder = pancreatic cancer until proven otherwise
  • Rationale: in gallstone disease, the gallbladder wall becomes fibrotic and cannot distend; in cancer, the slowly progressive obstruction allows the gallbladder to dilate gradually
  • Most pancreatic cancers arise in the head (70%) — these present earlier (with jaundice) than body/tail tumors (which present late with back pain and weight loss)
  • Periampullary cancers (cancers near the ampulla of Vater) have a relatively better prognosis than pancreatic adenocarcinoma

Whipple Procedure (Pancreaticoduodenectomy)

  • Standard surgical treatment for resectable pancreatic head cancer or periampullary tumors
  • Resected structures: head of pancreas, entire duodenum (C-loop), distal stomach (classic) or pylorus (pylorus-preserving), gallbladder and distal CBD
  • Reconstruction: pancreatic remnant (body/tail) anastomosed to jejunum (pancreaticojejunostomy A), bile duct anastomosed to jejunum (hepaticojejunostomy B), stomach/duodenum anastomosed to jejunum (gastrojejunostomy C) — all in a Roux-en-Y configuration
  • 5-year survival remains poor (~20–25%) even with surgery

Acute Pancreatitis

  • Two most common causes: gallstones and alcoholism (GET SMASHED mnemonic)
  • ~10% mortality; hemorrhagic necrotizing pancreatitis has higher mortality
  • Pain: epigastric, radiates to back (retroperitoneal location), relieved by leaning forward
  • Elevated serum amylase and lipase (lipase more specific)
  • Chronic calcifying pancreatitis (X-ray): calcifications visible in pancreatic distribution on plain film — classic in chronic alcoholism
🎯 Step 1 High-Yield — Obstructive Jaundice Syndromes
  • Painless jaundice + palpable gallbladder = pancreatic head cancer (Courvoisier's sign)
  • Painful jaundice + fever = cholangitis (Charcot's triad: jaundice + RUQ pain + fever)
  • Jaundice + epigastric pain + pancreatitis = gallstone at ampulla of Vater
  • Lab pattern of obstructive jaundice: ↑ direct (conjugated) bilirubin, ↑ ALP, ↑ GGT, normal ALT/AST (unless secondary hepatocyte damage)
  • Steatorrhea occurs when pancreatic lipase is absent (CBD + main duct obstructed)

▼ Liver Functions in Digestion

Bile Production

  • Liver produces 500–1,000 mL of bile per day — continuously secreted by hepatocytes into bile canaliculi
  • Bile composition: bile salts (primary — cholate & chenodeoxycholate; secondary — deoxycholate & lithocholate from bacterial modification), cholesterol, bilirubin, phospholipids, water, electrolytes
  • Function of bile salts: emulsify dietary fats (break into small droplets → ↑ surface area for lipase) and form micelles to carry fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) and fatty acids to enterocytes
  • Bilirubin: breakdown product of heme from RBC destruction → conjugated with glucuronic acid by hepatocytes → water-soluble → secreted into bile → converted to urobilinogen/stercobilin in gut

First-Pass Metabolism

  • All nutrients absorbed from the GI tract (except fat, which goes via chylomicrons/lymphatics) drain into the portal vein → liver first
  • The liver metabolizes, activates, detoxifies, or stores nutrients before they enter systemic circulation
  • Clinically important: oral drugs with high first-pass metabolism have much lower bioavailability (e.g., nitroglycerin, morphine, propranolol) — liver metabolism reduces effective dose reaching systemic circulation
  • In liver failure or portosystemic shunting: first-pass is bypassed → drugs and toxins (e.g., ammonia) reach systemic circulation → hepatic encephalopathy

▼ Gallbladder Function

Storage & Concentration

  • The gallbladder stores bile between meals and concentrates it 10-fold by actively absorbing water and electrolytes (Na⁺ absorption drives water reabsorption)
  • Concentrated bile is more effective for fat emulsification when released into the duodenum

CCK-Mediated Contraction

  • Fat and protein in the duodenum stimulate I-cells (in duodenal/jejunal mucosa) to release CCK (cholecystokinin)
  • CCK acts on the gallbladder → contraction → bile expelled through cystic duct into CBD
  • CCK simultaneously causes relaxation of the sphincter of Oddi → bile flows into the duodenum at the major papilla
  • CCK also stimulates pancreatic acinar cells → enzyme secretion
  • Vagal tone (parasympathetic) also promotes gallbladder contraction; sympathetic tone inhibits it
🎯 Step 1 High-Yield — CCK vs. Secretin
  • CCK: released by fat + protein in duodenum; causes gallbladder contraction + sphincter of Oddi relaxation + pancreatic enzyme secretion (acinar cells)
  • Secretin: released by acid (H⁺) in duodenum; causes pancreatic ductal cell secretion of HCO₃⁻-rich fluid (neutralizes chyme); also promotes bile secretion by cholangiocytes
  • Together, CCK + secretin coordinate the delivery of bile and pancreatic juice to the duodenum to handle fat digestion

▼ Pancreatic Exocrine Function

Exocrine Secretion

  • Acinar cells (majority of pancreatic mass) secrete digestive enzymes as inactive zymogens into pancreatic ducts → main duct → duodenum
  • Ductal cells (centroacinar cells) secrete large volumes of HCO₃⁻-rich fluid (stimulated by secretin) → neutralizes acidic chyme entering duodenum from stomach; creates optimal pH (~7–8) for enzyme activity

Pancreatitis: Autodigestion

In acute pancreatitis, zymogens are activated within the pancreas (rather than in the duodenum) — leading to autodigestion of pancreatic tissue. Mechanisms include gallstone obstruction, alcohol-induced acinar cell injury, and CFTR mutations (in chronic pancreatitis). Trypsinogen → trypsin activation within pancreatic ducts initiates the cascade.

▼ Anatomical Basis of Digestive Integration

Portal Circulation & Nutrient Delivery

  • Nutrients absorbed in the small intestine (amino acids, sugars, short-chain fatty acids) drain via the SMV and IMV → portal vein → liver before reaching systemic circulation
  • Long-chain fatty acids and fat-soluble vitamins are packaged as chylomicrons in enterocytes → enter lacteals → thoracic duct → left subclavian vein → systemic circulation, bypassing the liver initially
  • This is why fat-soluble drugs/vitamins have lower first-pass metabolism than water-soluble ones

Enterohepatic Circulation of Bile Salts

  • Bile salts are reabsorbed in the terminal ileum (active transport) → portal vein → liver → re-secreted into bile
  • ~95% of bile salts are recycled with each meal; only ~5% lost in feces (replaced by new synthesis from cholesterol)
  • Ileal disease or resection (e.g., Crohn's disease, terminal ileum resection) → loss of bile salts → ↓ bile salt pool → fat malabsorption, steatorrhea, fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (A, D, E, K)
  • Also: excess bile salts reaching the colon → osmotic/secretory diarrhea (bile salt diarrhea)

Coordinated Fat Digestion: The Full Sequence

  1. Fat enters duodenum → stimulates CCK release from I-cells
  2. Acid chyme → stimulates secretin release from S-cells
  3. CCK: gallbladder contracts → bile released via major papilla into duodenum; pancreatic acinar cells secrete digestive enzymes
  4. Secretin: pancreatic ductal cells secrete HCO₃⁻ → neutralizes acid → optimal pH for enzyme activity
  5. Bile salts emulsify fat → lipase (with co-lipase) hydrolyzes triglycerides → fatty acids + monoglycerides form micelles with bile salts
  6. Micelles diffuse to enterocyte brush border → lipids released and absorbed → packaged as chylomicrons → enter lymphatics → bypass liver
  7. Bile salts absorbed in terminal ileum → enterohepatic circulation
🎯 Step 1 High-Yield — Fat Malabsorption Causes
  • Pancreatic insufficiency (chronic pancreatitis, cystic fibrosis) → ↓ lipase → steatorrhea; treat with pancreatic enzyme replacement
  • Bile salt deficiency (ileal disease/resection, cholestasis, bile acid sequestrants) → impaired micelle formation → fat + fat-soluble vitamin malabsorption
  • Mucosal disease (celiac, Crohn's) → damaged enterocytes cannot absorb micelles
  • Steatorrhea in CF is due to exocrine pancreatic insufficiency (thick secretions block pancreatic duct → enzyme deficiency)

Question 1 — LO 1

A 60-year-old man with cirrhosis presents with hematemesis. Endoscopy shows large esophageal varices. The surgeon elects to perform a portosystemic shunt procedure. Which of the following correctly identifies the portal tributary most directly involved in the formation of esophageal varices?

A) Right gastric vein

B) Left gastric (coronary) vein

C) Superior mesenteric vein

D) Inferior mesenteric vein

E) Paraumbilical veins

Answer: B — Left gastric (coronary) vein.

The left gastric vein drains the lesser curvature and lower esophagus. It connects the portal system to the azygos (systemic) system at the gastroesophageal junction. In portal hypertension, elevated portal pressure causes reversal of flow through the left gastric vein, engorging submucosal esophageal veins → esophageal varices. Paraumbilical veins → caput medusae (C). IMV → sigmoid/rectal varices (D).

Question 2 — LO 1

During a hepatic resection for a tumor, the surgeon identifies Cantlie's line (the true functional midplane of the liver) to plan the extent of resection. Cantlie's line runs between which two structures?

A) The falciform ligament and the ligamentum teres

B) The left triangular ligament and the porta hepatis

C) The gallbladder fossa and the groove for the inferior vena cava

D) The right hepatic vein and the left hepatic duct

E) The coronary ligament and the bare area

Answer: C — The gallbladder fossa and the groove for the IVC.

Cantlie's line (the true surgical/functional midplane) runs from the gallbladder fossa anteriorly to the IVC groove posteriorly. This plane separates the true right and left functional lobes (defined by portal and hepatic vein supply/drainage). The falciform ligament (A) is a superficial landmark that does NOT correspond to functional midplane — the caudate and quadrate lobes appear to be right-sided but are functionally left-sided by Couinaud classification.

Question 3 — LO 1

A 55-year-old woman with known gallstones develops sudden right upper quadrant pain radiating to the right shoulder tip, followed by fever and jaundice. On exam, she is tender in the RUQ. Labs show elevated bilirubin, ALP, and WBC. Which diagnosis does this presentation best represent, and what is the anatomical basis for right shoulder pain?

A) Acute cholecystitis; cystic duct obstruction causing referred pain via the phrenic nerve

B) Ascending cholangitis; CBD obstruction with bile irritating the right diaphragm via phrenic nerve

C) Acute pancreatitis; retroperitoneal inflammation compressing the right phrenic nerve

D) Hepatic abscess; pus in the right lobe irritating the overlying diaphragm

E) Right renal colic; ureteric irritation referring pain to the shoulder via vagal pathways

Answer: B — Ascending cholangitis; CBD obstruction with bile/inflammatory contents irritating the right diaphragm via phrenic nerve.

Charcot's triad (RUQ pain + fever/chills + jaundice) = ascending cholangitis. Elevated ALP supports biliary obstruction, and leukocytosis confirms infection. Right shoulder pain occurs via the phrenic nerve (C3–C5) — inflammation or fluid under the right diaphragm stimulates phrenic afferents, which refer to the C3–C5 dermatome (right shoulder tip). This is the same mechanism as Kehr's sign (left shoulder in splenic rupture).

Question 4 — LO 1

A surgeon is performing a laparoscopic cholecystectomy. To safely clip the cystic duct and cystic artery, she must identify "the critical view of safety" within Calot's triangle. What are the correct boundaries of Calot's triangle?

A) Cystic duct, common bile duct, and portal vein

B) Cystic duct, hepatic artery proper, and liver surface

C) Cystic duct, common hepatic duct, and inferior surface of the liver

D) Common hepatic duct, right hepatic duct, and left hepatic duct

E) Cystic duct, gastroduodenal artery, and porta hepatis

Answer: C — Cystic duct, common hepatic duct, and inferior surface of the liver.

Calot's triangle (hepatocystic triangle) is bounded by: (1) the cystic duct (inferiorly), (2) the common hepatic duct (medially), and (3) the inferior surface of the liver (superiorly). The cystic artery (typically from the right hepatic artery) runs within this triangle. Correctly identifying these structures and achieving the "critical view of safety" (clearing Calot's triangle fat until only two structures enter the gallbladder) prevents inadvertent clipping of the common bile duct — the most feared complication of laparoscopic cholecystectomy.

Question 5 — LO 2

A 35-year-old woman with hereditary spherocytosis develops splenomegaly and severe hemolytic anemia requiring splenectomy. Six weeks post-operatively, she develops a fever, rigors, and hypotension. Blood cultures grow Streptococcus pneumoniae. What should have been done to prevent this complication?

A) Prophylactic anticoagulation to prevent splenic vein thrombosis

B) Vaccination against encapsulated organisms before splenectomy

C) Prophylactic antifungals to prevent opportunistic infections

D) Administration of IVIG to replace immunoglobulin production

E) Corticosteroids to reduce post-splenectomy inflammatory response

Answer: B — Vaccination against encapsulated organisms before splenectomy.

This is overwhelming post-splenectomy infection (OPSI) — a life-threatening infection caused by encapsulated organisms. The spleen is the primary site for clearing opsonized encapsulated bacteria (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae type b, N. meningitidis — "SHiN"). Without splenic macrophages and IgM production, these organisms cause fulminant sepsis. Vaccinations should ideally be given 2 weeks before elective splenectomy (when immune response is best). Post-splenectomy patients also receive prophylactic penicillin (especially in children).

Question 6 — LO 2

A radiologist reviewing an abdominal CT notes that the splenic vein is thrombosed, but the portal vein and superior mesenteric vein are patent. Which of the following findings would you expect in this patient?

A) Diffuse portal hypertension with ascites and esophageal varices

B) Isolated gastric varices and splenomegaly with normal hepatic portal pressure

C) Right-sided heart failure causing hepatic congestion

D) Duodenal varices only, with normal splenic size

E) Intestinal ischemia due to loss of SMV drainage

Answer: B — Isolated gastric varices and splenomegaly with normal hepatic portal pressure.

Splenic vein thrombosis causes sinistral (left-sided) portal hypertension. Because the splenic vein is thrombosed, blood cannot drain normally from the spleen. Back-pressure causes splenomegaly. Collateral flow develops through the short gastric veins and left gastroepiploic vein → gastric fundal varices. Because the portal vein and SMV are patent, hepatic portal pressure is normal — this distinguishes sinistral PHT from diffuse portal hypertension (cirrhosis). Treatment is splenectomy (removes the pressure source).

Question 7 — LO 2

A patient involved in a motor vehicle collision has tenderness in the left upper quadrant and hypotension. On physical examination, you note pain in the left shoulder when you press on the abdomen. The most likely injured structure lies deep to which ribs?

A) Ribs 5–7

B) Ribs 7–9

C) Ribs 9–11

D) Ribs 11–12

E) Ribs 6–8

Answer: C — Ribs 9–11.

The spleen lies in the LUQ, deep to the 9th, 10th, and 11th ribs in the midaxillary line. Left shoulder pain (Kehr's sign) results from blood under the left diaphragm irritating the phrenic nerve (C3–C5). Rib 9–11 fractures on the left are the classic mechanism of traumatic splenic laceration. The spleen is the most commonly injured abdominal organ in blunt trauma.

Question 8 — LO 2

A medical student is asked to palpate for an enlarged spleen during a physical exam. The attending asks: "At what point does the spleen become palpable?" The correct answer is:

A) When it doubles its normal size

B) When it triples its normal size (3× enlargement)

C) At any size, if the patient takes a deep breath

D) Only when it reaches more than 500 grams

E) When it descends below the 12th rib

Answer: B — When it triples its normal size (3× enlargement).

The spleen is normally not palpable because it lies deep to the ribs. It must enlarge to at least 3× its normal size before it descends below the left costal margin and becomes palpable. When palpable, the characteristic notches on the superior border help identify it as the spleen (distinguishing it from other LUQ masses like an enlarged left kidney). Splenomegaly enlarges toward the right iliac fossa following the direction of the 10th rib.

Question 9 — LO 3

A 70-year-old man presents with 8 weeks of progressive painless jaundice, 20-lb weight loss, and new-onset diabetes. Physical exam reveals a palpable, nontender gallbladder. CA 19-9 is markedly elevated. Imaging shows a 3 cm mass in the head of the pancreas with biliary ductal dilation. Which anatomical fact best explains the jaundice?

A) Portal vein thrombosis compressing the biliary system

B) The common bile duct runs in a groove on the posterior surface of the pancreatic head

C) The cystic duct is obstructed by extrinsic compression

D) Hepatic artery compression reducing bile production

E) Duodenal wall invasion blocking the major papilla from outside

Answer: B — The common bile duct runs in a groove on the posterior surface of the pancreatic head.

This is classic pancreatic head adenocarcinoma. The CBD travels in a groove along the posterior surface of the pancreatic head before joining the pancreatic duct at the ampulla. A tumor in the head grows and compresses the CBD within this groove, causing obstructive (post-hepatic) jaundice. The palpable gallbladder (Courvoisier's sign) occurs because slowly progressive obstruction allows the gallbladder to dilate without prior fibrosis. New-onset diabetes can occur as tumor destroys islet cells.

Question 10 — LO 3

A 45-year-old man with a history of heavy alcohol use presents with acute epigastric pain radiating to the back. Serum lipase is 3× the upper limit of normal. CT shows peripancreatic fat stranding. The two most common causes of this condition share which common anatomical mechanism?

A) Both cause direct viral infection of pancreatic acinar cells

B) Both cause premature activation of pancreatic zymogens within the pancreatic ducts

C) Both compress the SMV, causing venous outflow obstruction

D) Both cause fibrosis of the pancreatic parenchyma before enzyme release

E) Both primarily affect the tail of the pancreas, sparing the head

Answer: B — Both cause premature activation of pancreatic zymogens within the pancreatic ducts.

Acute pancreatitis is caused most commonly by gallstones (obstruct the ampulla of Vater → backpressure/reflux of bile into pancreatic duct) and alcohol (toxic to acinar cells, causes protein plugs and ductal obstruction). In both cases, pancreatic zymogens — especially trypsinogen — are activated within the pancreatic tissue rather than in the duodenum. Activated trypsin then activates all other zymogens → autodigestion → pancreatitis. The retroperitoneal location explains back pain radiation.

Question 11 — LO 3

During fetal development, the ventral pancreatic bud fails to rotate properly around the duodenum. What congenital anomaly results, and what is its clinical presentation?

A) Pancreas divisum — presents with recurrent pancreatitis

B) Annular pancreas — presents with duodenal obstruction and bilious vomiting

C) Heterotopic pancreas — presents with GI bleeding from ectopic tissue

D) Agenesis of the dorsal pancreatic bud — presents with exocrine insufficiency

E) Choledochal cyst — presents with intermittent jaundice and palpable mass

Answer: B — Annular pancreas — presents with duodenal obstruction and bilious vomiting.

The ventral pancreatic bud normally rotates clockwise (posterior to the duodenum) to fuse with the dorsal bud. If the ventral bud fails to complete this rotation normally, it wraps around the duodenum as an annular pancreas — a ring of pancreatic tissue encircling the 2nd part of the duodenum. This causes duodenal obstruction presenting in neonates with bilious vomiting and the "double bubble sign" on X-ray (stomach + proximal duodenum filled with gas). Pancreas divisum (A) = failure of ventral/dorsal duct fusion → different anomaly.

Question 12 — LO 3

A patient undergoes a Whipple procedure (pancreaticoduodenectomy) for pancreatic head cancer. Which of the following structures is removed en bloc in the standard procedure?

A) Head of pancreas, body of pancreas, entire duodenum, gallbladder

B) Head of pancreas, entire duodenum, distal stomach, gallbladder and distal CBD

C) Entire pancreas, spleen, gallbladder, and duodenum

D) Head of pancreas, proximal jejunum, distal CBD, right hepatic lobe

E) Head of pancreas, sigmoid colon, gallbladder, and distal CBD

Answer: B — Head of pancreas, entire duodenum, distal stomach, gallbladder and distal CBD.

The classic Whipple procedure (pancreaticoduodenectomy) removes: (1) head of pancreas, (2) entire duodenum (the C-loop), (3) distal stomach (antrectomy — omitted in pylorus-preserving variant), (4) gallbladder, and (5) distal common bile duct. Reconstruction involves three anastomoses: pancreatic remnant (body/tail) → jejunum (A), bile duct → jejunum (B), stomach → jejunum (C). The body and tail of the pancreas are preserved and re-anastomosed to provide remaining exocrine/endocrine function.

Question 13 — LO 4

A 50-year-old man with chronic alcoholic pancreatitis develops greasy, foul-smelling stools and significant weight loss. His stool Sudan stain is positive for fat. Laboratory studies show a prolonged PT. Which of the following best explains the PT prolongation in this patient?

A) Alcohol-induced thrombocytopenia from bone marrow suppression

B) Impaired fat-soluble vitamin (K) absorption due to pancreatic lipase deficiency and bile salt disruption

C) Hepatic failure from concurrent alcoholic hepatitis

D) Disseminated intravascular coagulation from acute pancreatitis

E) Direct inhibition of clotting factor synthesis by alcohol

Answer: B — Impaired fat-soluble vitamin (K) absorption due to pancreatic lipase deficiency and bile salt disruption.

Chronic pancreatitis causes exocrine insufficiency → loss of pancreatic lipase + protease + bicarbonate → fat malabsorption (steatorrhea). Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) are carried in micelles and require lipase for their absorption. Vitamin K deficiency impairs carboxylation of clotting factors II, VII, IX, X (and protein C/S) → elevated PT. Vitamin K is not synthesized in adequate amounts by the body and must be absorbed from the diet. This is correctable with parenteral vitamin K administration (distinguishing it from hepatic synthesis failure where IV vitamin K does not fully correct PT).

Question 14 — LO 4

A patient with well-compensated cirrhosis takes an oral dose of a drug with high first-pass metabolism. Compared to a healthy individual, what would you expect regarding the drug's bioavailability and why?

A) Decreased bioavailability — cirrhosis increases CYP450 activity in residual hepatocytes

B) Increased bioavailability — portosystemic shunting bypasses hepatic first-pass metabolism

C) Unchanged bioavailability — first-pass metabolism occurs in the gut wall, not the liver

D) Decreased bioavailability — impaired enterohepatic circulation reduces drug recycling

E) Increased bioavailability — cirrhosis impairs renal drug excretion

Answer: B — Increased bioavailability — portosystemic shunting bypasses hepatic first-pass metabolism.

In cirrhosis, portal hypertension forces blood through portosystemic collaterals (esophageal varices, caput medusae, paraumbilical veins, etc.), bypassing the hepatic sinusoids. Drugs absorbed from the GI tract and normally metabolized by the liver on their first pass reach systemic circulation largely unmetabolized. This is why cirrhotic patients can have increased and unpredictable drug levels — medications with high first-pass metabolism (propranolol, morphine, lidocaine, nitroglycerin) reach much higher systemic concentrations → risk of toxicity at standard doses.

Question 15 — LO 4

A physiologist is studying fat digestion. She gives a volunteer a fatty meal and then measures duodenal hormones. Which hormone rises first in response to fat entering the duodenum, and what are its two primary actions relevant to bile and pancreatic enzyme delivery?

A) Secretin — stimulates gallbladder contraction and pancreatic HCO₃⁻ secretion

B) Gastrin — stimulates gastric acid secretion and gallbladder relaxation

C) CCK — stimulates gallbladder contraction and pancreatic enzyme secretion

D) GIP — stimulates insulin release and inhibits gastric emptying

E) Motilin — coordinates MMC and gallbladder emptying during fasting

Answer: C — CCK — stimulates gallbladder contraction and pancreatic enzyme secretion.

CCK (cholecystokinin) is released from I-cells in the duodenum and proximal jejunum in response to fat and protein. Its two primary actions in fat digestion: (1) contracts the gallbladder → expels concentrated bile through the cystic duct and CBD into the duodenum via the major papilla; (2) stimulates pancreatic acinar cells → releases digestive enzymes (lipase, proteases, amylase). CCK also relaxes the sphincter of Oddi. Secretin (A) primarily stimulates HCO₃⁻ secretion from ductal cells (not gallbladder contraction). Together, CCK and secretin coordinate the complete digestive response to a meal.

Question 16 — LO 1 (Hepatobiliary System)

A 52-year-old woman with alcoholic cirrhosis develops massive hematemesis. Endoscopy reveals large esophageal varices. Which vessel's increased pressure most directly explains the formation of esophageal varices in this patient?

A) Azygos vein

B) Left gastric (coronary) vein

C) Inferior mesenteric vein

D) Splenic vein

E) Superior mesenteric vein

Answer: B — Left gastric (coronary) vein.

The left gastric (coronary) vein drains the lesser curvature of the stomach and lower esophagus into the portal vein. In portal hypertension, elevated portal pressure is transmitted retrograde into the left gastric vein, engorging the submucosal esophageal veins that normally drain into the azygos (systemic) system. The azygos vein is the systemic side of this anastomosis — the direction blood flows toward, not the pressure source. The IMV and splenic vein contribute to overall portal pressure but the direct venous connection at the esophageal level is via the left gastric vein.

Question 17 — LO 2 (The Spleen)

A 28-year-old man sustains left-sided rib fractures in a motor vehicle collision and develops hypotension. He reports left shoulder pain. CT shows free fluid in the abdomen. Which of the following structures is most likely injured, and why does shoulder pain occur?

A) Left kidney; retroperitoneal blood tracking to the psoas muscle

B) Spleen; phrenic nerve irritation by subdiaphragmatic blood

C) Descending colon; colonic contents irritating parietal peritoneum

D) Left adrenal gland; cortisol release causing referred pain

E) Splenic vein; portal hypertension causing acute pain

Answer: B — Spleen; phrenic nerve irritation by subdiaphragmatic blood.

The spleen is the most commonly injured abdominal organ in blunt trauma. Left rib fractures (especially ribs 9–11) can directly lacerate the spleen. Free intraperitoneal blood from splenic rupture tracks upward under the left diaphragm, irritating the phrenic nerve (C3–C5), which refers sensation to the left shoulder tip. This is called Kehr's sign — a classic clinical indicator of splenic rupture. Hypotension from intraperitoneal hemorrhage and free fluid on CT confirm hemoperitoneum.

Question 18 — LO 3 (Pancreas & Obstructive Jaundice)

A 68-year-old man presents with 6 weeks of progressive painless jaundice, 15-lb weight loss, and pruritus. Examination reveals a nontender palpable mass in the right upper quadrant. Labs show elevated direct bilirubin, alkaline phosphatase, and GGT. Which anatomical relationship best explains the jaundice in this case?

A) Tumor compressing the cystic duct and preventing gallbladder emptying

B) Tumor in the pancreatic head compressing the CBD in its posterior groove

C) Portal vein thrombosis causing back-pressure on the biliary system

D) Splenic vein thrombosis causing left-sided portal hypertension

E) Stone at the junction of the cystic duct and common hepatic duct

Answer: B — Tumor in the pancreatic head compressing the CBD in its posterior groove.

This is a classic presentation of pancreatic head adenocarcinoma. The CBD travels in a groove along the posterior surface of the pancreatic head. A growing tumor slowly compresses the CBD, causing progressive obstructive jaundice (dark urine, pale stools, pruritus). The presentation is characteristically painless because the tumor grows slowly. The palpable nontender gallbladder is Courvoisier's sign — the gallbladder dilates because it has no prior fibrosis from stone disease. Cystic duct compression (A) would not cause obstructive jaundice because the CBD would remain open.

Question 19 — LO 4 (Digestive Integration)

A 45-year-old woman with Crohn's disease involving the terminal ileum undergoes surgical resection of 80 cm of terminal ileum. She subsequently develops oily, foul-smelling stools and deficiencies in vitamins A, D, E, and K. What is the primary anatomical-physiological explanation for her symptoms?

A) Reduced gastric acid production leading to impaired protein digestion

B) Impaired bile salt reabsorption disrupting enterohepatic circulation and micelle formation

C) Loss of CCK-producing I-cells in the resected segment

D) Pancreatic exocrine insufficiency from retrograde pressure on the pancreatic duct

E) Reduced absorptive surface area for direct fat absorption

Answer: B — Impaired bile salt reabsorption disrupting enterohepatic circulation and micelle formation.

The terminal ileum is the exclusive site of active bile salt reabsorption. After resection, bile salts are lost in feces instead of being recycled via enterohepatic circulation. The bile salt pool depletes → inadequate bile in the duodenum → impaired fat emulsification → fat cannot be packaged into micelles → fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) and long-chain fatty acids are malabsorbed → steatorrhea + fat-soluble vitamin deficiencies. I-cells are in the proximal duodenum/jejunum (not the terminal ileum), so CCK is unaffected. The pancreas is not involved.
