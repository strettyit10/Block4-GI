LO 1 · Intraperitoneal vs Retroperitoneal Organs

Distinguish the intraperitoneal and retroperitoneal organs of the GI tract, understand how a developmental rotation creates "secondary" retroperitoneal organs, and appreciate the clinical relevance of the distinction.

▼1. The Peritoneum — Key Definitions

Parietal vs visceral peritoneum

  • Parietal peritoneum — lines the abdominal wall (“outer” layer). Somatic innervation → pain is sharp and localized.
  • Visceral peritoneum — covers the organs (“inner” layer). Autonomic innervation → pain is dull, poorly localized, referred to midline dermatomes.
  • Peritoneal cavity — potential space between them. Normally holds only a small amount of serous fluid.

Intraperitoneal vs retroperitoneal

  • Intraperitoneal (IP) — organ is suspended within the peritoneal cavity by a mesentery; almost completely covered by visceral peritoneum.
  • Retroperitoneal (RP) — organ lies posterior to the parietal peritoneum against the posterior abdominal wall; only its anterior surface is covered.
High-yield: IP organs have a mesentery = a mobility lifeline carrying their neurovascular supply. RP organs are fixed to the posterior body wall. This anatomy drives everything that follows — where pathology spreads, where bleeds collect, and what the surgeon sees.

▼2. Layers of the Retroperitoneal Space

Compartments around the kidney

  • Perinephric (perirenal) space — between the renal capsule and Gerota’s (renal) fascia. Contains the kidney, adrenal, renal vessels, and perinephric fat.
  • Gerota’s fascia (renal fascia) — the tough connective-tissue envelope; fused superiorly with the diaphragmatic fascia, open inferiorly (why perinephric collections can track down).
  • Paranephric space — external to Gerota’s fascia, contains paranephric fat; bounded anteriorly by the transversalis fascia.
▼Exam Trap: Why retroperitoneal bleeds are silent
Retroperitoneal structures lack a peritoneal cavity to distend; large-volume bleeds from the aorta, IVC, or kidney can hide for a long time before peritoneal signs develop. Classic example: ruptured AAA with flank bruising (Grey-Turner sign) and hypotension without frank peritoneal irritation.

▼3. SAD PUCKER — Retroperitoneal Organs

▼Mnemonic: SAD PUCKER
Suprarenal (adrenal) glands · Aorta & IVC · Duodenum (2nd & 3rd parts) · Pancreas (except tail) · Ureters · Colon (ascending & descending) · Kidneys · Esophagus (lower thoracic) · Rectum (middle & lower thirds).

Retroperitoneal GI organs (LO focus)

  • Duodenum — all but the 1st part; the duodenal bulb is IP.
  • Pancreas — head, neck, body are RP; the tail is IP (it lies in the splenorenal ligament).
  • Ascending & descending colon (the transverse and sigmoid colon are IP on their own mesenteries).
  • Rectum — middle & inferior thirds are RP/subperitoneal (the upper third is IP).

Intraperitoneal GI organs (for contrast)

  • Stomach, 1st part of duodenum, jejunum, ileum
  • Transverse colon, sigmoid colon
  • Cecum (variable — usually IP but can be RP)
  • Appendix, liver, gallbladder, spleen, tail of pancreas
High-yield twist: USMLE loves the tail of pancreas (IP in splenorenal ligament), 1st part of duodenum (IP), and cecum (variable) as exceptions. Everything else follows SAD PUCKER.

▼4. Primary vs Secondary Retroperitoneal

Primary retroperitoneal

  • Never had a mesentery during development.
  • Examples: kidneys, adrenals, ureters, aorta, IVC, lower esophagus, rectum.

Secondary retroperitoneal

  • Began development intraperitoneally with a mesentery, then rotated and fused with the posterior body wall; mesentery regressed.
  • Examples: most of the duodenum, pancreas (head, neck, body), ascending & descending colon.
▼Exam Trap: “Secondary retroperitoneal” tests embryology
If a stem asks which structures are secondary RP, you want the ones that rotated to get there: duodenum, pancreas, ascending colon, descending colon. The kidneys, ureters, adrenals, aorta, IVC, and rectum are primary RP and will be wrong answers for “secondary.”

▼5. Clinical Relevance

Trauma

  • RP bleeds (AAA rupture, pelvic fracture, kidney laceration) can cause life-threatening hemorrhage with minimal peritoneal signs.
  • Buzzword bruising: Grey-Turner sign (flank), Cullen sign (periumbilical) — indicate RP hemorrhage tracking along fascial planes.

Infection / abscess

  • Psoas abscess — from vertebral osteomyelitis or Crohn’s tracking into psoas sheath; presents with back pain, hip flexion position, positive psoas sign.
  • Appendicitis in a retrocecal appendix can present atypically with flank pain and less peritoneal signs.

Surgery

  • Pancreatitis behind the stomach is classic “pain radiating to the back” because the pancreas is RP.
  • Duodenal perforation of the posterior wall (2nd part) may wall off retroperitoneally rather than spill freely.
Clinical pearl: Any dull, deep, poorly-localized pain that radiates to the back should raise suspicion for a retroperitoneal structure — pancreatitis, AAA expansion, renal pathology, psoas process.

Testing Your Understanding

TYU 1

A 60-year-old man presents with sudden severe epigastric pain radiating to the back and hypotension. His CT scan shows blood tracking posteriorly along fascial planes with flank ecchymoses. Why does his physical exam lack frank peritoneal signs?

He likely has a ruptured abdominal aortic aneurysm. The aorta is retroperitoneal, so bleeding initially stays posterior to the parietal peritoneum and does not irritate the anterior peritoneum — hence no early rigidity or rebound. Grey-Turner sign (flank bruising) reflects blood tracking through fascial planes. This “silent” bleed is why RP pathology must be recognized clinically before peritoneal signs emerge.

TYU 2

Why is the tail of the pancreas considered intraperitoneal while the rest of the pancreas is retroperitoneal, and what surgical implication does this have?

The tail of the pancreas lies within the splenorenal (lienorenal) ligament, a double-layered peritoneal fold; it is therefore IP. The head, neck, and body fused with the posterior wall during rotation and are secondary RP. Clinically, splenectomy risks injury to the pancreatic tail, causing pancreatic fistula or pseudocyst — surgeons must ligate the splenorenal ligament carefully.

TYU 3

A young woman has back pain and hip-flexion posture after her Crohn disease flare. CT shows a collection abutting the psoas muscle. Where in the retroperitoneal space is this collection, and why does she hold her hip flexed?

Psoas abscess — a collection in the psoas sheath within the retroperitoneum, often from Crohn disease perforating through the bowel wall or vertebral osteomyelitis. The psoas is a hip flexor, so reflex spasm pulls the hip into flexion to reduce muscle stretch and pain; extension of the hip produces the positive psoas sign. Treatment is drainage plus antibiotics targeting enteric organisms.

▼1. Abdominal Aorta — Branches & Vertebral Levels

Entry and termination

  • Enters the abdomen through the aortic hiatus at T12.
  • Bifurcates into common iliac arteries at L4 (roughly at the umbilicus in thin patients).
  • Lies just to the left of midline, anterior to the vertebral bodies.

Anterior (unpaired) branches — feed the GI tract

  • Celiac trunk — T12 — foregut (stomach, liver, spleen, upper duodenum, pancreas head).
  • Superior mesenteric artery (SMA) — L1 — midgut (distal duodenum through proximal 2/3 transverse colon).
  • Inferior mesenteric artery (IMA) — L3 — hindgut (distal 1/3 transverse colon through superior rectum).

Lateral (paired) branches

  • Middle suprarenal arteries — L1
  • Renal arteries — L1/L2 (right is longer, passes behind IVC)
  • Gonadal arteries — L2 (testicular / ovarian)

Posterior (paired) branches

  • Inferior phrenic and lumbar arteries — body wall.
  • Inferior phrenic gives off the superior suprarenal artery.
▼Mnemonic: Aortic branch levels
T12: aorta enters & celiac trunk · L1: SMA, suprarenal, renal · L2: gonadals · L3: IMA · L4: bifurcation into common iliacs.

▼2. Inferior Vena Cava & Tributaries

Course

  • Formed at L5 by junction of common iliac veins, runs to the right of midline.
  • Passes through the caval hiatus at T8 and drains into the right atrium.
  • Anterior: crosses posterior to the epiploic foramen of Winslow (entrance to lesser sac), head of pancreas, 3rd part of duodenum, root of mesentery, and right liver.

Major tributaries (inferior to superior)

  • Common iliac veins
  • Lumbar veins
  • Right gonadal vein (the left gonadal drains into the left renal, not the IVC)
  • Renal veins
  • Right suprarenal (left suprarenal drains into left renal)
  • Inferior phrenic veins
  • Hepatic veins
High-yield asymmetry: The left gonadal and left suprarenal veins drain into the left renal vein, while the right equivalents drain directly into the IVC. This creates a long left gonadal drainage path → higher venous pressure → left-sided varicocele is much more common than right.

▼3. Renal Vessels & Nutcracker Syndrome

Renal arteries

  • Paired, arise at L1–L2. Each divides into 5 segmental arteries — anatomic end arteries: no meaningful collaterals, so segmental occlusion causes infarction.
  • The right renal artery is longer and passes posterior to the IVC.
  • Kidneys receive ~25% of cardiac output despite being ~0.5% of body mass.

Renal veins

  • Left renal vein is longer; crosses anterior to the aorta and posterior to the SMA — this is the “nutcracker angle.”
  • Receives: left gonadal, left suprarenal, and left 2nd lumbar veins.
  • Right renal vein is short, drains directly into IVC.

Nutcracker syndrome

  • Compression of the left renal vein between the aorta and SMA.
  • Produces left-sided flank pain, hematuria, and left-sided varicocele (in males) or pelvic congestion (in females) from elevated left renal venous pressure.
  • Diagnosis: Doppler US, CT angiography demonstrating narrowing.
▼Exam Trap: SMA syndrome vs nutcracker syndrome
Both involve compression between the aorta and SMA but different structures:
  • Nutcracker syndrome compresses the left renal vein → hematuria, flank pain, varicocele.
  • SMA syndrome compresses the 3rd part of the duodenum → postprandial nausea, vomiting, weight loss; classically after rapid weight loss (loss of the retroperitoneal fat pad) or body cast.

▼4. Adrenal (Suprarenal) Blood Supply

Three arterial sources (bilateral)

  • Superior suprarenal from the inferior phrenic artery
  • Middle suprarenal from the aorta directly (L1)
  • Inferior suprarenal from the renal artery

Single venous drainage (asymmetric)

  • Each adrenal has one vein.
  • Right adrenal vein → directly into IVC (short, surgically delicate).
  • Left adrenal vein → left renal vein.
Surgical pearl: Right adrenalectomy is technically harder because the short right adrenal vein tears off the IVC easily, threatening major hemorrhage.

▼5. Three Venous Networks of the Abdomen

Portal system

  • Drains abdominal GI tract (distal esophagus through upper rectum), spleen, pancreas, gallbladder → liver.
  • Formed by splenic vein + superior mesenteric vein; IMV joins splenic.

Caval system

  • IVC drains abdominal wall, kidneys, adrenals, gonads, liver via hepatic veins.

Azygos system

  • Posterior body wall veins (lumbar, ascending lumbar) that communicate with the azygos/hemiazygos system in the thorax via the aortic hiatus (T12).
  • Critical collateral pathway if IVC is obstructed.

Portosystemic anastomoses (where the 3 systems meet)

  • Distal esophagus: left gastric ↔ esophageal (azygos) → esophageal varices
  • Umbilicus: paraumbilical ↔ superficial epigastric → caput medusae
  • Rectum: superior rectal (portal) ↔ middle/inferior rectal (caval) → internal hemorrhoids
  • Retroperitoneal bare areas of liver and colon → silent but important in portal hypertension.
▼Mnemonic: Varices of portal hypertension
Esophageal varices, Caput medusae, Hemorrhoids — “portal hypertension = vARICES + varies everywhere.”

Testing Your Understanding

TYU 1

A 28-year-old tall, thin man presents with recurrent left-sided flank pain and hematuria. Imaging shows the left renal vein compressed between the aorta and superior mesenteric artery. What vessels are involved, and why is this condition named after a kitchen tool?

Nutcracker syndrome. The left renal vein must cross anterior to the aorta and posterior to the SMA to reach the IVC. A narrow aortomesenteric angle (typical in thin/tall patients with little mesenteric fat) “pinches” the vein like a nutcracker → venous hypertension, hematuria, varicocele, or pelvic congestion. Management ranges from observation to stenting or venous transposition.

TYU 2

A 17-year-old boy has recurrent painless left scrotal swelling that worsens with Valsalva. Physical exam describes a “bag of worms” above the left testicle. Why is this condition far more common on the left than the right?

Varicocele. The left testicular (gonadal) vein drains into the left renal vein at a right angle, a longer and higher-pressure path than the right gonadal vein, which drains directly into the IVC. That hydrostatic disadvantage (plus possible valvular incompetence) predisposes to varicosity of the pampiniform plexus on the left. New-onset left varicocele in an older adult should raise concern for a left renal cell carcinoma obstructing the left renal vein.

TYU 3

Which single artery, if occluded, most directly causes infarction of the midgut (distal duodenum to proximal 2/3 of the transverse colon), and at what vertebral level does it branch off the aorta?

The superior mesenteric artery (SMA) arises from the aorta at L1. It supplies the midgut — distal duodenum, jejunum, ileum, cecum, appendix, ascending colon, and proximal 2/3 of the transverse colon. Acute SMA embolus classically causes “pain out of proportion to exam” with normal initial labs, then bowel infarction. Remember: watershed areas (splenic flexure = SMA/IMA boundary; rectosigmoid = IMA/rectal) are most vulnerable to hypoperfusion.

▼1. The Diaphragm — Overview

Structure & embryology

The diaphragm is a dome-shaped musculotendinous sheet separating the thoracic and abdominal cavities. It develops from four components: the septum transversum (central tendon), pleuroperitoneal membranes, dorsal mesentery of the esophagus (crura), and body wall (muscular periphery).

Failure of pleuroperitoneal membrane fusion → congenital diaphragmatic hernia (most often left-sided Bochdalek), with abdominal viscera herniating into the thorax and pulmonary hypoplasia.

Motor innervation — phrenic nerves

💡 Mnemonic — Phrenic roots▼

"C3, 4, 5 keep the diaphragm alive."

Each phrenic nerve (one per hemidiaphragm) carries motor fibers from ventral rami of C3–C5, descends through the thorax on the pericardium, and supplies its own dome of the diaphragm. High cervical cord injury (above C3) abolishes diaphragmatic breathing.

Sensory innervation

The phrenic nerve is also sensory to the central portion of the diaphragm (both pleural and peritoneal surfaces). The peripheral diaphragm is supplied by the lower intercostal nerves (T5–T11) and the subcostal nerve (T12).

High-Yield: Central diaphragm irritation (e.g. subphrenic abscess, blood under diaphragm from splenic rupture) → referred shoulder pain via C3–C5 dermatomes (Kehr sign).

▼2. Caval Hiatus — T8

Location & contents

The caval hiatus lies in the central tendon at the level of the T8 vertebra. Because it pierces the tendon (not muscle), the opening is held open during inspiration — actually aiding venous return as the diaphragm contracts.

Contents:

IVC Right phrenic nerve (terminal branches)

Why the right phrenic and not the left?

The left phrenic nerve pierces the muscular dome of the diaphragm separately (to the left of the pericardium). Only the right phrenic nerve accompanies the IVC through the caval hiatus before innervating its dome from below.

▼3. Esophageal Hiatus — T10

Location & contents

The esophageal hiatus is a sling in the right crus of the diaphragm at the level of T10. Unlike the caval hiatus, it is muscular, so crural contraction compresses the distal esophagus — contributing to the anti-reflux barrier at the gastro-esophageal junction.

Contents:

Esophagus Anterior & posterior vagal trunks (CN X) Esophageal branches of the left gastric artery & vein

Clinical — hiatal hernia

Widening of the esophageal hiatus → hiatal hernia:

Sliding (95%): GE junction slides up → "hourglass stomach", worsens GERD Paraesophageal (5%): fundus herniates alongside a fixed GE junction → risk of strangulation

▼4. Aortic Hiatus — T12

Location & contents

The aortic hiatus is a posterior opening at T12, bounded by the median arcuate ligament (a fibrous arch connecting the right and left crura) anteriorly and the vertebral body posteriorly. Because the aorta lies behind the diaphragm rather than piercing muscle, it is not compressed during inspiration.

Contents:

Abdominal aorta Thoracic duct Azygos vein

Median arcuate ligament syndrome

Abnormally low or tight median arcuate ligament compresses the celiac trunk just below the aortic hiatus → post-prandial epigastric pain, weight loss, epigastric bruit. Treated surgically by ligament release.

⚠️ Exam Trap — Vertebral levels of the hiatuses▼

Mnemonic: "I 8 10 EGGs AT 12"

I (IVC) — 8 (T8) — + R phrenic n. 10 (T10) — EGGs = Esophagus, vaGus (x2), Gastric branches AT (Aorta, Thoracic duct) — 12 (T12) — + Azygos

Classic wrong answer: putting the thoracic duct in the caval hiatus. It travels with the aorta at T12.

▼5. Phrenic Nerve & Referred Pain

Dermatomal referral

The phrenic nerve (C3–C5) shares spinal cord levels with the supraclavicular nerves (C3–C4), which innervate the shoulder and neck skin. Central diaphragm irritation is therefore referred to the ipsilateral shoulder tip.

Classic clinical scenarios

Subphrenic abscess → ipsilateral shoulder pain Splenic rupture (blood under L diaphragm) → L shoulder pain (Kehr sign) Ruptured ectopic pregnancy with hemoperitoneum → shoulder pain when supine Diaphragmatic pleurisy → shoulder + neck pain

▼6. Other Clinical Correlates

Phrenic nerve injury

Injury (surgical, malignant invasion, viral neuritis) → hemidiaphragm paralysis, visible on chest X-ray as an elevated hemidiaphragm with paradoxical movement on sniff test.

Traumatic diaphragmatic rupture

Blunt abdominal trauma most commonly ruptures the left hemidiaphragm (the liver shields the right). Herniation of the stomach/colon into the left chest causes respiratory distress and a "bowel in chest" sign on imaging.

Testing Your Understanding (TYU)

TYU 1: Which vertebral level is the aortic hiatus, and what else passes through it besides the aorta?

T12. The aortic hiatus also transmits the thoracic duct and the azygos vein. Unlike the caval and esophageal openings, the aorta passes behind the diaphragm (under the median arcuate ligament), not through muscle.

TYU 2: A patient with a subphrenic abscess complains of right shoulder pain. What is the anatomic basis of this referred pain?

The right phrenic nerve provides sensory innervation to the central diaphragm (both pleural and peritoneal surfaces) and arises from C3–C5. These same cord segments supply the supraclavicular nerves to the shoulder skin, so central diaphragm irritation is referred to the ipsilateral shoulder tip (Kehr sign when blood is the irritant).

TYU 3: A patient has a large sliding hiatal hernia. Which diaphragmatic opening has widened, and which nerves that normally pass through it could be stretched or compressed?

The esophageal hiatus (T10), which sits in the right crus. Besides the esophagus, it transmits the anterior and posterior vagal trunks and esophageal branches of the left gastric vessels. Vagal involvement can produce gastroparesis-like symptoms in some patients.

▼1. The Kidneys — Location & Urine Formation

Retroperitoneal position

The kidneys lie in the perinephric space between T12 and L3 vertebral levels, enclosed by Gerota's (renal) fascia and surrounded by perinephric fat. The right kidney sits ~1–2 cm lower than the left (displaced inferiorly by the liver).

The posterior relationships differ for the superior and inferior poles:

Superior pole: diaphragm & pleural reflection (risk of pneumothorax during renal surgery) Inferior pole: psoas, quadratus lumborum & transversus abdominis

Renal pelvis, calyces & stone seeding

Urine flows from the collecting ducts into the minor calyces → major calyces → renal pelvis → ureter. Special smooth-muscle pacemaker cells in the minor calyces generate ~4 peristaltic waves/min that propel urine.

Stones most often form at the renal papilla (site of supersaturation and Randall's plaques) and may lodge anywhere along the urinary tract.

▼2. The Ureters — Course Through the Retroperitoneum

Retroperitoneal path

Each ureter is ~25 cm long. It descends anterior to the psoas major and is crossed anteriorly by the gonadal vessels ("water — ureter — under the bridge — gonadal vessels"). The ureters travel at roughly the level of the tips of the lumbar transverse processes.

At the pelvic brim, the ureter crosses over the bifurcation of the common iliac artery, then courses posteriorly along the lateral pelvic wall to the bladder.

Blood supply

The ureter receives multiple small arterial branches that approach from the medial side in the abdomen and from the lateral side in the pelvis — a key surgical principle for preserving ureteric viability.

▼3. Three Anatomic Ureteric Constrictions

Where stones get stuck

Stones characteristically impact at three physiologic narrowings:

1. Uretero-pelvic junction (UPJ) — at the renal pelvis 2. Pelvic brim — where the ureter crosses the common iliac bifurcation 3. Uretero-vesical junction (UVJ) — as the ureter enters the bladder wall
High-Yield: The UVJ is the narrowest point and the most common site of stone impaction. The oblique intramural course through the detrusor also prevents vesicoureteric reflux.
💡 Mnemonic — Three constrictions▼

"UPJ, Pelvic brim, UVJ" — top, middle, bottom. Another way: "Stones stop where the pipe kinks."

▼4. Referred Pain — "Loin to Groin"

Visceral innervation

The ureter is innervated by sympathetic fibers traveling with segments T11–L2. Because these cord levels also receive somatic afferents from the flank, scrotum/labium, and medial thigh, ureteric colic is felt as a classic loin-to-groin pain pattern.

Pain by stone location

Upper ureter stone → flank / CVA pain (T11–T12) Mid-ureter stone → iliac fossa / lower abdominal pain (L1) Lower ureter / UVJ stone → groin, scrotum/labium, inner thigh (L1–L2, genitofemoral n.); urinary urgency & dysuria
High-Yield: Ureteric colic patients are restless (writhe, cannot lie still) — unlike peritonitis patients who lie motionless. Hematuria (micro or gross) + colicky flank pain radiating to groin is the classic triad.

▼5. "Water Under the Bridge" — Surgical Pitfall

Ureter vs artery — pelvic crossings

Classic rule: "water under the bridge" — the ureter ("water") passes under the overlying artery ("bridge"):

Females: ureter passes under the uterine artery (at the level of the ischial spine, ~2 cm lateral to the cervix) Males: ureter passes under the vas deferens
⚠️ Exam Trap — Iatrogenic ureter injury▼

During hysterectomy, the most common site of ureteric injury is where the ureter passes under the uterine artery near the cervix. Post-operative flank pain + urinoma or fistula should prompt CT urogram.

During left hemicolectomy, the left ureter is at risk where it crosses the pelvic brim near the IMA — identify and protect it before ligating vessels.

▼6. Clinical Approach to Urolithiasis

Imaging & diagnosis

Non-contrast helical CT — gold standard, sensitivity >95% Ultrasound — first-line in pregnancy / children KUB plain film — calcium stones only (radiopaque); uric acid stones are radiolucent

Stone types & conditions

Calcium oxalate / phosphate (~80%) — envelope or dumbbell crystals Struvite (Mg-NH4-PO4) — coffin-lid, urease-producing bugs (Proteus, Klebsiella), forms staghorn calculi Uric acid — radiolucent, acidic urine (gout, tumor lysis) Cystine — hexagonal crystals, cystinuria (AR)

Management principles

Stones <5 mm usually pass spontaneously with hydration, analgesia, and tamsulosin (medical expulsive therapy). Stones >10 mm or with obstruction/infection require ureteroscopy, shock wave lithotripsy, or percutaneous nephrolithotomy (staghorn stones).

Testing Your Understanding (TYU)

TYU 1: Name the three anatomic constrictions of the ureter where stones typically impact, and identify the narrowest of the three.

From proximal to distal: (1) uretero-pelvic junction (UPJ), (2) pelvic brim where the ureter crosses the common iliac artery bifurcation, and (3) uretero-vesical junction (UVJ). The UVJ is the narrowest and most common site of stone impaction.

TYU 2: A 45-year-old man presents with sudden-onset right flank pain radiating to the scrotum and microscopic hematuria. What is the anatomic explanation for the radiation pattern?

Visceral afferents from the ureter enter the spinal cord at T11–L2. These same segments supply the flank, scrotum/labium, and medial thigh (via ilioinguinal and genitofemoral nerves), producing the classic "loin-to-groin" referred pain of ureteric colic. Radiation to the scrotum suggests a lower ureteric stone.

TYU 3: During a hysterectomy, the surgeon is concerned about protecting the ureter. At what anatomic landmark is the ureter most vulnerable to iatrogenic injury, and what is the classic rule describing its relationship to nearby structures?

The ureter passes posterior (under) to the uterine artery near the cervix, approximately 2 cm lateral to it at the level of the ischial spine. The mnemonic "water under the bridge" — water = ureter, bridge = uterine artery. This is the most common site of iatrogenic ureter injury during hysterectomy.

LO 5 · Innervation of the Adrenal Gland

Distinguish the innervation of different parts of the adrenal gland — appreciating why the cortex is hormonally regulated while the medulla receives direct sympathetic input.

Covers LO: 5 (distinguish the innervation of different parts of the adrenal gland)
In this tab:
  • 1. Adrenal gland — gross anatomy & layers
  • 2. Adrenal cortex — hormonal (not neural) control
  • 3. Adrenal medulla — direct sympathetic innervation
  • 4. Embryologic basis of the split
  • 5. Vascular reminder — 3 arteries, 1 vein (asymmetric)
  • 6. Clinical correlates (pheochromocytoma, Addisonian crisis)
  • 7. Testing Your Understanding (TYU)

▼1. Adrenal Gland — Gross Anatomy

Shape, location & layers

The paired suprarenal (adrenal) glands cap the superior poles of the kidneys within Gerota's fascia, but in their own fascial compartment (separate from the kidney — so they don't descend with ptotic kidneys).

Right adrenal — triangular (pyramidal) Left adrenal — semilunar (crescent)

Each gland has two functionally and embryologically distinct parts:

Cortex (outer) — steroid hormones Medulla (inner) — catecholamines

Cortical zones (outer → inner)

💡 Mnemonic — "GFR makes Salt, Sugar, Sex; deeper you go, sweeter it gets"▼
Zona Glomerulosa → Salt (aldosterone, mineralocorticoid) Zona Fasciculata → Sugar (cortisol, glucocorticoid) Zona Reticularis → Sex (androgens: DHEA)

▼2. Adrenal Cortex — Hormonal Control

No direct neural drive

The cortex has no direct autonomic innervation controlling its secretion. Steroid production is instead regulated by circulating hormones and local factors:

Zona glomerulosa — renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (angiotensin II); K+; ACTH (minor) Zona fasciculata — ACTH from the anterior pituitary (HPA axis) Zona reticularis — ACTH (and local factors)
High-Yield: Because the cortex is hormone-driven, loss of the pituitary (secondary adrenal insufficiency) spares the zona glomerulosa — aldosterone is preserved because it's driven by renin-angiotensin, not ACTH. This is why hyponatremia (from cortisol deficiency) occurs but hyperkalemia does not in secondary adrenal insufficiency.

▼3. Adrenal Medulla — Direct Sympathetic Innervation

Pre-ganglionic sympathetic fibers

The medulla is unique: it is innervated directly by pre-ganglionic sympathetic fibers (thoracic splanchnic nerves, T5–T11, primarily via the greater splanchnic nerve) that synapse on chromaffin cells. These cells function as modified post-ganglionic sympathetic neurons without axons — they release catecholamines (~80% epinephrine, ~20% norepinephrine) directly into the bloodstream.

High-Yield: Chromaffin cells use ACh at nicotinic receptors (the same neurotransmitter pre-ganglionic sympathetic neurons use at all autonomic ganglia). No second synapse exists — this is why the response is so rapid and the hormones act systemically.
⚠️ Exam Trap — Cortex vs medulla innervation▼

Do not confuse the two:

Cortex → hormonal (ACTH, angiotensin II); no direct innervation Medulla → direct pre-ganglionic sympathetic (T5–T11 splanchnics)

USMLE will ask what happens in a patient whose splanchnic nerves are transected — answer: catecholamine release is impaired, but cortisol/aldosterone are preserved.

▼4. Embryologic Basis of the Split

Two different origins

Cortex — mesoderm (coelomic mesothelium); behaves like an endocrine organ Medulla — neural crest (chromaffin cells); behaves like post-ganglionic sympathetic neurons

The dual origin explains the dual control system: the mesodermal cortex responds to circulating hormones, while the neural-crest medulla responds to direct neural input — the same logic that governs all sympathetic ganglia.

▼5. Vascular Reminder — 3 Arteries, 1 Vein

Arterial supply

Each adrenal receives blood from three arteries:

Superior suprarenal a. ← inferior phrenic a. Middle suprarenal a. ← abdominal aorta directly Inferior suprarenal a. ← renal a.

Venous drainage — asymmetric

Right suprarenal vein → directly into IVC (short) Left suprarenal vein → left renal vein (long; same asymmetry as gonadal v.)
High-Yield: This asymmetry matters for adrenal venous sampling in primary hyperaldosteronism — the right adrenal vein is short and notoriously hard to cannulate.

▼6. Clinical Correlates

Pheochromocytoma

Catecholamine-secreting tumor of chromaffin cells. Classic episodic triad: headache, palpitations, sweating, plus hypertension. Lab: elevated urinary/plasma metanephrines. Tx: α-blockade before β-blockade to avoid hypertensive crisis.

Addisonian crisis (primary adrenal insufficiency)

Loss of both cortex layers → hypotension, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, hypoglycemia, hyperpigmentation (from ↑ ACTH/MSH). Because the defect is cortical, catecholamine release is preserved.

Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome

Hemorrhagic adrenal infarction (classically with Neisseria meningitidis septicemia) → acute adrenal failure → shock.

Testing Your Understanding (TYU)

TYU 1: Which part of the adrenal gland receives direct autonomic innervation, and from which nerve fibers?

Only the adrenal medulla receives direct innervation — from pre-ganglionic sympathetic fibers (thoracic splanchnic nerves, T5–T11). Chromaffin cells act as modified post-ganglionic sympathetic neurons and release epinephrine/norepinephrine into the systemic circulation. The cortex has no direct neural control and is regulated hormonally (ACTH, angiotensin II).

TYU 2: How does the embryologic origin of the adrenal cortex vs medulla explain their different control mechanisms?

The cortex derives from mesoderm (coelomic epithelium) and behaves like a true endocrine organ regulated by circulating hormones (ACTH, angiotensin II). The medulla derives from neural crest and behaves like post-ganglionic sympathetic neurons — explaining its direct pre-ganglionic sympathetic innervation and catecholamine output.

TYU 3: A patient with a pituitary infarct develops cortisol deficiency. Why is aldosterone largely preserved?

Aldosterone release from the zona glomerulosa is primarily driven by the renin-angiotensin system and plasma K+, not ACTH. Loss of pituitary ACTH therefore causes cortisol (and androgen) deficiency while sparing aldosterone — so hyperkalemia is characteristically absent in secondary (central) adrenal insufficiency.

Practice Questions — USMLE Step 1 Style

Clinical vignettes covering all 5 LOs of the Retroperitoneum & Posterior Abdominal Wall lecture. Attempt each question before revealing the answer.

Covers LOs: 1 (IP vs RP), 2 (vessels), 3 (diaphragm), 4 (urolithiasis), 5 (adrenal innervation)

Q1. A 68-year-old man with a history of hypertension presents to the ED with sudden-onset severe tearing back pain and hypotension. FAST exam is negative for free intraperitoneal fluid, but CT shows a large retroperitoneal hematoma tracking down the psoas fascia. Which vessel is the most likely source?

Ruptured infrarenal abdominal aortic aneurysm. The aorta is a primary retroperitoneal structure, so rupture typically produces a retroperitoneal hematoma (not free intraperitoneal blood — FAST will be negative). Blood tracks along the psoas fascia and Gerota's fascia, and can present as flank ecchymosis (Grey-Turner sign). Infrarenal AAAs (below the renal arteries) account for the majority.

Q2. A 32-year-old woman presents with left flank pain and intermittent hematuria. Imaging shows compression of the left renal vein between the aorta and a second vessel. Which vessel is compressing the renal vein, and what is this syndrome called?

Nutcracker syndrome. The left renal vein travels anterior to the aorta and posterior to the superior mesenteric artery (SMA). Narrowing of the aorta-SMA angle compresses the vein, causing left flank pain, hematuria (from venous hypertension rupturing renal fornices) and, in males, a left-sided varicocele (the left gonadal vein drains into the left renal vein).

Q3. A 45-year-old man presents with severe colicky right flank pain radiating to the groin and microscopic hematuria. Non-contrast CT shows a 6-mm stone at the level of the pelvic brim on the right. Which anatomic landmark corresponds to the second constriction of the ureter?

The ureter crosses over the bifurcation of the common iliac artery at the pelvic brim — the second anatomic constriction (the first is the UPJ; the third and narrowest is the UVJ). Stones are most likely to impact at the UVJ, but the pelvic brim is the classic mid-ureter impaction site.

Q4. During a routine left hemicolectomy, the surgeon identifies and preserves the left ureter before ligating the IMA. What anatomic landmark is most useful for locating the left ureter in the retroperitoneum?

The ureter descends on the anterior surface of the psoas major muscle and is crossed anteriorly by the gonadal vessels. Near the IMA, it crosses the common iliac vessels at the pelvic brim. Identifying the ureter on psoas and tracing it down is the standard surgical maneuver — ligating the IMA without first identifying the ureter risks ligating it along with the vessels.

Q5. A trauma patient presents after blunt abdominal injury. Chest X-ray shows the stomach bubble in the left hemithorax, and a nasogastric tube curls up into the chest. Which injury is most likely, and why is it more common on this side?

Traumatic rupture of the left hemidiaphragm with gastric herniation into the thorax. Left-sided rupture is more common because the liver acts as a shield protecting the right hemidiaphragm from blunt force. Right-sided ruptures tend to be larger and herniate the liver.

Q6. A 28-year-old woman presents with sudden-onset LLQ pain and hemodynamic instability. FAST exam shows free fluid in the pelvis. She reports "pain in my left shoulder" when lying flat. What is the anatomic explanation for the shoulder pain?

Kehr sign. Blood (from a ruptured ectopic pregnancy or splenic injury) pooling under the left hemidiaphragm irritates the central diaphragm. The phrenic nerve (C3–C5) provides sensory innervation to the central diaphragm and shares spinal segments with the supraclavicular nerves, so pain is referred to the ipsilateral shoulder tip.

Q7. A 55-year-old man with a long history of GERD is found to have a large sliding hiatal hernia. Which diaphragmatic opening is affected, and at what vertebral level does it lie?

The esophageal hiatus at T10, located within the right crus of the diaphragm. It transmits the esophagus, the anterior and posterior vagal trunks, and esophageal branches of the left gastric vessels. Widening of this hiatus allows the GE junction to slide into the thorax.

Q8. During a gynecologic procedure, a surgeon encounters the ureter in the pelvis. Which mnemonic describes its relationship to the uterine artery?

"Water under the bridge." The ureter (water) passes posterior (under) to the uterine artery (bridge) approximately 2 cm lateral to the cervix at the level of the ischial spine. This is the most common site of iatrogenic ureter injury during hysterectomy. In males, the analogous crossing is under the vas deferens.

Q9. A 40-year-old woman with resistant hypertension is found to have a right adrenal mass. Laboratory work-up reveals episodic hypertension, tachycardia, sweating, and elevated urinary metanephrines. Which embryologic tissue gives rise to the cells of this tumor?

Neural crest. The diagnosis is pheochromocytoma — a tumor of chromaffin cells in the adrenal medulla. Chromaffin cells derive from neural crest and function as modified post-ganglionic sympathetic neurons. They are innervated directly by pre-ganglionic sympathetic fibers (T5–T11 splanchnics).

Q10. A patient with a pituitary infarct develops secondary adrenal insufficiency. Which of the following hormones is preserved, and what is the anatomic/physiologic explanation?

Aldosterone is preserved. Aldosterone release from the zona glomerulosa is regulated primarily by the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (not ACTH). Loss of pituitary ACTH causes cortisol and adrenal androgen deficiency, but aldosterone (and therefore Na+/K+ handling) is largely intact — distinguishing secondary from primary (Addisonian) adrenal insufficiency, which has hyperkalemia.

Q11. Which of the following abdominal organs is primarily intraperitoneal, not retroperitoneal?

Jejunum / ileum / transverse colon / sigmoid colon / stomach / liver / spleen / tail of pancreas are all intraperitoneal. Retroperitoneal (SAD PUCKER): Suprarenal, Aorta/IVC, Duodenum (2–4), Pancreas (head/neck/body), Ureters, Colon (ascending & descending), Kidneys, Esophagus (thoracic), Rectum. Tail of pancreas is intraperitoneal (in splenorenal ligament) — classic trap.

Q12. During which procedure is the azygos vein most likely to be encountered passing through the diaphragm alongside the aorta?

The aortic hiatus at T12 transmits the aorta, thoracic duct, and azygos vein. A classic exam distractor places the azygos with the IVC in the caval hiatus — remember azygos travels with the aorta, not the IVC.

Q13. A 60-year-old man is evaluated for cirrhosis. On exam he has distended, serpentine veins radiating from the umbilicus. Which portosystemic anastomosis is responsible for this finding?

Caput medusae: paraumbilical veins (portal) ↔ superficial epigastric veins (systemic caval) draining into the inferior epigastric, lateral thoracic, and superficial circumflex iliac veins. Other classic portosystemic anastomoses: esophageal varices (left gastric ↔ esophageal) and internal hemorrhoids (superior rectal ↔ middle/inferior rectal).

Q14. A 30-year-old thin woman develops post-prandial epigastric pain, early satiety, and bilious vomiting after significant weight loss. Imaging shows compression of the third part of the duodenum. What anatomic syndrome is this, and how does it relate to nutcracker syndrome?

SMA (Wilkie) syndrome. The third part of the duodenum is compressed between the aorta and the superior mesenteric artery (SMA) when the aorto-SMA angle narrows (normally >25°). This is the duodenum version of the same anatomic squeeze that compresses the left renal vein in nutcracker syndrome. Both worsen with weight loss (loss of mesenteric fat cushion).

Q15. A 48-year-old man presents with right flank ecchymosis and hemodynamic instability 2 days after severe blunt trauma. What is the name of this physical exam finding and what does it indicate?

Grey-Turner sign — flank ecchymosis indicating retroperitoneal hemorrhage. Blood tracking along the psoas fascia or renal fascia reaches the flank subcutaneous tissue. Cullen sign (periumbilical ecchymosis) has similar significance. Classic causes: severe hemorrhagic pancreatitis, ruptured AAA, ectopic pregnancy (peritoneal route).

Q16. Which of the following best explains why a left-sided varicocele is much more common than a right-sided varicocele in a young, otherwise healthy man?

The left gonadal (testicular) vein drains into the left renal vein at a right angle (longer path), while the right gonadal vein drains directly into the IVC obliquely (better flow). Left-sided venous pressure is higher, and any compression of the left renal vein (nutcracker) or renal vein pathology (e.g., renal cell carcinoma tumor thrombus) can produce or worsen a left varicocele. A new-onset left varicocele in an adult → rule out renal cell carcinoma.

Q17. A 22-year-old man undergoing unilateral nephrectomy for a congenital anomaly has an uncomplicated recovery. Why are renal segmental arteries clinically important to identify before partial nephrectomy?

Renal segmental arteries are end arteries — they do not anastomose with adjacent segments. Ligation of a segmental branch causes infarction of that entire renal segment. The kidney receives ~25% of cardiac output and typically has 5 segmental arteries per side. Accessory / polar renal arteries (present in ~25%) can easily be missed and must be identified to avoid parenchymal loss.

Q18. During spinal cord transection at T4, which of the following autonomic responses is most severely impaired?

Adrenal medullary catecholamine release. The medulla is innervated by pre-ganglionic sympathetic fibers originating from T5–T11 (greater splanchnic nerve). A cord transection above T5 disconnects all sympathetic outflow to the adrenal medulla, markedly blunting the epinephrine/norepinephrine stress response. The cortex (ACTH-driven, systemic) is preserved.
