▼ Thirst & Volume Regulation

⭐ Thirst is regulated by both osmolarity and blood volume, with hypothalamic osmoreceptors as the primary sensor.

💧 Osmotic Thirst

  • Stimulus: ↑ plasma osmolarity (even +1–2%) → shrinkage of hypothalamic osmoreceptors
  • Location: Anterior hypothalamus (organum vasculosum of lamina terminalis, OVLT)
  • Response: Thirst sensation + ADH (vasopressin) release from posterior pituitary → water retention
  • Threshold: Osmolarity >290 mOsm/kg triggers thirst; ADH release begins ~280 mOsm/kg

🧠 Hypovolemic Thirst

  • Stimulus: ↓ blood volume (≥10% decrease) → activates low-pressure atrial receptors and arterial baroreceptors
  • Pathway: ↓ renal perfusion → ↑ renin → ↑ angiotensin II → acts on subfornical organ (SFO) → thirst + ↑ ADH + ↑ aldosterone
  • Key mediator: Angiotensin II is the most potent dipsogen (thirst stimulus)
🎯 Step 1 High-Yield — Thirst
  • Osmolarity > volume in sensitivity: a small ↑ osmolarity triggers thirst more reliably than modest volume depletion
  • ADH threshold (280 mOsm) is lower than thirst threshold (290 mOsm) — body conserves water before you feel thirsty
  • Angiotensin II acts on the SFO (circumventricular organ — outside BBB) to stimulate thirst

▼ Hunger & Satiety Hormones

⭐ The hypothalamic arcuate nucleus integrates hunger and satiety signals via NPY/AgRP (hunger) and POMC/CART (satiety) neurons.

🍕 Ghrelin — "The Hunger Hormone"

  • Source: Gastric oxyntic glands (X/A-like cells) — primarily fundus of stomach
  • When released: Fasting state; levels peak just before meals
  • Receptor: GHS-R1a (growth hormone secretagogue receptor)
  • Mechanism: Acts on arcuate nucleus → stimulates NPY (neuropeptide Y) and AgRP neurons → ↑ appetite, ↑ food intake, ↑ fat storage
  • Additional effects: Stimulates GH release from anterior pituitary; promotes gastric emptying; prokinetic
  • After eating: Ghrelin falls rapidly post-meal
🧠 Mnemonic: GHRELIN = GRumbling stomach
▼

Ghrelin = GRowing hungry — rises before you eat, falls after. "Ghre-lin = Grow-lin" because it also stimulates GH release.

🧦 Peptide YY (PYY) — Satiety from the Gut

  • Source: L-cells of distal ileum and colon
  • When released: After eating; stimulated by fat and protein entering the distal gut
  • Mechanism: Acts on arcuate nucleus → inhibits NPY/AgRP neurons → ↓ appetite, ↓ food intake
  • Also known as: "Ileal brake" — slows gastric emptying and intestinal transit

🧮 GLP-1 (Glucagon-Like Peptide-1) — Incretin & Satiety

  • Source: L-cells of distal ileum and colon (same as PYY)
  • When released: After nutrient ingestion (especially carbohydrates and fats)
  • Key effects:
    • ↑ Insulin secretion (glucose-dependent) — the incretin effect
    • ↓ Glucagon secretion
    • ↓ Gastric emptying (slows GI transit)
    • Acts on hypothalamus → ↓ appetite (satiety)
  • Clinical pearl: GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide) exploit these effects for T2DM and obesity

🪸 Leptin — Long-Term Adiposity Signal

  • Source: Adipocytes (white adipose tissue); proportional to fat mass
  • Mechanism: Binds receptors in arcuate nucleus → inhibits NPY/AgRP neurons + activates POMC/CART neurons → ↓ appetite, ↑ energy expenditure
  • Key point: Long-term regulation (days–weeks); not meal-to-meal
  • In obesity: High leptin levels but leptin resistance (analogous to insulin resistance)
  • Starvation: ↓ Fat mass → ↓ leptin → ↑ NPY → ↑ hunger + ↓ metabolic rate + ↓ reproductive axis (amenorrhea)

⚠️ TRAP: Ghrelin vs. Leptin — Don't Confuse the Direction ▼
  • Ghrelin = the only hunger-stimulating gut hormone tested on Step 1. All other gut hormones (PYY, GLP-1, CCK) reduce appetite.
  • In obesity: leptin is HIGH (more fat = more leptin) but there is leptin resistance — do not say leptin is low in obesity.
  • After bariatric surgery, ghrelin falls and PYY/GLP-1 rise — mechanistically explaining reduced appetite post-surgery.

▼ Salivary Gland Anatomy & Composition

⭐ Saliva is produced at ~1.5 L/day; it lubricates, begins starch digestion, and provides antimicrobial protection.

🧞 Three Major Salivary Glands

Gland Type Primary Secretion Key Enzyme/Protein % of Total Saliva
Parotid Purely serous Watery, enzyme-rich α-amylase (ptyalin) ~25%
Submandibular Mixed (serous + mucous) Serous + mucus α-amylase + mucin ~70%
Sublingual Predominantly mucous Thick, lubricating Mucin (mucus glycoproteins) ~5%

🧪 Acinar Cells vs. Ductal Cells — Two-Stage Model

Saliva production occurs in two stages:

  • Stage 1 — Acinar cells (primary secretion): Produce an isotonic fluid similar to plasma in electrolyte composition (plasma-like Na+, K+, Cl⁻, HCO3⁻)
  • Stage 2 — Striated duct cells (modification):
    • Na+ is actively reabsorbed (not permeable to water → tubular fluid becomes hypotonic)
    • K+ is actively secreted
    • HCO3⁻ is secreted (relative HCO3⁻ excess vs. plasma)
    • Cl⁻ is reabsorbed

Net result: Final saliva is hypotonic to plasma, with ↑ K+, ↑ HCO3⁻, and ↓ Na+, ↓ Cl⁻ compared to plasma.

📈 Flow Rate & Tonicity Relationship

  • Low flow rate: More time in duct for Na+ reabsorption → maximally hypotonic saliva; K+ very high
  • High flow rate: Less modification time → saliva approaches isotonicity (more plasma-like); K+ concentration decreases slightly but remains above plasma
  • Key rule: Saliva is always hypotonic to plasma, regardless of flow rate
  • Na+ and K+ relationship with flow rate:
    • Na+ ↑ as flow rate ↑ (more plasma-like at high flow)
    • K+ ↓ slightly as flow rate ↑ (still always > plasma)
⚠️ TRAP: Salivary Electrolytes vs. Plasma — Common Exam Confusion ▼
  • Saliva has higher K+ and higher HCO3⁻ than plasma — reversed from what you might expect!
  • Saliva has lower Na+ and lower Cl⁻ than plasma
  • Saliva is always hypotonic to plasma (even at high flow rates)
  • In Sjögren's syndrome (↓ saliva) → dry mouth, ↓ dental protection, ↓ antimicrobial IgA → ↑ dental caries

▼ Autonomic Control of Salivary Secretion

⭐ Parasympathetics are the dominant controller of sustained salivary flow; both ANS divisions stimulate secretion but via different mechanisms and timescales.

🧠 Parasympathetic Control (Primary)

  • Cranial nerves:
    • CN IX (glossopharyngeal) → parotid gland (via otic ganglion)
    • CN VII (facial) → submandibular and sublingual glands (via submandibular ganglion)
  • Neurotransmitters: ACh (M3 receptors) and VIP (vasoactive intestinal peptide)
  • Effect: Sustained, large-volume watery secretion; vasodilation of gland blood vessels
  • Mechanism (ACh): IP3/Ca2+ pathway → ↑ Cl⁻ channel opening → water follows osmotically
  • Atropine: Blocks muscarinic receptors → dry mouth (xerostomia)

🧰 Sympathetic Control (Secondary)

  • Origin: T1–T3 preganglionic → superior cervical ganglion → postganglionic fibers to glands
  • Neurotransmitter: Norepinephrine (NE) → β-adrenergic receptors (primarily)
  • Effect:
    • Initial brief increase in saliva from myoepithelial cell contraction (squeezes pre-formed secretion)
    • Followed by vasoconstriction → reduced blood flow → cessation of secretion (dry mouth during stress)
    • Promotes protein-rich, viscous secretion (mucus)
  • Bottom line: Sympathetics produce small volume, viscous secretion; parasympathetics produce large volume, watery secretion

⚗️ Hormonal Modification of Saliva

Hormone Effect on Salivary Ducts Net Effect on Saliva
ADH (vasopressin) ↑ H2O reabsorption from ducts More concentrated saliva (↓ volume, ↑ solute concentration)
Aldosterone ↑ Na+ reabsorption + ↑ K+ secretion in ducts Even lower salivary Na+; higher K+ (similar to effect on renal tubule)

Note: Aldosterone acts on salivary ducts the same way it acts on renal collecting ducts — a useful Step 1 parallel to remember.

⚠️ TRAP: Vagus Nerve Does NOT Control Salivary Glands ▼
  • Students frequently write "vagus nerve" for salivary gland control. This is WRONG.
  • Salivary glands are controlled by CN VII (facial) and CN IX (glossopharyngeal) — NOT CN X (vagus)
  • The vagus controls most thoracic and abdominal viscera, but not the salivary glands
  • Parotid: CN IX via otic ganglion | Submandibular + sublingual: CN VII via submandibular ganglion
🎯 Step 1 High-Yield — Salivary Autonomic Control
  • PSNS = sustained, watery saliva (ACh + VIP); SNS = brief, viscous blip then cessation
  • Atropine → xerostomia (dry mouth) — blocks M3 receptors on acinar cells
  • Aldosterone effect on salivary ducts mimics renal collecting duct (↑ Na+ reabsorption, ↑ K+ secretion)
  • Salivary HCO3⁻ neutralizes gastric acid in the esophagus (protective)

🧠 Testing Your Understanding!

Testing Your Understanding — LO 1, Question 1

A researcher administers a compound that selectively blocks NPY/AgRP neurons in the hypothalamic arcuate nucleus. Predict what happens to appetite and energy balance. Which hormones would be most affected in their ability to regulate food intake?

Consider: What normally activates vs. inhibits NPY neurons? Think about ghrelin vs. leptin/PYY. What would chronic blockade do to body weight over time?

✓ Answer: Appetite decreases and the animal would lose weight over time.

Blocking NPY/AgRP neurons removes the primary hunger-promoting circuit in the arcuate nucleus. NPY normally stimulates food intake, decreases energy expenditure, and promotes fat storage. Blocking it would: (1) reduce basal hunger drive, (2) blunt the hunger response to ghrelin (which normally acts by activating NPY neurons), (3) not affect the satiety response to leptin/PYY/GLP-1 (which work by inhibiting NPY neurons — so there would be less to inhibit). Net result: chronically decreased food intake and progressive weight loss.

Testing Your Understanding — LO 1, Question 2

A patient with primary aldosteronism (Conn's syndrome) — chronically elevated aldosterone — undergoes salivary testing. Compare the expected salivary electrolyte composition to that of a healthy person. Explain the mechanism.

Consider: How does aldosterone act on the renal collecting duct? Does it have the same effect on salivary ducts? What happens to Na+ and K+ concentrations in the final saliva?

✓ Answer: Even lower salivary Na+; even higher salivary K+ (compared to a healthy control).

Aldosterone acts on salivary duct cells identically to how it acts on the renal collecting duct: it upregulates epithelial Na+ channels (ENaC) to increase Na+ reabsorption, and Na+/K+-ATPase to increase K+ secretion into the duct lumen. In Conn's syndrome, excess aldosterone would maximally drive this exchange, resulting in saliva with even less Na+ and more K+ than normal. This is an important Step 1 clinical parallel — aldosterone's effects are not kidney-specific.

▼ Esophageal Secretory Products

⭐ The esophagus is primarily a conduit, not a digestive organ. Its secretory products are limited and focused on lubrication and mucosal protection.

📈 Esophageal Secretory Products — Master List

Secretory Product Source Function
Mucus Esophageal mucous glands (submucosal, throughout esophagus) Lubricates food bolus passage; thin layer of protection against mild acid reflux
Mucus (cardiac type) Cardiac glands (at upper esophagus near pharynx and near gastroesophageal junction) Alkaline mucus secretion — extra protection near the GEJ where acid reflux risk is highest
Swallowed saliva (HCO3⁻-rich) Salivary glands (not esophageal, but protective in esophagus) Neutralizes refluxed gastric acid; clearance of acid after reflux events ("acid clearance")

The esophagus has NO digestive enzymes and NO acid secretion of its own. It produces only mucus for lubrication and protection.

💉 Factors Reducing Esophageal Protection

  • ↓ Salivary flow (e.g., Sjögren's, anticholinergics, radiation) → less HCO3⁻ available to neutralize reflux → ↑ esophageal acid exposure
  • GERD: Repeated acid reflux overwhelms mucus barrier → esophageal mucosa injury → heartburn → Barrett's esophagus (intestinal metaplasia of esophageal squamous epithelium) if chronic
  • Lower esophageal sphincter (LES) dysfunction: Not a secretory issue, but the primary defense against reflux reaching the esophageal mucosa
⚠️ TRAP: Esophageal vs. Gastric vs. Duodenal Mucosa — Different Vulnerabilities ▼
  • Esophageal mucosa = stratified squamous epithelium — has NO mucus-secreting cells on the surface (only submucosal glands) and no alkaline bicarbonate surface coat. Very vulnerable to acid.
  • Gastric mucosa = simple columnar epithelium with surface mucous cells secreting a thick alkaline gel (pH gradient from pH 2 in lumen to pH ~7 at cell surface).
  • Duodenal mucosa = protected by Brunner's glands (alkaline mucus) + pancreatic HCO3⁻ + bile.
  • This explains why GERD damages the esophagus first — it lacks the gastric-type mucosal armor.

▼ Gastric Gland Cell Types

⭐ The gastric gland is a tubular structure in the gastric mucosa containing specialized cells, each with distinct secretory products essential for digestion.

📈 Complete Cell Type Reference Table

Cell Type Location in Gland Secretory Product(s) Function / Key Details Stimulated By
Surface Mucous Cells Gastric surface + pit Alkaline mucus Forms protective gel layer (pH 6–7 at surface despite luminal pH 2); first line barrier Prostaglandins, ACh
Mucous Neck Cells Neck of gastric gland Soluble mucus Thinner mucus; also progenitor cells for mucosal renewal ACh
Parietal Cells (Oxyntic) Upper to mid gland body HCl + Intrinsic Factor (IF) HCl: activates pepsin, kills bacteria, denatures proteins. IF: essential for B12 absorption in terminal ileum ACh (M3), Gastrin (CCK2), Histamine (H2)
Chief Cells (Zymogenic) Base of gland Pepsinogen I & II + Gastric Lipase Pepsinogen: activated by HCl → pepsin (endopeptidase). Gastric lipase: begins fat digestion ACh, secretin, ↓ pH
G Cells Pyloric antrum Gastrin Stimulates parietal + chief cells; promotes gastric motility; trophic to GI mucosa Peptides/AAs in lumen, vagal ACh, stomach distension

ECL Cells (Enterochromaffin-like) Fundus / oxyntic gland Histamine + Serotonin Histamine amplifies parietal cell HCl secretion (H2 receptor); stimulated by gastrin Gastrin, ACh

🔬 Parietal Cell — The Most Tested Gastric Cell

  • Two unique products: HCl and Intrinsic Factor — only cell that makes IF
  • Stimulated by THREE pathways (all must be known):
    1. ACh via M3 (muscarinic) receptors → IP3/Ca2+ → ↑ H+/K+-ATPase
    2. Gastrin via CCK-B (CCK2) receptors → IP3/Ca2+ pathway (also stimulates ECL cells)
    3. Histamine via H2 receptors → cAMP/PKA → ↑ H+/K+-ATPase (most potent direct stimulator)
  • Clinical importance of IF: Autoimmune destruction of parietal cells (autoimmune atrophic gastritis) → ↓ IF → B12 malabsorption → pernicious anemia (megaloblastic)
🎯 Step 1 High-Yield — Gastric Cell Types
  • Pernicious anemia = autoimmune destruction of parietal cells → no IF → no B12 absorption (terminal ileum requires IF-B12 complex)
  • Zollinger-Ellison syndrome = gastrinoma (G-cell tumor) → massively elevated gastrin → hyperstimulated parietal cells → severe peptic ulcer disease + diarrhea
  • ECL cells are the intermediary: gastrin → ECL → histamine → parietal cell (this is why H2 blockers can partially reduce acid even when gastrin is elevated)
  • Chief cells produce pepsinogen (inactive zymogen) — HCl activates it to pepsin at pH < 2

▼ HCl Secretion Mechanism (Parietal Cell)

⭐ The parietal cell uses carbonic anhydrase and the H+/K+-ATPase (proton pump) to secrete H+ at concentrations 3 million times higher than blood.

⚗️ Step-by-Step HCl Secretion

  1. CO2 + H2O → H2CO3 (catalyzed by carbonic anhydrase inside parietal cell)
  2. H2CO3 → H+ + HCO3⁻ (spontaneous dissociation)
  3. H+ is pumped into the gastric lumen via H+/K+-ATPase (antiport — simultaneously imports K+)
  4. HCO3⁻ exits the basolateral side via HCO3⁻/Cl⁻ antiporter → enters blood → "alkaline tide"
  5. Cl⁻ (imported from blood) then exits into the gastric lumen through apical Cl⁻ channels → combines with H+ to form HCl
Summary Equation:
CO2 + H2O →CA H2CO3 → H+ + HCO3⁻
H+ → [H+/K+-ATPase] → gastric lumen
HCO3⁻ → [HCO3⁻/Cl⁻ antiport] → blood (alkaline tide)
Cl⁻ → [apical Cl⁻ channel] → gastric lumen
Net: secretion of HCl into stomach lumen

⚡ Rate-Limiting Step

The H+/K+-ATPase (proton pump) is the rate-limiting step. This is why PPIs (which block H+/K+-ATPase) are so effective at reducing gastric acid — they target the final, obligate step.

🌊 The Alkaline Tide

  • Definition: The rise in blood (and urine) pH that occurs after a meal
  • Mechanism: For every H+ pumped into the stomach, one HCO3⁻ is secreted into the blood via the HCO3⁻/Cl⁻ exchanger
  • Magnitude: Significant — can cause postprandial alkaline urine ("alkaline tide" measurable in urine)
  • Teleological logic: The body is "preparing" alkaline buffering capacity to neutralize food bolus entering the duodenum (secretin later triggers pancreatic HCO3⁻ release)
⚠️ TRAP: PPIs vs. H2 Blockers — Mechanism Distinction ▼
  • PPIs (e.g., omeprazole, pantoprazole): Irreversibly block H+/K+-ATPase (the proton pump itself). Most effective. Must be taken before meals (need active proton pumps). Requires acid activation (prodrug).
  • H2 blockers (e.g., ranitidine, cimetidine, famotidine): Block H2 receptors on parietal cells → reduce cAMP → less proton pump activity. Less effective than PPIs because other pathways (ACh, gastrin) can still stimulate the pump.
  • Key: H2 blockers block histamine signaling; PPIs block the pump itself — they work at different levels of the same pathway.
🎯 Step 1 High-Yield — HCl Mechanism
  • Rate-limiting step: H+/K+-ATPase (proton pump)
  • Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (acetazolamide) can ↓ HCl secretion — tested in glaucoma/altitude sickness context
  • Alkaline tide: Post-meal rise in blood HCO3⁻ due to parietal cell HCO3⁻/Cl⁻ exchange
  • In pyloric stenosis (projectile vomiting): lose HCl → hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis + hypokalemia (renal K+ wasting to preserve Na+ when Cl⁻ is low)

▼ Mucosal Barrier & Gastric Protection

⭐ The gastric mucosa protects itself from its own acid via a multilayer defense system.

🛡️ Layers of Gastric Defense

  • Mucus-bicarbonate barrier: Surface mucous cells secrete thick alkaline mucus gel; trapped HCO3⁻ creates a pH gradient (pH ~6–7 at cell surface, pH ~2 in lumen)
  • Tight junctions: Prevent back-diffusion of H+ into mucosa
  • Mucosal blood flow: Delivers HCO3⁻ and removes acid that penetrates; prostaglandins (PGE2, PGI2) maintain mucosal blood flow
  • Rapid cell turnover: Gastric epithelium renews every 3–5 days

💊 NSAIDs & Mucosal Injury

  • Mechanism: COX inhibition → ↓ prostaglandin synthesis → ↓ mucus/HCO3⁻ secretion + ↓ mucosal blood flow → erosions and ulcers
  • Prevention: Misoprostol (PGE1 analog) to restore mucosal protection; or take with food; or use selective COX-2 inhibitors (celecoxib)
  • NSAIDs also cause ulcers systemically via direct topical acid damage even at non-gastric sites when absorbed
⚠️ TRAP: Peptic Ulcer — Anterior vs. Posterior Duodenal Complications ▼
  • Anterior duodenal ulcer → perforation → free air under diaphragm (pneumoperitoneum)
  • Posterior duodenal ulcer → erodes gastroduodenal artery → massive upper GI bleed
  • Most common location for PUD: first part of duodenum (DU > GU overall in frequency)

🧠 Testing Your Understanding!

Testing Your Understanding — LO 2, Question 1

A patient with autoimmune atrophic gastritis presents with profound fatigue, macrocytic anemia, and neurological symptoms including subacute combined degeneration. What specific cell is being destroyed, what two products are lost, and how does each loss contribute to the clinical picture?

Consider: What does each parietal cell product do? Why would HCl loss alone not cause neurological symptoms? What extra step is needed for B12 absorption?

✓ Answer: Autoimmune destruction of parietal cells → loss of HCl + loss of Intrinsic Factor.

Loss of HCl: achlorhydria causes hypergastrinemia (no acid feedback inhibition of G cells), impaired protein digestion, and increased susceptibility to GI infections. HCl loss alone does not cause neurological symptoms. Loss of Intrinsic Factor: IF is the glycoprotein secreted exclusively by parietal cells that binds dietary B12 (cobalamin) and enables its absorption at the terminal ileum (cubam receptor). Without IF, B12 cannot be absorbed → B12 deficiency → megaloblastic anemia (macrocytosis, hypersegmented neutrophils) AND subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord (demyelination of dorsal columns + lateral corticospinal tracts → loss of vibration/proprioception and weakness). This is pernicious anemia.

Testing Your Understanding — LO 2, Question 2

Explain why, after a large meal, the urine becomes transiently more alkaline (the "alkaline tide"). Trace the specific molecular events from CO2 in the parietal cell to HCO3⁻ in the blood.

Consider: What happens to HCO3⁻ after H+ is secreted? Which transporter is responsible? Where does this HCO3⁻ go?

✓ Answer: Each H+ secreted by the parietal cell is coupled to extrusion of one HCO3⁻ into the blood.

Inside the parietal cell: CO2 + H2O → (carbonic anhydrase) → H2CO3 → H+ + HCO3⁻. The H+ is pumped into the gastric lumen by H+/K+-ATPase. The HCO3⁻ exits the basolateral side via the HCO3⁻/Cl⁻ antiporter (exchanges intracellular HCO3⁻ for extracellular Cl⁻). This HCO3⁻ enters the portal blood → raises blood pH → kidneys excrete more HCO3⁻ in urine → alkaline urine after meals. This phenomenon (the "alkaline tide") is measurable and proportional to the amount of acid secreted. The Cl⁻ imported from blood then exits apically into the stomach lumen with H+ to form HCl.

▼ Three Phases of Gastric Secretion

⭐ Gastric acid secretion is coordinated in three overlapping phases: cephalic (30–40% of acid), gastric (50–60%), and intestinal (5–10%).

🧠 Phase 1 — Cephalic Phase

  • Trigger: Sight, smell, taste, thought, or anticipation of food — before food enters the stomach
  • Pathway: Cerebral cortex/hypothalamus → dorsal vagal nucleus → vagus nerve (CN X) → postganglionic neurons → gastric enteric nervous system
  • Mediators (vagal):
    • Direct ACh on parietal cells (↑ HCl)
    • ACh on G cells (↑ gastrin → ↑ HCl)
    • ACh inhibits D cells → ↓ somatostatin → allows more gastrin release
    • GRP (gastrin-releasing peptide) from vagal nerve terminals → G cells → ↑ gastrin
  • Contribution: ~30–40% of total acid response to a meal
  • Clinical: Highly vagotomized patients (truncal vagotomy) lose this phase → reduced acid; also why sham feeding studies show acid response before any food is ingested

🥕 Phase 2 — Gastric Phase

  • Trigger: Food actually in the stomach — distension, proteins/amino acids, ↑ pH (food buffers acid)
  • Mediators:
    • Stomach distension → vagovagal reflex + local ENS → ↑ ACh → ↑ HCl
    • Peptides and amino acids in antrum → stimulate G cells → ↑ gastrin → ↑ HCl
    • ↓ Luminal pH (food temporarily buffers acid) → relieves inhibition of G cells → ↑ gastrin
  • Contribution: ~50–60% of total acid output
  • Feedback inhibition: When luminal pH <3, somatostatin from D cells inhibits G cells → prevents runaway acid production

🥘 Phase 3 — Intestinal Phase

  • Trigger: Chyme entering the duodenum
  • Initial stimulation: Amino acids/peptides in duodenum stimulate intestinal G cells → brief gastrin rise
  • Predominant effect = INHIBITION of gastric acid:
    • Acidic chyme (pH <4.5) → S-cells release secretin → inhibits gastrin release + ↓ HCl + ↑ pancreatic HCO3⁻
    • Fat + protein in duodenum → I-cells release CCK → inhibits gastric emptying (enterogastric reflex), ↑ pancreatic enzymes, ↑ gallbladder contraction
    • Fat + protein → K-cells release GIP → inhibits gastric acid + ↑ insulin secretion
    • Neural: enterogastric reflex (duodenal distension/acid/fat → inhibitory reflexes → ↓ gastric emptying and acid)
  • Contribution: ~5–10% of total acid (and mostly inhibitory)

📈 Three Phases — At a Glance

Phase Trigger Primary Mediators % Acid Secretion Net Effect
Cephalic Sight/smell/thought of food Vagus → ACh + GRP → gastrin 30–40% ↑ HCl
Gastric Food in stomach (distension, AAs) ACh, Gastrin, Histamine 50–60% ↑ HCl (dominant phase)
Intestinal Chyme in duodenum Secretin, CCK, GIP; enterogastric reflex 5–10% ↓ HCl (mainly inhibitory)
⚠️ TRAP: Which Phase Uses the Vagus? ▼
  • The cephalic phase is 100% vagal (CN X). Vagotomy eliminates the cephalic phase.
  • The gastric phase also has a vagal component (vagovagal reflex), but gastrin and local ENS reflexes can maintain it even after vagotomy.
  • The intestinal phase is largely hormonal (secretin, CCK, GIP) — vagotomy has minimal effect on it.

▼ Stimulators & Inhibitors of HCl Secretion

⭐ Three potent stimulators (histamine, acetylcholine, gastrin) converge on the parietal cell; multiple inhibitors act to turn off acid production.

⇧ Stimulators of HCl

Stimulator Source Receptor on Parietal Cell Second Messenger Potency
Histamine ECL cells (gastric fundus) H2 receptor cAMP → PKA Most potent direct stimulator
Acetylcholine (ACh) Vagal + ENS neurons M3 (muscarinic) IP3/Ca2+ Moderate
Gastrin G cells (antrum) CCK-B (CCK2) IP3/Ca2+ Moderate (also via ECL→histamine)

These three stimulators act synergistically — blocking one (e.g., H2 blockers) reduces the effectiveness of the others. This is the basis for combination therapy.

⇩ Inhibitors of HCl

  • Somatostatin (D cells): Stimulated by low luminal pH (<3), fat, secretin → inhibits G cells (↓ gastrin) and parietal cells directly; the primary "off switch"
  • Secretin (S cells): Released when duodenal pH <4.5; inhibits gastrin release + gastric acid; stimulates pancreatic HCO3⁻
  • CCK (I cells): Released by fat + protein; inhibits gastric emptying; weak direct acid inhibition
  • GIP (K cells): Released by fat + AAs + glucose; inhibits gastric acid; strong incretin effect (↑ insulin)
  • Prostaglandins (PGE2, PGI2): Inhibit parietal cell cAMP → ↓ HCl; maintain mucosal blood flow; hence NSAIDs (block PG synthesis) → ↑ acid injury
🧠 Mnemonic: HCl Stimulators = "HAG"
▼

Histamine · ACh · Gastrin — all stimulate parietal cell HCl secretion. All three are needed together for maximal acid output.

▼ H. pylori Pathophysiology

⭐ Helicobacter pylori is a gram-negative microaerophilic rod that colonizes the gastric antrum and is the #1 cause of peptic ulcer disease worldwide.

🧰 H. pylori Virulence Mechanisms

  • Urease: Hydrolyzes urea → NH3 (ammonia) + CO2 → ammonia neutralizes local H+ → creates protective alkaline microenvironment around the bacteria; also directly toxic to epithelium
  • Mucinase: Degrades mucin in the protective mucus gel → disrupts the mucus-bicarbonate barrier → allows acid and pepsin direct access to epithelium
  • CagA protein (cytotoxin-associated gene A): Virulence factor injected into host epithelial cells → promotes inflammation, cellular proliferation, and increased cancer risk
  • VacA toxin: Forms pores in epithelial cell membranes → vacuolation and cell death
  • Phospholipase A: Degrades phospholipid layer of mucus barrier

📎 Consequences of H. pylori Infection

  • Type B (antral) gastritis: Chronic active inflammation of antrum → damages D cells (↓ somatostatin) → G cells disinhibited → ↑ gastrin → ↑ HCl → duodenal ulcers
  • Peptic ulcer disease (PUD): DU 90%, GU 70–80% H. pylori positive
  • Gastric ulcers: H. pylori can directly infect gastric corpus → loss of parietal cell mass → achlorhydria → hypergastrinemia
  • Gastric cancer: Chronic H. pylori gastritis → intestinal metaplasia → dysplasia → adenocarcinoma (WHO Class I carcinogen). CagA+ strains have higher cancer risk.
  • MALT lymphoma: H. pylori-driven chronic antigenic stimulation of gastric mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue → B-cell lymphoma; can regress with H. pylori eradication!

⚗️ Diagnosis and Treatment

  • Urea breath test: Patient ingests 13C-labeled urea; H. pylori urease cleaves it → 13CO2 detected in exhaled breath. Non-invasive, best for confirming eradication.
  • Stool antigen test: Detects H. pylori antigens in feces. Non-invasive screening.
  • CLO test (biopsy): Rapid urease test on endoscopic biopsy sample
  • Serology: IgG antibodies — doesn't distinguish current vs. past infection; not useful for eradication confirmation
  • Treatment — Triple Therapy: PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin (or metronidazole) x 7–14 days
  • Quadruple therapy (if resistance): PPI + bismuth + metronidazole + tetracycline
⚠️ TRAP: H. pylori Makes LESS Acid in Some Patients ▼
  • H. pylori infection of the antrum (most common) → destroys D cells → ↓ somatostatin → ↑ gastrin → ↑ acid → duodenal ulcers
  • H. pylori infection of the corpus (body/fundus) → destroys parietal cells → ↑ gastrin (from loss of feedback) but ↓ HCl output → gastric ulcers + gastric cancer risk
  • So the same organism can cause opposite acid effects depending on which part of the stomach is infected. The clinical manifestation (DU vs. GU) depends on the distribution of infection.

📌 Vagotomy — Surgical Acid Reduction

  • Truncal vagotomy: Cuts both vagal trunks → eliminates cephalic phase and vagal gastric phase → significant ↓ acid; but also impairs gastric emptying → requires drainage procedure (pyloroplasty)
  • Highly selective vagotomy (parietal cell vagotomy): Denervates only acid-secreting mucosa; spares antrum (no gastric stasis); no drainage procedure needed; lower ulcer recurrence than truncal
  • Largely replaced by PPIs for medical management, but still relevant surgically for refractory cases
🎯 Step 1 High-Yield — Drugs & Acid
  • PPIs are irreversible — recovery requires synthesis of new H+/K+-ATPase (takes ~18 h); H2 blockers are competitive/reversible
  • Cimetidine inhibits CYP1A2, CYP2C9, CYP2D6 → multiple drug interactions (warfarin, theophylline); also causes gynecomastia (anti-androgen effect)
  • PPIs long-term: ↑ risk of C. difficile, community-acquired pneumonia, hypomagnesemia, osteoporosis (↓ Ca2+ absorption)
  • Misoprostol is used both for ulcer prophylaxis (with NSAIDs) AND as an abortifacient — know both uses

Testing Your Understanding — LO 3, Question 1

A medical student is studying a famous experiment in which a subject merely looks at and smells food without actually eating it (sham feeding). Remarkably, gastric acid secretion increases significantly. Which neural pathway mediates this, and what would happen if the subject had previously undergone a truncal vagotomy?

Consider: Which phase of gastric secretion is initiated by sight/smell of food? Which cranial nerve is the main conductor? What percentage of total acid does this phase contribute, and what would eliminating it do?

✓ Answer: This is the cephalic phase, mediated entirely by the vagus nerve (CN X).

The cephalic phase is triggered by cortical/hypothalamic responses to the sight, smell, and thought of food. The signal travels from the dorsal vagal nucleus via CN X to the stomach, where it: (1) directly stimulates parietal cells via ACh (M3), (2) stimulates G cells via GRP → ↑ gastrin, and (3) inhibits D cells → ↓ somatostatin → further disinhibiting G cells. The cephalic phase accounts for ~30–40% of total acid output from a meal. After truncal vagotomy, the cephalic phase is completely eliminated, so sham feeding would produce little to no gastric acid response. The remaining acid secretion (gastric and intestinal phases) would be partially preserved via local ENS reflexes and hormonal pathways.

Testing Your Understanding — LO 3, Question 2

A 45-year-old man is found to have H. pylori antral gastritis. Explain the chain of events from H. pylori colonizing the antrum to the development of a duodenal ulcer, naming each molecular step.

Consider: H. pylori → effect on D cells → effect on somatostatin → effect on G cells → gastrin → parietal cells → acid in duodenum → mucosal damage.

✓ Answer: H. pylori antral colonization → antral D cell damage → ↓ somatostatin → G cells disinhibited → ↑ gastrin → ↑ parietal cell HCl → duodenal mucosal injury.

H. pylori's urease and mucinase disrupt the antral mucosa, inflaming and destroying D cells (which normally secrete somatostatin as a paracrine inhibitor of G cells). With less somatostatin, antral G cells are disinhibited → chronically elevated gastrin → maximal stimulation of parietal cells → high acid output. This acid overwhelms the duodenal mucosal defenses (Brunner's glands and bicarbonate secretion cannot keep up) → duodenal mucosal erosion → duodenal ulcer. The #1 location is the first part of the duodenum (duodenal bulb). Treatment: H. pylori eradication with triple therapy reverses the elevated gastrin and allows ulcer healing.

▼ Pancreatic Enzyme Activation Cascade

⭐ Pancreatic proteases are secreted as inactive zymogens to prevent autodigestion. Activation occurs in the duodenal lumen via a tightly controlled cascade.

▶ The Activation Cascade

Step 1: Trypsinogen → Enterokinase (brush border enzyme, duodenum) → Trypsin
Step 2: Trypsin activates ALL other zymogens:
   Chymotrypsinogen → Chymotrypsin
   Proelastase → Elastase
   Procarboxypeptidase → Carboxypeptidase
   Pro-colipase → Colipase (lipase cofactor)
   Prophospholipase A2 → Phospholipase A2
Step 3: Colipase anchors lipase to lipid droplet → enables fat digestion

Key: Enterokinase is the trigger — it's not a pancreatic enzyme; it's a duodenal brush border enzyme. Enterokinase deficiency → protein malabsorption (cannot activate trypsin).

🛡️ Pancreatic Self-Protection

  • Secretory trypsin inhibitor (SPINK1): Stored in zymogen granules with trypsinogen; inactivates any trypsin accidentally activated within the acinar cell
  • Compartmentalization: Zymogens kept in membrane-bound granules until exocytosis
  • pH: Pancreatic juice is alkaline (high HCO3⁻); proteases work best at neutral-alkaline pH, but autoactivation is minimized without enterokinase

Fat + protein in duodenum CCK (I-cells) ↑ Enzyme secretion (acinar cells); also potentiates secretin effect on HCO3⁻
Cephalic/gastric phase Vagus (ACh) ↑ Enzyme secretion (acinar); minor HCO3⁻ increase

⚠️ TRAP: Acute Pancreatitis — Why Premature Activation is Catastrophic ▼
  • Acute pancreatitis occurs when zymogens are activated within the pancreas (not in the duodenum)
  • Causes: gallstone obstructing the pancreatic duct, alcohol (disrupts zymogen granules), hyperlipidemia, trauma
  • Activated phospholipase A2 → fat necrosis + saponification → Ca2+ sequestration → hypocalcemia (Chvostek's sign, Trousseau's sign)
  • Elastase → digests blood vessel walls → hemorrhagic pancreatitis → periumbilical bruising (Cullen's sign) or flank bruising (Grey Turner's sign)
🎯 Step 1 High-Yield — Enzyme Cascade
  • Enterokinase is the master trigger — brush border enzyme, not pancreatic
  • Trypsin activates all other proteolytic zymogens (plus colipase and phospholipase A2)
  • Lipase is active as secreted but requires colipase (activated by trypsin) to function in the presence of bile salts
  • Amylase is unique: secreted in active form (no zymogen), not harmful to pancreas
  • In cystic fibrosis: CFTR mutation → thick, viscous secretions → blocked pancreatic ducts → autodigestion → exocrine pancreatic insufficiency → fat malabsorption + steatorrhea

▼ Intestinal Secretions Overview

⭐ The small intestinal mucosa secretes fluid, ions, and protective substances via specialized cells in the crypts of Lieberkühn and Brunner's glands.

🧪 Crypts of Lieberkühn

  • Location: Base of intestinal villi throughout the small and large intestine
  • Function: Secrete isotonic fluid (NaCl + water) to lubricate and solubilize digested food for absorption; serve as progenitor cell niche
  • Primary secretory mechanism: CFTR (Cl⁻ channel) at apical membrane → Cl⁻ secretion into lumen → Na+ follows paracellularly → water follows osmotically
  • Stimulated by: cAMP (VIP, secretin), cGMP (heat-stable enterotoxins), Ca2+ (ACh, VIP at high doses)

⚗️ CFTR & Chloride Secretory Diarrhea

  • Normal CFTR function: cAMP activates PKA → phosphorylates CFTR → opens Cl⁻ channel → Cl⁻ secretion → draws water into lumen
  • Cholera toxin (V. cholerae): Permanently activates Gs (adenylate cyclase) → cAMP → massive CFTR activation → rice-water diarrhea (up to 20 L/day)
  • E. coli heat-labile toxin (LT): Same mechanism as cholera — activates Gs via ADP-ribosylation
  • E. coli heat-stable toxin (ST): Activates guanylate cyclase → ↑ cGMP → ↑ CFTR activity → secretory diarrhea
  • Cystic fibrosis: Loss-of-function CFTR → thick mucus plugging intestinal glands → meconium ileus (newborn), distal intestinal obstruction syndrome (older children), fat malabsorption

💉 Brunner's Glands (Submucosal Glands of the Duodenum)

  • Location: Submucosa of the duodenum (only part of GI tract with submucosal glands)
  • Products:
    • Thick, alkaline mucus (HCO3⁻-rich) — primary role
    • Urogastrone (EGF) — promotes mucosal cell proliferation
    • Pepsinogen (minor)
  • Function: Protects duodenal mucosa from the acid chyme entering from the stomach; neutralizes luminal acid immediately
  • Stimulated by: Secretin (primary), vagal stimulation (ACh), ↓ luminal pH
  • Inhibited by: Sympathetic stimulation (epinephrine → vasoconstriction → ↓ gland activity)
  • Clinical pearl: Brunner's gland hyperplasia can cause duodenal polyposis; stress (↑ SNS tone) impairs Brunner's glands → contributes to stress ulcers
⚠️ TRAP: Paneth Cells Only in the Small Intestine ▼
  • Paneth cells are normally absent from the colon. Their presence in colon biopsies is called Paneth cell metaplasia and is a pathological finding associated with chronic mucosal injury (e.g., Crohn's disease, ulcerative colitis).
  • Paneth cells secrete defensins (antimicrobial peptides) — mutations in defensin genes are associated with increased Crohn's disease risk.
  • Do not confuse Paneth cells (in crypts, defense function) with Chief cells (in gastric glands, pepsinogen).

Testing Your Understanding — LO 4, Question 2

Explain why cholera causes such massive diarrhea (up to 20 L/day). What is the specific molecular target of cholera toxin, and why can't the cell "turn off" the signal?

Consider: What second messenger is involved? Which protein does cholera toxin modify, and how? What does CFTR do when maximally activated?

✓ Answer: Cholera toxin ADP-ribosylates Gsα → locks it in active state → constitutive adenylate cyclase activation → maximal cAMP → CFTR wide open → massive Cl⁻ secretion → Na+ and water follow.

Cholera toxin enters intestinal epithelial cells and ADP-ribosylates the α-subunit of the stimulatory G protein (Gsα), preventing GTP hydrolysis. Normally, Gsα inactivates itself by hydrolyzing GTP to GDP; this modification locks it in the active (GTP-bound) form permanently. Gsα continuously activates adenylate cyclase → continuous cAMP production → PKA constitutively active → CFTR apical Cl⁻ channels fully open → massive Cl⁻ efflux into lumen → Na+ follows paracellularly → water follows osmotically → "rice water" secretory diarrhea. The diarrhea is isotonic and enormous because absorption continues (Na+-glucose co-transport) — this is why oral rehydration solution (ORS with glucose) is so effective: glucose drives Na+ absorption even while CFTR-mediated secretion is maximal.

▼ Colonic Mucus Secretion

⭐ The colon secretes less fluid than the small intestine but maintains critical mucus and electrolyte secretion for fecal lubrication and protection.

🧞 Goblet Cells in the Colon

  • Density: Goblet cells are more numerous in the colon than in the small intestine — the colon relies more on mucus-based protection than enzymatic processing
  • Product: Mucin 2 (MUC2) — the major secreted mucin of the colon; forms a two-layer gel:
    • Inner (sterile) layer: firmly attached to epithelium, bacteria-free
    • Outer layer: loosely attached, inhabited by commensal bacteria
  • Functions:
    • Lubricates fecal matter for transit
    • Barrier against bacterial invasion (inner layer is impenetrable to bacteria)
    • Contains secretory IgA (from plasma cells) and antimicrobial peptides
  • Stimulated by: PSNS (ACh via M3), ENS reflexes, local inflammatory mediators
  • Inhibited by: SNS (epinephrine reduces mucus secretion)

▼ Colonic Bicarbonate & Potassium Secretion

⭐ Unlike the small intestine, the colon is a net secretor of HCO3⁻ and K+, while being a net absorber of Na+ and water.

⚗️ Bicarbonate Secretion (HCO3⁻/Cl⁻ Exchange)

  • Mechanism: HCO3⁻/Cl⁻ antiporter on apical membrane → secretes HCO3⁻ into colonic lumen in exchange for Cl⁻ absorption
  • Function: Neutralizes acids produced by colonic bacteria (short-chain fatty acids from fermentation); maintains luminal pH
  • Stimulated by: Short-chain fatty acids (butyrate, propionate, acetate) from bacterial fermentation; these serve as the primary energy source for colonocytes
  • Clinical relevance: In diarrhea, loss of colonic HCO3⁻ → non-anion gap metabolic acidosis (hyperchloremic)

⇧ Potassium Secretion

  • Mechanism: K+ secreted into colonic lumen via apical K+ channels (electrogenic); passive secretion down electrochemical gradient
  • Regulated by: Aldosterone — increases K+ secretion in colon (same as renal collecting duct); also increases Na+ absorption
  • Clinical significance:
    • Severe diarrhea → massive K+ loss → hypokalemia
    • VIPoma (vasoactive intestinal peptide-secreting tumor) → watery diarrhea, hypokalemia, achlorhydria (WDHA syndrome / "pancreatic cholera")
    • Secretory diarrhea: stool K+ concentration very high (unlike osmotic diarrhea)
⚠️ TRAP: Diarrhea Causes Metabolic Acidosis, Not Alkalosis ▼
  • Vomiting = loss of HCl → hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis
  • Diarrhea = loss of HCO3⁻ from the colon → hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis (non-anion gap) + hypokalemia
  • This is a classic board question: identify the acid-base disturbance from a patient with prolonged diarrhea — expect metabolic acidosis with normal anion gap and ↓ K+

Testing Your Understanding — LO 5, Question 1

A patient is found to have a pancreatic tumor secreting somatostatin (somatostatinoma). Predict the clinical triad of symptoms, explaining the mechanism behind each finding from a GI hormone perspective.

✓ Answer: Classic triad of somatostatinoma: Diabetes mellitus + Cholelithiasis (gallstones) + Steatorrhea (with achlorhydria).

Diabetes: Somatostatin inhibits pancreatic insulin AND glucagon secretion; the net effect is dominant inhibition of insulin → hyperglycemia/diabetes. Gallstones: Somatostatin inhibits CCK release and directly inhibits gallbladder smooth muscle contraction → bile stasis → cholelithiasis (pigment or cholesterol stones from poor GB emptying). Steatorrhea: Somatostatin inhibits CCK → ↓ pancreatic lipase and other enzyme secretion → fat maldigestion → fat malabsorption → steatorrhea. Testing Your Understanding — LO 5, Question 2

Explain why a patient with a VIPoma presents with severe watery diarrhea AND hypokalemia AND achlorhydria. What is the specific molecular mechanism linking elevated VIP to each component?

Question 1

A 28-year-old woman with a 2-year history of obesity undergoes Roux-en-Y gastric bypass. Six months postoperatively, she reports markedly reduced hunger despite eating only small meals. Which of the following best explains the hormonal changes responsible for her reduced appetite?

A. Increased ghrelin secretion from the bypassed gastric fundus

B. Decreased secretion of peptide YY and GLP-1 due to reduced caloric intake

C. Decreased ghrelin with increased PYY and GLP-1 due to altered anatomy

D. Increased leptin production from enlarged adipose stores

E. Decreased activity of NPY/AgRP neurons due to leptin hypersensitivity

✓ Correct: C — Decreased ghrelin with increased PYY and GLP-1 due to altered anatomy

Roux-en-Y gastric bypass has several hormonal effects: (1) Ghrelin falls — the gastric fundus (primary ghrelin source) is excluded from the alimentary limb → less ghrelin stimulation of NPY/AgRP → less hunger. (2) Food now enters the jejunum rapidly → early, robust stimulation of L-cells in the distal gut → ↑ PYY and GLP-1 → satiety and reduced gastric emptying. Choice A is wrong: ghrelin falls, not rises. B is wrong: PYY/GLP-1 actually increase. D is wrong: 6 months post-op, she has lost weight → less adipose → less leptin. E has no evidence base for leptin hypersensitivity post-bypass.

Question 2

A researcher collects saliva from a subject at rest (low flow rate) and again after maximal gustatory stimulation (high flow rate). Which of the following correctly describes how the electrolyte composition of saliva changes with increasing flow rate?

A. Na+ decreases; K+ increases; osmolarity increases toward isotonicity

B. Na+ increases; K+ decreases slightly; osmolarity remains far below plasma

C. Na+ increases; K+ increases; osmolarity rises to become isotonic with plasma

D. Na+ decreases; K+ remains unchanged; osmolarity remains hypotonic

E. Na+ remains constant; K+ decreases; osmolarity becomes hypertonic

✓ Correct: B — Na+ increases; K+ decreases slightly; osmolarity remains far below plasma

At low flow rates, ductal cells have more time to reabsorb Na+ and secrete K+ → saliva is very hypotonic with very low Na+ and very high K+. At high flow rates, there is less time for ductal modification → the saliva is closer to the primary (acinar) secretion, which is isotonic and plasma-like → Na+ rises (becomes more like plasma), K+ decreases slightly (less time for K+ secretion). However, saliva is ALWAYS hypotonic to plasma — it never reaches isotonicity at any physiological flow rate. Choice C (isotonic) is wrong. Choice A reverses the Na+ direction. Choice E (hypertonic) is never correct for saliva.

Question 3

A 65-year-old woman is prescribed a medication for overactive bladder. Two weeks later, she reports dry mouth and difficulty swallowing. Which of the following is the most likely mechanism by which this drug caused xerostomia?

A. Activation of H2 receptors on acinar cells, reducing cAMP

B. Blockade of M3 muscarinic receptors on salivary acinar cells, reducing IP3/Ca2+ signaling

C. Stimulation of alpha-1 adrenergic receptors, causing vasoconstriction of salivary glands

D. Blockade of nicotinic receptors at the parasympathetic ganglion

E. Inhibition of the submandibular ganglion by CN IX

✓ Correct: B — Blockade of M3 muscarinic receptors on salivary acinar cells, reducing IP3/Ca2+ signaling

Overactive bladder is treated with anticholinergic drugs (e.g., oxybutynin, tolterodine, solifenacin) that block M3 muscarinic receptors. Question 4

A 52-year-old woman presents with progressive fatigue, a beefy-red, smooth tongue, and tingling in her hands and feet. Lab work shows macrocytic anemia with hypersegmented neutrophils. Serum gastrin is markedly elevated. Anti-parietal cell antibodies are positive. Which of the following best explains her elevated gastrin?

A. A gastrin-secreting tumor in the pancreas (gastrinoma)

B. Loss of HCl production eliminating the normal acid-mediated inhibition of G cells

C. Excess vagal stimulation of G cells via GRP

D. Decreased somatostatin from D cell hyperplasia

E. Increased CCK from excess fat in the duodenum

✓ Correct: B — Loss of HCl production eliminating normal acid-mediated inhibition of G cells

This is pernicious anemia from autoimmune atrophic gastritis. Anti-parietal cell antibodies destroy parietal cells → no HCl and no intrinsic factor. Normally, low luminal pH (<3) stimulates D cells to release somatostatin → inhibits G cells → limits gastrin. Without HCl, the luminal pH remains high → no somatostatin release → G cells are tonically active → hypergastrinemia. The elevated gastrin cannot stimulate acid because the parietal cells are destroyed. Neurological symptoms (tingling, subacute combined degeneration) + megaloblastic anemia confirm B12 deficiency from IF loss. Choice A (gastrinoma) would not show anti-parietal cell antibodies. Choices C, D, E do not fit this clinical scenario.

Question 5

A pharmacology student is reviewing the mechanism of a new acid-suppressing drug. The drug is a prodrug that is activated by acid in the secretory canaliculi of parietal cells, after which it irreversibly binds its target by forming a disulfide bond. Which of the following is the rate-limiting step in HCl production that this drug targets?

A. Carbonic anhydrase catalyzing CO2 + H2O → H2CO3

B. H2CO3 dissociation into H+ and HCO3⁻

C. HCO3⁻/Cl⁻ antiporter at the basolateral membrane

D. H+/K+-ATPase (proton pump) at the apical secretory canalicular membrane

E. Cl⁻ channel at the apical membrane secreting Cl⁻ into the lumen

✓ Correct: D — H+/K+-ATPase (proton pump) at the apical secretory canalicular membrane

The drug described is a proton pump inhibitor (PPI) — activated by acid in the secretory canaliculi of the parietal cell → forms a sulfenamide → irreversibly binds cysteine residues of H+/K+-ATPase → inactivates the pump. The H+/K+-ATPase is the rate-limiting step in HCl secretion — it directly pumps H+ into the canalicular lumen against a concentration gradient of 3 million-fold. Without this pump, no H+ reaches the gastric lumen regardless of upstream signaling. Choice A (carbonic anhydrase) is not the rate-limiting step and is not targeted by PPIs. Choices B, C, E are not the rate-limiting steps. PPIs are the most effective acid suppressants precisely because they target the final obligatory step.

Question 6

A 35-year-old man undergoes endoscopy for recurrent peptic ulcers. Biopsy of the gastric fundus shows loss of parietal cells and chief cells with replacement by intestinal-type epithelium (intestinal metaplasia). Which of the following digestive functions would be MOST compromised?

A. Digestion of starch by α-amylase in the duodenum

B. Activation of vitamin B12 absorption in the terminal ileum

C. Secretion of CCK from I-cells in response to fat

D. Activation of pancreatic zymogens by enterokinase

E. Synthesis of cholecystokinin from the duodenal mucosa

✓ Correct: B — Activation of vitamin B12 absorption in the terminal ileum

Loss of parietal cells eliminates both HCl and Intrinsic Factor (IF). IF is exclusively made by parietal cells and is required for B12 absorption at the terminal ileum (cubam receptor). Without IF, dietary B12 cannot be absorbed → B12 deficiency → megaloblastic anemia and neurological complications. While HCl loss also impairs protein digestion (no pepsin activation) and kills bacteria, the question asks what is MOST compromised — B12 absorption has no backup mechanism (unlike, say, protein digestion, which can be partially maintained by pancreatic proteases). Choice A: amylase is from salivary glands and pancreas — not affected. Choices C, D, E: these are duodenal functions unrelated to gastric parietal cells.

Question 7

During an experiment, a patient smells and sees food but does not eat (sham feeding). Gastric acid output rises to 40% of maximal. This response is completely abolished by atropine. A week later, the experiment is repeated after truncal vagotomy — no acid response is observed with sham feeding. Which phase of gastric secretion and which cranial nerve are responsible?

A. Gastric phase; CN IX (glossopharyngeal)

B. Intestinal phase; CN VII (facial)

C. Cephalic phase; CN X (vagus)

D. Cephalic phase; CN IX (glossopharyngeal)

E. Gastric phase; CN X (vagus)

✓ Correct: C — Cephalic phase; CN X (vagus)

The cephalic phase is triggered by sight, smell, and anticipation of food — before food enters the stomach. It is mediated 100% by CN X (vagus nerve) via the dorsal motor nucleus. Question 8

A 45-year-old man with confirmed H. pylori infection is started on "triple therapy." The treatment regimen includes a proton pump inhibitor, clarithromycin, and amoxicillin. After completing 14 days of therapy, a urea breath test is performed to confirm eradication. The test involves ingestion of 13C-labeled urea. If H. pylori has been successfully eradicated, what result is expected?

B. No labeled 13CO2 will be detected in exhaled breath, confirming eradication

E. Increased ammonia in the stool, confirming successful eradication

✓ Correct: B — No labeled 13CO2 will be detected in exhaled breath, confirming eradication

The urea breath test relies on H. pylori's urease enzyme: if H. pylori is present, its urease cleaves the ingested 13C-labeled urea → 13CO2 + NH3 → 13CO2 is absorbed into blood → exhaled → detected in breath. Question 9

A pharmaceutical company develops a new drug that acts as a specific agonist at prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) receptors on gastric parietal and mucosal cells. Which of the following best describes the expected GI effects of this drug?

A. Increased HCl secretion and decreased mucosal blood flow

B. Decreased HCl secretion, increased mucus secretion, and maintained mucosal blood flow

C. Increased HCl secretion and increased mucus secretion

D. Decreased HCl secretion and decreased mucus secretion

E. Uterine relaxation and decreased gastric motility only

✓ Correct: B — Decreased HCl secretion, increased mucus secretion, and maintained mucosal blood flow

Endogenous prostaglandins (PGE2, PGI2) exert a cytoprotective effect on the gastric mucosa via multiple mechanisms: (1) Inhibit parietal cell cAMP → ↓ H+/K+-ATPase activity → ↓ HCl secretion; (2) Stimulate mucus and bicarbonate secretion from surface mucous cells; (3) Maintain mucosal blood flow (vasodilation). Question 10

A newborn is found to have severe protein malabsorption despite adequate feeding. Endoscopy reveals normal pancreatic structure. Serum trypsinogen levels are markedly elevated, but trypsin activity in the duodenal aspirate is absent. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?

A. Cystic fibrosis with exocrine pancreatic insufficiency

Enterokinase (enteropeptidase) is a brush border enzyme of the duodenal epithelium that cleaves trypsinogen to trypsin. This is a rare but classic board vignette about the obligate role of enterokinase in the activation cascade. Choice D (SPINK1 overexpression) would suppress pancreatitis but would not prevent enterokinase-mediated activation in the duodenum.

A. Decreased CCK secretion from I-cells leads to reduced pancreatic lipase output
