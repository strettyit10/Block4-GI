Questions 1.1 · 1.2 · 1.3

History, Causes of Elevated LFTs & Physical Exam

What to ask, what to consider, and what to look for on exam when a patient presents with unexplained weight loss and elevated liver enzymes.

1.1 — Additional PMH / Social History Questions

The presenting triad — fatigue, weight loss, elevated liver enzymes — could represent a wide range of hepatic pathology. Your history must efficiently screen the major risk categories for liver disease. Each question targets a specific mechanistic pathway:

  • 1Alcohol use — quantity, frequency, duration. Alcoholic hepatitis and alcoholic cirrhosis are dose-dependent. Even moderate chronic alcohol can cause steatohepatitis. The AST:ALT ratio >2:1 is a classic clue for alcoholic liver disease.
  • 2IV drug use — needle sharing is the primary transmission route for Hepatitis C (and B). Even past use decades ago is relevant — HCV can be asymptomatic for 20–30 years before causing cirrhosis or HCC.
  • 3Hepatitis history — prior diagnosis, treatment, vaccination status. HBV and HCV are the two dominant infectious causes of chronic hepatitis and HCC worldwide.
  • 4History of cancer — liver metastases from colorectal, breast, lung, pancreatic primaries are far more common than primary HCC. This changes the workup entirely.
  • 5High-risk sexual activity — HBV is transmitted sexually. Multiple partners or unprotected sex raises HBV risk significantly. HIV co-infection also accelerates HCV progression to cirrhosis.
  • 6Tattoos — poorly sterilized tattoo equipment is a well-documented transmission route for both HBV and HCV, especially from older tattoos or informal settings.
Why these questions matter together: The overlap is intentional. HCV + alcohol is a particularly dangerous combination — alcohol accelerates HCV-related fibrosis ~2–3× faster, dramatically shortening the timeline to cirrhosis and HCC. Always ask both.
1.2 — Causes of Elevated Liver Enzymes

Liver enzymes reflect two different processes: hepatocellular injury (AST, ALT leak from damaged hepatocytes) vs. cholestatic injury (Alk Phos, GGT elevated when bile flow is impaired). Understanding which pattern dominates narrows the differential dramatically.

Hepatocellular Pattern (↑↑ AST/ALT)
  • AAcute viral hepatitis — HAV, HBV, HCV, EBV, CMV. AST/ALT can reach 1000–3000 U/L in acute injury.
  • BDrug/toxin-induced — acetaminophen, statins, isoniazid, NSAIDs, alcohol. Pattern varies by drug; DILI is always on the differential.
  • CChronic hepatitis B or C — often mild transaminase elevation (2–5× ULN) for years/decades. This patient's pattern.
  • DAutoimmune hepatitis — affects women predominantly, associated with other autoimmune conditions, responds to steroids.
  • EMetabolic: NAFLD/NASH — increasingly common, associated with metabolic syndrome, obesity, T2DM.
Cholestatic Pattern (↑↑ Alk Phos / GGT)
  • APrimary Biliary Cholangitis (PBC) — autoimmune destruction of small intrahepatic bile ducts. Anti-mitochondrial antibody (AMA) positive. Predominantly women.
  • BPrimary Sclerosing Cholangitis (PSC) — fibro-inflammatory destruction of bile ducts. Strongly associated with IBD (ulcerative colitis). Beaded appearance on MRCP.
  • CMalignancy — HCC, cholangiocarcinoma, metastases. Alk Phos is disproportionately elevated when bile ducts are compressed by tumor.
  • DHemochromatosis — iron overload; elevated ferritin, transferrin saturation >45%. Can cause cirrhosis and HCC even without viral hepatitis.
  • EWilson's Disease — copper accumulation; younger patients, Kayser-Fleischer rings, low ceruloplasmin. Rare but important to not miss.
This patient's pattern: AST 125 (↑ ~3×), ALT 95 (↑ ~2×), Alk Phos 280 (↑ ~2×). Mixed pattern — hepatocellular + cholestatic. The disproportionate Alk Phos elevation alongside elevated transaminases in a patient with known HCV antibody and elevated AFP points strongly to HCC complicating chronic HCV infection.
1.3 — Physical Exam Findings to Seek

The physical exam in suspected liver disease is looking for two things simultaneously: evidence of chronic hepatocellular failure (the liver can no longer perform its metabolic functions) and evidence of portal hypertension (blood backing up from the obstructed portal circulation).

Jaundice / Scleral icterus
Bilirubin >2–3 mg/dL becomes visible. Reflects inability to conjugate or excrete bilirubin. Sclera are examined first — yellow color appears there before skin due to high albumin binding affinity of scleral tissue.
Hepatomegaly / RUQ tenderness
Enlarged liver felt below right costal margin. Early cirrhosis → hepatomegaly from inflammation. Late cirrhosis → shrunken, nodular liver. HCC within a cirrhotic liver may produce a palpable mass or disproportionate enlargement.
Ascites
Fluid in peritoneal cavity. Results from portal hypertension (↑ hydrostatic pressure) + hypoalbuminemia (↓ oncotic pressure) + secondary hyperaldosteronism (sodium/water retention). Fluid wave and shifting dullness on percussion.
Spider angiomas
Central arteriole with radiating vessels on upper trunk, face, arms. Caused by elevated estrogen (liver normally metabolizes estrogens — failure → estrogen accumulation → vasodilation). >5 spider angiomas = significant liver disease.
Caput medusae
Dilated periumbilical veins radiating outward like Medusa's snakes. Represents portal-systemic shunting via reopened paraumbilical veins as portal pressure rises. A classic sign of portal hypertension.
Palmar erythema / Edema
Palmar erythema (reddening of thenar/hypothenar eminences): estrogen accumulation → cutaneous vasodilation. Peripheral edema: hypoalbuminemia → ↓ oncotic pressure → fluid leaks into interstitium, especially dependent areas.

Physical Findings, Labs, Liver Function & HCC

Interpreting what was found, what the labs mean mechanistically, and how chronic HCV progresses to hepatocellular carcinoma.

2.1 — Explaining the Physical Findings

Mr. Q has moderate hepatomegaly, RUQ tenderness, soft non-distended abdomen, no splenomegaly, no ascites, sclera not jaundiced. This profile represents early-to-moderate chronic liver disease — significant damage has occurred, but the compensatory mechanisms are still holding. Here's why each finding is present or absent:

Present — Moderate hepatomegaly: The liver is enlarged, likely from ongoing inflammatory infiltrate from chronic HCV, early regenerative nodules, and possibly the HCC mass itself occupying space within the parenchyma. In advanced cirrhosis the liver would be shrunken and nodular — the fact that it's enlarged suggests the disease is at an earlier architectural stage.
Present — RUQ tenderness: Stretching of Glisson's capsule (the fibrous liver capsule) by hepatomegaly or the HCC mass produces pain. The liver parenchyma itself has no pain receptors — it's always the capsule.
Absent — Jaundice: Bilirubin is only modestly elevated at 2.0 mg/dL (just above the normal 1.2 ceiling). Visible jaundice typically requires >2.5–3 mg/dL. The liver still has enough functional hepatocytes to conjugate most bilirubin.
Absent — Ascites: Portal pressure hasn't risen enough yet to overwhelm oncotic forces. Albumin synthesis, though likely reduced, is still sufficient to maintain some oncotic pressure. Ascites is a late sign of portal hypertension.
Absent — Splenomegaly: Splenomegaly occurs when sustained portal hypertension causes splenic venous congestion and progressive enlargement. Its absence suggests portal pressure, while elevated (mild coagulopathy, low platelets), hasn't yet caused severe congestion.
Clinical stage: This pattern — hepatomegaly + mild coagulopathy + thrombocytopenia + elevated LFTs but without ascites/jaundice/encephalopathy — is consistent with compensated cirrhosis or advanced fibrosis. The liver is damaged but not yet decompensated.

2.3 — Normal Liver Functions Inhibited by Chronic HCV

The liver performs hundreds of functions. Chronic HCV → progressive hepatocyte destruction → fibrosis → cirrhosis → these core functions are systematically lost. The lab abnormalities in this patient map directly onto each:

Bilirubin Excretion → Jaundice
Hepatocytes take up unconjugated bilirubin (from heme breakdown), conjugate it with glucuronic acid via UGT1A1, and excrete it into bile canaliculi. Chronic hepatocyte loss reduces all three steps → conjugated and unconjugated bilirubin accumulate in blood → jaundice, dark urine (conjugated bilirubin is water-soluble and excreted in urine), pale stools (less urobilinogen reaching gut).
Ammonia Clearance → Encephalopathy
Intestinal bacteria generate ammonia from protein digestion and urea hydrolysis. The liver converts ammonia to urea via the urea cycle (hepatocyte-specific) for renal excretion. Failing hepatocytes → ammonia escapes into systemic circulation → crosses blood-brain barrier → astrocyte swelling, glutamine accumulation → hepatic encephalopathy (confusion, asterixis, coma in severe cases). Also worsened by portosystemic shunting.
Clotting Factor Synthesis → Coagulopathy
The liver synthesizes all coagulation factors except vWF. Vitamin K-dependent factors (II, VII, IX, X, Protein C, Protein S) require hepatic gamma-carboxylation. Loss of hepatocytes → reduced synthesis of all clotting factors → elevated PT/INR → bleeding risk. This patient's INR of 1.8 confirms significant synthetic dysfunction. Factor VII has the shortest half-life (~4–6h), so PT/INR is the most sensitive early marker of hepatic synthetic failure.
Albumin Synthesis → Edema / Ascites
Albumin is exclusively synthesized by hepatocytes. It maintains plasma oncotic pressure and transports hormones, drugs, fatty acids, and bilirubin. Loss of hepatocyte mass → hypoalbuminemia → reduced oncotic pressure → fluid leaks into interstitium → peripheral edema and ascites. Albumin has a half-life of ~20 days, so hypoalbuminemia indicates chronic rather than acute failure.
Estrogen Metabolism → Gynecomastia / Spider Angiomas
The liver deactivates estrogens via conjugation and excretion. Hepatocyte loss → estrogen accumulates → stimulates estrogen receptors in skin (spider angiomas, palmar erythema), breast tissue (gynecomastia in men), and causes testicular atrophy. Also contributes to loss of axillary/pubic hair. The elevated estrogen state also activates the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system → secondary hyperaldosteronism → sodium and water retention → worsens ascites.
2.4 — How HCC Manifests: The HCV → Cirrhosis → HCC Progression

HCC rarely arises in a normal liver. It is almost always a complication of chronic liver disease. Understanding the mechanism of progression is essential for boards.

0–24 weeks — Acute HCV Infection
HCV enters hepatocytes via CD81/SR-BI receptors. Only 30% of infected individuals clear the virus (via IFNα/IFNγ immune response). The remaining 70% develop chronic infection with viral persistence. Acute phase is usually asymptomatic or mildly symptomatic — most patients have no idea they've been infected.
24 weeks – 50 years — Chronic Hepatitis C
Persistently infected hepatocytes are recognized by CD8+ cytotoxic T cells → ongoing necroinflammatory injury. Activated Kupffer cells (liver macrophages) release TGF-β and other fibrogenic cytokines → activate hepatic stellate cells (quiescent → activated, myofibroblast-like) → stellate cells deposit collagen into the Space of Disse → progressive fibrosis. With each cycle of injury and repair, fibrous bands accumulate and displace normal parenchyma. Over 20–30 years this reaches cirrhosis in 15–25% of chronically infected patients. Alcohol use, male sex, older age at infection, HIV co-infection all accelerate this timeline.
Cirrhosis — The Critical Architectural Change
Fibrosis bands link portal triads to central veins, carving the liver into regenerative nodules. Contracting scar tissue compresses hepatic veins → postsinusoidal resistance to flow → portal hypertension. Loss of sinusoidal fenestrae (capillarization of sinusoids) → impaired exchange between blood and hepatocytes → further functional loss. The regenerating hepatocytes within nodules are under chronic proliferative stress — this is where oncogenic transformation happens.
10–40 years post-infection — Hepatocellular Carcinoma
HCC develops in ~3% of cirrhotic patients per year. HCV doesn't integrate into the genome (unlike HBV), so carcinogenesis is indirect — via chronic inflammation, oxidative stress, and the proliferative pressure of cirrhotic regeneration. Key oncogenic events: TP53 mutations (loss of tumor suppressor), CTNNB1 (β-catenin pathway activation), Wnt signaling, telomere shortening. Tumors are hypervascular — fed by the hepatic artery rather than the portal vein — which is why arterial-phase enhancement on CT (washout pattern) is diagnostic of HCC. AFP is re-expressed because tumor cells revert to a fetal hepatocyte-like gene expression state.
Clinical presentation of HCC: Often asymptomatic until advanced. When symptomatic: RUQ pain (capsule stretching), weight loss, anorexia (as in Mr. Q), constitutional symptoms (fatigue), paraneoplastic syndromes (hypoglycemia from IGF-2 production, erythrocytosis from ectopic EPO, hypercalcemia). A new hepatic mass in a cirrhotic patient = HCC until proven otherwise.
Surveillance in cirrhosis: All cirrhotic patients — regardless of etiology — should undergo ultrasound + AFP every 6 months. This is the surveillance protocol because it catches HCC at an earlier, potentially resectable or transplantable stage. The fact that Mr. Q was not under active surveillance is why his HCC was discovered incidentally on workup of symptoms rather than at an earlier stage.
Part III — Complications, Transplant Criteria & Ethics

Portal Hypertension, Milan Criteria, MELD Score & Ethical Allocation

The downstream consequences of cirrhosis, how transplant candidacy is determined, and the ethics of a scarce resource.

Complications of Chronic Hepatitis → Cirrhosis

All complications of cirrhosis derive from two root mechanisms: portal hypertension (backed-up portal blood pressure) and hepatocellular failure (the liver can no longer perform its metabolic functions).

Portal Hypertension
Normal portal pressure ~5–10 mmHg. Cirrhosis → >12 mmHg → varices form. Mechanism: regenerative nodules + contracting fibrous bands compress hepatic veins → postsinusoidal obstruction → blood backs up into the portal system → splanchnic vasodilation (via NO) → increased portal inflow worsens pressure further.
Esophageal Varices
Portal blood diverted via left gastric vein → esophageal venous plexus → azygos vein → superior vena cava. These thin-walled collaterals under high pressure can rupture → massive upper GI hemorrhage. Variceal bleeding has 15–20% mortality per episode. Prevented/managed with propranolol/nadolol (↓ portal pressure) or endoscopic band ligation.
Hepatic Encephalopathy
Ammonia (and other gut-derived toxins) bypass the failing liver via portosystemic shunts → reach the brain. Astrocytes swell as they convert ammonia to glutamine. Spectrum: mild confusion/asterixis → stupor → coma. Precipitants: GI bleeding (protein load), infection, constipation, hypokalemia, sedatives. Treatment: lactulose (acidifies colon, traps NH4+), rifaximin (reduces gut bacterial ammonia production).
Ascites
Portal hypertension → splanchnic vasodilation → reduced effective circulating volume → RAAS activation → Na/water retention → hepatic lymph production exceeds lymphatic drainage → peritoneal fluid accumulation. Managed with sodium restriction, spironolactone (aldosterone antagonist), furosemide. Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP) is a life-threatening complication.
Splenomegaly / Hypersplenism
Portal hypertension → splenic venous congestion → splenomegaly → splenic sequestration of blood cells → pancytopenia (thrombocytopenia most prominent, as in Mr. Q with platelets of 100). Splenomegaly also reduces thrombopoietin sensitivity. Thrombocytopenia + coagulopathy = high bleeding risk.
Hepatorenal Syndrome
Profound splanchnic vasodilation in advanced cirrhosis → renal vasoconstriction (compensatory) → acute kidney injury without intrinsic renal disease. Kidneys are structurally normal — they recover if transplanted into a healthy recipient. Creatinine rises; urine sodium <10 mEq/L (avid sodium retention). Carries very poor prognosis without transplant.
Milan Criteria — Transplant Eligibility for HCC

The Milan Criteria define when liver transplant offers acceptable long-term outcomes for HCC patients. Transplant removes both the tumor AND the pre-malignant cirrhotic liver, addressing both problems simultaneously.

Milan Criteria (patient must meet ALL):
1. Single lesion <5 cm, OR up to 3 lesions each <3 cm
2. No extrahepatic involvement (no metastases)
3. No macrovascular invasion (no portal/hepatic vein tumor thrombus)

Patients within Milan: ~70% 5-year survival, <15% recurrence. Outside Milan: outcomes decline sharply.

Mr. Q had a lesion detected on triphasic CT — the triphasic protocol (non-contrast, arterial, portal venous phases) is specifically designed to demonstrate HCC's characteristic arterial enhancement + portal venous washout pattern, distinguishing it from benign hepatic lesions. AFP 413 + this CT pattern = diagnosis without biopsy under AASLD guidelines.

HCC exception points: Patients with HCC within Milan automatically receive MELD exception points (+22 points currently) — because HCC can progress and metastasize while waiting, making time-on-list particularly dangerous for this subgroup.
MELD Score — Prioritizing the Sickest

The Model for End-Stage Liver Disease (MELD) replaced time-on-list as the primary allocation metric in 2002. It uses objective lab values to predict 90-day mortality without transplant.

MELD = 3.78 × ln(bilirubin) + 11.2 × ln(INR) + 9.57 × ln(creatinine) + 6.43

Components reflect hepatic function (bilirubin, INR) + renal function (creatinine) — the two systems most predictive of short-term mortality in liver disease.

Ethical Issues in Liver Transplant Allocation

Liver transplantation is a paradigm case of medical resource allocation ethics. There are ~8,000 liver transplants performed in the US annually but ~100,000 patients on the waiting list. Every allocation decision is a tradeoff.

  • 1Sickest-first vs. best-outcomes: MELD prioritizes the sickest patients (greatest need, most to gain from transplant). But the sickest patients — highest MELD — also have the most comorbidities and highest surgical risk. A utilitarian argument would allocate livers to patients most likely to survive long-term; a justice argument prioritizes those who will die without it. MELD leans toward justice.
  • 2Alcoholic liver disease: Most programs require 6 months of documented abstinence before listing ("6-month rule"). The rationale: sobriety predicts post-transplant compliance; relapse damages the graft. Critics argue this is discriminatory against a disease (alcohol use disorder) rather than a moral failing, and that 6 months of abstinence doesn't reliably predict long-term sobriety. Some centers now consider transplant for acute alcoholic hepatitis without the 6-month rule.
  • 3Geographic inequities: MELD scores at transplant vary dramatically by UNOS region — historically ranging from MELD ~18 in some regions to >30 in others for the same organ. The 2017 "acuity circles" policy replaced fixed regional boundaries with a distance-based allocation model to reduce geographic disparity. Wealthy patients can list at multiple centers in favorable regions ("list shopping") — a practice inaccessible to lower-income patients.
  • 4Living donor ethics: A living donor undergoes a major operation (right lobe hepatectomy) with ~0.5% mortality risk for no personal medical benefit. Informed consent must be truly voluntary — financial incentives are prohibited, family pressure is a real concern. The donor's interests must be balanced against the recipient's need. Long-term donor outcomes are generally good but complications occur in ~30% of donors to some degree.
Supplemental — Liver Anatomy & Cellular Pathology

Hepatic Anatomy, Sinusoidal Biology & The Fibrosis Mechanism

The structural context behind why chronic HCV causes the lab and clinical findings it does — from sinusoid to cirrhotic nodule.

Liver Blood Supply — The Dual Inflow

The liver is unique in receiving blood from two sources simultaneously:

Portal vein (~75% of hepatic blood flow, ~50% of O₂): Carries nutrient-rich but relatively deoxygenated blood from the entire GI tract (stomach, small bowel, colon), spleen, pancreas, and gallbladder. This is why the liver is the first organ to encounter absorbed nutrients, drugs, and gut-derived toxins — including ammonia, bacterial products, and absorbed alcohol.
Hepatic artery (~25% of flow, ~50% of O₂): Oxygenated blood from the celiac axis. Supplies the bile ducts primarily — bile ducts are dependent on arterial supply and are vulnerable when hepatic artery flow is compromised (e.g., after liver transplant, hepatic artery thrombosis → biliary ischemia → bile leaks and strictures).

Both streams mix in the hepatic sinusoids — fenestrated capillaries lined by sinusoidal endothelial cells, where hepatocytes on either side are in direct contact with the plasma via the Space of Disse.

Sinusoidal Architecture — Key Cell Types
  • 1Hepatocytes (60% of liver cells): The functional workhorse — metabolism, synthesis, detoxification, bile production. Arranged in plates 1–2 cells thick, maximizing surface exposure to sinusoidal blood.
  • 2Kupffer cells (hepatic macrophages): Reside within sinusoidal lumen, attached to endothelium. First-line immune surveillance for gut-derived pathogens. In chronic HCV: Kupffer cells are chronically activated → release TNF-α, IL-6, TGF-β → drive fibrogenic stellate cell activation and maintain the inflammatory state.
  • 3Hepatic stellate cells (HSC, cells of Ito): Quiescent state — store Vitamin A (retinoids) in lipid droplets, reside in the Space of Disse. Activated by TGF-β (from Kupffer cells) and PDGF → transdifferentiate into myofibroblasts → proliferate, lose retinoid stores, and produce type I and III collagen. This is the primary cellular mechanism of hepatic fibrosis. Activated HSCs are the direct cause of cirrhosis.
  • 4Sinusoidal endothelial cells: Fenestrated (have pores, no basement membrane) — allows free exchange between plasma and hepatocytes. In cirrhosis: fenestrae are lost ("capillarization") → impaired hepatocyte-plasma exchange → worsens metabolic dysfunction even beyond hepatocyte loss.
Portal Anatomy & Porto-Systemic Collaterals — Why Varices Form Where They Do

The portal venous system collects blood from the entire abdominal GI tract. The portal vein forms at the confluence of: Splenic vein + Superior Mesenteric Vein + Inferior Mesenteric Vein. When portal pressure rises, blood seeks alternative routes to the systemic venous system — the porto-systemic collaterals. Each collateral location produces a specific clinical sign:

Why esophageal varices are the most dangerous: The esophageal submucosal venous plexus is superficially located and thinly covered. Under high portal pressure, these vessels expand dramatically and can be easily torn by swallowing, vomiting, or NSAIDs. A single bleeding episode can exsanguinate a patient quickly. Variceal bleeding precipitates hepatic encephalopathy (blood in the gut = protein load = ammonia) and often triggers spontaneous bacterial peritonitis — a "triple threat."
Liver segments (Couinaud anatomy): The liver has 8 functional segments, each with its own portal vein branch, hepatic artery branch, bile duct, and hepatic vein drainage. This segmental independence is what makes partial hepatectomy and living-donor lobectomy possible — segment(s) can be resected without compromising the remaining segments' vascular or biliary supply. Mr. Q's tumor location within segments determines resectability and whether right vs. left lobe donation is feasible.
