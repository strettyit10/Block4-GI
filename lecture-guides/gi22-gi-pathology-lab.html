▼ Esophagus — Normal Histology

🔬 Lab Slide — Normal Esophagus

Gross: white-to-tan smooth mucosa. Histology: stratified squamous epithelium lines the lumen; underlying submucosa contains mucous glands and ducts.

Key Histologic Features

  • Lining epithelium: Non-keratinized stratified squamous epithelium — identical in appearance to skin but without keratin layer
  • Submucosa: Contains esophageal mucous glands (submucosal glands) that secrete mucus to lubricate food passage
  • Muscularis propria: Upper 1/3 = skeletal muscle; Middle 1/3 = mixed; Lower 1/3 = smooth muscle
  • No serosa: The esophagus lacks a serosal layer — facilitates rapid spread of esophageal carcinoma into mediastinum
  • GEJ (gastroesophageal junction): Abrupt transition from squamous → columnar epithelium (the Z-line/squamocolumnar junction, visible endoscopically)
⚠️ Exam Trap

The esophagus is the only GI organ lined by stratified squamous epithelium (not columnar). Any columnar epithelium in the esophagus above the GEJ = metaplasia (Barrett's).

▼ Stomach — Normal Histology

🔬 Lab Slide — Normal Stomach

Gross: rugae (large mucosal folds). Histology: short gastric pits leading into long tubular glands containing parietal cells (bright pink, secrete HCl and intrinsic factor) and chief cells (basal, secrete pepsinogen).

Key Histologic Features

  • Epithelium: Simple columnar mucous epithelium lining the luminal surface and pits
  • Rugae: Prominent longitudinal folds of mucosa and submucosa visible grossly; allow distension
  • Gastric pits: Surface invaginations leading into glands; vary by region
  • Parietal cells (oxyntic cells): Large, pyramidal cells with abundant bright pink eosinophilic cytoplasm; secrete HCl and intrinsic factor; found mainly in fundus/body
  • Chief cells (zymogenic cells): Basophilic cells in the lower glands; secrete pepsinogen (activated to pepsin by HCl)
  • G cells: Enteroendocrine cells in the antrum; secrete gastrin → stimulates parietal cells
  • Mucous neck cells: In upper gland neck; secrete protective mucus

Regional Differences

RegionGland TypeKey Cell Types
CardiaMucous glandsMucous cells
Fundus / BodyFundic (gastric) glandsParietal cells, Chief cells, Mucous neck cells
Antrum / PylorusPyloric glandsMucous cells, G cells (gastrin)
⭐ High-Yield:
  • Parietal cells → H⁺/K⁺-ATPase pump → HCl. Target of PPIs (omeprazole).
  • Parietal cells also make intrinsic factor → B12 absorption. Loss in autoimmune gastritis → pernicious anemia.
  • Chief cells → pepsinogen. Think: Chief cells are at the base (bottom of the gland)

▼ Small Intestine — Normal Histology

🔬 Lab Slide — Normal Small Intestine

Histology shows long finger-like villi projecting into the lumen with occasional goblet cells. Crypts of Lieberkühn at the base. The villi provide a large surface area for digestion and absorption.

Key Histologic Features

  • Villi: Finger-like mucosal projections unique to the small intestine; dramatically increase absorptive surface area (~200–300× over a flat tube)
  • Enterocytes: Tall columnar cells with a brush border (microvilli = striated border) on the apical surface; site of digestion and absorption
  • Goblet cells: Scattered among enterocytes; secrete protective mucus; increase in number from duodenum → ileum
  • Crypts of Lieberkühn: Tubular glands at base of villi; contain stem cells that renew the epithelium every 3–5 days; also contain Paneth cells (secrete antimicrobial defensins)
  • Lamina propria: Contains lacteals (lymphatic capillaries) for fat absorption as chylomicrons
  • Brunner's glands: Submucosal mucous glands found only in the duodenum; neutralize gastric acid
  • Peyer's patches: Lymphoid aggregates in the submucosa, concentrated in the ileum; immune surveillance (MALT)

Duodenum vs. Jejunum vs. Ileum

🧠 Mnemonic

"Brunner's Duodenum, Peyer's Ileum" — the two unique submucosal structures of the small intestine, each confined to one segment. Brunner's neutralize acid; Peyer's fight bacteria.

▼ Large Intestine / Colon — Normal Histology

🔬 Lab Slide — Normal Colon

Histology: straight crypts (no villi) lined by numerous goblet cells. A lymphoid nodule (GALT) is visible in the submucosa. The gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT) represents the largest lymphoid organ in the body.

Key Histologic Features

  • No villi: Unlike the small intestine, the colon has no villi — the luminal surface is flat with straight tubular crypts
  • Crypts of Lieberkühn: Prominent, straight, parallel tubular glands extending full thickness of the mucosa
  • Abundant goblet cells: Far more goblet cells than in the small intestine — mucus production for lubrication of stool
  • Colonocytes: Columnar absorptive cells; primary function = water and electrolyte reabsorption (no brush border enzyme activity)
  • Taeniae coli: Three narrow bands of outer longitudinal smooth muscle (instead of a complete layer) — cause haustra (sacculations) visible grossly
  • GALT: Lymphoid nodules in submucosa are a normal finding; the gut-associated lymphoid tissue = largest lymphoid organ in the body
  • No Brunner's glands, no Peyer's patches
⚠️ Key Distinguisher on Exam

If a biopsy shows straight crypts + many goblet cells + NO villi → colon. If it shows tall villi → small intestine. If it shows stratified squamous → esophagus.

▼ USMLE Step 1 High-Yield Summary

🎯 Step 1 High-Yield — GI Histology
  • Esophagus = non-keratinized stratified squamous; NO serosa (cancer spreads fast)
  • Stomach parietal cells = HCl + intrinsic factor; chief cells = pepsinogen; G cells (antrum) = gastrin
  • Small intestine = villi + crypts; Brunner's (duodenum only); Peyer's patches (ileum only); longest villi = jejunum
  • Colon = straight crypts + abundant goblet cells; NO villi; taeniae coli → haustra
  • B12 absorbed only in ileum (with intrinsic factor); bile acids also recirculated there
  • Gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT) = largest lymphoid organ; important in mucosal immunity

▼ Esophageal Disorders

▼GERD & Reflux Esophagitis

Pathogenesis: Incompetent lower esophageal sphincter (LES) → acid reflux → squamous mucosal damage. Worsened by obesity, fatty foods, alcohol, tobacco, hiatal hernia, pregnancy.

  • Symptoms: Heartburn (pyrosis), regurgitation, dysphagia (late)
  • Histology: Basal zone hyperplasia of squamous epithelium, elongated papillae, eosinophilic infiltrate (eosinophils in squamous mucosa)
  • Complications: Barrett's esophagus, stricture, ulceration, adenocarcinoma
▼Barrett's Esophagus
🔬 Lab Slide — Barrett's Esophagus

Histology: columnar (glandular) epithelium with goblet cells replaces normal squamous epithelium above the GEJ. On gross specimen the distal esophagus appears darker/erythematous compared to the normal pale proximal squamous mucosa.

Pathogenesis: Chronic GERD → metaplasia: normal squamous epithelium → intestinal-type columnar epithelium with goblet cells (intestinal metaplasia). This is a pre-malignant condition.

  • Risk: 30–40× increased risk of esophageal adenocarcinoma
  • Buzzword histology: Goblet cells in esophageal biopsy = Barrett's until proven otherwise
  • Progression: Barrett's → low-grade dysplasia → high-grade dysplasia → adenocarcinoma
  • Screening: Regular endoscopic surveillance with biopsies in patients with chronic GERD
⚠️ Exam Trap

Barrett's is a metaplasia (not dysplasia and not carcinoma). The presence of goblet cells is the defining histologic feature — not just columnar cells (cardiac-type columnar cells also exist and are not sufficient for Barrett's diagnosis).

▼Esophageal Varices
🔬 Lab Slide — Esophageal Varices

Gross: Linear dark blue dilated submucosal veins at the lower esophagus. Histology: dilated, thin-walled vessels in the submucosa; ruptured varix shows surrounding inflammation and hemorrhage.

Pathogenesis: Portal hypertension (most commonly from liver cirrhosis) → increased pressure in portal system → blood diverted through portosystemic anastomoses → esophageal submucosal veins become engorged and dilated.

  • Location: Lower 1/3 of esophagus (where esophageal veins connect with portal system via left gastric vein)
  • Complication: Variceal rupture → massive upper GI hemorrhage → hematemesis → can be fatal (mortality 20–30% per episode)
  • Clinical: Painless, sudden massive hematemesis in a patient with known cirrhosis/portal HTN
  • Treatment: Endoscopic band ligation, sclerotherapy, TIPS (transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt), propranolol (prophylaxis)
⭐ Portal-Systemic Anastomoses (Step 1):
  • Esophageal varices (left gastric ↔ esophageal veins)
  • Caput medusae (paraumbilical ↔ epigastric veins)
  • Anorectal varices / hemorrhoids (superior rectal ↔ middle/inferior rectal)
⚠️ Exam Trap

Esophageal varices do not cause pain at baseline — they are only noticed when they bleed. A painless massive hematemesis in a cirrhotic patient = esophageal varices until proven otherwise.

▼ Gastric Disorders

▼Acute Gastritis & Stress Ulcers
🔬 Lab Slides — Gastric Erosions & Stress Ulcers

Gross: hemorrhagic erosions with rounded outlines ("gastric erosions"). Histology of acute gastritis: neutrophil infiltration of the gastric mucosa. Stress ulcers: small, shallow, multiple mucosal ulcerations in the proximal stomach.

Causes of acute gastritis / erosions:

  • NSAIDs / Aspirin: Inhibit COX → ↓ prostaglandin synthesis → ↓ mucus and bicarb protection → mucosal injury
  • Alcohol: Direct mucosal irritant; disrupts mucosal barrier
  • H. pylori: See below
  • Stress ulcers: Occur in critically ill patients (major trauma, burns, sepsis, shock, CNS injury)
Stress Ulcer TypeSettingMechanism
Curling ulcerSevere burns↓ plasma volume → mucosal ischemia → ulceration
Cushing ulcerCNS injury / increased ICPVagal stimulation → ↑ gastric acid secretion (most likely to perforate)
🧠 Mnemonic

Curling = Burns (fire → curl up); Cushing = CNS (brain). Cushing ulcers are most dangerous because hypersecretion is particularly severe.

▼Peptic Ulcer Disease
🔬 Lab Slide — Peptic Ulcer

Histology: sharply demarcated breach through the muscularis mucosae; ulcer base contains inflamed, necrotic debris; an arterial branch at the ulcer base may be eroded and bleeding. Normal gastric mucosa on one side falls away abruptly into the deep ulcer crater.

Definition: A breach in the mucosa extending through the muscularis mucosae into the submucosa (or deeper) — distinguishing it from a mere erosion (which is superficial).

  • Locations: Duodenum (most common, anterior wall of first part) > Stomach (lesser curvature of antrum/body)
  • Pathogenesis: Imbalance between aggressive factors (acid, pepsin) and defensive factors (mucus, bicarb, prostaglandins, mucosal blood flow)
  • Symptoms: Epigastric pain; DU classically relieved by eating; GU classically worsened by eating
  • Complications: Bleeding (most common — melena/hematemesis), perforation, obstruction, penetration into pancreas
▼H. pylori Infection
🔬 Lab Slide — H. pylori Histology

Methylene blue stain shows small curved to spiral rod-shaped bacteria in the surface epithelial mucus. These rods are found in the surface epithelial mucus of most patients with active gastritis.

Organism: Helicobacter pylori — gram-negative, microaerophilic, urease-positive spiral rod

  • Virulence: Urease → NH₃ → neutralizes acid → allows survival in gastric mucus; also produces CagA protein and vacuolating toxin (VacA)
  • Epidemiology: Affects ~50% of the world's population; fecal-oral transmission; higher prevalence in developing nations
  • Diagnosis: Urea breath test (gold standard non-invasive); serology (IgG); fecal antigen test; biopsy (CLO test / histology with methylene blue or Giemsa stain)
  • Associated diseases: Chronic gastritis (antral), peptic ulcer disease (duodenal > gastric), gastric adenocarcinoma (intestinal type), gastric MALT lymphoma
  • Treatment: Triple therapy (PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin × 14 days) or quadruple therapy if resistant
⭐ High-Yield Associations:
  • 95% of duodenal ulcers and 70–80% of gastric ulcers are H. pylori-associated
  • H. pylori eradication can cause regression of low-grade gastric MALT lymphoma
  • H. pylori is a Group 1 carcinogen per WHO

▼ Small Intestinal Disorders

▼Celiac Disease (Gluten-Sensitive Enteropathy)
🔬 Lab Slide — Celiac Disease

Histology comparison: Normal mucosa (left) shows tall villi. Celiac-involved mucosa (right) shows blunting and flattening of villi, with increased intraepithelial lymphocytes, loss of brush border, loss of crypts, and infiltration with lymphocytes and plasma cells (B-cells sensitized to gliadin).

Pathogenesis: Sensitivity to gliadin, a protein component of gluten (found in wheat, barley, oats, rye) → immune-mediated destruction of small bowel mucosa → malabsorption

  • Genetics: >95% of patients express HLA-DQ2 (or HLA-DQ8); strong genetic basis
  • Histology: Villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, ↑ intraepithelial lymphocytes (IELs), plasma cell infiltration of lamina propria
  • Symptoms: Chronic diarrhea, steatorrhea, bloating; malabsorption → iron deficiency anemia, folate deficiency, fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (A, D, E, K), osteoporosis
  • Serology: Anti-tissue transglutaminase (anti-tTG) IgA; anti-endomysial antibodies (IgA)
  • Treatment: Strict gluten-free diet → mucosal recovery
  • Complication: Enteropathy-associated T-cell lymphoma (EATL) in refractory cases
▼Bowel Obstruction & Ischemia
🔬 Lab Slides — Ischemia & Obstruction

Gross: Dark red, hemorrhagic loops of small intestine (ischemia/infarction). Adhesion between loops of small intestine visible at laparotomy. Intussusception: telescoped bowel segment creating "bowel within a bowel" appearance on cross-section.

  • 2️⃣ Hernias (inguinal, femoral, umbilical)
  • 3️⃣ Neoplasms (in adults >50)
▼Meckel Diverticulum

Embryology: Failure of involution of the vitelline (omphalomesenteric) duct → persistent outpouching from the antimesenteric border of the ileum

  • Rule of 2's: 2% of population; 2 feet from ileocecal valve; 2 inches long; 2% become symptomatic; 2 types of ectopic tissue (gastric, pancreatic); presents in first 2 years of life
  • Contains: All 3 layers of normal bowel wall (true diverticulum)
  • Complications: Painless lower GI bleeding (ectopic gastric mucosa secretes acid → ulceration); intussusception; volvulus; diverticulitis (mimics appendicitis)
  • Diagnosis: Technetium-99m pertechnetate scan (detects ectopic gastric mucosa)
🧠 Mnemonic — Rule of 2's

"2% of people, 2 feet from ileocecal valve, 2 inches long, 2 types of ectopic tissue, 2% symptomatic, first 2 years of life"

▼ Large Intestinal Disorders

▼Pseudomembranous Colitis (C. difficile)
🔬 Lab Slides — Pseudomembranous Colitis

Gross: Mucosal surface is hyperemic and partially covered by a yellow-green exudate (pseudomembrane); mucosa itself is not eroded. Histology: pseudomembrane composed of inflammatory cells + necrotic epithelium + mucus; underlying mucosa remains intact (congested).

Pathogenesis: Broad-spectrum antibiotics (clindamycin, fluoroquinolones, ampicillin, cephalosporins) → disruption of normal colonic flora → overgrowth of Clostridium difficile → toxin A (enterotoxin) + toxin B (cytotoxin) → mucosal damage

  • Symptoms: Profuse, watery diarrhea (may become bloody); crampy abdominal pain; fever; leukocytosis (WBC often >15,000)
  • Diagnosis: Stool PCR for C. diff toxin genes (most sensitive); GDH antigen + toxin immunoassay
  • Treatment: Stop offending antibiotic; oral vancomycin or fidaxomicin (first line); metronidazole (alternative); fecal microbiota transplant (FMT) for recurrent cases
  • Complication: Toxic megacolon (life-threatening dilation of colon → perforation → sepsis)
⚠️ Exam Trap

The pseudomembrane is not a true membrane — it is composed of fibrin, mucus, necrotic cells, and inflammatory cells adherent to an intact underlying mucosa. Don't confuse with ulcerative colitis where the mucosa is actually ulcerated/destroyed.

▼Diverticulosis & Diverticulitis
🔬 Lab Slide — Diverticulosis

Gross: Sigmoid colon shows multiple rounded bluish-gray outpouchings (diverticula) protruding from the taeniae coli. More common in the left colon (sigmoid) than the right colon or small intestine. Associated with low-fiber Western diet.

Diverticulosis: Outpouching of colonic mucosa and submucosa through defects in the muscularis propria at sites where nutrient arteries penetrate — these are false (pulsion) diverticula (lack the muscularis layer)

  • Epidemiology: Very common (>50% of Americans >60); associated with low-fiber diet → ↑ intraluminal pressure
  • Location: Sigmoid colon most common (left colon); right-sided diverticula more common in Asian populations
  • Complications:
    • Diverticulitis: Infection/inflammation of a diverticulum → LLQ pain, fever, leukocytosis ("left-sided appendicitis")
    • Diverticular bleeding: Painless hematochezia (most common cause of massive lower GI bleed in elderly) — from erosion of nutrient artery
    • Perforation, abscess, fistula formation
▼Angiodysplasia

Definition: Rare, tortuous dilation of submucosal and mucosal blood vessels in the cecum or right colon

  • Occurs in older adults; often associated with aortic stenosis (Heyde syndrome)
  • Presents as painless, recurrent lower GI bleeding → iron deficiency anemia
  • Diagnosis: Colonoscopy (cherry-red arteriovenous lesions) or mesenteric angiography during active bleed
  • Treatment: Endoscopic cauterization or APC (argon plasma coagulation)

▼ USMLE Step 1 High-Yield Summary

🎯 Step 1 High-Yield — GI Pathogenesis
  • Barrett's = GERD → intestinal metaplasia (goblet cells) → adenocarcinoma risk. Goblet cells = diagnostic.
  • Candida esophagitis = tan-yellow plaques; immunosuppressed. HSV esophagitis = punched-out ulcers; ground-glass inclusions; multinucleation.
  • Esophageal varices = portal hypertension (cirrhosis) → dilated submucosal veins → painless massive hematemesis.
  • Curling ulcer (burns) vs. Cushing ulcer (CNS, hypersecretion, most likely to perforate).
  • H. pylori: Group 1 carcinogen; urease+; associated with DU, GU, gastric adenocarcinoma, MALT lymphoma. Treat with triple therapy.
  • Celiac disease: HLA-DQ2/DQ8; gliadin sensitivity; villous atrophy + ↑ IELs; anti-tTG IgA; gluten-free diet.
  • Most common SBO cause = adhesions (prior surgery) > hernias > neoplasm.
  • C. diff: Antibiotics → overgrowth → toxins A+B → pseudomembranous colitis. Treat with oral vancomycin or fidaxomicin.
  • Diverticulosis: Low-fiber diet → false pulsion diverticula in sigmoid; bleeding (painless) or diverticulitis (LLQ pain = "left-sided appendicitis").
  • Meckel diverticulum: Rule of 2's; true diverticulum; painless lower GI bleeding (ectopic gastric mucosa). Tc-99m scan.

  • Curative surgery = UC only (colectomy cures UC)

▼ Crohn's Disease — In Depth

🔬 Lab Slides — Crohn's Disease

Gross: Terminal ileum with thickened wall, loss of regular mucosal folds, cobblestone pattern, deep fissuring ulcers, creeping mesenteric fat, skip lesions. Histology: Non-caseating granulomas with epithelioid cells and multinucleated giant cells; transmural chronic inflammation; special stains for organisms negative.

Pathogenesis

Aberrant Th1/Th17 immune response to normal gut flora in genetically predisposed individuals → chronic transmural granulomatous inflammation. Key gene: NOD2/CARD15 mutation (recognizes bacterial muramyl dipeptide).

Clinical Presentation

  • Recurrent episodic abdominal pain (often RLQ — mimics appendicitis when terminal ileum involved)
  • Chronic non-bloody or mildly bloody diarrhea (steatorrhea if significant ileal disease)
  • Weight loss, fatigue, low-grade fever
  • Perianal disease: fissures, fistulas, abscesses, skin tags
  • Complications: strictures (obstructive symptoms), fistulas (enterovesical → pneumaturia; enterocutaneous; entero-enteric), abscesses

Nutritional / Metabolic Consequences (ileal disease)

  • B12 deficiency → macrocytic anemia (ileum absorbs B12-IF complex)
  • Bile acid malabsorption → fat malabsorption → steatorrhea + fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (A, D, E, K)
  • Calcium oxalate kidney stones: Fat malabsorption → calcium binds fat instead of oxalate → free oxalate absorbed in colon → hyperoxaluria → calcium oxalate nephrolithiasis

Treatment

  • 5-ASA compounds (mesalamine) for mild-moderate disease
  • Corticosteroids (budesonide, prednisone) for flares
  • Immunomodulators: azathioprine, 6-mercaptopurine, methotrexate
  • Biologics: anti-TNF-α (infliximab, adalimumab) → for moderate-severe disease; anti-IL-12/23 (ustekinumab); anti-integrins (vedolizumab)
  • Surgery: reserved for complications (strictures, fistulas, abscess); NOT curative — recurs

▼ Ulcerative Colitis — In Depth

🔬 Lab Slides — Ulcerative Colitis

Gross (extensive UC): Continuous mucosal inflammation from the ileocecal valve (beginning of cecum) extending proximally; erythema and granularity → coalescing linear ulcers that undermine remaining mucosa → pseudopolyps (raised red islands of inflamed mucosa between ulcers). Histology: Crypt abscesses (neutrophils within crypts); mucosal ulceration; process is primarily mucosal — no transmural inflammation, no fistulas.

Pathogenesis

Aberrant Th2 immune response → mucosal inflammation limited to the colon; begins at the rectum and extends proximally in a continuous fashion. Associated with HLA-DR2 and p-ANCA positivity.

Clinical Presentation

  • Bloody diarrhea with mucus — the hallmark (vs. Crohn's which is less reliably bloody)
  • Tenesmus (sensation of incomplete evacuation); urgency; lower abdominal cramping
  • Episodic flares separated by remissions
  • Extraintestinal manifestations (see below)

Complications

  • Toxic megacolon: Severe transmural inflammation → colonic dilation >6 cm → risk of perforation and sepsis; medical emergency
  • Colorectal carcinoma: Significant ↑ risk; proportional to extent (pancolitis > left-sided) and duration (>8–10 years); surveillance colonoscopy required
  • Primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC): Fibro-obliterative inflammation of bile ducts → cholestasis, cirrhosis, cholangiocarcinoma risk; 70% of PSC patients have UC

Treatment

  • 5-ASA compounds (mesalamine, sulfasalazine) — mainstay for mild-moderate
  • Corticosteroids for flares
  • Immunomodulators: azathioprine, 6-MP
  • Biologics: anti-TNF-α (infliximab, adalimumab); anti-integrin (vedolizumab); JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib)
  • Surgery (colectomy) = curative — unlike Crohn's; indicated for refractory disease, toxic megacolon, dysplasia/cancer

Both Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis are systemic inflammatory diseases with extraintestinal manifestations (EIMs). Some EIMs correlate with intestinal disease activity; others run an independent course.

▼ USMLE Step 1 High-Yield Summary

🎯 Step 1 High-Yield — IBD
  • Crohn's: transmural, skip lesions, any GI segment (classically terminal ileum), non-caseating granulomas, fistulas, NOT curative with surgery
  • UC: mucosal only, continuous from rectum, crypt abscesses, NO granulomas, NO fistulas, curative colectomy
  • Bloody diarrhea → think UC first; RLQ pain + fistulas + perianal disease → think Crohn's
  • PSC strongly associated with UC (70% of PSC = UC); PSC → cholangiocarcinoma risk
  • Toxic megacolon = UC complication; medical emergency; colonic dilation >6 cm
  • Colorectal cancer risk: UC > Crohn's (if colonic); screen after 8–10 years with pancolitis
  • Smoking paradox: worsens Crohn's; paradoxically protective in UC (nicotine patches used therapeutically in UC)
  • B12 malabsorption = Crohn's with terminal ileum involvement (not UC)
  • Both have SPEAR extraintestinal manifestations

  • Krukenberg tumor: Gastric (or colon) cancer metastasizing to both ovaries → bilateral ovarian enlargement with signet ring cells
  • Sister Mary Joseph nodule: Periumbilical subcutaneous metastasis → palpable umbilical nodule
  • Virchow's node (Troisier sign): Left supraclavicular lymphadenopathy from GI (especially gastric) cancer metastasis via thoracic duct
▼Gastrointestinal Stromal Tumor (GIST)
🔬 Lab Slides — GIST

Gross: Prominent mass arising from the muscular wall of the stomach; grows exophytically into the lumen; covered by mucosa except in the central ulcerated area (4 cm scale shown). Histology: Bundles of spindle cells — biologic potential assessed by mitotic count, size, and cellularity.

  • Cell of origin: Interstitial cells of Cajal (pacemaker cells of the myenteric plexus)
  • Mutation: Activating mutation of c-KIT (CD117) receptor tyrosine kinase (85% of cases); PDGFRA mutation in remaining cases
  • Location: Stomach most common (60%), then small intestine (30%); arise in muscularis propria → submucosal mass
  • Histology: Spindle cells in interlacing bundles (most common); ± epithelioid pattern
  • Prognosis determinants: Mitotic rate, tumor size, and location (gastric = better prognosis than small intestine)
  • Treatment: Surgical resection; imatinib (Gleevec) — tyrosine kinase inhibitor targeting c-KIT — for unresectable/metastatic disease; revolutionary targeted therapy
  • IHC: CD117 (c-KIT) positive; CD34 positive; desmin negative (distinguishes from leiomyoma)
⚠️ Exam Trap

GIST is not a smooth muscle tumor. It arises from interstitial cells of Cajal, not from leiomyocytes. Key marker = c-KIT (CD117). Treatment = imatinib.

▼ Small Intestine Tumors

▼Carcinoid Tumor (Well-Differentiated Neuroendocrine Tumor)
🔬 Lab Slides — Carcinoid Tumor

Gross: Yellow-tan submucosal nodule at the ileocecal valve (most common SI location). Benign tumors are incidental submucosal lesions; rarely can be large enough to obstruct. Histology: Nests of uniform small round cells with small round nuclei and pink-to-pale blue cytoplasm — typical "endocrine appearance."

  • Origin: Neuroendocrine cells (Kulchitsky/enterochromaffin cells) in the gut epithelium; secrete serotonin (5-HT) and other vasoactive substances
  • Location: Appendix (most common overall, usually benign); small intestine/ileum (most likely to metastasize); rectum
  • Histology: Uniform nests of small round cells with "salt-and-pepper" chromatin; IHC: chromogranin A, synaptophysin positive
  • Clinical: Usually asymptomatic; large tumors → pain, obstruction
  • Carcinoid syndrome: Occurs when serotonin reaches systemic circulation (requires liver metastases to bypass first-pass hepatic metabolism of serotonin)
🔥 Carcinoid Syndrome — Step 1 Must Know

Requires hepatic metastases (serotonin bypasses hepatic inactivation). Classic features:

  • Flushing (episodic, cutaneous)
  • Diarrhea (watery, secretory)
  • Wheezing (bronchospasm)
  • Right-sided valvular heart disease (tricuspid regurgitation, pulmonic stenosis — serotonin fibroses right heart; left heart is protected by lung metabolism)

Diagnosis: ↑ urine 5-HIAA (5-hydroxyindoleacetic acid — serotonin metabolite) · Treatment: Octreotide (somatostatin analog) for symptom control; surgical resection

▼Small Bowel Adenocarcinoma
  • Rare — accounts for only 1–2% of all GI malignancies despite the small bowel comprising 75% of GI length
  • Most common in duodenum (near ampulla of Vater) > jejunum > ileum
  • Risk factors: Crohn's disease (ileal), celiac disease, FAP, HNPCC/Lynch syndrome
  • Presents late: obstructive symptoms, weight loss, occult bleeding, iron deficiency anemia
  • Poor prognosis — difficult to detect early

▼Adenomatous Polyps (Tubular & Villous)
🔬 Lab Slides — Adenomatous Polyp

Gross: Hemorrhagic polypoid lesion with long narrow stalk; detected by stool occult blood testing. Size >2 cm → increased malignancy risk. Histology (tubular adenoma): Crowded, disorganized back-to-back glands; goblet cells are less numerous; cells lining glands show hyperchromatic nuclei (dysplasia). ▼Familial Adenomatous Polyposis (FAP)
🔬 Lab Slide — FAP

Gross: Hundreds to thousands of small polyps carpeting the entire colonic mucosa. This patient had the adenomatous polyposis coli (APC) gene mutation.

  • Genetics: APC tumor suppressor gene mutation on chromosome 5q21; autosomal dominant
  • Phenotype: Hundreds to thousands of adenomatous polyps throughout the colon beginning in early adolescence
  • Cancer risk: 100% lifetime risk of colorectal cancer if untreated; average age of CRC = 39 years
  • Treatment: Prophylactic total colectomy (usually by age 20–25)
  • Variants: Gardner syndrome (FAP + osteomas + desmoid tumors + epidermoid cysts); Turcot syndrome (FAP + brain tumors)
▼Colorectal Adenocarcinoma
🔬 Lab Slides — Colorectal Adenocarcinoma

Gross: Annular "napkin-ring" mass — heaped-up margins on each side of a central area of ulceration; encircles the colon and infiltrates the wall. Normal mucosa appears on right. Staging based on degree of wall invasion. Histology: Invasive glandular structures in desmoplastic stroma; large glands filled with necrotic debris.

Adenoma-Carcinoma Sequence (Vogelstein Model):

  • APC mutation (loss of tumor suppressor) → hyperproliferative epithelium → small adenoma
  • KRAS oncogene mutation → intermediate adenoma (↑ growth signal)
  • SMAD4 / DCC loss (18q) → late adenoma
  • TP53 mutation → invasive carcinoma
  • Process takes ~10–15 years — key rationale for colonoscopy screening at age 45

Gross patternAnnular "napkin-ring" / encircling → lumen narrowingFungating/polypoid → into lumen

Staging & Prognosis

  • TNM staging: Depth of wall invasion (T), lymph node involvement (N), distant metastasis (M)
  • Dukes staging (classic): A = confined to mucosa; B = through wall; C = lymph nodes involved; D = distant mets
  • Tumor marker: CEA (carcinoembryonic antigen) — not for screening but for monitoring recurrence after resection
  • Metastasis: Liver (hematogenous via portal vein) → lungs → peritoneum; bone/brain less common

Lynch Syndrome (HNPCC)

  • Most common hereditary CRC syndrome; autosomal dominant
  • Mutations in DNA mismatch repair genes: MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2 → microsatellite instability (MSI)
  • Right-sided colon predominance; mucinous histology; better prognosis than sporadic CRC
  • Amsterdam criteria: 3 relatives with Lynch-associated cancers (CRC, endometrial, ovarian, gastric, urinary tract), across 2 generations, with 1 diagnosed <50
  • Screening: annual colonoscopy from age 20–25; prophylactic surgery considered
⭐ Screening Recommendations (Step 1):
  • Average risk: colonoscopy starting at age 45 (updated from 50); repeat every 10 years if normal
  • FAP: Start at age 10–12; prophylactic colectomy by 20–25
  • Lynch syndrome: Colonoscopy every 1–2 years starting age 20–25

🎯 Step 1 High-Yield — GI Tumors

  • Esophageal adenocarcinoma: lower 1/3 / GEJ; GERD + Barrett's; now more common in US than SCC
  • Gastric adenocarcinoma intestinal type: H. pylori, smoked foods; glandular; Virchow's node, Krukenberg tumor, Sister Mary Joseph nodule
  • Diffuse gastric adenocarcinoma (linitis plastica): signet ring cells; CDH1/E-cadherin mutation; leather bottle stomach
  • GIST: c-KIT (CD117) mutation; interstitial cells of Cajal; spindle cells; treat with imatinib

  • Liver = most common site of CRC metastasis (via portal vein)

Practice Questions

GI Pathology Lab — 15 USMLE-Style Questions · HoyaDocs GI Block

Q1 (LO 1 — Histology). A medical student examines a histology slide of the stomach. Which cell type, located primarily in the fundus and body, secretes hydrochloric acid AND intrinsic factor?
Parietal (oxyntic) cells in the fundus/body secrete both HCl (via H⁺/K⁺-ATPase) and intrinsic factor. Chief cells secrete pepsinogen. G cells (antrum) secrete gastrin. Goblet cells are in the intestine. Loss of parietal cells in autoimmune gastritis → loss of intrinsic factor → pernicious anemia (B12 deficiency).
Q2 (LO 1 — Histology). A biopsy shows straight tubular crypts lined by numerous goblet cells with no visible villi. The submucosa contains a prominent lymphoid nodule. Which organ does this describe?
The colon has NO villi — only straight crypts (crypts of Lieberkühn) lined by abundant goblet cells. The GALT (submucosal lymphoid tissue) is a normal finding in colonic submucosa. All small intestinal segments have villi (tallest in jejunum). The esophagus has stratified squamous epithelium.
Q3 (LO 2 — Pathogenesis). A 50-year-old man with 15 years of GERD undergoes endoscopy. Biopsy of the distal esophagus shows columnar epithelium with goblet cells above the gastroesophageal junction. What is the most significant long-term concern?
This describes Barrett's esophagus — GERD-induced intestinal metaplasia with goblet cells replacing normal squamous epithelium. The goblet cells are the hallmark. Barrett's carries a 30–40× increased risk of esophageal adenocarcinoma (not SCC). Regular endoscopic surveillance is required. Barrett's = metaplasia, not yet dysplasia or carcinoma.
Q4 (LO 2 — Pathogenesis). A 48-year-old woman with chronic epigastric burning has a gastric biopsy showing small curved organisms in the surface mucus on methylene blue staining. Which of the following best describes the clinical associations of this organism?
This is H. pylori — a curved/spiral gram-negative rod visible with methylene blue or Giemsa stain. It is urease-positive (produces NH₃ to survive in acid) and is classified as a WHO Group 1 carcinogen. It is associated with 95% of duodenal ulcers, 70–80% of gastric ulcers, gastric adenocarcinoma (intestinal type), and gastric MALT lymphoma (which can regress with eradication). Treat with triple therapy (PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin).
Q5 (LO 2 — Pathogenesis). A 26-year-old woman with iron deficiency anemia and chronic diarrhea has a small bowel biopsy showing blunted villi and increased intraepithelial lymphocytes. HLA typing reveals HLA-DQ2. Which dietary protein triggers this immune response?
This is celiac disease (gluten-sensitive enteropathy). Gliadin — the alcohol-soluble fraction of gluten (found in wheat, barley, rye, oats) — triggers an immune-mediated attack on small bowel mucosa in HLA-DQ2 or HLA-DQ8 individuals. Histology: villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, increased IELs. Serology: anti-tTG IgA. Treatment: strict gluten-free diet. Complication: EATL lymphoma in refractory cases.
Q6 (LO 2 — Pathogenesis). A 70-year-old hospitalized patient on clindamycin for aspiration pneumonia develops profuse watery diarrhea. Colonoscopy shows a yellow-green membrane covering the hyperemic colonic mucosa. Histology shows inflammatory cells, necrotic epithelium, and mucus adherent to an intact underlying mucosa. What is the most likely causative organism?
Pseudomembranous colitis caused by C. difficile. Clindamycin (and fluoroquinolones, ampicillin, cephalosporins) disrupts normal flora → C. diff overgrowth → toxins A + B → mucosal damage with pseudomembrane formation. The key histologic finding is the pseudomembrane over an INTACT underlying mucosa (unlike UC where mucosa is ulcerated). Diagnosis: stool PCR for C. diff toxin genes. Treatment: oral vancomycin or fidaxomicin.
Q7 (LO 2 — Pathogenesis). Which of the following is the most common cause of small bowel obstruction in developed countries?
Adhesions from prior abdominal surgery are the #1 cause of SBO in developed nations (50–70% of cases). Fibrous bands form between loops of bowel and can kink or compress the lumen. Hernias are the second most common cause. In developing nations, hernias may rank first. Intussusception is more common in children; volvulus is more common in sigmoid colon (large bowel).
Q8 (LO 3 — IBD). A 24-year-old woman presents with bloody diarrhea, tenesmus, and lower abdominal cramping over 6 weeks. Colonoscopy shows continuous mucosal erythema starting from the rectum extending to the transverse colon. Biopsy shows crypt abscesses and mucosal ulceration but NO granulomas. The process does not extend into the submucosa. Which diagnosis best fits?
Ulcerative colitis. Key features: continuous disease from rectum, mucosal-only inflammation, crypt abscesses, bloody diarrhea, NO granulomas. UC always involves the rectum and extends proximally in a continuous pattern (never skip lesions). Crohn's has skip lesions, transmural inflammation, and granulomas. Treatment of UC includes 5-ASA compounds; colectomy is curative.
Q9 (LO 3 — IBD). Which of the following features is characteristic of Crohn's disease but is NOT typically seen in ulcerative colitis?
Non-caseating granulomas with transmural inflammation = Crohn's disease hallmark. UC is mucosal-only and does NOT have granulomas. Crypt abscesses can occur in both (more typical of UC). Rectal involvement is almost always present in UC (Crohn's can spare the rectum). Pseudopolyps (islands of inflamed mucosa) are characteristic of UC. PSC strongly associates with UC (70% of PSC = UC).
Q10 (LO 3 — IBD). A 32-year-old woman with a 10-year history of pancolitis develops right upper quadrant pain and jaundice. Labs show elevated alkaline phosphatase and total bilirubin. MRCP shows "beaded" appearance of intra- and extrahepatic bile ducts. She has never used alcohol. What complication of her IBD does this most likely represent?
Primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC) — fibro-obliterative inflammation of bile ducts causing the classic "beaded" appearance on MRCP (alternating strictures and dilations). PSC is strongly associated with UC (70% of PSC patients have UC), runs independent of disease activity, and carries risk of cholangiocarcinoma. Toxic megacolon causes colonic dilation/perforation, not biliary disease.
Q11 (LO 4 — Tumors). A 62-year-old male smoker and heavy drinker presents with progressive dysphagia (solids → liquids) and 25-pound weight loss over 4 months. Endoscopy reveals an irregular ulcerated exophytic mass in the mid-esophagus. Biopsy shows nests of cells with abundant pink cytoplasm and distinct cell borders. What is the most likely diagnosis?
Esophageal squamous cell carcinoma (SCC). Key clues: upper/mid esophagus location, male smoker + alcohol drinker (major risk factors in the US), abundant pink cytoplasm with distinct cell borders (squamous differentiation). Adenocarcinoma arises in the lower 1/3 from Barrett's esophagus and is associated with GERD/obesity. Both carry very poor prognosis due to the esophagus lacking a serosa.
Q12 (LO 4 — Tumors). A gastrectomy specimen shows a stomach with rigid, thickened walls and a "leather bottle" gross appearance. Histology reveals individual cells with large cytoplasmic mucin vacuoles displacing the nucleus to one side. Which of the following best describes this tumor?
Linitis plastica — the diffuse type of gastric adenocarcinoma. The leather-bottle gross appearance results from diffuse infiltration of the gastric wall by poorly cohesive signet ring cells (intracytoplasmic mucin pushes the nucleus to the periphery). It is associated with CDH1/E-cadherin mutations and is highly invasive with poor prognosis. It can metastasize to both ovaries (Krukenberg tumor) and the umbilicus (Sister Mary Joseph nodule).
Q13 (LO 4 — Tumors). A 52-year-old man has a resected submucosal gastric mass. Histology shows bundles of spindle cells. Immunohistochemistry is strongly positive for CD117 (c-KIT) and CD34. He is found to have liver metastases on staging CT. Which of the following is the most appropriate targeted therapy?
GIST (gastrointestinal stromal tumor) treated with imatinib (Gleevec). GISTs arise from interstitial cells of Cajal and harbor activating c-KIT (CD117) mutations. Imatinib is a tyrosine kinase inhibitor that specifically targets the c-KIT receptor — a landmark example of targeted cancer therapy. CD34 positivity is also characteristic. Infliximab = anti-TNF-α (IBD). Rituximab = anti-CD20 (lymphoma). Octreotide = carcinoid syndrome.
Q14 (LO 4 — Tumors). A 35-year-old man with a family history of colorectal cancer undergoes colonoscopy and is found to have hundreds of polyps carpeting his entire colon. Genetic testing reveals a mutation in the APC tumor suppressor gene. What is the most appropriate management?
Familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP) — autosomal dominant APC gene mutation — carries 100% lifetime risk of colorectal cancer without intervention. Prophylactic total colectomy (usually by age 20–25) is the definitive treatment. Surveillance alone is insufficient given the near-certain cancer risk. Variants include Gardner syndrome (osteomas, desmoid tumors) and Turcot syndrome (brain tumors).
Q15 (LO 4 — Tumors). A 55-year-old man presents with fatigue and iron deficiency anemia. Stool guaiac is positive. Colonoscopy reveals an exophytic mass in the cecum. Histology shows invasive glandular structures in a desmoplastic stroma with large glands filled with necrotic debris. Where does this tumor most commonly metastasize first?
Colorectal adenocarcinoma (right-sided, presenting with occult bleeding and iron deficiency anemia — classic right colon presentation). It metastasizes most commonly to the liver via the portal venous system. This is the classic hematogenous route for all colorectal cancers draining into the portal circulation. Liver metastases appear as "target lesions" on CT. Lungs are the next most common site after liver. CEA is used to monitor for recurrence post-resection (not for screening).
