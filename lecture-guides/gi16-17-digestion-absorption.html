▼ Small Intestine Structure

The small intestine achieves enormous absorptive surface area (~250 m²) through three levels of amplification:

  • Plicae circulares (valves of Kerckring): Permanent circular folds of mucosa and submucosa — increase area ~3×
  • Villi: Finger-like projections of mucosa (0.5–1.5 mm tall) — increase area ~10×; each villus contains a central lacteal (lymphatic capillary) and a capillary network
  • Microvilli (brush border): Dense apical membrane projections (~1 µm tall) on each enterocyte — increase area ~20×; contain brush border enzymes (saccharidases, peptidases, lipase)

Combined amplification: ~600-fold compared to a smooth tube of the same length.

🎯 Step 1 Buzzwords — Structural Pathology
  • Villous atrophy + crypt hyperplasia → celiac disease (anti-tTG, anti-endomysial Ab; HLA-DQ2/DQ8)
  • PAS-positive macrophages in lamina propria → Whipple's disease (Tropheryma whipplei)
  • Dilated lacteals → intestinal lymphangiectasia → fat and protein malabsorption

▼ Unstirred Water Layer

Immediately adjacent to the brush border lies an unstirred water layer (UWL) — a thin, relatively stagnant aqueous film (~100–500 µm thick) through which all absorbed molecules must diffuse before reaching the enterocyte membrane.

  • The UWL is a rate-limiting step for absorption of highly lipophilic compounds
  • Bile salt micelles ferry lipid digestion products across the UWL, dramatically increasing effective lipid absorption
  • Without micelles, fat-soluble nutrients cannot traverse the UWL efficiently → steatorrhea
  • The UWL also creates a microclimate of slightly lower pH near the brush border (due to Na+/H+ exchange), which facilitates proton-coupled transport of di/tripeptides via PepT1
⚠️ Exam Trap — UWL ▼

Students often think micelles are absorbed whole. They are not. Micelles carry lipids across the UWL; lipid products then passively diffuse across the enterocyte membrane while bile salts stay in the lumen and are recycled in the terminal ileum.

🎯 Step 1 — The Sodium Gradient is Everything

The Na+/K+-ATPase on the basolateral membrane keeps intracellular Na+ low. This drives secondary active transport of glucose (SGLT1), amino acids, and peptides (PepT1) across the apical brush border — even against their concentration gradients. Cholera toxin disrupts this by locking CFTR open → massive Cl⁻/water secretion, not Na+ failure.

▼ Fiber & Cellulose

Dietary fiber consists primarily of cellulose, a glucose polymer linked by β-1,4 glycosidic bonds.

  • Human digestive enzymes (amylase, maltase, etc.) can only cleave α-glycosidic bonds
  • No β-glucosidase (cellulase) is produced by the human GI tract → cellulose passes through undigested
  • Colonic bacteria ferment some soluble fiber → short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs: butyrate, propionate, acetate) → energy source for colonocytes
  • Insoluble fiber (cellulose) adds bulk, reduces transit time, and binds water in stool
🧠 Mnemonic — α vs. β ▼

"Alpha we Absorb, Beta we Bypass" — humans absorb α-linked sugars (starch, glycogen) but bypass β-linked sugars (cellulose, chitin). Ruminants have cellulases from gut bacteria; we don't.

▼ Carbohydrate Digestion

Dietary carbohydrates arrive as starch (~50–60%), sucrose (~30%), and lactose (~10%). Digestion is a sequential, two-phase process: luminal (salivary + pancreatic amylase) → brush border (saccharidases).

Phase 1 — Luminal Digestion

  • Salivary amylase (ptyalin) begins starch digestion in the mouth and proximal stomach; inactivated by gastric acid (pH < 4) — accounts for only ~5% of total digestion
  • Pancreatic amylase (secreted into duodenum) is the dominant luminal amylase; cleaves internal α-1,4 bonds → maltose, maltotriose, and α-limit dextrins (which contain α-1,6 branch points)
  • Neither amylase can cleave the α-1,6 branch points of starch — that requires isomaltase

Phase 2 — Brush Border Digestion

  • Maltase: maltose + maltotriose → glucose
  • Sucrase-isomaltase complex: sucrose → glucose + fructose; α-limit dextrins → glucose
  • Lactase (β-galactosidase): lactose → glucose + galactose; highest activity in jejunum; lowest activity at birth, declines with age in most populations
  • End products entering the enterocyte: glucose, galactose, fructose (all monosaccharides)
⭐ High-Yield — Dietary Starch Cannot Be Absorbed Intact
  • Starch must be fully hydrolyzed to monosaccharides before absorption; oligosaccharides and disaccharides are not transported across the enterocyte membrane
  • Only monosaccharides are absorbed: glucose, galactose (via SGLT1), fructose (via GLUT5)

▼ Carbohydrate Absorption (SGLT1 / GLUT5 / GLUT2)

▼ Disaccharidase Deficiency & Lactose Intolerance

Primary lactase deficiency is the most common disaccharidase deficiency — physiologic decline in lactase activity after weaning, especially prevalent in non-Northern European populations.

Pathophysiology of Symptoms

  • Undigested lactose remains in the lumen → osmotic effect: draws water into lumen → watery diarrhea
  • Lactose reaches the colon → colonic bacteria ferment it → H₂, CO₂, short-chain fatty acids → bloating, cramping, flatulence
  • Symptoms begin 30–60 minutes after lactose ingestion
  • Diagnosis: hydrogen breath test (↑H₂ after lactose load) or empirical dietary trial

Secondary Disaccharidase Deficiency

  • Any condition causing villous atrophy or brush border damage can secondarily reduce all disaccharidase activity: celiac disease, Crohn's disease, rotavirus enteritis, giardiasis
  • Note: sucrase-isomaltase deficiency is a separate AR disorder — rare; sucrose ingestion causes diarrhea; starch digestion also impaired
🎯 Step 1 — Osmotic vs. Secretory Diarrhea
  • Osmotic diarrhea (e.g., lactose intolerance): stops with fasting; stool osmolar gap > 50 mOsm/kg; caused by unabsorbed solutes in lumen
  • Secretory diarrhea (e.g., cholera, VIPoma): continues despite fasting; normal stool osmolar gap; caused by active Cl⁻/water secretion

▼ Protein Digestion

Dietary protein (~50–100 g/day) undergoes a three-stage cascade of proteolysis: gastric → pancreatic → brush border/cytoplasmic.

Stage 1 — Gastric

  • HCl (parietal cells): denatures proteins (unfolds) → more peptide bonds accessible; converts pepsinogen → pepsin (autocatalytic at pH < 5)
  • Pepsin: endopeptidase; cleaves Phe, Trp, Tyr, and Leu bonds → large polypeptide fragments; accounts for ~10–15% of total protein digestion; inactivated at duodenal pH > 5

Stage 2 — Pancreatic (in Duodenum)

The critical enterokinase (enteropeptidase) cascade:

  • Duodenal brush border enterokinase cleaves trypsinogen → trypsin
  • Trypsin then activates: chymotrypsinogen → chymotrypsin; proelastase → elastase; procarboxypeptidase A & B → carboxypeptidases A & B; trypsinogen → more trypsin (autocatalytic)
  • Trypsin: cleaves Lys/Arg bonds (basic AA)
  • Chymotrypsin: cleaves aromatic AA bonds (Phe, Trp, Tyr, Leu)
  • Elastase: cleaves Ala, Gly, Ser bonds
  • Carboxypeptidases A & B: exopeptidases; cleave from C-terminus
  • Products: oligopeptides (30%), tripeptides, dipeptides, and free amino acids (70%)
⚠️ Exam Trap — Trypsinogen Activation ▼

Enterokinase, not trypsin itself, is the initiating activator. Without enterokinase (e.g., congenital deficiency), no pancreatic protease becomes active → severe protein malabsorption despite normal pancreatic function. Contrast with pancreatitis (premature intrapancreatic trypsin activation).

Also: trypsin inhibitor (SPINK1) normally prevents premature trypsin activation in the pancreatic duct. SPINK1 mutations → chronic pancreatitis.

▼ Protein Absorption

Amino acids and small peptides are absorbed across the brush border by multiple transporters:

  • Free amino acids: multiple Na+-dependent cotransporters (grouped by AA type: neutral, basic, acidic, imino) + some Na+-independent carriers; enter portal blood via basolateral transporters
  • Di- and tripeptides: absorbed intact via PepT1 (H+-peptide cotransporter) — uses the proton gradient created by the Na+/H+ antiporter in the brush border microclimate; di/tripeptides are absorbed MORE efficiently than free AAs
  • Cytoplasmic peptidases then hydrolyze di/tripeptides to free amino acids within the enterocyte before they exit basolaterally
⭐ High-Yield — Hartnup Disease & Cystinuria
  • Hartnup disease (AR): defective neutral amino acid transporter (intestinal + renal); tryptophan malabsorption → niacin deficiency → pellagra-like rash, ataxia, psychiatric symptoms; di/tripeptide absorption is intact (PepT1 unaffected) → symptoms are mild/variable
  • Cystinuria (AR): defective basic AA transporter (cystine, ornithine, arginine, lysine — "COAL"); forms cystine kidney stones; intestinal absorption also impaired but clinical manifestations are renal

▼ Lipid Digestion

Dietary fat is primarily triglycerides (TGs). Because fats are hydrophobic, digestion requires a stepwise process of emulsification → enzymatic hydrolysis → micellar solubilization before absorption.

Step 1 — Lingual & Gastric Lipase

  • Lingual lipase (von Ebner's glands): begins TG hydrolysis in mouth; active at pH 2–7.5 → continues in stomach; preferentially hydrolyzes short- and medium-chain FA at sn-3 position → diacylglycerol + FA
  • Gastric lipase (gastric chief cells): active at pH 3–6; primarily short/medium-chain TGs; accounts for ~10–30% of total TG digestion in adults; critical in neonates (pancreatic lipase immature) and pancreatic exocrine insufficiency
  • Gastric churning creates a coarse emulsion of TG droplets → increases surface area for further digestion

Step 2 — Pancreatic Lipase + Co-lipase

  • Bile salts displace pancreatic lipase from the lipid-water interface → co-lipase (secreted by pancreas as pro-co-lipase; activated by trypsin) anchors pancreatic lipase at the interface, restoring activity
  • Pancreatic lipase cleaves sn-1 and sn-3 ester bonds of TG → 2-monoglyceride + 2 free fatty acids (the dominant products)
  • Pancreatic lipase accounts for ~70–80% of TG digestion; it requires an alkaline pH (>6.5) — this is why bicarbonate secretion by the pancreas is essential
⚠️ Exam Trap — Co-lipase is Required for Pancreatic Lipase ▼

Without co-lipase, bile salts inhibit pancreatic lipase by competitively occupying the lipid interface. Co-lipase anchors pancreatic lipase to the droplet surface. Orlistat (weight-loss drug) works by irreversibly inhibiting pancreatic lipase → prevents TG digestion → steatorrhea as side effect.

▼ Bile Salts & Micelle Formation

Bile salts (amphipathic molecules — hydrophilic face + hydrophobic face) serve two key roles in fat digestion: emulsification and micelle formation.

  • Emulsification: Bile salts coat large TG droplets → break them into smaller droplets (1–2 µm) → increased surface area for lipase action
  • Micelle formation: After lipase action, bile salts surround 2-monoglycerides, free fatty acids, cholesterol, and fat-soluble vitamins → form mixed micelles (4–8 nm diameter, cylindrical) with hydrophobic core + hydrophilic exterior
  • Micelles transport lipid digestion products across the unstirred water layer to the brush border; at the brush border surface, the products diffuse out of the micelle and passively cross the lipid bilayer into the enterocyte
  • Bile salts remain in the lumen after releasing their contents — they are recycled in the terminal ileum (enterohepatic circulation)
⭐ High-Yield — Cholestasis → Fat Malabsorption
  • Any condition reducing bile salt delivery to the duodenum (cholestasis, biliary obstruction, ileal disease) → ↓ micelle formation → steatorrhea + fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (A, D, E, K)
  • Vitamin K deficiency → ↑ PT (clotting factor synthesis requires vitamin K)
  • Vitamin D deficiency → ↓ Ca²+ absorption → osteomalacia/rickets
  • Vitamin A deficiency → night blindness

▼ Lipid Absorption & Chylomicron Assembly

Once lipid products (2-monoglycerides, free fatty acids, cholesterol, fat-soluble vitamins) arrive at the brush border, they diffuse passively across the enterocyte membrane (the lipid bilayer is permeable to hydrophobic molecules).

Intracellular Processing

  • Inside the enterocyte, free fatty acids and 2-monoglycerides are carried to the smooth endoplasmic reticulum (SER) by fatty acid binding proteins
  • In the SER: re-esterification — 2-MAG + 2 FA → triglyceride (via monoacylglycerol pathway, the dominant route in enterocytes)
  • TG, cholesterol esters, fat-soluble vitamins, and phospholipids are assembled into chylomicrons using apolipoprotein B-48 (apoB-48) — made from editing of the apoB-100 mRNA (unique to intestine)
  • Chylomicrons are packaged in the Golgi → exocytosed at the basolateral membrane → enter lacteals (not portal capillaries — too large, ~75–1200 nm)
  • Lacteals → mesenteric lymphatics → thoracic duct → left subclavian vein → systemic circulation

Short- & Medium-Chain Fatty Acids — Exception

  • Short-chain (C<8) and medium-chain (C8–C12) fatty acids are sufficiently water-soluble to bypass micelles and be absorbed directly into portal blood (not packaged into chylomicrons)
  • Clinical application: MCT oil is used in patients with fat malabsorption (abetalipoproteinemia, lymphangiectasia) because it bypasses the chylomicron pathway
🎯 Step 1 — Chylomicron Route Summary

Lipids → enterocyte (passive diffusion) → SER re-esterification → chylomicron (apoB-48) → basolateral exocytosis → lacteal → thoracic duct → left subclavian vein. This is why a fatty meal causes chylous-appearing lymph (chyle) and a turbid plasma sample (chylomicronemia) after eating.

▼ Abetalipoproteinemia & Steatorrhea

apoB-48 absentfat in enterocytesacanthocyteslow cholesterolfat-soluble vitamin deficiencyataxia

Abetalipoproteinemia (AR) is caused by a defect in microsomal triglyceride transfer protein (MTP), which is required to load apoB-48 onto chylomicrons. Without functional MTP, chylomicrons cannot be assembled.

  • Fat digestion is normal; lipids enter enterocytes normally
  • Re-esterification occurs in SER but chylomicrons cannot be exported → fat accumulates in enterocytes (vacuoles visible on biopsy)
  • Steatorrhea (foul-smelling, fatty, floating stools)
  • Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) cannot be absorbed → night blindness, osteomalacia, neurologic symptoms (vitamin E deficiency → spinocerebellar degeneration → ataxia)
  • Acanthocytes (spur cells) on peripheral blood smear — altered red cell membrane lipid composition
  • Very low serum cholesterol and triglycerides (no chylomicrons or VLDL)
  • Treatment: low-fat diet + high-dose fat-soluble vitamin supplementation; MCT oil for calories
⚠️ Exam Trap — Don't Confuse with Familial Hypobetalipoproteinemia ▼

Familial hypobetalipoproteinemia = heterozygous truncating mutations in APOB gene → reduced (not absent) apoB-48 and apoB-100. Milder phenotype than abetalipoproteinemia. Abetalipoproteinemia = MTP gene mutation → no assembly of any apoB-containing lipoprotein.

▼ Enterohepatic Circulation of Bile Acids

The body conserves bile acids by recycling them 6–10 times per day. The terminal ileum is the site of active reabsorption of conjugated bile acids.

The Recycling Loop

  1. Liver synthesizes primary bile acids (cholic acid, chenodeoxycholic acid) from cholesterol → conjugated with glycine or taurine (→ bile salts) → secreted in bile → stored in gallbladder
  2. Meal stimulates CCK → gallbladder contraction → bile salts enter duodenum → emulsify fat + form micelles
  3. Terminal ileum: conjugated bile salts actively reabsorbed via ASBT (apical sodium-dependent bile acid transporter) — Na+/bile acid co-transport; enters portal blood → hepatic re-uptake → re-secreted (the "loop")
  4. Colonic bacteria deconjugate remaining bile salts → unconjugated bile acids (secondary: deoxycholic acid, lithocholic acid) reabsorbed by passive diffusion in colon
  5. ~5% of bile acids are lost in feces daily — replenished by hepatic de novo synthesis

Ileal Resection — Consequences

  • Loss of active bile acid reabsorption → ↑ bile acids in colon → cholerheic (bile acid) diarrhea (bile acids stimulate colonic water secretion)
  • If resection > ~100 cm: bile acid pool depleted → ↓ micelle formation → fat malabsorption & steatorrhea
  • Reduced micelles → ↓ absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) and vitamin B12 (ileum also required for B12-IF complex absorption)
  • Increased hepatic bile acid synthesis (upregulated to compensate) but often insufficient
  • ↑ oxalate absorption (unbound by calcium in lumen) → enteric hyperoxaluria → calcium oxalate kidney stones
⚠️ Exam Trap — Terminal Ileum Resection Hits Two Systems ▼

Terminal ileum resection causes both (1) bile acid malabsorption (→ steatorrhea if large resection) AND (2) vitamin B12 malabsorption (→ megaloblastic anemia). The two things the terminal ileum is uniquely responsible for. Cholestyramine (bile acid sequestrant) can help bind bile acids in the colon to reduce diarrhea — but worsens fat malabsorption.

▼ Iron Absorption

duodenumFe2+ (ferrous) absorbedDMT1ferritin (storage)ferroportin (export)transferrin (blood)

Iron is absorbed primarily in the duodenum and proximal jejunum. Body iron stores tightly regulate absorption since there is no active excretion mechanism.

Heme Iron (Most Bioavailable)

  • From meat (hemoglobin, myoglobin); absorbed intact by heme carrier protein (HCP1)
  • Inside enterocyte: heme oxygenase releases Fe2+
  • Bioavailability ~25%; not affected by gastric pH or chelators

Non-Heme Iron (Lower Bioavailability)

  1. Fe3+ → Fe2+: ferrireductase (duodenal cytochrome B, DcytB) on brush border converts Fe3+ (ferric, dietary) to Fe2+ (ferrous)
  2. Fe2+ + H+ → enterocyte: DMT1 (divalent metal transporter 1) — H+-coupled co-transport (requires acidic microenvironment; explains why achlorhydria → iron deficiency)
  3. Inside enterocyte: Fe2+ is either stored as ferritin (if body stores replete — cells shed into lumen, iron lost) OR exported via ferroportin on the basolateral membrane
  4. Hephaestin (ferroxidase) on basolateral membrane re-oxidizes Fe2+ → Fe3+ for loading onto transferrin in blood
  5. Transferrin (β-globulin, made by liver) carries Fe3+ in blood (2 iron-binding sites); delivers to bone marrow, liver, etc.

Regulation

  • Hepcidin (liver peptide hormone): ↑ when body iron stores high → binds ferroportin → causes internalization and degradation → ↓ Fe export from enterocytes (and macrophages) → ↓ serum iron
  • Iron deficiency: ↓ hepcidin → ↑ ferroportin expression → ↑ absorption
  • Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) reduces Fe3+ → Fe2+ in the gut → enhances non-heme iron absorption
  • Tannins (tea, coffee) and phytates chelate iron → ↓ absorption
⚠️ Exam Trap — Iron Absorption Requires Acid ▼
  • PPIs → ↓ gastric acid → ↓ DcytB activity (needs low pH) → ↓ Fe2+ availability → iron deficiency with chronic PPI use
  • Gastrectomy / achlorhydria → same mechanism
  • Hemochromatosis (HFE mutation): ↓ hepcidin signaling → unregulated ↑ ferroportin → excessive iron absorption → iron deposition in liver, heart, pancreas, skin, joints ("bronze diabetes")

▼ Calcium Absorption

Calcium is absorbed in the duodenum and proximal jejunum via two mechanisms: active (transcellular) and passive (paracellular).

Active (Transcellular) Absorption — Duodenum

  1. Ca2+ enters enterocyte via TRPV6 (and TRPV5) channels on the apical membrane, driven by low intracellular Ca2+
  2. Inside cell: Ca2+ binds calbindin D9k (CaBP, calcium-binding protein) → shuttled across cytoplasm (prevents toxic intracellular Ca2+ accumulation)
  3. Ca2+ exits basolaterally via PMCA1b (Ca2+-ATPase, active) and NCX1 (Na+/Ca2+ exchanger)
  4. This pathway is upregulated by 1,25-(OH)₂-vitamin D (calcitriol) → increases expression of TRPV6, calbindin, PMCA

Passive (Paracellular) Absorption

  • Occurs throughout the small intestine when luminal Ca2+ is high (large dietary load)
  • Does not require vitamin D; depends on electrochemical gradient and tight junction permeability

Regulation

  • Vitamin D (1,25-(OH)₂-D3): primary regulator of active Ca2+ absorption; upregulates all transcellular components
  • PTH: indirectly increases Ca2+ absorption by stimulating renal 1α-hydroxylase → ↑ calcitriol → ↑ intestinal Ca2+ absorption; also ↓ urinary Ca2+ excretion
  • Gastric acid: converts insoluble Ca2+ salts to soluble Ca2+ → available for absorption; achlorhydria → ↓ Ca2+ absorption
🎯 Step 1 — Vitamin D Deficiency Chain

↓ Vitamin D → ↓ calbindin → ↓ Ca2+ absorption → ↓ serum Ca2+ → ↑ PTH (secondary hyperparathyroidism) → bone resorption → rickets (children) / osteomalacia (adults). Fat malabsorption (any cause) → fat-soluble vitamin D malabsorption → same chain.

Fat-Soluble Vitamins (A, D, E, K)

  • Incorporated into chylomicrons → transported via lymphatics
  • Any cause of fat malabsorption (cholestasis, pancreatic insufficiency, ileal resection) → fat-soluble vitamin deficiency

Vitamin B12 — The Unique Pathway

Vitamin B12 absorption is a multi-step process and each step is a potential exam question:

  1. Dietary B12 is bound to protein; gastric acid + pepsin free it
  2. Free B12 binds transcobalamin I (TC1, R-protein) secreted in saliva → TC1-B12 complex travels to duodenum
  3. Pancreatic proteases digest TC1 → B12 released → binds intrinsic factor (IF) secreted by gastric parietal cells
  4. IF-B12 complex travels to terminal ileum → binds cubilin receptor → endocytosis into ileal enterocytes
  5. Inside enterocyte: IF degraded; B12 released → binds transcobalamin II (TC2) → secreted into portal blood → delivered to all cells
⚠️ Exam Trap — Every Step of B12 Absorption is Testable ▼
  • Pernicious anemia: autoimmune destruction of parietal cells → no IF → no B12 absorption → megaloblastic anemia + subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord (posterior + lateral columns)
  • Gastrectomy: removes parietal cells → same result; B12 supplementation required lifelong
  • Chronic pancreatitis: no pancreatic proteases → TC1 not cleaved → B12 stays bound to TC1 (not IF) → not absorbed; mild B12 deficiency possible
  • Terminal ileum resection / Crohn's of terminal ileum: no IF-B12 receptor → B12 malabsorption
  • D. latum (fish tapeworm): competes for B12 in the gut → deficiency
  • Veganism: no dietary B12 → deficiency over years (B12 stores last 3–5 years)

▼ Role of Colon & Diarrhea

Colonic Functions

  • Absorbs remaining ~1.5–2 L of water per day (capacity ~5 L/day before diarrhea)
  • Na+ absorbed via electrogenic ENaC (aldosterone-sensitive); K+ secreted → diarrhea causes hypokalemia
  • Bacterial fermentation of undigested carbohydrates → SCFAs (butyrate, propionate, acetate) → energy source for colonocytes (butyrate preferred fuel)
  • Slow transit → more water absorption → formed stool; fast transit → less absorption → watery stool

🎯 Step 1 — Stool Osmolar Gap

Stool osmolar gap = 290 − 2×([Na+] + [K+]). Normal <50 mOsm/kg (ions account for most osmolarity). Gap >125 = osmotic diarrhea (nonabsorbed solutes contributing to osmolarity). Gap <50 = secretory (ions the dominant solutes — electrolytes being actively secreted).

Practice Questions

15 USMLE-Style MCQs — Digestion & Absorption I & II

Question 1

A medical student is reviewing the structural basis of absorptive efficiency in the small intestine. Which structural feature of the enterocyte is primarily responsible for the approximately 20-fold increase in apical surface area per cell and houses the final enzymatic steps of carbohydrate and protein digestion?

A. Plicae circulares

B. Villi

C. Microvilli (brush border)

D. Lacteals

E. Crypts of Lieberkühn

Correct Answer: C — Microvilli (brush border)

Microvilli are the dense apical membrane projections (~1 µm tall) present on each enterocyte. They increase the apical surface area by approximately 20-fold and bear brush border enzymes including lactase, sucrase-isomaltase, maltase, aminopeptidases, and dipeptidases — the final step in carbohydrate and protein digestion before absorption. Plicae circulares (A) and villi (B) provide larger-scale amplification but do not bear enzymes on their surface. Lacteals (D) absorb chylomicrons. Crypts (E) are secretory structures containing stem cells.

Question 2

A 45-year-old woman undergoes a Whipple procedure (pancreaticoduodenectomy) for pancreatic head adenocarcinoma, resulting in loss of exocrine pancreatic secretion into the duodenum. Which step of carbohydrate digestion would be most severely impaired?

A. Salivary amylase digestion of starch in the mouth

B. Brush border lactase activity on lactose

C. Pancreatic amylase cleavage of starch to oligosaccharides and maltose

D. SGLT1-mediated glucose uptake in the jejunum

E. GLUT2-mediated basolateral glucose export

Correct Answer: C — Pancreatic amylase cleavage of starch

Pancreatic amylase is the dominant luminal amylase, accounting for the vast majority of starch digestion. Salivary amylase (A) begins starch digestion but is inactivated by gastric acid and accounts for only ~5% of total digestion. Brush border enzymes (B) are unaffected by pancreatic insufficiency — they are expressed by enterocytes, not secreted by the pancreas. SGLT1 (D) and GLUT2 (E) are transport proteins on the enterocyte and would not be impaired by loss of pancreatic secretion.

Question 3

Glucose is absorbed across the apical membrane of the enterocyte via SGLT1 co-transport. This is an example of which transport mechanism, and what drives it?

A. Primary active transport driven directly by ATP hydrolysis

B. Secondary active transport driven by the Na+ electrochemical gradient

C. Facilitated diffusion via a carrier protein

D. Passive diffusion down a concentration gradient

E. Pinocytosis requiring clathrin-coated vesicles

Correct Answer: B — Secondary active transport driven by the Na+ gradient

SGLT1 co-transports 2 Na+ ions and 1 glucose molecule into the enterocyte. The Na+ electrochemical gradient (low intracellular Na+ maintained by basolateral Na+/K+-ATPase) provides the energy — this is secondary active transport. The Na+/K+-ATPase is the primary active transporter that ultimately drives the whole system. Facilitated diffusion (C) is the mechanism used by GLUT5 (fructose) and GLUT2 (basolateral); it requires no Na+ or energy. Glucose can move against its own concentration gradient via SGLT1, which distinguishes it from passive mechanisms.

Question 4

A 22-year-old woman develops abdominal cramping, bloating, and watery diarrhea within 30 minutes of consuming a glass of milk. She reports feeling well otherwise and has no blood in her stool. A hydrogen breath test following lactose administration is positive. Which statement best explains the mechanism of her diarrhea?

A. Lactase deficiency → lactose activates Cl⁻ secretion via CFTR → secretory diarrhea

B. Lactase deficiency → undigested lactose in lumen → osmotic water retention → osmotic diarrhea; bacterial fermentation → gas

C. Lactase deficiency → impaired SGLT1 transport → reduced Na+ absorption → secretory diarrhea

D. Autoimmune destruction of enterocytes → villous atrophy → global malabsorption

E. Lactose directly stimulates mast cell degranulation in the gut → inflammatory diarrhea

Correct Answer: B — Osmotic diarrhea + gas from bacterial fermentation

Lactase deficiency → undigested lactose remains in the small intestinal lumen → acts as an osmotic agent → draws water into lumen → watery diarrhea. Lactose reaching the colon is fermented by bacteria → H₂, CO₂, short-chain fatty acids → bloating, cramping, flatulence. This is an osmotic diarrhea (stops with fasting; elevated stool osmolar gap). CFTR-mediated secretion (A, C) describes secretory diarrhea, which continues with fasting. Autoimmune villous atrophy (D) describes celiac disease. Mast cell involvement (E) would suggest an IgE-mediated food allergy.

Question 5

A 6-month-old infant is found to have absent enterokinase (enteropeptidase) activity due to a rare congenital deficiency. Which of the following best describes the downstream consequence of this defect?

A. Impaired carbohydrate digestion due to failure of pancreatic amylase secretion

B. Failure to activate trypsinogen → all pancreatic proteases remain inactive → severe protein malabsorption

C. Defective micelle formation due to impaired bile acid secretion

D. Selective malabsorption of fructose due to GLUT5 downregulation

E. Increased gastric pepsin activity compensates for the defect

Correct Answer: B — Failure to activate trypsinogen → all proteases remain inactive

Enterokinase is the master initiator of the pancreatic protease activation cascade. It cleaves trypsinogen → trypsin; trypsin then activates chymotrypsinogen, proelastase, and procarboxypeptidases. Without enterokinase, no protease is activated → severe protein malabsorption, hypoproteinemia, failure to thrive. Note that pancreatic enzymes are secreted normally; the defect is in activation, not secretion. Carbohydrate and fat digestion are unaffected (those pathways don't require enterokinase). Pepsin (E) is insufficient to compensate for loss of all pancreatic proteases.

Question 6

A patient with chronic pancreatitis develops steatorrhea and greasy, foul-smelling stools. Which mechanism is the primary cause of fat malabsorption in this condition?

A. Impaired bile acid synthesis by the liver

B. Loss of pancreatic lipase and bicarbonate → impaired TG hydrolysis and suboptimal intraluminal pH

C. Defective apoB-48 synthesis by enterocytes

D. Loss of terminal ileum bile acid reabsorption

E. Impaired lingual lipase activity in the mouth

Correct Answer: B — Loss of pancreatic lipase and bicarbonate

Chronic pancreatitis destroys exocrine pancreatic tissue → loss of pancreatic lipase (and co-lipase) → failure to hydrolyze TGs to 2-MAG + free fatty acids. Additionally, loss of pancreatic HCO₃⁻ → low duodenal pH → pancreatic lipase further inactivated (requires pH > 6.5) and bile salts precipitate (pKa ~6) → impaired micelle formation. All downstream events (micelle formation, enterocyte uptake, chylomicron assembly) depend on prior TG hydrolysis. Bile acid synthesis (A) and apoB-48 (C) are intact. Terminal ileum dysfunction (D) would cause bile acid malabsorption and secondarily impair fat absorption, but this is not the mechanism in chronic pancreatitis.

Question 7

A 42-year-old man with Crohn's disease affecting the terminal ileum undergoes surgical resection of 120 cm of terminal ileum. Which combination of deficiencies is most expected?

A. Iron deficiency anemia and vitamin C deficiency

B. Fat malabsorption and vitamin B12 deficiency, with risk of oxalate kidney stones

C. Lactose intolerance and folate deficiency

D. Protein malabsorption and hyperkalemia

E. Calcium malabsorption and hypomagnesemia only

Correct Answer: B — Fat malabsorption and vitamin B12 deficiency + oxalate stones

The terminal ileum has two unique absorptive functions: (1) active reabsorption of conjugated bile acids (ASBT transporter) and (2) absorption of the IF-B12 complex (cubilin receptor). With 120 cm of resection, the bile acid pool is depleted → impaired micelle formation → fat malabsorption and fat-soluble vitamin deficiency. B12 malabsorption → megaloblastic anemia and subacute combined degeneration of the cord (if untreated). Additionally, fat malabsorption → intraluminal fat binds calcium → less Ca²+ available to bind oxalate → oxalate freely absorbed → hyperoxaluria → calcium oxalate kidney stones ("enteric hyperoxaluria"). Iron (A) is absorbed in the duodenum, not the ileum. Lactose intolerance and folate deficiency (C) relate to proximal small intestine.

Question 8

A 9-year-old boy presents with failure to thrive, fatty diarrhea, and neurological symptoms including ataxia and loss of proprioception. Laboratory evaluation reveals very low serum cholesterol, absent serum triglycerides, and acanthocytes on peripheral smear. Intestinal biopsy shows lipid vacuoles in enterocytes. What is the underlying defect?

A. Congenital absence of pancreatic lipase

B. Defective microsomal triglyceride transfer protein (MTP) → failure to assemble apoB-48-containing chylomicrons

C. Loss of SGLT1 function → glucose-galactose malabsorption

D. Autoimmune destruction of parietal cells → vitamin B12 deficiency

E. Primary bile acid synthesis defect → impaired micelle formation

Correct Answer: B — Defective MTP → failure to assemble chylomicrons

This is the classic presentation of abetalipoproteinemia (AR). MTP is required to transfer lipids onto apoB-48 during chylomicron assembly in the enterocyte SER. Without MTP, lipids enter the enterocyte normally but cannot be packaged → fat accumulates (lipid vacuoles on biopsy). No chylomicrons (or VLDL, which uses apoB-100) enter the blood → absent serum TG and very low cholesterol. Acanthocytes result from abnormal red cell membrane lipid composition. Neurological symptoms are due to vitamin E deficiency (fat-soluble; cannot be exported in chylomicrons). Treatment: very low fat diet + high-dose fat-soluble vitamins + MCT oil.

Question 9

A cholera patient presents with profuse "rice-water" diarrhea and severe volume depletion. Oral rehydration solution containing sodium and glucose is administered. Which transport mechanism explains why ORS works despite active intestinal secretion?

A. ENaC-mediated Na+ absorption in the colon is intact and compensates

B. SGLT1-mediated Na+-glucose co-transport remains functional and drives Na+ and water absorption even when CFTR is constitutively active

C. Glucose stimulates CCK → inhibits cAMP → reverses CFTR opening

D. Fructose absorption via GLUT5 draws Na+ through tight junctions paracellularly

E. Oral glucose suppresses V. cholerae toxin production

Correct Answer: B — SGLT1 co-transport remains intact

Cholera toxin activates Gs protein permanently → ↑ adenylyl cyclase → ↑ cAMP → PKA phosphorylates CFTR → constitutive Cl⁻ and water secretion. However, SGLT1 on the apical membrane is unaffected by cAMP. Providing glucose + Na+ in ORS exploits SGLT1 to absorb Na+ (and osmotically drive water absorption) simultaneously. This net absorption can partially offset secretory losses. ORS does not reverse cholera toxin action — it simply uses an intact absorption pathway that runs in parallel. This is one of medicine's great clinical insights: even in the face of massive secretory diarrhea, absorption can continue via SGLT1.

Question 10

A 28-year-old woman with iron deficiency anemia is counseled on dietary iron. Which statement correctly describes the mechanism of non-heme iron uptake from the intestinal lumen into the duodenal enterocyte?

A. Fe3+ (ferric) is directly transported across the brush border by DMT1

B. Fe3+ is reduced to Fe2+ by ferrireductase (DcytB) at the brush border, then transported by DMT1 via H+-coupled co-transport

C. Fe2+ diffuses passively across the lipid bilayer of the brush border

D. Fe3+ binds ferritin on the brush border and is endocytosed intact

E. Iron absorption occurs primarily in the terminal ileum via a vitamin D-dependent mechanism

Correct Answer: B — DcytB reduces Fe3+ → Fe2+, then DMT1 transports Fe2+

Dietary non-heme iron is mostly in the Fe3+ (ferric) form, which is insoluble at intestinal pH. Duodenal cytochrome B (DcytB, a ferrireductase) on the brush border reduces Fe3+ → Fe2+ (ferrous). Fe2+ is then transported into the enterocyte by DMT1 via H+-coupled co-transport (the proton gradient comes from the NHE3 Na+/H+ exchanger creating a slightly acidic microenvironment). DMT1 cannot transport Fe3+ (A). Iron does not passively diffuse (C) — it requires a carrier. Ferritin is an intracellular storage protein, not a membrane receptor (D). Iron absorption is in the duodenum/proximal jejunum, not the terminal ileum (E).

Question 11

A 35-year-old woman with primary biliary cholangitis develops fat-soluble vitamin deficiency and hypocalcemia. A bone densitometry scan shows osteomalacia. Which mechanism best explains the calcium malabsorption in this patient?

A. PTH deficiency directly impairing colonic Ca2+ reabsorption

B. Impaired micelle formation → fat-soluble vitamin D malabsorption → ↓ calcitriol → ↓ calbindin expression → impaired transcellular Ca2+ absorption

C. Loss of DMT1 function in the duodenum affecting both iron and calcium transport

D. High luminal pH dissolving calcium salts → excessive passive Ca2+ absorption

E. Destruction of goblet cells → loss of mucus protecting Ca2+ absorption

Correct Answer: B — Cholestasis → ↓ bile acids → ↓ vitamin D absorption → ↓ calcitriol → ↓ calbindin → impaired Ca2+ absorption

Biliary obstruction/cholangitis reduces bile salt delivery to the duodenum → impaired micelle formation → fat-soluble vitamins (including vitamin D) cannot be absorbed → ↓ circulating calcitriol (1,25-(OH)₂-D3) → ↓ expression of TRPV6 channels and calbindin in enterocytes → impaired transcellular Ca2+ absorption in the duodenum → hypocalcemia → secondary hyperparathyroidism → bone resorption → osteomalacia. PTH deficiency (A) would not occur here — PTH rises reactively. DMT1 (C) transports iron (Fe2+), not calcium. High luminal pH (D) would reduce Ca2+ solubility, not increase absorption. Goblet cells (E) have no role in Ca2+ absorption.

Question 12

A 58-year-old man presents with fatigue, glossitis, and tingling in his feet. Labs show macrocytic anemia (MCV 112 fL) and low serum B12. He has a history of autoimmune thyroiditis. Anti-parietal cell and anti-intrinsic factor antibodies are positive. Which step in B12 absorption is directly impaired?

A. Release of B12 from dietary protein by gastric acid

B. Binding of B12 to transcobalamin I (TC1/R-protein) in saliva

C. Cleavage of TC1 by pancreatic proteases in the duodenum

D. Binding of free B12 to intrinsic factor (IF), preventing IF-B12 complex formation

E. Release of B12 from IF within the terminal ileal enterocyte

Correct Answer: D — Impaired B12-IF binding (pernicious anemia)

This is pernicious anemia. Anti-parietal cell antibodies destroy gastric parietal cells → no intrinsic factor (IF) secreted. Anti-IF antibodies (blocking type) directly prevent B12 from binding IF. Without IF, the B12-IF complex cannot form → no binding to cubilin receptor in the terminal ileum → B12 not absorbed. Note that TC1 (B) still binds B12 in the saliva, but when TC1 is cleaved by pancreatic proteases (C) in the duodenum, free B12 cannot find IF → passes through unabsorbed. The result is megaloblastic anemia + subacute combined degeneration (posterior column + corticospinal tract). Treatment: IM hydroxocobalamin or high-dose oral B12 (some passive absorption).

Question 13

A 65-year-old man with a 3-cm VIPoma (vasoactive intestinal peptide-secreting tumor) presents with profuse watery diarrhea (6–10 L/day), hypokalemia, and achlorhydria (WDHA syndrome). A stool sample collected during a fast shows high-volume watery stool with a stool osmolar gap of 20 mOsm/kg. Which type of diarrhea is this?

A. Osmotic diarrhea — unabsorbed VIP draws water into lumen

B. Exudative diarrhea — VIPoma causes mucosal inflammation

C. Secretory diarrhea — VIP activates adenylyl cyclase → ↑ cAMP → Cl⁻ and water secretion into lumen

D. Malabsorptive diarrhea — VIPoma destroys pancreatic lipase

E. Motility diarrhea — VIP accelerates GI transit via smooth muscle stimulation only

Correct Answer: C — Secretory diarrhea

VIPoma (Verner-Morrison syndrome / WDHA: Watery Diarrhea, Hypokalemia, Achlorhydria) causes profuse secretory diarrhea. VIP binds its receptor → activates adenylyl cyclase → ↑ cAMP → PKA → phosphorylation of CFTR and other channels → massive Cl⁻ and water secretion into the gut lumen. Key features of secretory diarrhea: (1) continues with fasting (unlike osmotic), (2) low stool osmolar gap (<50 mOsm/kg) because electrolytes dominate the stool osmolarity, (3) large-volume watery stools, (4) hypokalemia (K+ secreted with the large volumes). Osmotic diarrhea (A) stops with fasting and has a high osmolar gap. VIPoma does not cause mucosal damage (B) or fat malabsorption (D).

Question 14

Researchers study sugar transport in a patient with a novel mutation selectively inactivating SGLT1. Which finding would be expected in this patient?

A. Impaired fructose absorption; glucose and galactose absorbed normally

B. Impaired glucose and galactose absorption; fructose absorption unaffected

C. Impaired glucose absorption only; galactose uses an alternative SGLT2 transporter in the jejunum

D. Impaired absorption of all three monosaccharides because GLUT2 depends on SGLT1 function

E. Glucose absorbed normally via GLUT5 when SGLT1 is absent

Correct Answer: B — Glucose and galactose impaired; fructose normal

SGLT1 transports both glucose and galactose across the apical brush border (2 Na+: 1 sugar co-transport). Inactivation of SGLT1 prevents both glucose and galactose from entering the enterocyte via this route → osmotic diarrhea with glucose or galactose ingestion. Fructose uses a completely separate transporter, GLUT5 (facilitated diffusion), which does not depend on Na+ or SGLT1 → fructose absorption is unaffected. SGLT2 (C) is the renal glucose transporter (proximal tubule), not a small intestinal transporter. GLUT2 (D) is the basolateral exit transporter — it would not function without glucose/galactose entering first, but fructose would still exit normally via GLUT2. GLUT5 (E) does not transport glucose.

Question 15

A 7-year-old boy presents with steatorrhea, failure to thrive, and fat-soluble vitamin deficiencies. Peripheral blood smear shows acanthocytes. Serum lipid panel reveals absent chylomicrons and VLDL, very low LDL, and undetectable apolipoprotein B. His pancreatic enzyme levels are normal. Which dietary modification and supplement would be most appropriate?

A. Gluten-free diet and oral folic acid

B. Low-fat diet with MCT oil supplementation, plus high-dose fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K)

C. High-fat diet to overcome the absorption defect by mass action

D. Lactose-free diet and iron supplementation

E. Low-protein diet to prevent enterokinase activation cascade overload

Correct Answer: B — Low-fat diet + MCT oil + high-dose fat-soluble vitamins

This is abetalipoproteinemia (absent apoB-48 and apoB-100 → no chylomicrons or VLDL). Normal pancreatic enzymes confirm digestion is intact; the defect is in chylomicron assembly (MTP deficiency). Management: (1) Low-fat diet — restricts long-chain TG, which cannot be exported in chylomicrons; (2) MCT oil — medium-chain fatty acids bypass the chylomicron pathway, absorbed directly into portal blood; (3) High-dose fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) — water-miscible preparations are used because regular fat-soluble preparations cannot be absorbed; vitamin E supplementation is critical to prevent the progressive spinocerebellar degeneration. Gluten-free diet (A) treats celiac disease. High fat (C) would worsen symptoms.
