▼ Embryonic Tissue Lineage: From Blastocyst to Endoderm

⭐ The definitive (embryonic) endoderm arises from epiblast cells that ingress through the primitive streak — NOT from the hypoblast.

🔬 Inner Cell Mass (ICM) → Two Lineages

StructureGives Rise ToFinal Fate
EpiblastPrimitive streak → embryonic ectoderm, mesoderm, definitive endodermAll 3 embryonic germ layers
Epiblast (also)Amniotic ectodermAmnion lining
HypoblastExtraembryonic endoderm → yolk sacYolk sac only — NOT the gut

🏥 Trophoblast (Extraembryonic)

Trophoblast → cytotrophoblast → syncytiotrophoblast → placenta. Not relevant to gut development but commonly tested in implantation/hCG questions.

⚠️ TRAP: Hypoblast ≠ Gut Endoderm ▼
  • The hypoblast (visceral endoderm) forms the extraembryonic yolk sac — it does NOT form the GI tract.
  • True gut endoderm (definitive endoderm) = epiblast cells that ingressed through the primitive streak and displaced the hypoblast.
  • On boards: "definitive endoderm" always = embryonic origin from epiblast via primitive streak.

▼ Gastrulation: Creating the Definitive Endoderm (~Days 14–16)

⭐ Gastrulation converts the bilaminar disc into a trilaminar disc; the depth of ingression through the primitive streak determines germ layer fate.

📍 The Primitive Streak

  • Appears at the caudal end of the bilaminar germ disc at ~day 14–15; extends cranially
  • Primitive node at cranial tip → notochord (body axis organizer)
  • Key landmarks: buccopharyngeal membrane (cranial = future mouth); cloacal membrane (caudal = future anus)
🔑 Ingression Depth = Germ Layer Fate
  • Deepest ingression → displaces hypoblast → definitive endoderm
  • Intermediate ingression → spreads laterally → intraembryonic mesoderm
  • No ingression → stays on surface → ectoderm
  • Primitive node specifically → notochord

🔄 Endoderm Intercalation (~Days 14–16)

  • Days 14–15: nascent endoderm intercalates into the hypoblast from the primitive streak
  • Day 16: endoderm progressively displaces the hypoblast laterally; mesoderm extends outward simultaneously
  • Result: trilaminar disc — ectoderm (dorsal), mesoderm (middle), endoderm (ventral)
  • Displaced hypoblast remains as extraembryonic yolk sac endoderm

Once the trilaminar disc is established, embryonic folding incorporates the ventral endodermal sheet into the true gut tube (vitelline duct connects remaining yolk sac portion).

🧠 Mnemonic: "Epiblast Enters, Endoderm Emerges"
▼

Epiblast cells → Enter the primitive streak → Emerge deepest as definitive Endoderm; intermediate → Mesoderm. Hypoblast = "Extraembryonic" — gets kicked out. Not the gut source.

▼ Molecular Mechanisms of Endoderm Development (Primer Level)

⭐ This lecture is a primer — molecular detail is conceptual. Know the key factors and what they do.

🔬 Key Signals & Transcription Factors

Signal / FactorRole
Nodal (TGF-β family)High Nodal → endoderm fate; lower Nodal → mesoderm. Drives primitive streak ingression.
Sox17Master TF for definitive endoderm specification; required for gut tube formation
Foxa2 (HNF3β)Pioneer TF for definitive endoderm; required for anterior foregut and liver induction

📋 Step 1 Required Level
  • Know: definitive endoderm comes from epiblast via primitive streak ingression
  • Know: it displaces the hypoblast; Sox17 and Foxa2 are key endoderm TFs
  • Deep signaling (Nodal concentration gradients) is rarely tested on Step 1

🧠 Testing Your Understanding!

Testing Your Understanding — LO 1, Question 1

A researcher creates a mouse knockout for Sox17 specifically in cells migrating through the primitive streak. Predict what happens to the gut endoderm and explain the mechanism.

Consider: what Sox17 does, where definitive endoderm comes from, what the alternative fate of these cells might be

✓ Answer: No definitive endoderm forms — gut tube fails to develop

Sox17 is the master transcription factor for definitive endoderm specification. Without Sox17, epiblast cells that ingress through the primitive streak cannot adopt an endoderm fate. They would either fail to differentiate properly or default toward mesoderm. The result: no gut tube endoderm → no GI epithelium, no liver bud, no lung bud. This is distinct from the hypoblast (yolk sac), which is Sox17-independent.

Testing Your Understanding — LO 1, Question 2

A 15-day embryo has a defect in which no cells can ingress through the primitive streak. Which germ layers will be absent, and what structure remains?

Consider: what requires primitive streak ingression, what forms without it

✓ Answer: Both mesoderm and definitive endoderm are absent; only ectoderm (and hypoblast) remain

The primitive streak is the only route for epiblast cells to become mesoderm or definitive endoderm during gastrulation. If no ingression occurs, the embryo remains as a bilaminar disc: epiblast on top (would become ectoderm only) and hypoblast below (extraembryonic yolk sac). No gut tube, no body wall mesoderm, no notochord — the embryo is incompatible with normal development.

⭐ The gut tube is divided into three regions defined by their arterial supply — the single most testable fact from this lecture.

⚠️ TRAP: Duodenum Spans TWO Regions ▼

  • Distal half (D3 + D4) = midgut → SMA.
  • Similarly, transverse colon: right 2/3 = SMA; left 1/3 = IMA.

🧠 Mnemonic: Foregut Accessories — "LLGP"
▼

All four arise as outgrowths/diverticula from the foregut endoderm. If the epithelium is gut-endoderm-derived and it's NOT in the tube itself, it's almost certainly foregut.

⚠️ TRAP: The Anal Canal Has Two Zones ▼
  • Upper 2/3 of anal canal (above dentate line) = hindgut endoderm → columnar epithelium, IMA supply, visceral innervation (pain-insensitive)
  • Lower 1/3 (below dentate line) = ectoderm (proctodeum) → stratified squamous, internal pudendal supply, somatic innervation (pain-sensitive)
  • Clinical pearl: internal hemorrhoids above dentate line = painless; external hemorrhoids below = painful

▼ Arterial Supply Map & Clinical Relevance

⭐ The embryonic arterial territories are preserved in the adult — the three unpaired ventral aortic branches define their gut regions.

🩸 Adult Branches from Each Artery

  • Celiac trunk (T12) → foregut: left gastric, splenic, common hepatic arteries
  • SMA (L1) → midgut: inferior pancreaticoduodenal, jejunal/ileal, ileocolic, right colic, middle colic
  • IMA (L3) → hindgut: left colic, sigmoid arteries, superior rectal
💡 Clinical Application: Watershed Zones
  • Splenic flexure (SMA↔IMA boundary) and rectosigmoid junction are the two classic ischemia-prone watershed zones
  • Ischemic colitis most commonly affects the splenic flexure during low-flow states — a direct consequence of the embryologic SMA/IMA boundary

🧠 Testing Your Understanding!

Testing Your Understanding — LO 2, Question 1

A patient undergoes angiography showing complete occlusion of the inferior mesenteric artery. List all GI structures at risk of ischemia, and explain why the splenic flexure is particularly vulnerable.

Consider: IMA territory, SMA territory, watershed zones, marginal artery of Drummond

✓ Answer: Left 1/3 transverse colon, descending colon, sigmoid colon, rectum (upper 2/3)

The IMA supplies the hindgut: left 1/3 of transverse colon, descending colon, sigmoid colon, and upper rectum. The splenic flexure is at special risk because it is the watershed between SMA (middle colic) and IMA (left colic) territory. The connecting marginal artery of Drummond is often tenuous at this location — so during IMA occlusion or low-flow states, the splenic flexure may not receive adequate collateral supply from the SMA side, making it the most common site of ischemic colitis.

▼ The Tube-Within-a-Tube Body Plan

⭐ The embryo and amniotic sac grow much more rapidly than the yolk sac — this uneven growth drives 3-dimensional embryonic folding that creates the gut tube.

📐 Mechanism of Folding

  • Cranio-caudal (longitudinal) folding: head fold and tail fold bring the buccopharyngeal and cloacal membranes to cranial and caudal ends
  • Lateral folding: two body wall folds roll ventrally → pinch the flat endodermal sheet into a tube; remaining yolk sac connection = vitelline duct
  • Result: inner tube (gut = endoderm-lined) inside outer tube (body wall = ectoderm-lined), separated by the intraembryonic coelom (→ peritoneal cavity)
🔑 Cross-Section at ~20 days (inside → out):
  • Gut tube (endoderm) → splanchnic mesoderm (→ gut wall smooth muscle + serosa) → intraembryonic coelom → somatic mesoderm (→ body wall) → ectoderm (→ skin)
  • Neural tube sits dorsal to the gut tube

📅 Timeline of Gut Tube Formation

TimepointKey Event
~20 daysEndodermal sheet begins forming; foregut and hindgut pockets appear
~24 daysForegut and hindgut more defined; midgut still open to yolk sac via vitelline duct
~26 daysLateral folding nearly complete; vitelline duct narrowing
~30 daysAll major organ buds visible: respiratory diverticulum, stomach, liver cords, pancreatic rudiments, gallbladder

Week 11Midgut returns to abdomen; further rotation completes 270° total

▼ Mesoderm Layers Surrounding the Gut Tube

⭐ Lateral plate mesoderm splits into two layers around the intraembryonic coelom — this split defines the gut wall vs. the body wall.

🔬 Three Mesodermal Layers

LayerLocationWhat It Becomes
Splanchnic mesodermLines the gut tube (inner coelom wall)Smooth muscle, connective tissue, serosa of gut wall; also gives rise to mesenteries
Somatic mesodermLines body wall (outer coelom wall)Parietal peritoneum, body wall musculature/CT, limb connective tissue
Intermediate mesodermBetween splanchnic and somaticKidney (nephrogenic cord), gonadal ridge, adrenal cortex

▼ Mesenteries — Formation, Types & Fates

⭐ Mesenteries are double-layered peritoneal folds derived from splanchnic mesoderm that suspend the gut tube and carry neurovascular structures. They also give rise to key adult structures.

📍 Dorsal Mesentery

  • Present along the entire gut tube (posterior attachment)
  • Regional names:
    • Dorsal mesogastrium → suspends stomach → becomes greater omentum as stomach rotates
    • Mesoduodenum → largely disappears as duodenum becomes secondarily retroperitoneal
    • Mesentery proper → suspends small bowel (persists in adult)
    • Transverse and sigmoid mesocolon → suspend respective colon segments

📍 Ventral Mesentery

  • Present only in the foregut region (cranial abdomen); absent caudal to the umbilicus
  • Fates:
    • Falciform ligament: liver → anterior abdominal wall; contains ligamentum teres (obliterated left umbilical vein)
    • Lesser omentum: liver → stomach (gastrohepatic) and liver → duodenum (hepatoduodenal = portal triad)
    • Hepatic diverticulum scaffold: liver bud grows from ventral mesentery into septum transversum

🔄 Secondarily Retroperitoneal Organs

Some organs begin intraperitoneal then fuse their dorsal mesentery to the posterior wall:

  • Duodenum (D2–D4), pancreas head/body, ascending colon, descending colon
⚡ Step 1 High-Yield — Retroperitoneum
  • Intraperitoneal: stomach, spleen, transverse colon, sigmoid colon, liver, gallbladder, D1, jejunum, ileum
  • Secondarily retroperitoneal: duodenum D2–D4, pancreas (head/body), ascending colon, descending colon
  • Primarily retroperitoneal (never had mesentery): kidneys, ureters, adrenals, aorta, IVC, esophagus (thoracic), rectum (lower 2/3)
  • Falciform ligament carries ligamentum teres (obliterated umbilical vein) — visible in laparoscopy
⚠️ TRAP: Ventral Mesentery is Foregut-Only ▼
  • The ventral mesentery is present only cranially (above umbilicus).
  • Small intestine and colon have no ventral mesentery — suspended only by dorsal mesentery.
  • This is why physiologic umbilical herniation is possible — no ventral attachment to prevent it.

🧠 Testing Your Understanding!

Testing Your Understanding — LO 3, Question 1

During embryologic development, which specific structure does the falciform ligament derive from, and what vessel runs within it in the adult? What is the clinical significance of that vessel?

Consider: ventral mesentery fate, umbilical circulation during fetal life, portal hypertension

✓ Answer: Falciform ligament = ventral mesentery remnant; contains ligamentum teres (obliterated left umbilical vein)

The falciform ligament is the remnant of the ventral mesentery in the upper abdomen, connecting the liver to the anterior abdominal wall. The ligamentum teres runs within it — the obliterated left umbilical vein, which carried oxygenated blood from the placenta to the fetal liver. In portal hypertension, this vein can recanalize, creating prominent paraumbilical collateral veins visible on the anterior abdominal wall (caput medusae).

▼ Stomach Formation & Rotation

⭐ The stomach rotates 90° clockwise (ventral view) at ~day 35, which repositions both its curvatures and drags the vagal trunks to new positions.

📅 Stomach Primordium (~Day 27)

  • Spindle-shaped foregut dilation at ~day 27; initially straight craniocaudal tube
  • Suspended by dorsal mesogastrium (posterior) and ventral mesentery (anterior)
  • Posterior wall grows faster than anterior wall → posterior wall → greater curvature; anterior wall → lesser curvature

🔄 90° Clockwise Rotation (~Day 35)

  • Stomach rotates 90° clockwise (viewed from ventral/front)
  • Left surface → rotates to face anteriorly → left vagus → anterior vagal trunk
  • Right surface → rotates to face posteriorly → right vagus → posterior vagal trunk
  • Greater curvature faces left and inferiorly; lesser curvature faces right and superiorly
  • Dorsal mesogastrium is pulled to the left → forms the greater omentum (dangles from greater curvature)
  • Rotation creates the lesser sac (omental bursa) posterior to stomach
⚠️ TRAP: Vagal Nerve Positions After Stomach Rotation ▼
  • After rotation: left vagus → anterior vagal trunk; right vagus → posterior vagal trunk
  • Because the left side of the stomach rotates anteriorly, it drags the left vagus with it
  • Surgical vagotomy must cut both trunks; Step 1 may ask which trunk is affected after specific procedures

▼ Liver, Gallbladder & Omentum Development

⭐ The liver bud (hepatic diverticulum) grows from the ventral mesentery into the septum transversum — the endoderm forms hepatocytes and bile duct epithelium; the mesoderm provides the connective tissue scaffold.

🌱 Hepatic Diverticulum (Week 4–5)

  • Ventral outgrowth (liver bud) from caudal foregut/duodenal endoderm
  • Grows from the ventral mesentery into the septum transversum (mesoderm structure separating pericardial from peritoneal cavity)
  • Endoderm of hepatic diverticulum → hepatocytes and bile duct epithelium
  • Septum transversum mesoderm → Kupffer cells, stellate cells, connective tissue, blood sinusoids
  • Gallbladder, cystic duct, and common bile duct arise from a separate cystic diverticulum off the hepatic diverticulum

🫙 Greater Omentum & Lesser Sac (Days 35–42)

  • Dorsal mesogastrium is dragged left and elongates as stomach rotates → forms the greater omentum (apron hanging from greater curvature)
  • Space created behind the stomach = lesser sac (omental bursa), accessible via the epiploic foramen of Winslow
  • Lienorenal (splenorenal) ligament: spleen → left kidney — dorsal mesogastrium remnant
  • Gastrosplenic ligament: spleen → stomach — dorsal mesogastrium remnant
  • Lesser omentum (ventral mesentery): gastrohepatic + hepatoduodenal ligaments

▼ Intestinal Rotation & Return to Abdomen

⭐ The midgut undergoes 270° total counterclockwise rotation around the SMA axis, with temporary physiologic herniation into the umbilical cord at week 6.

📍 Primary Intestinal Loop & Physiologic Herniation (~Week 6–7)

  • Midgut forms a primary intestinal loop with apex attached to vitelline duct
  • Cranial limb → jejunum/proximal ileum; caudal limb → distal ileum, cecum, appendix, ascending colon
  • Liver growth makes abdomen too small → loop herniates into umbilical cord (physiologic umbilical herniation)
  • While herniated: 90° counterclockwise rotation (viewed from front, around SMA)

🔄 Return to Abdomen & Further Rotation (~Week 11)

  • Gut returns to abdomen at ~week 10–11, rotating an additional 180° counterclockwise → 270° total CCW around the SMA
  • Cecum initially below liver (RUQ) → descends to RLQ by week 12, pulling proximal hindgut up → forms ascending colon
  • Ascending and descending colons fuse to posterior wall → secondarily retroperitoneal
  • GI tract complete by week 14
🧠 Mnemonic: "270° CCW Around the SMA"
▼

Week 6: 90° CCW (while herniated in cord) → Week 11: 180° more CCW (on return) → 270° total

The SMA is the fixed axis. Incomplete rotation = malrotation → cecum in RUQ, Ladd's bands, risk of midgut volvulus.

  • Omphalocele: herniation THROUGH umbilicus → membrane present → liver may be inside → associated chromosomal anomalies
  • Gastroschisis: defect to the RIGHT of umbilicus → NO membrane → liver NOT inside → bowel inflamed → usually isolated (no chromosomal association)
  • Key differentiator on boards: membrane = omphalocele; no membrane = gastroschisis
⚡ Step 1 Summary — GI Rotation High-Yield
  • Physiologic umbilical herniation = normal at 6 weeks; resolves by week 11
  • Total rotation = 270° CCW around SMA (90° while herniated + 180° on return)
  • Malrotation → cecum in RUQ, Ladd's bands, risk of midgut volvulus (surgical emergency)
  • Double bubble = duodenal atresia → Trisomy 21
  • Stomach: 90° clockwise; left vagus → anterior trunk; right vagus → posterior trunk
  • Liver bud: ventral mesentery + septum transversum; endoderm → hepatocytes + bile ducts; mesoderm → Kupffer/stellate cells

🧠 Testing Your Understanding!

Testing Your Understanding — LO 4, Question 1

A newborn presents with bilious vomiting and an abdominal X-ray shows the stomach and colon on the left side, small bowel on the right. Contrast enema shows the cecum in the RUQ. What is the diagnosis, what embryologic process failed, and what is the life-threatening complication?

Consider: normal midgut rotation, the SMA pedicle, Ladd's bands, cecal position

✓ Answer: Malrotation; incomplete 270° CCW rotation; life-threatening complication = midgut volvulus

Malrotation occurs when the midgut fails to complete its full 270° CCW rotation around the SMA. The cecum remains in the RUQ (or midline), and fibrous Ladd's bands form across the duodenum causing partial obstruction. The critical danger is that the entire midgut hangs on a narrow SMA pedicle and can twist (volvulus) → occludes the SMA → infarction of the entire midgut (duodenum to transverse colon). This is a surgical emergency requiring Ladd's procedure (detorsion, divide Ladd's bands, appendectomy, cecum placed in LLQ).

Testing Your Understanding — LO 4, Question 2

Explain how the greater omentum forms embryologically. What is its mesenteric origin, and what triggers its formation?

Consider: dorsal mesogastrium, stomach rotation, lesser sac

✓ Answer: Greater omentum = dorsal mesogastrium, dragged left and elongated by stomach rotation

The greater omentum is a specialization of the dorsal mesogastrium — the dorsal mesentery suspending the stomach. When the stomach rotates 90° clockwise at ~day 35, the dorsal mesogastrium is pulled to the left and elongates, forming an apron-like fold that hangs from the greater curvature. This creates a double-layered peritoneal fold. The space created behind the stomach, bounded anteriorly by the stomach and lesser omentum and posteriorly by the posterior abdominal wall, becomes the lesser sac (omental bursa), accessible through the epiploic foramen of Winslow.

LO 1 — Definitive Endoderm Formation (Q1–Q4)

Question 1

A researcher labels epiblast cells just before gastrulation. These labeled cells are tracked and found lining the stomach at day 30. Which structure must these cells have passed through to contribute to gut epithelium?

A. Notochord

B. Primitive streak

C. Buccopharyngeal membrane

D. Hypoblast

E. Neural crest

✓ Correct: B — Primitive streak

Definitive endoderm arises from epiblast cells that ingress through the primitive streak during gastrulation, migrating deepest to intercalate into and displace the hypoblast. The hypoblast itself gives rise to extraembryonic yolk sac (not gut epithelium). The notochord forms from the primitive node (cranial tip); neural crest is ectoderm-derived.

Question 2

During gastrulation, the hypoblast is displaced by ingressing epiblast cells. Which structure does the hypoblast give rise to?

A. Gastric epithelium

B. Hepatocytes

C. Yolk sac lining (extraembryonic endoderm)

D. Parietal peritoneum

E. Intestinal villi

✓ Correct: C — Yolk sac lining (extraembryonic endoderm)

The hypoblast (visceral endoderm) is extraembryonic — it lines the yolk sac and does NOT contribute to GI epithelium. All gut-lining cells (gastric, intestinal, hepatocytes, bile duct) derive from definitive endoderm originating from epiblast through the primitive streak.

Question 3

A molecular biology study finds that a transcription factor knockout in primitive-streak-ingressing cells results in complete failure of gut tube formation. Which factor is most likely responsible?

A. Sox2

B. Pax6

C. Sox17

D. MyoD

E. Nkx2.5

✓ Correct: C — Sox17

Sox17 is the master transcription factor for definitive endoderm specification, downstream of high Nodal signaling. Loss of Sox17 prevents gut tube formation. Sox2 = pluripotency/ectoderm; Pax6 = eye/pancreas; MyoD = skeletal muscle; Nkx2.5 = cardiac TF. Foxa2 is also key for liver induction and anterior foregut patterning.

Question 4

A 15-day embryo has a defect preventing all cell ingression through the primitive streak. Which statement best describes the resulting embryo?

A. The embryo has ectoderm and endoderm but lacks mesoderm

B. The embryo has ectoderm only; both mesoderm and definitive endoderm are absent

C. The embryo has mesoderm only; ectoderm and endoderm are absent

D. The embryo develops normally; the primitive streak is not required for endoderm formation

E. Only neural crest cells are affected; the gut tube forms normally from hypoblast

✓ Correct: B — Ectoderm only; mesoderm and definitive endoderm absent

The primitive streak is the only route for epiblast cells to become mesoderm or definitive endoderm. If no ingression occurs, the embryo remains bilaminar: epiblast (→ ectoderm only) and hypoblast (→ extraembryonic yolk sac). No gut tube, no body wall mesoderm, no notochord — incompatible with normal development.

LO 2 — Regions, Derivatives & Arterial Supply (Q5–Q8)

Question 5

A 3-day-old neonate presents with bilious vomiting. Abdominal X-ray shows two air-fluid levels with no gas distally — a "double bubble" sign. A karyotype reveals Trisomy 21. Which embryologic process has failed?

A. Neural crest cell migration to the hindgut

B. Recanalization of the duodenal lumen after the solid cord stage

C. Rotation of the primary intestinal loop around the SMA

D. Fusion of dorsal and ventral pancreatic buds

E. Descent of the cecum to the RLQ

✓ Correct: B — Failure of duodenal recanalization

This is duodenal atresia. The duodenum passes through a solid cord stage (epithelial proliferation occludes the lumen) and normally recanalizes by ~week 8. Failure → atresia → double bubble (stomach + dilated duodenum). Associated with Trisomy 21 in ~30% of cases. Most common site of intestinal atresia.

Question 6

A patient develops acute mesenteric ischemia from SMA occlusion. Which portion of the GI tract is MOST directly affected?

A. Pharynx, esophagus, stomach, and proximal duodenum

B. Left 1/3 transverse colon, descending colon, sigmoid colon, and rectum

C. Distal duodenum, jejunum, ileum, cecum, appendix, ascending colon, right 2/3 transverse colon

D. Liver, gallbladder, pancreas, and spleen

E. Descending colon and sigmoid colon only

✓ Correct: C — Distal duodenum through right 2/3 of transverse colon

The SMA supplies the midgut: distal duodenum (inferior to ampulla of Vater) through the right 2/3 of the transverse colon. This is the largest GI vascular territory. SMA occlusion causes massive midgut ischemia — one of the most lethal abdominal emergencies. Foregut = celiac; hindgut = IMA.

Question 7

The hepatic diverticulum (liver bud) gives rise to which of the following structures?

A. Pancreatic ductal cells only

B. Liver parenchyma, bile duct epithelium, gallbladder, cystic duct, and common bile duct

C. Liver parenchyma only; bile ducts arise separately

D. Liver and pancreas, as they share a common diverticulum

E. Gallbladder only; the liver derives from the septum transversum

✓ Correct: B — Liver parenchyma, bile duct epithelium, gallbladder, cystic duct, and CBD

The hepatic diverticulum (foregut endoderm outgrowth, week 4) gives rise to all of these. Connective tissue scaffold (Kupffer cells, sinusoids, stellate cells) comes from septum transversum mesoderm. The pancreas arises from separate dorsal and ventral pancreatic buds — independent diverticula, also foregut endoderm.

Question 8

A surgeon performs a left hemicolectomy for colon cancer. Based on embryologic blood supply, which artery's territory has been removed?

A. Celiac trunk

B. Superior mesenteric artery

C. Inferior mesenteric artery

D. Internal iliac artery

E. Superior rectal artery only

✓ Correct: C — Inferior mesenteric artery

A left hemicolectomy removes the left colon (left 1/3 transverse, descending, sigmoid) — all hindgut derivatives supplied by the IMA (left colic and sigmoid arteries). The IMA is ligated during this procedure; collateral flow is maintained via SMA through the marginal artery of Drummond. The splenic flexure watershed makes this the most vulnerable anastomosis point.

LO 3 — Gut Tube Folding & Mesenteries (Q9–Q11)

Question 9

Which of the following best explains the mechanical basis for the tube-within-a-tube body plan in human embryonic development?

A. The neural tube grows faster than the gut tube, pushing the gut ventrally

B. The embryo and amniotic sac grow more rapidly than the yolk sac, causing 3D folding that incorporates the endoderm into an inner tube

C. The endoderm proliferates faster than the mesoderm, causing it to buckle inward

D. The cloacal membrane breaks down prematurely, allowing caudal folding

E. Apoptosis of lateral plate mesoderm creates a space for the gut tube

✓ Correct: B — Embryo and amniotic sac outgrow the yolk sac

The tube-within-a-tube body plan results from differential growth rates. The embryo and amniotic sac grow much faster than the yolk sac, driving cranio-caudal and lateral folding. The lateral folds pinch the flat endodermal sheet into a tube, incorporating its dorsal portion as the primitive gut tube (connected to yolk sac by the vitelline duct). Inner tube = gut endoderm; outer tube = ectoderm-lined body wall; intraembryonic coelom lies between them.

Question 10

A surgeon notes the falciform ligament during laparoscopy. Which embryologic structure is the falciform ligament a remnant of, and what clinically important vessel does it contain?

A. Dorsal mesogastrium; left gastroepiploic artery

B. Dorsal mesentery of the small bowel; vitelline artery

C. Ventral mesentery; ligamentum teres (obliterated left umbilical vein)

D. Mesoduodenum; superior mesenteric vein

E. Lienorenal ligament; splenic vein

✓ Correct: C — Ventral mesentery; ligamentum teres (obliterated left umbilical vein)

The falciform ligament is a remnant of the ventral mesentery (foregut region only). It connects the liver to the anterior abdominal wall and contains the ligamentum teres hepatis — the obliterated left umbilical vein that carried oxygenated placental blood to the fetal liver. In portal hypertension, this vein can recanalize → caput medusae (visible collateral veins radiating from the umbilicus).

Question 11

A 4-year-old presents with constipation and abdominal distension since birth. Rectal biopsy shows absent ganglion cells in the myenteric and submucosal plexuses of the distal colon. Which embryologic process failed?

A. Failure of endodermal folding to form the hindgut

B. Failure of neural crest cells to migrate to and populate the distal colon

C. Incomplete rotation of the midgut around the SMA

D. Persistence of the vitelline duct in the hindgut

E. Failure of the cloacal membrane to rupture

✓ Correct: B — Failure of neural crest cell migration to the distal colon

This is Hirschsprung disease. Neural crest cells normally migrate craniocaudally to form myenteric and submucosal plexuses. Failure of migration to the distal colon → aganglionic segment → tonic contraction → functional obstruction → proximal megacolon. The gut endoderm is normal; the problem is the neural crest-derived enteric neurons.

LO 4 — Organ Development & Rotation (Q12–Q15)

Question 12

A newborn has bowel loops and liver protruding through the umbilicus, covered by a translucent membrane. Karyotype shows Trisomy 18. What is the diagnosis and underlying embryologic failure?

A. Gastroschisis — defect in lateral body wall to the right of umbilicus

B. Omphalocele — failure of physiologic midgut herniation to return to the abdomen

C. Meckel's diverticulum — persistent vitelline duct

D. Malrotation — incomplete 270° rotation of the midgut

E. Duodenal atresia — failure of lumen recanalization

✓ Correct: B — Omphalocele

Omphalocele: bowel (and sometimes liver) herniates through the umbilicus and is covered by a membrane (amnion + peritoneum). Associated with chromosomal anomalies (Trisomy 13, 18; Beckwith-Wiedemann). Gastroschisis: defect to the RIGHT of umbilicus, NO membrane, no liver — bowel free-floating and inflamed. Key differentiator: membrane = omphalocele; no membrane = gastroschisis.

Question 13

After stomach rotation, the left vagus nerve lies on the anterior surface (anterior vagal trunk). Which statement best explains why?

A. The right vagus grows faster and reaches the stomach first, pushing the left posteriorly

B. The stomach rotates 90° clockwise — the left surface rotates to face anteriorly, dragging the left vagus with it

C. The stomach rotates 90° counterclockwise — the right vagus follows the right surface anteriorly

D. Vagal fibers cross at the esophageal plexus, reversing their sides before reaching the stomach

E. The dorsal mesogastrium rotation independently repositions the right vagus posteriorly

✓ Correct: B — Stomach rotates 90° clockwise; left surface → anterior, dragging left vagus

The stomach rotates 90° clockwise (ventral view). The original left surface (carrying the left vagus) rotates to become the anterior surface → left vagus = anterior vagal trunk. The right surface → posterior → right vagus = posterior vagal trunk. Surgeons performing vagotomy must cut both trunks to denervate the stomach completely.

Question 14

A 3-week-old presents with sudden bilious vomiting and imaging shows the cecum in the RUQ. What is the diagnosis and the most feared operative complication if untreated?

A. Duodenal atresia — risk of double bubble progression

B. Malrotation with midgut volvulus — risk of entire midgut infarction

C. Hirschsprung disease — risk of toxic megacolon

D. Meckel's diverticulum — risk of intussusception

E. Omphalocele — risk of membrane rupture

✓ Correct: B — Malrotation with midgut volvulus; risk of entire midgut infarction

Malrotation: cecum in RUQ, Ladd's bands across duodenum. The entire midgut hangs on a narrow SMA pedicle → can twist (volvulus) → SMA occlusion → infarction of entire midgut (duodenum to transverse colon). Bilious vomiting in a neonate = malrotation/volvulus until proven otherwise. Treatment: Ladd's procedure (detorsion, divide Ladd's bands, appendectomy, cecum placed LLQ).

Question 15

A 2-year-old presents with painless bright red rectal bleeding. A ⁹⁹ᵐTc-pertechnetate scan shows a hot spot in the right lower quadrant. Which embryologic anomaly is responsible?

A. Failure of the cloacal membrane to rupture

B. Persistence of the vitelline duct as a true ileal diverticulum containing ectopic gastric mucosa

C. Failure of neural crest migration to the ileum

D. Incomplete midgut rotation leaving the cecum in the RLQ with ectopic mucosa

E. Cystic dilation of the common bile duct descending to the RLQ

✓ Correct: B — Persistence of the vitelline duct (Meckel's diverticulum)

Meckel's diverticulum: the vitelline duct normally obliterates by week 6–7. Persistence = true diverticulum on the antimesenteric border of the ileum, 2 feet from the ICV. Contains ectopic gastric mucosa → acid secretion → ulceration of adjacent ileal mucosa → painless rectal bleeding. ⁹⁹ᵐTc-pertechnetate is taken up by ectopic gastric mucosa → hot spot in RLQ. Rule of 2's: 2%, 2 inches, 2 feet from ICV, 2 types of ectopic tissue, first 2 years of life.
