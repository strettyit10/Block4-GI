Overview
Lecture 7 — Overview
GI Block — GI Primer: Organs from the Endoderm / Gut Tube & GI Tract Development
🎯 Big Picture The definitive endoderm — one of the three primary germ layers formed during gastrulation — gives rise not only to the entire GI tract but also to the lungs, liver, pancreas, gallbladder, and pharyngeal derivatives. This lecture traces the endoderm's journey from a primitive epiblast sheet through gastrulation, embryonic folding, and successive organ-specific rotations to yield the final fetal GI arrangement by week 14.
LO 1 — Definitive Endoderm Formation
  • Epiblast cells ingress through primitive streak → definitive endoderm
  • Nascent endoderm intercalates and displaces hypoblast (visceral endoderm)
  • Gastrulation occurs ~14–16 days
  • Molecular signals: Nodal, Sox17, Foxa2 (primer-level)
LO 2 — Regions, Derivatives & Arterial Supply
  • Foregut → celiac artery (pharynx to proximal duodenum + liver, pancreas, GB)
  • Midgut → SMA (distal duodenum to right 2/3 transverse colon)
  • Hindgut → IMA (left 1/3 transverse colon to rectum)
LO 3 — Gut Tube Folding & Mesenteries
  • Tube-within-tube body plan: embryo + amniotic sac outgrow yolk sac
  • Dorsal mesentery suspends most of gut; ventral mesentery = liver/falciform
  • Splanchnic mesoderm → gut wall smooth muscle; somatic → body wall
LO 4 — Organ Development & Rotation
  • Stomach rotates 90° clockwise on long axis (~35 days) → greater/lesser curvatures
  • Liver bud from ventral mesentery → hepatic diverticulum into septum transversum
  • Midgut herniates → 90° CCW rotation around SMA → returns week 11 → cecum descends
  • GI tract complete by week 14

⚡ Step 1 High-Yield Preview

  • Physiologic umbilical herniation: normal midgut herniation at ~6 weeks; failure to return = omphalocele (covered in membrane, fetal origin) vs. gastroschisis (no membrane, no fetal liver)
  • Malrotation: incomplete 270° CCW rotation → abnormal positioning → risk of midgut volvulus around SMA pedicle (bilious vomiting in newborn)
  • Duodenal atresia: failure of recanalization → "double-bubble" sign (Down syndrome association)
  • Vascular supply defines gut regions: celiac = foregut, SMA = midgut, IMA = hindgut — used clinically to localize ischemia and plan surgery
  • Annular pancreas: bifid ventral pancreatic bud wraps around duodenum → duodenal obstruction

Embryonic Tissue Lineage: From Blastocyst to Endoderm ▼

All embryonic tissues trace back to the blastocyst, which has two compartments: the inner cell mass (ICM) and the trophoblast.

Inner Cell Mass (ICM) → Two Lineages ▼

StructureGives Rise ToFinal Fate
Epiblast Embryonic epiblast → primitive streak → embryonic ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm All three embryonic germ layers
Epiblast (also) Amniotic ectoderm Amnion lining
Hypoblast Extraembryonic endoderm → yolk sac → extraembryonic mesoderm Yolk sac (NOT the GI tract)
⚠️ TRAP: Hypoblast ≠ Gut Endoderm ▼

The hypoblast (visceral endoderm) forms the extraembryonic yolk sac — it does NOT form the GI tract. The true gut endoderm (definitive endoderm) arises from epiblast cells that ingress through the primitive streak during gastrulation and intercalate into / displace the hypoblast. On boards: "definitive endoderm" = embryonic endoderm from epiblast via primitive streak.

Trophoblast Lineage (Extraembryonic) ▼

Trophoblast → cytotrophoblast → syncytiotrophoblast → forms the placenta; NOT relevant to gut development but tested in embryology questions about implantation and hCG production.

Gastrulation: Creating the Definitive Endoderm (~Days 14–16) ▼

The Primitive Streak ▼

  • Appears at the caudal end of the bilaminar germ disc at ~day 14–15; extends cranially toward the prochordal plate
  • The primitive streak is the site of epiblast cell ingression — epiblast cells migrate medially, dive through the primitive groove, and emerge as mesoderm (laterally) or definitive endoderm (deepest layer)
  • Primitive node (at the cranial tip) → notochord; organizes the body axis
  • Key landmarks: buccopharyngeal membrane (cranial — future mouth) and cloacal membrane (caudal — future anus)
🔑 Key Concept: Primitive Streak Direction "P.S. starts here, extends cranially" — the primitive streak initiates at the caudal end of the embryo and ingression proceeds in the cranial direction. Cells passing most deeply (below hypoblast) become definitive endoderm.

Endoderm Intercalation: Displacing the Hypoblast ▼

  • At days 14–15: nascent (definitive) endoderm cells intercalate into the hypoblast layer from the primitive streak region
  • By day 16: endoderm progressively displaces the hypoblast laterally; mesoderm extends outward from the primitive streak simultaneously
  • Result: trilaminar disc — ectoderm (dorsal), mesoderm (middle), endoderm (ventral/visceral)
  • The displaced hypoblast cells remain as extraembryonic endoderm (yolk sac lining)

From Flat Disc to True Gut Endoderm ▼

Once the trilaminar disc is established, embryonic folding (both longitudinal/cranio-caudal and lateral/transverse) converts the flat endodermal sheet into a tube. The lateral folds pinch the yolk sac — the portion incorporated into the embryo becomes the true gut endoderm; the remaining portion stays attached as the vitelline duct connecting to the yolk sac.

  • The septum transversum (mesoderm) closes the cranial opening; the cloacal membrane closes the caudal end
  • The buccopharyngeal membrane ruptures → oral opening (~week 4)

Molecular Mechanisms of Endoderm Development (Primer Level) ▼

This lecture is a primer — molecular detail is introduced conceptually, not exhaustively. High-yield for Step 1 context.

Signal / FactorRole in Endoderm Formation
Nodal (TGF-β family)High Nodal signaling at primitive streak drives endoderm fate (vs. mesoderm at lower levels)
Sox17Master transcription factor for definitive endoderm specification; required for gut tube formation
Foxa2 (HNF3β)Pioneer TF expressed in definitive endoderm; required for anterior foregut and liver induction

📋 Step 1 Level Required Know that: (1) definitive endoderm comes from epiblast via primitive streak ingression; (2) it displaces the hypoblast; (3) Nodal/Sox17/Foxa2 are the key signals at a conceptual level. Deep molecular signaling is rarely asked on Step 1.
🧠 Mnemonic: "Epiblast Enters, Endoderm Emerges" ▼

Epiblast cells → Enter the primitive streak → Emerge as definitive Endoderm (deepest) or Mesoderm (lateral). The hypoblast (= "Extra-embryonic" endoderm) gets kicked out — it is NOT the source of gut epithelium.

The Three Gut Tube Regions ▼

The primitive gut tube is divided into three regions based on developmental origin, blood supply, and final adult derivatives. The boundaries are defined by the arterial supply — the arteries provide the most reliable clinical and anatomical landmark.

🔑 Rule: Artery = Region Identifier Celiac artery = foregut region; SMA = midgut region; IMA = hindgut region. This is the single most testable fact from this lecture.
Region Arterial Supply Boundaries
Foregut Celiac artery (celiac trunk, T12) Pharynx → proximal duodenum (superior to ampulla of Vater)
Midgut Superior Mesenteric Artery (SMA, L1) Distal duodenum (inferior to ampulla) → right 2/3 of transverse colon
Hindgut Inferior Mesenteric Artery (IMA, L3) Left 1/3 of transverse colon → upper anal canal (dentate line)
⚠️ TRAP: Duodenum Spans TWO Regions ▼

The duodenum straddles the foregut-midgut boundary at the ampulla of Vater. The proximal half (D1 + D2 superior to ampulla) is foregut → celiac supply. The distal half (D3 + D4) is midgut → SMA supply. Similarly, the transverse colon straddles midgut-hindgut: right 2/3 = SMA, left 1/3 = IMA.

🧠 Mnemonic: Foregut Accessories — "LLGP" ▼

The foregut gives rise to four accessory organs: Liver, Lungs, Gallbladder, Pancreas. Remember: if an organ's epithelium comes from gut endoderm and it's NOT in the tube itself, it's almost certainly a foregut derivative.

⚠️ TRAP: The Anal Canal Has Two Zones ▼

The upper 2/3 of the anal canal (above the dentate/pectinate line) is hindgut endoderm → columnar epithelium, IMA supply, visceral innervation (pain-insensitive). The lower 1/3 (below dentate line) is ectoderm (proctodeum) → stratified squamous epithelium, internal pudendal supply, somatic innervation (pain-sensitive). This boundary is HIGH YIELD for hemorrhoid vs. anal fissure questions.

Arterial Supply Map — Clinical Relevance ▼

The embryonic arterial territories are preserved in the adult. The three unpaired ventral aortic branches each define their gut region:

  • Celiac trunk (T12) → foregut: left gastric, splenic, common hepatic arteries
  • SMA (L1) → midgut: inferior pancreaticoduodenal, jejunal/ileal branches, ileocolic, right colic, middle colic
  • IMA (L3) → hindgut: left colic, sigmoid arteries, superior rectal
💡 Clinical Application: Ischemic Colitis The splenic flexure (midgut-hindgut boundary, SMA↔IMA watershed) and the rectosigmoid junction are the two watershed zones most vulnerable to ischemia during low-flow states. Knowing that the splenic flexure is the SMA/IMA boundary is pure developmental anatomy — the artery defines the region.

LO 5 (Part 1) — Understand how the definitive gut develops to form the digestive tract: embryonic folding, tube-within-tube body plan, and mesenteries

The Tube-Within-a-Tube Body Plan ▼

Human gut development is built on a fundamental architectural principle: the tube-within-a-tube body plan.

Why a Tube-in-a-Tube? ▼

  • The embryo and amniotic sac grow much more rapidly than the yolk sac
  • This uneven growth drives 3-dimensional embryonic folding:
    • Cranio-caudal (longitudinal) folding: head fold and tail fold bring the buccopharyngeal and cloacal membranes to the cranial and caudal ends, respectively
    • Lateral folding: the two lateral body walls fold ventrally → the flat endodermal sheet pinches into a tube; the yolk sac is progressively incorporated
  • Result: an inner tube (gut tube = endoderm-lined) sits suspended within an outer tube (body wall = ectoderm-lined), separated by the intraembryonic coelom
🔑 Cross-Section Summary (~20–26 days) Inner: gut tube (endoderm) → surrounded by splanchnic mesoderm (→ smooth muscle, connective tissue of gut wall) → intraembryonic coelom → somatic mesoderm (→ body wall) → ectoderm (→ skin). The neural tube sits dorsal to the gut tube.

Timeline of Gut Tube Formation ▼

Days / WeeksKey Event
~20 daysEndodermal sheet begins to form; foregut and hindgut pockets visible
~24 daysForegut and hindgut more defined; midgut still open to yolk sac via vitelline duct
~26 daysLateral folding nearly complete; gut tube largely enclosed; vitelline duct narrowing
~30 daysAll major organ buds visible: respiratory diverticulum, stomach, liver cords, pancreatic rudiments, gallbladder

Mesoderm Layers Surrounding the Gut Tube ▼

Lateral plate mesoderm splits into two layers around the intraembryonic coelom (future peritoneal cavity):

LayerLocationWhat It Becomes
Splanchnic mesoderm Lines the gut tube (inner surface of coelom) Smooth muscle, connective tissue, and serosa of the gut wall; also gives rise to the mesenteries
Somatic mesoderm Lines the body wall (outer surface of coelom) Parietal peritoneum, body wall musculature/CT, limb connective tissue
Intermediate mesoderm Between splanchnic and somatic layers Kidney (nephrogenic cord), gonadal ridge, adrenal cortex

Mesenteries — Formation, Types & Fates ▼

Mesenteries are double layers of peritoneum (derived from splanchnic mesoderm) that suspend the gut tube within the coelom and carry neurovascular structures to the bowel.

Dorsal Mesentery ▼

  • Present along the entire gut tube (posterior attachment)
  • Holds most organs in place within the peritoneal cavity
  • Specialized regional names:
    • Dorsal mesogastrium → suspends stomach → eventually becomes the greater omentum (as stomach rotates)
    • Mesoduodenum → largely disappears as duodenum becomes secondarily retroperitoneal
    • Dorsal mesentery proper → suspends small bowel; persists as the mesentery of the small intestine
    • Mesocolon (transverse, sigmoid) → suspends respective colon segments

Ventral Mesentery ▼

  • Present only in the foregut region (cranial part of abdomen); NOT present posterior to the umbilicus
  • Specialized fates:
    • Falciform ligament: attaches liver to anterior abdominal wall; contains ligamentum teres (remnant of left umbilical vein)
    • Lesser omentum: connects liver to stomach (gastrohepatic ligament) and duodenum (hepatoduodenal ligament = portal triad)
    • Hepatic diverticulum source: the ventral mesentery provides the scaffold into which the liver bud grows → grows into the septum transversum
⚠️ TRAP: Ventral Mesentery is Foregut-Only ▼

The ventral mesentery is present only cranially (above the umbilicus). Structures distal to the stomach/proximal duodenum have only a dorsal mesentery. The small intestine and colon have NO ventral mesentery — they are suspended solely by dorsal mesentery. This is why a large portion of the gut can undergo physiologic herniation through the umbilicus — there is no ventral attachment holding it.

Secondarily Retroperitoneal Organs ▼

Some organs begin as intraperitoneal (suspended by mesentery) but later fuse their dorsal mesentery to the posterior abdominal wall → become secondarily retroperitoneal:

  • Duodenum (D2–D4): mesoduodenum fuses to posterior wall
  • Pancreas (head, body): dorsal mesenteries fuse
  • Ascending colon: mesocolon fuses after cecal descent
  • Descending colon: mesocolon fuses

⚡ Step 1 High-Yield — Mesenteries & Retroperitoneum

  • Duodenum D2–D4, pancreas (head/body), ascending colon, descending colon = secondarily retroperitoneal
  • Stomach, spleen, transverse colon, sigmoid colon, liver, gallbladder, proximal duodenum (D1), jejunum, ileum = intraperitoneal
  • True retroperitoneal (never had mesentery): kidneys, ureters, adrenals, aorta, IVC, rectum (lower 2/3), esophagus (thoracic)
  • Falciform ligament carries ligamentum teres (obliterated left umbilical vein) — this is the cord seen in laparoscopy dividing left from right liver

Stomach Formation & Rotation ▼

Stomach Primordium (~Day 27) ▼

  • Stomach forms from the foregut, first visible as a spindle-shaped dilation at ~day 27
  • Initially straight tube oriented craniocaudally, suspended by dorsal mesogastrium (posterior) and ventral mesogastrium/mesentery (anterior)
  • Left and right vagal nerve trunks initially run alongside the esophagus
  • The posterior wall grows faster than the anterior wall → posterior wall becomes the greater curvature; anterior wall → lesser curvature

90° Clockwise Rotation (~Day 35) ▼

Around day 35, the stomach rotates 90° clockwise when viewed from the front (ventral view):

  • The left side rotates anteriorly → becomes the anterior surface (left vagus → anterior vagal trunk)
  • The right side rotates posteriorly → becomes the posterior surface (right vagus → posterior vagal trunk)
  • The greater curvature (former posterior wall) moves to face left and inferiorly
  • The lesser curvature (former anterior wall) faces right and superiorly
  • The dorsal mesogastrium is pulled to the left → forms a pouch called the greater omentum / greater sac — eventually hangs from the greater curvature like an apron
  • Behind the stomach, the rotation creates the lesser sac (omental bursa), accessible via the epiploic foramen of Winslow
⚠️ TRAP: Vagal Nerve Positions After Stomach Rotation ▼

After stomach rotation: the left vagus nerve → anterior vagal trunk; the right vagus nerve → posterior vagal trunk. This is because the left side of the stomach rotates anteriorly, dragging the left vagus with it. Surgeons performing a vagotomy must remember to cut both trunks. Step 1 may ask which vagal trunk is affected after a specific stomach procedure.

Liver, Gallbladder & Omentum Development ▼

Hepatic Diverticulum (Week 4–5) ▼

  • A ventral outgrowth called the hepatic diverticulum (liver bud) emerges from the caudal foregut / duodenal region
  • The liver bud grows from the ventral mesentery into the septum transversum — a mesoderm structure that separates the pericardial cavity from the peritoneal cavity
  • The septum transversum (mesoderm) provides the connective tissue scaffold; the endoderm of the hepatic diverticulum forms the hepatocytes and bile duct epithelium
  • The gallbladder, cystic duct, and common bile duct arise from a separate cystic diverticulum off the hepatic diverticulum

Greater Omentum & Lesser Sac Formation ▼

As the liver grows and the stomach rotates between days 35–42:

  • The dorsal mesogastrium is dragged to the left and elongates → forms the greater omentum (a double-layered apron hanging from the greater curvature)
  • The space created posterior to the stomach and anterior to the posterior abdominal wall = the lesser sac (omental bursa)
  • The lienorenal (splenorenal) ligament connects the spleen to the left kidney; the gastrosplenic ligament connects spleen to stomach — both remnants of the dorsal mesogastrium
  • The falciform ligament (ventral mesentery remnant) connects liver to anterior abdominal wall
  • The lesser omentum = ventral mesentery between liver and stomach/duodenum (gastrohepatic + hepatoduodenal ligaments)

Intestinal Rotation & Return to Abdomen ▼

Primary Intestinal Loop & Physiologic Herniation (~Week 6) ▼

  • At ~7 weeks, the midgut has formed a primary intestinal loop attached at its apex to the vitelline duct (connected to yolk sac)
  • The loop has a cranial limb (→ jejunum/proximal ileum) and a caudal limb (→ distal ileum, cecum, appendix, ascending colon)
  • The liver is growing rapidly and the abdominal cavity is too small → the primary loop herniates into the umbilical cord — this is physiologic umbilical herniation
  • While herniated, the loop rotates 90° counterclockwise (viewed from the front, around the SMA axis)
⚠️ TRAP: Omphalocele vs. Gastroschisis ▼
FeatureOmphaloceleGastroschisis
MechanismFailure of physiologic herniation to return to abdomenDefect in lateral abdominal wall (right of umbilicus)
Covering✅ Covered by amnion + peritoneum membrane❌ No membrane — bowel exposed to amniotic fluid

Return to Abdomen & Further Rotation (~Week 11) ▼

  • At ~week 10–11, the midgut loop returns to the abdomen and rotates an additional 180° counterclockwise → total rotation = 270° counterclockwise around the SMA axis
  • Final positioning:
    • Cranial limb (jejunum/ileum) → occupies center of abdomen
    • Cecum initially sits below the liver (RUQ) then descends to the RLQ by week 12, pulling the proximal hindgut up → forming the ascending colon
    • The appendix hangs off the inferior cecum
  • As the ascending and descending colons fuse to the posterior wall → become secondarily retroperitoneal
  • GI tract arrangement complete by week 14
🧠 Mnemonic: Intestinal Rotation Summary ▼

"270 Counterclockwise Around the SMA"

Week 6: 90° CCW while herniated in umbilical cord → Week 11: 180° more CCW as gut returns → 270° total. The SMA is the axis of rotation. Incomplete rotation = malrotation → the cecum ends up in the RUQ or midline, with abnormal Ladd's bands that can obstruct the duodenum and predispose to midgut volvulus.

Key Malformations of Intestinal Rotation ▼

ConditionMechanismPresentation
Malrotation Incomplete 270° rotation; cecum stays in RUQ; Ladd's bands compress duodenum Bilious vomiting in neonate; risk of midgut volvulus → ischemia
Midgut volvulus Malrotated gut twists around SMA pedicle Sudden bilious vomiting + abdominal pain; surgical emergency (Ladd's procedure)
Duodenal atresia Failure of duodenal recanalization (normally solid cord → hollow tube by week 8) "Double bubble" on AXR (stomach + duodenum); associated with Trisomy 21
Annular pancreas Bifid ventral pancreatic bud encircles duodenum on both sides Duodenal obstruction; may present in adults
Meckel's diverticulum Persistent vitelline (omphalomesenteric) duct; most common GI congenital anomaly Rule of 2's (2%, 2 inches, 2 feet from ICV, 2 types ectopic tissue, 2 years of age); painless rectal bleeding

⚡ Step 1 Summary — GI Rotation High-Yield

  • Physiologic umbilical herniation = NORMAL at 6 weeks; resolves by week 11
  • Total rotation = 270° counterclockwise around the SMA (90° while herniated + 180° on return)
  • Malrotation → cecum in RUQ, Ladd's bands, risk of midgut volvulus (surgical emergency)
  • Double bubble = duodenal atresia (T21) — two air-fluid levels (stomach + dilated duodenum)
  • Stomach rotation: 90° clockwise → left vagus → anterior; right vagus → posterior; greater curvature faces left; lesser sac forms
  • Liver bud: from ventral mesentery (hepatic diverticulum) into septum transversum; endoderm → hepatocytes + bile duct epithelium; mesodermal septum transversum → Kupffer cells + connective tissue

LO 1 — Definitive Endoderm Formation (Q1–Q4)

Q1. A researcher studying GI development labels epiblast cells just before gastrulation. These labeled cells are tracked and found in the epithelial lining of the stomach at day 30. Which structure must these cells have passed through to contribute to the gut epithelium?

A. Notochord

B. Primitive streak

C. Buccopharyngeal membrane

D. Hypoblast

E. Neural crest

✓ Correct: B — Primitive streak

The definitive endoderm arises from epiblast cells that ingress through the primitive streak during gastrulation. These cells migrate ventrally, intercalate into, and displace the hypoblast to form the true gut endoderm. The hypoblast itself is extraembryonic endoderm (→ yolk sac lining) — it does NOT contribute to gut epithelium. The notochord forms from cells that pass through the primitive node (cranial tip); neural crest is ectoderm-derived.

Q2. During early embryogenesis, the hypoblast is displaced laterally by ingressing epiblast cells. The hypoblast gives rise to which of the following structures?

A. Gastric epithelium

B. Hepatocytes

C. Yolk sac lining (extraembryonic endoderm)

D. Parietal peritoneum

E. Intestinal villi

✓ Correct: C — Yolk sac lining (extraembryonic endoderm)

The hypoblast (= visceral endoderm) is extraembryonic. It lines the yolk sac and does not contribute to the epithelium of the GI tract. The gut epithelium (gastric, intestinal, hepatocytes, bile duct) all derive from definitive endoderm — epiblast cells that ingressed through the primitive streak during gastrulation. Parietal peritoneum is from somatic mesoderm. Intestinal villi epithelium = definitive endoderm.

Q3. A 15-day-old embryo is being described. The primitive streak has formed and cells are beginning to ingress. Which of the following correctly describes what happens to cells ingressing MOST DEEPLY through the primitive streak?

A. They form the notochord, which defines the midline axis

B. They intercalate into the hypoblast to form definitive endoderm

C. They migrate laterally to form somatic mesoderm

D. They migrate cranially to form the cardiac mesoderm

E. They remain as surface ectoderm

✓ Correct: B — They intercalate into the hypoblast to form definitive endoderm

Depth of ingression through the primitive streak determines cell fate. Cells ingressing MOST DEEPLY displace the hypoblast to form definitive (embryonic) endoderm. Cells ingressing at an intermediate depth spread laterally → intraembryonic mesoderm. Cells ingressing through the primitive node (cranial tip) → notochord. Cells that do not ingress → remain as ectoderm. This gradient of ingression depth = germ layer fate specification.

Q4. A molecular biology study finds that a specific transcription factor is required for specification of the definitive endoderm. Knockout embryos fail to form a gut tube. Which transcription factor is MOST likely responsible?

A. Sox2

B. Pax6

C. Sox17

D. MyoD

E. Nkx2.5

✓ Correct: C — Sox17

Sox17 is the master transcription factor for definitive endoderm specification. It is downstream of Nodal signaling (high Nodal → Sox17 → endoderm fate). Loss of Sox17 prevents gut tube formation. Sox2 = pluripotency/ectoderm/foregut marker; Pax6 = eye/pancreas; MyoD = skeletal muscle; Nkx2.5 = cardiac transcription factor. Foxa2 (HNF3β) is also a key endoderm pioneer factor and may appear in Step 1 context for liver development.

LO 2 — Regions, Derivatives & Arterial Supply (Q5–Q8)

Q5. A 3-day-old neonate presents with bilious vomiting and abdominal distension. An abdominal radiograph shows two air-fluid levels — one in the stomach and one in a dilated duodenum — with no gas distal to this point. Which embryologic process has most likely failed?

A. Neural crest cell migration to the hindgut

B. Recanalization of the duodenal lumen after the solid cord stage

C. Rotation of the primary intestinal loop around the SMA

D. Fusion of the dorsal and ventral pancreatic buds

E. Descent of the cecum to the right lower quadrant

✓ Correct: B — Recanalization of the duodenal lumen

This is duodenal atresia — the "double bubble" sign (dilated stomach + dilated duodenum, no gas beyond). The duodenum normally passes through a solid cord stage (epithelial proliferation occludes the lumen) and then recanalizes by ~week 8. Failure of recanalization → atresia. Associated with Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome) in ~30% of cases. The duodenum is the most common site of intestinal atresia.

Q6. A patient with mesenteric ischemia has occlusion of the superior mesenteric artery (SMA). Which of the following portions of the GI tract would be MOST directly affected?

A. Pharynx, esophagus, stomach, and proximal duodenum

B. Left 1/3 transverse colon, descending colon, sigmoid colon, rectum

C. Distal duodenum, jejunum, ileum, cecum, appendix, ascending colon, right 2/3 transverse colon

D. Liver, gallbladder, pancreas, and spleen

E. Descending colon, sigmoid colon only

✓ Correct: C — Distal duodenum through right 2/3 of transverse colon

The SMA supplies the midgut: from the distal duodenum (inferior to the ampulla of Vater) through the right 2/3 of the transverse colon. This is the largest vascular territory in the GI tract. SMA occlusion causes massive midgut ischemia — one of the most lethal abdominal emergencies. The foregut (pharynx → proximal duodenum) is celiac; the hindgut (left 1/3 transverse → rectum) is IMA.

Q7. During embryonic development, the hepatic diverticulum gives rise to which of the following structures?

A. Pancreatic ductal cells only

B. Liver parenchyma, bile duct epithelium, gallbladder, cystic duct, and common bile duct

C. Liver parenchyma only; bile ducts arise separately from the gallbladder

D. Liver and pancreas, as they share a common diverticulum

E. Gallbladder only; the liver derives from the septum transversum mesoderm

✓ Correct: B — Liver parenchyma, bile duct epithelium, gallbladder, cystic duct, and common bile duct

The hepatic diverticulum (liver bud) arises from the caudal foregut endoderm around week 4. It gives rise to: hepatocytes (liver cords), intrahepatic bile duct epithelium, gallbladder, cystic duct, and common bile duct — all from endoderm. The connective tissue stroma (Kupffer cells, sinusoidal cells) comes from the septum transversum mesoderm. The pancreas derives from a separate dorsal and ventral pancreatic bud (also foregut endoderm, but independent diverticula).

Q8. A surgeon performs a left hemicolectomy for colon cancer. Which artery's territory has been surgically removed, based on embryologic blood supply?

A. Celiac trunk

B. Superior mesenteric artery

C. Inferior mesenteric artery

D. Internal iliac artery

E. Superior rectal artery only

✓ Correct: C — Inferior mesenteric artery

A left hemicolectomy removes the left colon — specifically the left 1/3 of the transverse colon, descending colon, and sigmoid colon. These are all hindgut derivatives supplied by the IMA (left colic and sigmoid arteries). The rectum is also hindgut/IMA territory but is typically spared in a standard left hemi. The IMA is always ligated during this procedure; surgeons rely on collateral flow from the SMA via the marginal artery.

LO 3 — Gut Tube Folding & Mesenteries (Q9–Q11)

Q9. A first-year medical student is reviewing embryology and asks why humans have a "tube-within-a-tube" body plan. Which of the following best explains the mechanical basis for this plan?

A. The neural tube grows faster than the gut tube, pushing the gut ventrally

B. The embryo and amniotic sac grow much more rapidly than the yolk sac, causing lateral and cranio-caudal folding that incorporates the endoderm into an inner tube

C. The endoderm proliferates faster than the mesoderm, causing it to buckle inward

D. The cloacal membrane breaks down prematurely, allowing folding from the caudal direction

E. Apoptosis of lateral plate mesoderm creates a space for the gut tube

✓ Correct: B — Embryo and amniotic sac outgrow the yolk sac

The tube-within-a-tube body plan arises because the embryo and amniotic sac grow much more rapidly than the yolk sac. This differential growth drives 3-dimensional folding: cranio-caudal (head and tail folds) and bilateral lateral folding. The lateral folds pinch the flat endodermal sheet into a tube, incorporating the dorsal portion of the yolk sac as the primitive gut tube (connected to the yolk sac by the vitelline duct). The inner tube = gut endoderm; the outer tube = ectoderm-lined body wall; the intraembryonic coelom (→ peritoneal cavity) lies between.

Q10. A patient undergoes an emergency laparotomy. The surgeon notes that the falciform ligament divides the anterior surface of the liver into right and left lobes. Which embryologic structure is the falciform ligament a remnant of?

A. Dorsal mesogastrium

B. Dorsal mesentery of the small bowel

C. Ventral mesentery

D. Mesoduodenum

E. Lienorenal ligament

✓ Correct: C — Ventral mesentery

The falciform ligament is a remnant of the ventral mesentery, which is present only in the foregut region (cranial abdomen). It connects the liver to the anterior abdominal wall and contains the ligamentum teres hepatis (obliterated left umbilical vein). The dorsal mesogastrium → greater omentum + lienorenal/gastrosplenic ligaments. The lesser omentum (also ventral mesentery) connects liver to stomach and duodenum.

Q11. A 4-year-old boy with recurrent abdominal pain and constipation undergoes a rectal biopsy. The pathologist reports an absence of ganglion cells in both the myenteric and submucosal plexuses of the distal colon. During which embryologic process did the failure occur?

A. Failure of endodermal folding to form the hindgut

B. Failure of neural crest cells to migrate to and populate the distal colon

C. Incomplete rotation of the midgut around the SMA

D. Persistence of the vitelline duct in the hindgut

E. Failure of the cloacal membrane to rupture

✓ Correct: B — Failure of neural crest cell migration to the distal colon

This is Hirschsprung disease. Neural crest cells normally migrate craniocaudally into the gut wall to form the myenteric (Auerbach's) and submucosal (Meissner's) plexuses. Failure of neural crest migration to the distal colon → aganglionic segment → tonic contraction → functional obstruction → proximal megacolon. The gut endoderm itself is normal — the problem is with the neural crest-derived neurons that should populate the mesoderm of the gut wall.

LO 4 — Organ Development & Rotation (Q12–Q15)

Q12. A neonatal surgeon is evaluating a newborn with a large abdominal wall defect. Bowel loops and the liver are protruding through the umbilicus and are covered by a translucent membrane. Karyotype reveals Trisomy 18. What is the diagnosis, and what embryologic failure occurred?

A. Gastroschisis — failure of lateral body wall fusion to the right of the umbilicus

B. Omphalocele — failure of the physiologic midgut hernia to return to the abdominal cavity, with persistence of bowel in the umbilical cord

C. Meckel's diverticulum — persistence of the vitelline duct

D. Malrotation — incomplete 270° CCW rotation of the midgut

E. Duodenal atresia — failure of lumen recanalization

✓ Correct: B — Omphalocele

Omphalocele: bowel (and sometimes liver) herniates through the umbilicus and is covered by a membrane (amnion + peritoneum). Associated with chromosomal anomalies (Trisomy 13, 18; Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome). The liver CAN be in the omphalocele — it cannot in gastroschisis. Gastroschisis: defect to the RIGHT of the umbilicus, NO membrane, no liver involvement — bowel is free-floating and inflamed from amniotic fluid. Trisomy association = omphalocele (not gastroschisis, which is usually isolated).

Q13. Following embryonic stomach rotation, the left vagus nerve comes to lie on the anterior surface of the stomach (anterior vagal trunk), while the right vagus lies posteriorly. Which statement best explains why?

A. The right vagus nerve grows faster and reaches the stomach first, pushing the left vagus posteriorly

B. The stomach rotates 90° clockwise on its long axis, so the left surface (originally carrying the left vagus) rotates to face anteriorly

C. The stomach rotates 90° counterclockwise, and the right vagus follows the right surface anteriorly

D. Vagal fibers cross at the esophageal plexus before reaching the stomach, reversing their sides

E. The dorsal mesogastrium rotates 90° clockwise, dragging the right vagus to the posterior

✓ Correct: B — Stomach rotates 90° clockwise; left surface rotates to face anteriorly

The stomach rotates 90° clockwise (when viewed from the front/ventral). The original left surface (which carries the left vagus) rotates to become the anterior surface → left vagus = anterior vagal trunk. The original right surface (right vagus) rotates to become the posterior surface → right vagus = posterior vagal trunk. Clinical relevance: surgeons performing vagotomy for peptic ulcer disease must cut both the anterior and posterior trunks.

Q14. A 3-week-old boy presents with sudden onset bilious vomiting, abdominal distension, and bloody stools. Imaging shows the small bowel in the right abdomen with the colon on the left. A contrast enema shows the cecum in the right upper quadrant. What is the most likely diagnosis and the operative risk?

A. Duodenal atresia — risk of double bubble

B. Malrotation with midgut volvulus — risk of entire midgut infarction

C. Hirschsprung disease — risk of toxic megacolon

D. Meckel's diverticulum — risk of intussusception

E. Omphalocele — risk of bowel necrosis from membrane rupture

✓ Correct: B — Malrotation with midgut volvulus

Malrotation occurs when the midgut fails to complete its full 270° CCW rotation — the cecum remains in the RUQ and abnormal fibrous bands (Ladd's bands) cross the duodenum. The entire midgut hangs on a narrow SMA pedicle → prone to twist = midgut volvulus. This is a surgical emergency: the volvulus occludes the SMA → ischemia/infarction of the ENTIRE midgut (from duodenum to transverse colon). Bilious vomiting in a neonate = malrotation/volvulus until proven otherwise. Treatment: Ladd's procedure (detorsion, divide Ladd's bands, appendectomy, cecum placed in LLQ).

Q15. A 2-year-old girl presents with painless bright red rectal bleeding. A Meckel's scan (⁹⁹ᵐTc-pertechnetate) shows a hot spot in the right lower quadrant. Which of the following best describes the embryologic origin of this anomaly?

A. Failure of the cloacal membrane to rupture, trapping hindgut blood supply in the RLQ

B. Persistence of the vitelline (omphalomesenteric) duct as a true diverticulum of the ileum containing ectopic gastric or pancreatic mucosa

C. Failure of neural crest cell migration to the ileum causing a dilated, aganglionic segment

D. Incomplete rotation of the midgut leaving the cecum in the right lower quadrant with ectopic mucosa

E. A cystic dilation of the common bile duct that has descended into the RLQ

✓ Correct: B — Persistence of the vitelline duct as a Meckel's diverticulum

Meckel's diverticulum: the vitelline (omphalomesenteric) duct normally obliterates completely by week 6–7 as the gut returns to the abdomen. Persistence = true diverticulum on the antimesenteric border of the ileum, 2 feet from the ileocecal valve. It may contain ectopic gastric mucosa (most common, causing acid-induced ulceration → painless bleeding) and/or pancreatic tissue. The ⁹⁹ᵐTc-pertechnetate scan detects ectopic gastric mucosa → hot spot in the RLQ. Rule of 2's: 2%, 2 inches long, 2 feet from ICV, 2 types of ectopic tissue, presents in first 2 years.
