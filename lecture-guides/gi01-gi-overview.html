▼GI Segments: Mouth to Anus

The GI tract is a continuous tube approximately 9 meters (30 feet) long, specialized at each region for specific functions. Think of it as a specialized assembly line where food is progressively broken down and nutrients extracted.

Associated Organs (Accessory Glands)

Three organs secrete digestive products into the GI lumen but are not technically part of the tube itself:

  • Liver: Produces bile (emulsifies fats); receives all portal blood for first-pass metabolism; also produces clotting factors, albumin, and detoxifies drugs
  • Gallbladder: Stores and concentrates bile; releases via common bile duct in response to cholecystokinin (CCK)
  • Pancreas: Exocrine: secretes digestive enzymes (proteases, lipase, amylase) + bicarbonate into duodenum via pancreatic duct; Endocrine: insulin and glucagon into blood
⭐ High-Yield: Bile and pancreatic secretions both enter the duodenum at the ampulla of Vater, controlled by the sphincter of Oddi. CCK opens the sphincter; secretin stimulates pancreatic bicarbonate.

▼Wall Layers & Plexuses

The GI tract wall has four concentric layers (inside to outside), consistent throughout most of the tract:

Four Wall Layers (Inside → Outside)

  1. Mucosa — innermost layer; three sublayers:
    • Epithelium: absorptive/secretory cells; tight junctions regulate paracellular flow
    • Lamina propria: loose CT with capillaries, lymphatics (lacteals), immune cells (plasma cells, mast cells)
    • Muscularis mucosae: thin smooth muscle layer; produces local mucosal movements
  2. Submucosa — dense CT; contains blood vessels, lymphatics, Meissner's (submucosal) plexus
  3. Muscularis externa (propria) — two smooth muscle layers:
    • Inner circular muscle: contracts to narrow lumen (peristalsis constriction)
    • Outer longitudinal muscle: contracts to shorten segment
    • Auerbach's (myenteric) plexus lies between these two layers
  4. Serosa (intraperitoneal) / Adventitia (retroperitoneal) — outermost; connective tissue covering

Plexus Locations — Memory Anchor

🧡 Mnemonic: "My Sub"
  • Myenteric plexus = Motility (between circular & longitudinal muscle)
  • Submucosal plexus = Secretion (between circular muscle & submucosa)
PlexusLocationPrimary FunctionAlso Called

⚠️ Exam Trap: Hirschsprung Disease

Hirschsprung disease = failure of neural crest cells to migrate into the distal colon → absence of both myenteric and submucosal ganglia → tonic contraction of affected segment → functional obstruction (aganglionic megacolon). Presents in neonates as failure to pass meconium. The aganglionic segment is narrow; the dilated segment proximal has normal ganglion cells.

Surface Area Adaptations (Small Intestine)

The small intestine maximizes absorptive surface area through three levels of folding:

  1. Plicae circulares (valvulae conniventes) — large circular folds of mucosa + submucosa; visible to naked eye; most prominent in jejunum; ×3 surface area
  2. Villi — finger-like projections of mucosa; contain a central lacteal (lymphatic) and capillary network; ×10 surface area
  3. Microvilli (brush border) — tiny projections on each enterocyte; contain digestive enzymes; ×20 surface area

Together these increase surface area ~600-fold compared to a simple tube, enabling efficient absorption of 8–9 liters of fluid daily.

Skeletal vs. Smooth Muscle in GI Tract

Most of the GI tract uses smooth muscle (involuntary). There are only three locations where skeletal muscle (voluntary) is found:

  • Mouth, pharynx, and upper esophagus — allows voluntary initiation of swallowing
  • External anal sphincter — allows voluntary control of defecation
⚠️ Clinical Correlation: Dysphagia

In myasthenia gravis (autoimmune destruction of nicotinic ACh receptors at neuromuscular junction), swallowing difficulty (dysphagia) affects the upper esophagus (skeletal muscle). In contrast, dysphagia in achalasia or scleroderma affects the lower esophagus (smooth muscle) — these are NOT affected by myasthenia gravis.

🎯 USMLE Step 1 Summary — GI Anatomy
  • Four wall layers: mucosa → submucosa → muscularis externa → serosa
  • Myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus = between circular + longitudinal muscle = motility
  • Submucosal (Meissner's) plexus = between circular muscle + submucosa = secretion
  • Parietal cells: HCl + intrinsic factor; Chief cells: pepsinogen; G cells: gastrin
  • GI epithelium turns over every 3–5 days (vulnerable to chemo/radiation)
  • Hirschsprung = absent ganglia (neural crest migration failure) → tonic contraction of distal colon
  • Skeletal muscle only at: mouth/pharynx/upper esophagus + external anal sphincter

Testing Your Understanding — LO 1, Question 1

A 2-day-old neonate fails to pass meconium and develops progressive abdominal distension. Rectal biopsy reveals an absence of ganglion cells in the affected segment. What embryological event failed, and what would you expect to find histologically in the dilated (proximal) segment?

Consider: Neural crest migration, aganglionic vs. ganglionic segments, which segment is functionally obstructed

✓ Answer: Failed neural crest cell migration → Hirschsprung disease

Neural crest cells failed to migrate into the distal colon during embryogenesis, leaving it aganglionic. The aganglionic segment has tonic contraction (no relaxation), creating functional obstruction. The dilated proximal segment is normal — it has intact ganglion cells in both myenteric and submucosal plexuses; it dilates because stool cannot pass through the contracted segment below.

Testing Your Understanding — LO 1, Question 2

A patient with long-standing myasthenia gravis develops difficulty initiating swallowing. Which portion of the esophagus is involved, and why are the symptoms limited to that area?

Consider: Skeletal vs. smooth muscle distribution, nicotinic vs. muscarinic receptors, myasthenia mechanism

✓ Answer: Upper esophagus (skeletal muscle) — nicotinic NMJ affected

Myasthenia gravis destroys nicotinic acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction. Skeletal muscle — which requires nicotinic signaling — is affected; smooth muscle uses muscarinic receptors and is not targeted. The upper esophagus and pharynx are skeletal muscle, so dysphagia occurs there. The lower esophagus, stomach, and intestines are smooth muscle and are spared.

▼DEEP MASS — The 8 Functions of the GI Tract

🧡 Mnemonic: DEEP MASS
  • D — Digestion: Enzymatic breakdown of macromolecules (carbohydrates → monosaccharides, proteins → amino acids, fats → fatty acids + monoglycerides)
  • E — Elimination: Removal of waste products; acid kills most ingested bacteria; mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT) provides immune defense; bacteria in colon are eliminated in stool
  • E — Endocrine function: GI tract is the largest endocrine organ in the body; enteroendocrine cells release hormones (gastrin, CCK, secretin, GIP, GLP-1, motilin) into blood
  • P — Protection/Immune: Mucosal barrier; IgA secretion; gastric acid kills pathogens; Peyer's patches (ileum) and MALT provide adaptive immunity
  • M — Motility/Propulsion: Peristalsis moves content aborally; segmentation mixes content; must keep moving to allow new food in and get waste out
  • A — Absorption: Nutrients, electrolytes, water absorbed (almost everything is absorbed — even fiber is partially fermented by colonic bacteria)
  • S — Secretion: Digestive enzymes, acid, bile, bicarbonate secreted into lumen; ~7–8 L/day of secretions facilitate digestion
  • S — Storage: Stomach stores and releases chyme in controlled manner; colon stores stool while dehydrating it

Key Distinction: Hormones vs. Enzymes — Where Do They Go?

This is a frequently tested conceptual point:

TypeSecreted IntoExamplesWhy it matters

GI hormonesBlood (portal circulation)Gastrin, CCK, secretin, GIP, GLP-1Reach target organs systemically; measured in serum

⚠️ Exam Trap: Hormones Are Always Secreted INTO Blood

GI hormones (gastrin, CCK, secretin, etc.) are always secreted into the bloodstream, not the lumen. If a question asks where gastrin "acts," remember it travels through blood to reach parietal cells in the stomach. This also means they are measurable in serum and can be elevated in neoplastic conditions (e.g., Zollinger-Ellison syndrome = gastrinoma → very high serum gastrin → massive acid hypersecretion).

▼Fluid Volumes in the GI Tract

The scale of GI fluid handling is remarkable — the body secretes far more than it ingests, yet almost all of it is reabsorbed. Failure of even a small portion of this reabsorption leads to clinically significant diarrhea.

Sources of GI Secretions (~9–10 L/day total)

Saliva~1.5 LAmylase, mucin, IgA, bicarbonate; pH 6.0–7.0

Bile~0.5 LBile salts, bilirubin, cholesterol, phospholipids; pH 7.0–8.0
Pancreatic juice~1.5 LBicarbonate (neutralizes acid), zymogens (trypsinogen, chymotrypsinogen), lipase, amylase
Intestinal secretions~1.5–2 LWater, electrolytes, mucus from intestinal crypts
TOTAL entering small intestine~9–10 L/day

Where Is Fluid Absorbed?

SiteAmount AbsorbedKey Point

⭐ Clinical Significance: If the small intestine loses just 1% of its reabsorptive capacity, an extra 85 mL is lost — causing diarrhea. In cholera, the Vibrio cholerae toxin activates adenylyl cyclase → ↑cAMP → massive Cl⁻ secretion + water follows → stool output can exceed 20 L/day → life-threatening dehydration.
⚠️ Exam Trap: Origin of GI Secretions

Secretions are derived from the extracellular fluid (ECF). Every liter secreted into the GI lumen is drawn from the ECF. This is why vomiting (gastric fluid loss = HCl loss) causes metabolic alkalosis + hypokalemia, and diarrhea (lower GI fluid loss = HCO₃⁻ loss) causes metabolic acidosis + hypokalemia.

▼pH Gradient Along the GI Tract

pH changes dramatically along the GI tract, driven by acid secretion in the stomach and bicarbonate neutralization in the duodenum:

SegmentApproximate pHDriver
Mouth / Saliva6.0–7.0Bicarbonate in saliva
Stomach (fasting)1.5–2.0HCl from parietal cells (H⁺/K⁺ ATPase)
Duodenum (proximal)4.0–5.0Acid chyme enters → CCK/secretin → pancreatic HCO₃⁻ neutralizes
Jejunum6.0–7.0Progressive neutralization
Ileum (terminal)7.0–7.5Bicarbonate accumulation
Colon5.5–7.0Bacterial fermentation produces short-chain fatty acids (lowers pH)
⭐ Why pH Matters:
  • Pepsin is only active at pH <3.5 (denatured at higher pH) — explains why PPIs are effective ulcer treatment
  • Enteric-coated medications dissolve only when pH ≥5–6, targeting the small intestine
  • Pancreatic enzymes work optimally at pH 7–8 (explain why pancreatic insufficiency + acid hypersecretion compounds maldigestion)
🎯 USMLE Step 1 Summary — Functions & Fluid Balance
  • DEEP MASS = Digestion, Elimination, Endocrine, Protection, Motility, Absorption, Secretion, Storage
  • GI hormones → secreted into BLOOD; digestive enzymes → secreted into LUMEN
  • Total fluid entering GI: ~9–10 L/day (2L ingested + 7–8L secreted)
  • Small intestine absorbs 8.5 L; colon absorbs 400 mL; only 100–200 mL excreted
  • Vomiting → metabolic alkalosis (lose HCl); diarrhea → metabolic acidosis (lose HCO₃⁻)
  • Stomach pH ~1.5; duodenum pH rises to 4–5 after HCO₃⁻ neutralization; jejunum 6–7

🧠 Testing Your Understanding!

Testing Your Understanding — LO 2–3, Question 1

A patient presents with severe, watery diarrhea and is found to have metabolic acidosis with hypokalemia. Based on GI fluid physiology, explain the acid-base and electrolyte abnormalities.

Consider: What fluid is lost in diarrhea, HCO₃⁻ content of intestinal secretions, where K⁺ is secreted

✓ Answer: Diarrhea causes HCO₃⁻ loss → non-anion gap metabolic acidosis + K⁺ loss

Intestinal and pancreatic secretions are bicarbonate-rich. Diarrhea causes massive loss of HCO₃⁻ → non-anion gap metabolic acidosis. The colon normally absorbs Na⁺ and secretes K⁺; with high-volume diarrhea, this K⁺ secretion plus loss of K⁺ in stool → hypokalemia. Contrast with vomiting (HCl loss → metabolic alkalosis + hypokalemia).

Testing Your Understanding — LO 2–3, Question 2

A drug company claims their new antibiotic increases gastric acid secretion. A colleague says this cannot be a GI hormone mechanism because hormones aren't produced in the GI tract. How do you respond, and what evidence contradicts this claim?

Consider: DEEP MASS "Endocrine" function, enteroendocrine cells, gastrin source

✓ Answer: Incorrect — the GI tract is the largest endocrine organ in the body

The GI tract contains abundant enteroendocrine cells that secrete peptide hormones into the bloodstream. Gastrin (from G cells in gastric antrum and duodenum) is a prime example — it travels through blood to stimulate parietal cells to produce HCl. CCK, secretin, GIP, and GLP-1 are other GI hormones. The GI tract's endocrine function is the "E" in DEEP MASS.

▼ENS Overview — The "Second Brain"

The enteric nervous system (ENS) is a semi-autonomous network of ~500 million neurons embedded in the wall of the GI tract from esophagus to anus — more neurons than in the spinal cord. It can coordinate complex GI functions completely independently of the brain or spinal cord, earning it the name "the second brain" (Gershon, 1999).

Second Brain ~500 million neurons Intrinsic innervation Functions without CNS Myenteric = motility Submucosal = secretion

Key principle: The GI tract can perform its basic functions (peristalsis, secretion, absorption) without any input from the brain or spinal cord. However, extrinsic innervation (autonomic) makes these functions more coordinated, efficient, and responsive to the body's overall state.

⭐ Clinical Proof of ENS Autonomy: Transplanted intestinal segments that have been extrinsically denervated (all vagal and sympathetic connections cut) still undergo peristalsis. In patients with complete spinal cord injuries, the gut continues to function — though motility and bowel habits may be disrupted, the ENS still drives basal activity.

ENS Neuron Types

Like the CNS, the ENS has three functional neuron classes:

  • Sensory (afferent) neurons: Detect intraluminal stimuli (stretch, chemicals, osmolarity) → feed into ENS circuits and send signals to CNS via vagal afferents
  • Interneurons: Integrate signals; form local reflex circuits; allow ascending excitation and descending inhibition
  • Motor (efferent) neurons:
    • Excitatory motor neurons: Release acetylcholine (ACh) and Substance P → contract smooth muscle
    • Inhibitory motor neurons: Release VIP (vasoactive intestinal peptide) and nitric oxide (NO) → relax smooth muscle

Think of the ENS as the "local government" of the GI tract — it can run day-to-day operations. The autonomic nervous system is the "federal government" — it can override or amplify local decisions but isn't needed for basic operations.

▼Ileus: When ENS Fails

Ileus = absence or failure of GI motility. When the ENS cannot function properly (or sensory receptors that feed into it are absent), peristalsis ceases.

Types of Ileus

TypeCauseMechanism
Paralytic (adynamic) ileusPost-surgery, peritonitis, opiate medications, electrolyte imbalances (↓K⁺)SNS overactivation or direct smooth muscle inhibition → ENS inhibited → no peristalsis
Hirschsprung ileusAbsent ganglia (developmental)No ENS present → no relaxation → functional obstruction

⚠️ Exam Trap: Opiates and Ileus

Opioids (morphine, codeine) bind to μ-opioid receptors on ENS neurons → inhibit excitatory neurotransmitter release → decreased peristalsis + increased sphincter tone → constipation and paralytic ileus. This is the basis for drugs like methylnaltrexone (peripherally-acting μ-opioid antagonist that doesn't cross the BBB) to treat opioid-induced constipation without reversing analgesia.

🎯 USMLE Step 1 Summary — Enteric Nervous System
  • ENS = ~500 million neurons; functions independently of CNS ("second brain")
  • Myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus: between circular + longitudinal muscle → MOTILITY
  • Submucosal (Meissner's) plexus: between circular muscle + submucosa → SECRETION
  • Excitatory ENS neurons: ACh + Substance P; Inhibitory: VIP + NO
  • Loss of ENS → ileus (no peristalsis); Hirschsprung = absent ganglia → aganglionic segment contracts tonically
  • Opioids bind μ-receptors on ENS → constipation/ileus
  • Extrinsic innervation modulates ENS but is NOT required for basic GI function

🧠 Testing Your Understanding!

Testing Your Understanding — LO 4, Question 1

A patient on high-dose IV morphine for post-operative pain develops abdominal distension, no bowel sounds, and failure to pass gas or stool for 4 days. The team wants to treat the ileus without reversing analgesia. What is the mechanism and the targeted treatment?

Consider: Where opioid receptors are expressed in the GI tract, ENS neuron function, peripherally-restricted antagonists

✓ Answer: μ-opioid receptor activation on ENS neurons → paralytic ileus; Tx: methylnaltrexone

Morphine binds μ-opioid receptors expressed on ENS neurons throughout the GI tract. This inhibits excitatory neurotransmitter release (ACh, Substance P) from the myenteric plexus → decreases peristalsis + increases sphincter tone → paralytic ileus. Methylnaltrexone (Relistor) is a peripherally-acting μ-opioid antagonist that does not cross the blood-brain barrier, blocking GI effects without reversing CNS analgesia.

Testing Your Understanding — LO 4, Question 2

Explain why a completely transected spinal cord at T6 would NOT result in complete cessation of GI motility, but would significantly alter bowel habits.

Consider: ENS autonomy, what extrinsic innervation provides, how bowel habits are controlled voluntarily

✓ Answer: ENS is intrinsically autonomous; loss of extrinsic modulation alters coordinated control

The ENS can maintain basal peristalsis and secretion without any input from the CNS or extrinsic ANS. Therefore, a T6 spinal transection does not stop GI motility. However, bowel habits are significantly altered because: (1) the cephalic phase (CNS → vagal activation) is disrupted; (2) coordinated defecation requires integration of voluntary external anal sphincter control (pudendal nerve, S2–S4) with the involuntary internal sphincter; (3) sympathetic modulation is lost. "Neurogenic bowel" results — the ENS still functions, but reflex defecation is uncoordinated and voluntary control is absent below the lesion.

▼Three Sensory Receptor Types

The ENS "sees" the luminal environment through three types of sensory receptors embedded in the GI wall. These receptors feed into ENS reflex arcs that trigger appropriate motor and secretory responses.

⭐ Key Principle: If any receptor type is absent or dysfunctional → the ENS cannot initiate appropriate reflexes → ileus or dysmotility. This is why post-operative ileus occurs even when the ENS is intact — surgical manipulation and inflammation desensitize the mechanoreceptors.

1. Mechanoreceptors (Stretch Receptors)

  • Stimulus: Distension/stretch of the GI wall (intraluminal pressure, volume)
  • Location: Muscle layers and submucosa; respond to both circular and longitudinal stretch
  • Response: Primary trigger for peristaltic reflex; also activates vomiting reflex when over-distended
  • Clinical relevance: Post-operative ileus — handling bowel during surgery "stuns" mechanoreceptors; reduced activity → paralytic ileus. Also: gastric distension → satiety signal via vagal afferents

2. Chemoreceptors

  • Stimulus: Chemical composition of luminal contents — pH, specific nutrients (fatty acids, amino acids, glucose), bile acids, bacterial products
  • Location: Epithelial layer (enteroendocrine cells act as sensory cells) and submucosa
  • Response: Trigger hormone secretion (CCK released by fatty acids/amino acids in duodenum; secretin released by acid in duodenum); activate ENS secretory reflexes
  • Clinical relevance: Low intraluminal pH triggers secretin release → pancreatic HCO₃⁻ secretion (protects duodenum from acid)

3. Osmoreceptors

  • Stimulus: Osmolarity of luminal contents
  • Location: Duodenal and jejunal mucosa are most sensitive
  • Response: Hyperosmolar chyme → slows gastric emptying (gives more time to dilute before absorption); triggers fluid secretion into lumen to dilute content
  • Clinical relevance: Dumping syndrome — rapid gastric emptying of hyperosmolar content into small intestine (post-gastrectomy) overwhelms osmoreceptor regulation → fluid rushes into gut lumen → hypovolemia + nausea/diarrhea

▼The Peristaltic Reflex

The peristaltic reflex is the fundamental ENS-mediated reflex that propels content aborally (toward the anus). It has two simultaneous limbs, creating a "squeeze-and-relax" wave:

  1. Stimulus: Distension (mechanoreceptors) or chemical stimuli trigger ENS sensory neurons
  2. Ascending excitation (oral/proximal side of bolus):
    • ACh + Substance P released → circular muscle CONTRACTS above bolus → propels content forward
  3. Descending inhibition (aboral/distal side of bolus):
    • VIP + NO released → circular muscle RELAXES below bolus → creates space for content to move into
  4. Net effect: Content moves in the aboral direction (mouth → anus)
⚠️ Exam Trap: Achalasia and the Peristaltic Reflex

In achalasia, the inhibitory neurons (VIP/NO) at the LES are destroyed (autoimmune or idiopathic). Without descending inhibition, the LES fails to relax when food arrives → food cannot enter the stomach → progressive esophageal dilation. Key imaging: "bird-beak" appearance on barium swallow. Treatment: pneumatic dilation or surgical myotomy (Heller), or botulinum toxin injection (blocks ACh release from excitatory neurons to rebalance).

Serotonin in the GI Tract

The GI tract contains ~90% of the body's total serotonin, produced by enterochromaffin (EC) cells in the intestinal mucosa. 5-HT is the primary initiator of the peristaltic reflex:

  • Distension or chemical stimuli → EC cells release 5-HT into the lamina propria
  • 5-HT activates 5-HT3 receptors on intrinsic primary afferent neurons (IPANs) → initiates peristaltic reflex
  • 5-HT also acts on 5-HT4 receptors → enhances ACh release → prokinetic effect

Pharmacologic implications:

  • 5-HT3 antagonists (ondansetron, granisetron): Block 5-HT3 on vagal afferents → antiemetic; used for chemotherapy-induced nausea
  • 5-HT4 agonists (cisapride, prucalopride): ↑ACh release → prokinetic; used for gastroparesis
  • Carcinoid tumors (EC cell tumors) → massive 5-HT release → carcinoid syndrome: flushing, diarrhea, wheezing, right-sided heart disease
🎯 USMLE Step 1 Summary — Receptors & Neurotransmitters
  • Three GI receptor types: mechanoreceptors (stretch), chemoreceptors (composition), osmoreceptors (osmolarity)
  • All three required for ENS to function; absence → ileus
  • Peristaltic reflex: ACh/Substance P (ascending excitation) + VIP/NO (descending inhibition)
  • Achalasia = loss of inhibitory NO/VIP neurons at LES → LES fails to relax
  • 90% of body serotonin is in GI (enterochromaffin cells); 5-HT3 antagonists = antiemetics; 5-HT4 agonists = prokinetics
  • VIPoma → watery diarrhea (VIP acts as secretagogue); carcinoid tumor → flushing + diarrhea

Testing Your Understanding — LO 5, Question 1

A patient undergoes laparoscopic cholecystectomy and develops persistent dysphagia to solids and liquids six months later. Esophageal manometry reveals failure of the LES to relax with swallowing and absent peristalsis in the distal esophagus. What neurotransmitter/receptor deficit explains this finding?

Consider: LES relaxation mechanism, descending inhibitory limb of peristaltic reflex, achalasia pathophysiology

✓ Answer: Loss of inhibitory VIP/NO neurons at LES → achalasia

LES relaxation during swallowing is mediated by inhibitory ENS neurons that release VIP and nitric oxide (NO) — the descending inhibitory limb of the peristaltic reflex. In achalasia, these inhibitory neurons (likely nNOS/VIP-positive) are selectively destroyed (autoimmune, possibly triggered by HSV or Trypanosoma cruzi in Chagas disease). Without VIP/NO → LES cannot relax → obstruction. The remaining excitatory (ACh) tone is unopposed → hypertensive LES. Treatment with botulinum toxin blocks the excitatory ACh signal to temporarily rebalance, or myotomy divides the circular muscle to physically relieve obstruction.

Testing Your Understanding — LO 5, Question 2

A patient with a known ileal carcinoid tumor presents with flushing, watery diarrhea, and wheezing. You check a 24-hour urine for 5-HIAA and it's massively elevated. What is the mechanism linking the tumor to these symptoms, and why does right-sided heart disease develop but NOT left-sided?

Consider: Where 5-HT is metabolized, carcinoid heart disease anatomy, hepatic first-pass

✓ Answer: EC cell tumor → massive 5-HT release → enters portal circulation → right heart exposed before liver metabolism

Carcinoid tumors (from enterochromaffin cells) release massive amounts of serotonin (5-HT) and other vasoactive substances directly into the portal circulation. Normally, 5-HT is metabolized by monoamine oxidase (MAO) in the liver — hepatic first-pass metabolism. However, when the liver is overwhelmed (or there are liver metastases), 5-HT enters systemic circulation → carcinoid syndrome. The right heart (right atrium, tricuspid valve, pulmonary valve) receives venous return before it passes through the pulmonary vasculature. 5-HT causes fibrosis of right-sided valves (tricuspid regurgitation, pulmonary stenosis). The pulmonary vasculature inactivates 5-HT before it reaches the left heart → left heart is spared (unless there is a right-to-left shunt or pulmonary/bronchial carcinoid releasing 5-HT directly into pulmonary veins).

▼Arterial Supply: Celiac, SMA, IMA

The GI tract receives blood from three unpaired branches of the abdominal aorta, each supplying an embryologic division of the gut:

🧡 Mnemonic: Watershed Zones = Vulnerable Points
  • Splenic flexure = watershed between SMA and IMA territories → most vulnerable to ischemic colitis (borderline supply from both)
  • Rectosigmoid junction = watershed between IMA and pudendal/hemorrhoidal arteries → second most vulnerable

Celiac Artery Branches

  • Left gastric artery — stomach (lesser curvature); most common site of peptic ulcer bleeding if erodes into vessel
  • Common hepatic artery → proper hepatic artery (liver) + gastroduodenal artery (duodenum, head of pancreas)
  • Splenic artery → spleen + pancreatic branches + left gastroepiploic

SMA and IMA Key Branches

  • SMA branches: Inferior pancreaticoduodenal, jejunal arteries, ileal arteries, ileocolic, right colic, middle colic
  • IMA branches: Left colic, sigmoid arteries, superior rectal artery
  • Rectum collaterals: Superior rectal (IMA) + middle rectal (internal iliac) + inferior rectal (pudendal) → rich collateral supply explains why rectal ischemia is rare
⚠️ Exam Trap: SMA Syndrome / Ischemic Colitis

SMA syndrome: Compression of duodenum between aorta and SMA (in very thin patients or those with rapid weight loss) → upper GI obstruction. SMA embolism is a surgical emergency: sudden-onset severe periumbilical pain out of proportion to physical exam, +/- bloody diarrhea. The "pain out of proportion to exam" clue + atrial fibrillation (source of embolus) is classic Step 1.

▼Portal Circulation & First-Pass Metabolism

Blood draining the GI tract (small intestine, colon, stomach, pancreas, spleen) flows into the portal vein before entering systemic circulation. This ensures all absorbed nutrients are processed by the liver first.

Portal Venous Drainage Route

GI capillaries → portal vein → hepatic sinusoids → hepatic veins → inferior vena cava → right heart → systemic circulation

NutrientRoute After AbsorptionWhy
Glucose, amino acids, short-chain FAs, water-soluble vitaminsCapillaries → Portal vein → Liver first-passWater-soluble; enter capillaries directly
Long-chain fatty acids, fat-soluble vitamins (A,D,E,K)Chylomicrons → Lacteals → Thoracic duct → Left subclavian vein → Systemic circulationToo large for capillaries; packaged as chylomicrons in enterocytes

The Lipid Exception — Why It Matters

Dietary fats bypass the liver entirely on first absorption. This has clinical implications:

  • Fat-soluble drug administration: Drugs delivered in lipid carriers can partially bypass liver first-pass by entering lymphatics
  • Fat-soluble vitamins (ADEK): Malabsorption of fat (pancreatic insufficiency, cholestasis, celiac disease) → deficiency of ADEK specifically
  • Chylomicrons reach the right heart directly (via thoracic duct → SVC) → why hypercholesterolemia from fat absorption doesn't initially require liver processing

Splanchnic Blood Flow During Digestion

At rest, the splanchnic circulation receives ~25–30% of cardiac output. After a meal, this increases dramatically:

  • Post-prandial: Increased blood flow to intestines (hyperemia) supports nutrient absorption
  • This is diverted from skeletal muscle → explains why vigorous exercise immediately after eating is uncomfortable and can cause cramping
  • Sympathetic activation (exercise, fight-or-flight) → splanchnic vasoconstriction → blood redirected to muscles
⚠️ Portal Hypertension Consequences

Liver cirrhosis → ↑resistance in hepatic sinusoids → portal pressure rises → blood backs up into portal tributaries → portosystemic shunts open: (1) Esophageal varices (left gastric → azygos), (2) Caput medusae (paraumbilical veins), (3) Hemorrhoids (superior rectal ↔ inferior/middle rectal). Most dangerous: esophageal varices → can rupture → life-threatening hemorrhage.

▼Sympathetic Regulation — "Fight or Flight"

Sympathetic activation suppresses GI function and redirects blood flow to muscles — the body's "shut down digestion to deal with the threat" response.

⚠️ Exam Trap: Sympathetic Does NOT Just Inhibit Everything

While SNS generally decreases GI motility and secretion, it simultaneously contracts sphincters (ileocecal valve, internal anal sphincter). This is why stress/anxiety can cause "stress diarrhea" when SNS tone paradoxically triggers ENS-mediated mass movements in the colon via complex mechanisms — GI physiology is not a simple on/off switch.

▼Cephalic Phase — Brain Anticipates the Meal

The cephalic phase occurs before food even enters the GI tract, driven entirely by the CNS in response to anticipatory signals.

Stimuli and Mechanism

  • Stimuli: Sight, smell, taste, or even thought of food → cortical and hypothalamic activation
  • Pathway: CNS → dorsal motor nucleus of vagus → vagus nerve → ENS → GI effectors
  • Effects: ↑Salivation, ↑gastric acid secretion, ↑gastric motility, ↑pancreatic enzyme secretion
  • Magnitude: Accounts for ~20–30% of total gastric acid response to a meal

Pavlov's Classic Experiment

Ivan Pavlov demonstrated the cephalic phase by showing that dogs secreted gastric juice when shown food (before ingestion). This "psychic secretion" is mediated entirely by vagal efferents to the stomach.

🧡 Three Phases of Gastric Acid Secretion
  • Cephalic phase (20–30%): Sight/smell/thought → vagal activation → ↑HCl + pepsinogen
  • Gastric phase (60–70%): Food in stomach → distension (mechanoreceptors) + amino acids/peptides → gastrin release → ↑HCl
  • Intestinal phase (5–10%): Chyme in duodenum → some gastrin; also inhibitory hormones (secretin, CCK, GIP) dampen acid output
🎯 USMLE Step 1 Summary — Blood Supply & Autonomic Regulation
  • Celiac = foregut; SMA = midgut; IMA = hindgut. Watershed zones: splenic flexure (SMA/IMA) + rectosigmoid junction
  • Portal vein carries all absorbed nutrients to liver first-pass EXCEPT lipids (chylomicrons → lacteals → thoracic duct)
  • Portal hypertension → esophageal varices (most dangerous), caput medusae, hemorrhoids
  • PSNS: Vagus (CN X) → esophagus to transverse colon; pelvic nerve (S2–S4) → distal colon/rectum. Effect: ↑motility + ↑secretion
  • SNS: T5–L2 → splanchnic nerves → prevertebral ganglia (celiac, SMA, IMA). Effect: ↓motility + ↓secretion + splanchnic vasoconstriction
  • Cephalic phase: sight/smell/taste → vagus → 20–30% of gastric acid response; demonstrated by Pavlov's experiments

Testing Your Understanding — LO 6–7, Question 1

A 68-year-old man with atrial fibrillation presents with sudden-onset severe periumbilical pain for 3 hours. His abdomen is soft with minimal tenderness on physical exam. CT angiography reveals an embolus at the origin of the superior mesenteric artery. Which segments of bowel are at risk for ischemia, and what is the physiologic explanation for "pain out of proportion to exam"?

Consider: SMA territory, mechanism of ischemic pain, why peritoneal signs are absent early

✓ Answer: SMA embolism → midgut ischemia (small intestine + right colon); visceral pain without peritoneal signs early

The SMA supplies the entire midgut — duodenum (distal), jejunum, ileum, cecum, ascending colon, and proximal 2/3 of transverse colon. An embolus here threatens all of these structures. The "pain out of proportion to exam" occurs because intestinal ischemia initially produces severe visceral pain (crampy, diffuse, periumbilical) mediated by visceral afferents in the mesentery — this does not require transmural necrosis or peritoneal involvement. Physical exam only reveals peritoneal signs (guarding, rebound) when the transmural ischemia progresses to perforation/peritonitis — by which time irreversible necrosis has occurred. This is why early SMA embolism is easily missed and why the classic teaching is: "severe pain + benign exam = mesenteric ischemia until proven otherwise."

Testing Your Understanding — LO 6–7, Question 2

A patient is prescribed a highly lipophilic antiretroviral drug with extensive hepatic first-pass metabolism (oral bioavailability ~5%). A pharmacologist suggests formulating it as a lipid emulsion to improve bioavailability. Explain the physiologic basis for this strategy.

Consider: Route of lipid absorption, portal vs. lymphatic drainage, where first-pass metabolism occurs

✓ Answer: Lipid formulation → chylomicron incorporation → lymphatic absorption → bypasses hepatic first-pass

When dietary lipids are absorbed, long-chain fatty acids are re-esterified in the enterocyte and packaged into chylomicrons. Chylomicrons are too large to enter capillaries; instead, they enter lacteals (lymphatic vessels in the intestinal villi) → intestinal lymphatics → thoracic duct → left subclavian vein → systemic circulation. This entirely bypasses the portal vein and liver. If a highly lipophilic drug is co-formulated with lipids, it can be incorporated into chylomicrons during absorption and enter the lymphatics — dramatically reducing first-pass hepatic metabolism and increasing systemic bioavailability. This strategy is used for drugs like tacrolimus and some HIV medications.

Question 1

A histology slide shows a section of small intestine. The student identifies a layer of cells located between the inner circular and outer longitudinal smooth muscle layers that contains a dense network of neurons. Which structure is this, and what is its primary function?

A. Submucosal plexus (Meissner's) — controls secretion

C. Muscularis mucosae — controls local mucosal movement

D. Lamina propria — contains immune cells and capillaries

✓ Correct: B — Myenteric plexus (Auerbach's) — controls motility

The myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus lies between the circular and longitudinal smooth muscle layers of the muscularis externa. It is the primary controller of GI motility, including peristalsis and segmentation. The submucosal (Meissner's) plexus lies between the circular muscle and the submucosa and controls secretion and local blood flow. The muscularis mucosae is a thin layer within the mucosa itself (not in the muscularis externa).

Question 2

A 3-week-old male infant presents with projectile vomiting after every feeding. The vomitus contains undigested milk and is non-bilious. Ultrasound reveals a thickened, elongated pylorus with a "donut sign." Which layers of the GI wall are hypertrophied in this condition?

A. Mucosa and submucosa

B. Circular and longitudinal smooth muscle layers of the muscularis externa

C. Submucosal plexus neurons only

D. Lamina propria and muscularis mucosae

E. Serosa and adventitia

✓ Correct: B — Circular and longitudinal smooth muscle layers of the muscularis externa

Infantile hypertrophic pyloric stenosis involves hypertrophy of the smooth muscle of the muscularis externa (primarily the circular muscle layer) at the pylorus. This creates gastric outlet obstruction. The vomiting is non-bilious because obstruction is proximal to the ampulla of Vater (no bile has mixed with the content). Treatment is Ramstedt pyloromyotomy — cutting through the hypertrophied muscle while preserving the mucosa.

Question 3

Vitamin B12 absorption requires intrinsic factor produced by parietal cells. A 55-year-old woman with autoimmune destruction of parietal cells (pernicious anemia) would be expected to have which additional deficiency, given what parietal cells also produce?

A. Pancreatic lipase deficiency

B. Pepsinogen deficiency causing protein maldigestion

C. Inability to acidify the stomach (achlorhydria)

D. Bile salt deficiency

E. Salivary amylase deficiency

✓ Correct: C — Inability to acidify the stomach (achlorhydria)

Parietal cells produce both HCl (via H⁺/K⁺ ATPase) and intrinsic factor. Autoimmune destruction of parietal cells → simultaneous loss of HCl (achlorhydria) and intrinsic factor. Achlorhydria predisposes to bacterial overgrowth and impairs protein digestion (pepsinogen activation requires low pH). Pepsinogen is produced by chief cells (not parietal cells), so pepsinogen secretion itself is not impaired, but the enzyme cannot be activated without acid.

Question 4

A medical student sees a patient with Crohn's disease affecting the terminal ileum. The attending asks: "This patient has fat-soluble vitamin deficiency. Which specific vitamins would you expect to be low, and what GI mechanism explains this?" The best answer is:

A. Vitamins B1, B6, B12 — terminal ileum is primary site of water-soluble vitamin absorption

B. Vitamins A, D, E, K — bile salt reabsorption is disrupted, impairing fat absorption and fat-soluble vitamin absorption

C. Vitamin C only — ascorbic acid is absorbed exclusively in the ileum

✓ Correct: B — Vitamins A, D, E, K — bile salt disruption impairs fat absorption

The terminal ileum is the sole site of bile salt reabsorption (enterohepatic circulation). When the terminal ileum is diseased or resected, bile salts are not reabsorbed → lost in stool → bile salt pool depleted → inadequate micelle formation → fat malabsorption → fat-soluble vitamins (ADEK) are not absorbed. LO 2 — GI Functions: DEEP MASS (Q5–Q8)

Question 5

A researcher studies a compound that blocks secretion of a peptide hormone from enteroendocrine cells in the gastric antrum. Administration of protein-rich food no longer increases gastric acid output. Which hormone is most likely blocked, and where does it normally exert its effect?

A. Secretin — acts on parietal cells to stimulate HCl

B. Gastrin — acts on parietal cells to stimulate HCl

C. CCK — acts on the pyloric sphincter to decrease gastric emptying

✓ Correct: B — Gastrin — acts on parietal cells to stimulate HCl

Gastrin is produced by G cells in the gastric antrum and duodenum. It is released in response to protein (amino acids and peptides), stomach distension, and vagal stimulation. Gastrin is secreted into the blood and travels to parietal cells in the gastric body/fundus, where it activates CCK-B receptors → stimulates HCl secretion. This is the "Endocrine" function (E in DEEP MASS) — GI hormones are secreted into the bloodstream to act at distant targets.

Question 6

Which GI function in DEEP MASS explains why patients with complete bowel obstruction can develop life-threatening complications even without eating — and why they become malnourished if obstruction persists?

A. Digestion — enzymes continue to be released but cannot break down nutrients

B. Secretion + Absorption imbalance — secretions continue accumulating in the obstructed lumen but cannot be reabsorbed, leading to fluid/electrolyte loss and eventually dehydration

C. Storage — the stomach can no longer store food properly

D. Motility — peristalsis cannot overcome the obstruction

E. Elimination — bacteria accumulate proximal to the obstruction

✓ Correct: B — Secretion + Absorption imbalance

Even fasting patients secrete ~7–8 L of GI fluid daily from salivary glands, stomach, pancreas, bile, and intestinal crypts. Normally this fluid is almost entirely reabsorbed. With a complete small bowel obstruction, secretions continue to accumulate above the obstruction, but absorption is impaired. The result: massive third-spacing of fluid into the obstructed bowel → vomiting → dehydration → electrolyte abnormalities → shock. Without IV fluid replacement, this can be rapidly fatal — demonstrating how critical the Secretion + Absorption balance (two of the S/A components of DEEP MASS) is even without oral intake.

Question 7

A patient with chronic alcoholic pancreatitis has near-total destruction of the pancreatic exocrine function. Steatorrhea (oily stools) is present. Which specific component of the DEEP MASS functions is MOST critically impaired?

A. Motility — pancreatic enzymes normally slow peristalsis

B. Absorption — without pancreatic lipase, triglycerides cannot be digested, and long-chain fatty acid absorption fails

C. Endocrine — loss of endocrine pancreas is the primary problem

D. Storage — pancreatic enzymes control how long food is stored in the stomach

E. Elimination — inability to digest bacteria leads to infection

✓ Correct: B — Absorption impaired due to absent pancreatic lipase

Pancreatic lipase is essential for hydrolysis of triglycerides into monoglycerides and free fatty acids (the absorbable forms). Without it, fat passes undigested into the colon → bacterial fermentation of fat → steatorrhea (bulky, oily, malodorous stools). This also impairs absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K). Treatment: oral pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy (PERT) — enzymes given with meals to replace the missing exocrine function. Note: salivary lipase and gastric lipase provide some (~10–30%) residual fat digestion.

Question 8

The "P" in DEEP MASS stands for Protection/Immune. A medical student argues that the stomach actually creates danger because it is so acidic. How would you explain the "Protection" function of gastric acid?

A. Gastric acid has no protective function; it only activates pepsinogen

B. Gastric acid (pH 1.5) kills the vast majority of ingested pathogens before they can reach the intestine

C. Gastric acid stimulates IgA secretion by plasma cells in the lamina propria

D. Gastric acid prevents Paneth cell activation

E. Gastric acid activates bile acid synthesis in the liver

✓ Correct: B — Gastric acid kills ingested pathogens

Gastric acid (pH 1.5–2.0) is a critical first line of immune defense. Most bacteria, viruses, and parasites cannot survive exposure to this extremely low pH. Clinical evidence: patients on proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) have a significantly higher risk of Clostridioides difficile, Salmonella, Campylobacter, and Vibrio infections — because the protective acid barrier has been reduced. Similarly, patients with achlorhydria (e.g., pernicious anemia) are more susceptible to GI infections. This is why the GI tract — despite being in contact with the outside world through food — maintains such a hostile environment.

LO 3 — Fluid Balance (Q9–Q12)

Question 9

A child with cholera (Vibrio cholerae infection) produces 20 liters of watery stool ("rice water stools") per day. The mechanism involves cholera toxin activating Gs protein → ↑cAMP → CFTR channel opens → massive Cl⁻ secretion. What happens to the ~9–10 L of daily GI secretions in a normal person that prevents this from occurring?

A. The GI tract produces only ~1–2 L of secretions, so there is no risk of fluid loss

B. Under normal conditions, the small intestine reabsorbs ~8.5 L and the colon reabsorbs ~400 mL, excreting only 100–200 mL in stool

C. The stomach reabsorbs all secreted fluid before it reaches the intestine

D. Pancreatic secretions neutralize cholera toxin before it can act

E. The colon secretes water to dilute the concentrated toxin

✓ Correct: B — Small intestine reabsorbs 8.5 L; colon reabsorbs 400 mL; only 100–200 mL excreted

The GI tract secretes 7–8 L per day in addition to the ~2 L ingested, totaling ~9–10 L. The small intestine reabsorbs ~8.5 L (driven largely by Na⁺ co-transporters); the colon reabsorbs most of the remaining ~400 mL, leaving only 100–200 mL in stool. In cholera, the ↑cAMP → massive Cl⁻ secretion overwhelms this reabsorptive capacity, flipping the intestine from net absorber to net secretor. Oral rehydration solution (ORS) works by providing glucose + Na⁺ to drive Na⁺-glucose cotransport (SGLT1), which remains functional even during cholera, pulling water back across.

Question 10

A 45-year-old woman undergoes Roux-en-Y gastric bypass surgery. Post-operatively, she experiences sweating, palpitations, and lightheadedness 15–30 minutes after eating carbohydrate-rich meals. Which mechanism best explains "early dumping syndrome"?

A. Hypoglycemia from excess insulin secretion

B. Rapid gastric emptying of hyperosmolar food into the small intestine → osmoreceptors detect high osmolarity → fluid shifts from blood into lumen → hypovolemia

C. Vagal overstimulation from the bypass anatomy

D. Bacterial fermentation of undigested carbohydrates in the proximal bowel

E. Bile reflux into the gastric remnant

✓ Correct: B — Hyperosmolar chyme overwhelms osmoreceptors → fluid shifts → hypovolemia

After gastric bypass, the gastric remnant is small and cannot buffer or dilute food properly. Hyperosmolar food enters the small intestine rapidly without the normal pyloric regulation. Small intestinal osmoreceptors detect extreme hyperosmolarity and fluid pours from the blood into the intestinal lumen to dilute the content → intravascular hypovolemia → tachycardia, lightheadedness, sweating. This is "early" dumping (15–30 min post-meal). "Late" dumping occurs 1–3 hours later due to rebound hypoglycemia from exaggerated insulin response to the absorbed glucose load.

E. Loss of bile → metabolic acidosis + hypercalcemia

✓ Correct: B — Loss of HCl → metabolic alkalosis + hypokalemia + hypochloremia

Gastric juice is highly acidic (pH 1.5) — it contains HCl, K⁺, and some Na⁺. Continuous NG suction removes HCl → extracellular [H⁺] falls → metabolic alkalosis (↑pH, ↑HCO₃⁻). Simultaneously, K⁺ is lost in gastric juice AND the kidneys respond to volume depletion by activating the RAAS → aldosterone → exchanges K⁺ for Na⁺ in the kidney → further hypokalemia. The alkalosis drives K⁺ into cells (K⁺/H⁺ exchange) → worsening hypokalemia. Cl⁻ is directly lost in HCl → hypochloremia. This is "contraction alkalosis" or "hypokalemic hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis."

Question 12

A patient with chronic diarrhea from inflammatory bowel disease has a serum pH of 7.30, HCO₃⁻ of 16 mEq/L, and serum K⁺ of 3.0 mEq/L. An intern says this picture is opposite to what would be expected from vomiting. Explain why diarrhea produces the opposite acid-base disorder compared to vomiting.

A. Diarrhea causes respiratory acidosis, vomiting causes respiratory alkalosis

B. Diarrhea causes metabolic acidosis (HCO₃⁻ loss in stool); vomiting causes metabolic alkalosis (HCl loss in vomitus)

C. Diarrhea causes hyperkalemia because K⁺ is retained; vomiting causes hypokalemia

D. Diarrhea causes metabolic alkalosis because of lactic acid generation in the bowel

E. Both conditions produce metabolic acidosis through different mechanisms

✓ Correct: B — Diarrhea = metabolic acidosis (HCO₃⁻ loss); vomiting = metabolic alkalosis (HCl loss)

Intestinal and pancreatic secretions are bicarbonate-rich. Diarrhea causes loss of HCO₃⁻-rich fluid → non-anion gap metabolic acidosis (↓pH, ↓HCO₃⁻). Potassium is also lost (colon secretes K⁺, and aldosterone response worsens this) → hypokalemia. Vomiting causes loss of HCl → metabolic alkalosis (↑pH, ↑HCO₃⁻) + hypokalemia + hypochloremia. This is one of the most commonly tested acid-base patterns on Step 1: know the fluid composition at each level of the GI tract and you can predict the electrolyte/acid-base consequence of losing it.

LO 4 — Enteric Nervous System (Q13–Q16)

Question 13

A 2-year-old boy with Down syndrome presents with chronic constipation since birth. Rectal biopsy shows absence of ganglion cells in the rectosigmoid region. His dilated proximal colon shows normal ganglion cells. What is the pathophysiology of obstruction in this patient?

A. The proximal dilated colon contracts tonically, preventing passage of stool

B. The aganglionic distal segment lacks inhibitory relaxation → remains in tonic contraction → functional obstruction

C. Absence of ganglion cells leads to watery diarrhea as uncontrolled secretion occurs

D. The dilated segment lacks peristalsis; the narrow segment is normal

E. Ganglion cell absence causes paralytic ileus of the entire colon

✓ Correct: B — Aganglionic segment cannot relax → functional obstruction

In Hirschsprung disease, the aganglionic segment lacks both excitatory and inhibitory ENS neurons. Without inhibitory neurons (VIP/NO), smooth muscle remains in tonic contraction — the aganglionic segment cannot relax to allow stool to pass. The proximal segment, which has normal ganglia, dilates from the backed-up stool. This is counterintuitive: the narrow segment is the diseased one; the dilated segment is normal. Down syndrome (trisomy 21) has an increased association with Hirschsprung disease. Treatment is surgical resection of the aganglionic segment (Swenson pull-through).

Question 14

Which of the following correctly describes the ENS with respect to extrinsic autonomic innervation?

A. The ENS requires vagal input to initiate peristalsis and will not function after vagotomy

B. The ENS can maintain basic peristalsis and secretion independently; extrinsic autonomic innervation modulates but is not required for basic GI function

C. The ENS consists only of afferent sensory neurons that relay signals to the CNS

D. Sympathetic nerves stimulate the ENS to increase motility during stress

E. The ENS is only present in the small intestine; the colon is controlled solely by extrinsic autonomic nerves

✓ Correct: B — ENS functions independently; extrinsic ANS modulates ENS activity

The ENS contains ~500 million neurons organized into complete local reflex arcs (sensory, interneurons, motor). It can initiate and coordinate peristalsis, secretion, and local blood flow without any extrinsic input — demonstrated by experiments in which denervated bowel segments still contract rhythmically. Vagotomy (cutting the vagus nerve) does not stop peristalsis; it reduces the efficiency of coordination and decreases gastric acid secretion (used therapeutically). Sympathetic nerves generally inhibit GI motility (not stimulate). The ENS extends throughout the entire GI tract from esophagus to anus.

Question 15

A patient with Parkinson's disease develops severe constipation. Research shows that Lewy bodies (α-synuclein aggregates) are present in the ENS neurons of the colon — often years before they appear in the substantia nigra. This suggests which concept about the ENS?

A. The ENS is identical in structure to the CNS, including susceptibility to the same neurodegenerative processes

B. The ENS is merely a relay station for signals from the brain; its independent function means nothing clinically

C. The ENS contains neurons that share properties with CNS neurons, can be affected by neurodegeneration, and may serve as the origin or early site of Parkinson's pathology

D. Loss of ENS neurons in Parkinson's causes diarrhea, not constipation

E. The ENS is a separate organ entirely; its pathology is unrelated to CNS disease

✓ Correct: C — ENS neurons share CNS properties; Parkinson's pathology may begin in the gut

The "Braak hypothesis" (Braak et al., 2003) proposes that Parkinson's disease may originate in the gut ENS or olfactory bulb and then spread to the brain via vagal afferents. This is supported by the finding of Lewy bodies in ENS neurons before substantia nigra involvement, and by the observation that vagotomy is associated with reduced Parkinson's risk in some studies. Constipation from ENS neurodegeneration is a common early non-motor symptom of Parkinson's. This is an active and exciting area of gut-brain axis research.

Question 16

In the ENS, which neurotransmitter combination mediates the ascending excitatory limb of the peristaltic reflex (contraction proximal to the bolus)?

A. VIP + NO

B. Norepinephrine + Neuropeptide Y

C. Acetylcholine + Substance P

D. Serotonin (5-HT) + dopamine

E. GABA + glycine

✓ Correct: C — Acetylcholine + Substance P

The peristaltic reflex has two simultaneous limbs: (1) Ascending excitation — oral (proximal) to the bolus: excitatory ENS neurons release ACh and Substance P → circular muscle contracts → propels bolus forward. (2) Descending inhibition — aboral (distal) to the bolus: inhibitory ENS neurons release VIP and NO → circular muscle relaxes → creates space for the bolus to move into. This coordination creates the propagating peristaltic wave.

LO 5 — Receptors & Neurotransmitters (Q17–Q20)

Question 17

Ondansetron is a 5-HT3 receptor antagonist used to prevent chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting. Which physiologic role of 5-HT3 receptors does this drug block?

A. 5-HT3 receptors on parietal cells → reduces HCl secretion

B. 5-HT3 receptors on vagal afferent neurons in the gut wall → reduces afferent signaling to the vomiting center

C. 5-HT3 receptors on ENS inhibitory neurons → increases motility

D. 5-HT3 receptors on gastrin-secreting G cells → reduces gastrin release

E. 5-HT3 receptors on pancreatic acinar cells → reduces enzyme secretion

✓ Correct: B — 5-HT3 receptors on vagal afferents → afferent signaling to vomiting center

Enterochromaffin cells release 5-HT in response to luminal stimuli. 5-HT acts on 5-HT3 receptors located on intrinsic primary afferent neurons (IPANs) in the ENS and on vagal afferent fiber terminals in the gut wall. Activation of vagal afferents relays signals to the nucleus tractus solitarius and area postrema (chemoreceptor trigger zone) → nausea and vomiting. It also has central action at the area postrema (which lacks a blood-brain barrier). 5-HT4 agonists (metoclopramide) enhance ENS excitatory neuron ACh release → prokinetic effect.

Question 18

A patient with a VIPoma (pancreatic tumor secreting VIP) presents with profuse watery diarrhea, hypokalemia, and achlorhydria. Which receptor type mediates VIP's effects on GI secretion?

A. VIP acts on muscarinic M3 receptors on intestinal crypt cells → opens Cl⁻ channels

B. VIP acts on VPAC receptors (Gs-coupled) on intestinal crypt cells → ↑cAMP → ↑Cl⁻ secretion → water follows

C. VIP acts on nicotinic receptors on ENS neurons → increases ACh release

E. VIP acts on 5-HT4 receptors to increase intestinal motility

✓ Correct: B — VIP → VPAC receptors (Gs) → ↑cAMP → ↑Cl⁻ secretion

VIP acts on VPAC1/VPAC2 receptors, which are Gs-coupled. Activation → ↑adenylyl cyclase → ↑cAMP → activation of protein kinase A → opens CFTR Cl⁻ channels in intestinal crypt cells → Cl⁻ secretion → water follows osmotically → massive watery diarrhea. VIPoma (Verner-Morrison syndrome, WDHA = Watery Diarrhea-Hypokalemia-Achlorhydria): diarrhea causes K⁺ loss → hypokalemia; high VIP also suppresses gastric acid → achlorhydria. Question 19

A 35-year-old woman presents with progressive dysphagia to solids and liquids over 2 years. Esophageal manometry shows absent peristalsis in the distal esophagus and failure of LES relaxation. ✓ Correct: C — Clinical features distinguish; rapid weight loss + short history → malignancy

True achalasia (idiopathic or secondary to Chagas disease) is caused by loss of inhibitory ENS neurons (VIP/NO) at the LES → LES fails to relax. Manometrically, it shows aperistalsis in the distal esophagus and impaired LES relaxation. Pseudoachalasia from malignancy at the GEJ (e.g., adenocarcinoma, gastric cancer infiltrating the cardia) can appear identical on manometry. Key differentiating features: (1) Rapid weight loss — achalasia typically shows gradual weight loss; malignancy is rapid; (2) Short duration of symptoms (<1 year); (3) Older age at presentation; (4) EGD + CT scan to exclude mass lesion. Question 20

A 55-year-old man presents with flushing (especially facial), watery diarrhea, and wheezing. Serum serotonin and 24-hour urine 5-HIAA are markedly elevated. CT of the abdomen shows multiple liver metastases and a primary mass in the ileum. Why does this patient have right-sided valvular disease (tricuspid regurgitation) but NOT left-sided?

A. 5-HT selectively damages right-sided valves due to the higher pressure on the right side

B. Serotonin enters the portal vein → liver metabolism inactivates most of it → but liver metastases impair this → serotonin reaches the right heart via systemic veins; pulmonary vasculature inactivates 5-HT before it reaches the left heart

C. 5-HT is inactivated by the right heart muscle itself before reaching the left heart

E. 5-HT only acts on receptors present on right-sided valve leaflets

✓ Correct: B — Liver metastases impair first-pass metabolism; pulmonary vasculature inactivates 5-HT before left heart

Normally, serotonin released by enterochromaffin cells is almost entirely cleared by MAO in hepatocytes during portal first-pass metabolism. Liver metastases overwhelm this capacity → excess 5-HT enters systemic circulation → reaches the right heart (right atrium and ventricle) → fibrotic plaques on tricuspid and pulmonary valves → tricuspid regurgitation + pulmonary stenosis. LO 6 — Blood Supply (Q21–Q24)

Question 21

During repair of a large abdominal aortic aneurysm, the surgeon must clamp the aorta above the inferior mesenteric artery. Post-operatively, the patient develops bloody diarrhea and left-sided colonic ischemia. Which specific vascular territory has been compromised?

A. Celiac artery territory — ischemia of the stomach and duodenum

B. SMA territory — ischemia of the small intestine and right colon

C. IMA territory — ischemia of the left colon (descending colon, sigmoid) and upper rectum

D. Pudendal artery territory — ischemia of the lower rectum and anal canal

E. Portal vein territory — global intestinal ischemia

✓ Correct: C — IMA territory (left colon, sigmoid)

The IMA supplies the hindgut: distal 1/3 of transverse colon, descending colon, sigmoid colon, and upper rectum. During AAA repair, the IMA is often sacrificed or may go into spasm after aortic clamping. If collateral flow from the SMA (through the marginal artery of Drummond) is insufficient, the left colon develops ischemia → bloody diarrhea ("ischemic colitis"). This most commonly affects the watershed area at the splenic flexure (junction of SMA and IMA territories) — the "Griffith's point." The rectum is usually spared because of its collateral blood supply from middle/inferior rectal arteries (internal iliac).

Question 22

A 25-year-old man with no prior medical history presents with sudden severe abdominal pain, nausea, and rapid blood loss. Endoscopy reveals an actively bleeding posterior duodenal ulcer. Which artery is most commonly eroded by a posterior duodenal ulcer, and why does it cause more severe hemorrhage than an anterior ulcer?

A. Superior mesenteric artery — large caliber vessel with high pressure flow

D. Splenic artery — posterior to the pancreas and adjacent to the duodenum

E. Right hepatic artery — runs along the posterior duodenal wall

✓ Correct: B — Gastroduodenal artery (GDA)

The gastroduodenal artery (a branch of the common hepatic artery from the celiac trunk) runs posterior to the first part of the duodenum. A posterior duodenal ulcer (most commonly H. pylori-related) can erode through the posterior duodenal wall into the GDA — a large, high-pressure artery — causing massive arterial hemorrhage. The GDA is also closely related to the head of the pancreas — severe pancreatitis can also erode it.

Question 23

A patient takes oral metoprolol (a β-blocker) for hypertension. The drug undergoes extensive hepatic first-pass metabolism, resulting in only 40% oral bioavailability. A lipid-soluble formulation of a different drug bypasses this limitation. Which absorbed nutrient type uses this same alternative pathway?

Question 24

Question 25

A 60-year-old man undergoes truncal vagotomy for peptic ulcer disease (now largely historical, but still tested). Post-operatively, his gastric acid output is dramatically reduced. Which phase of gastric acid secretion is most directly abolished by vagotomy?

A. Intestinal phase — blocked because vagus innervates the duodenum

B. Cephalic phase — sight/smell/thought of food no longer signals via vagus to the stomach; gastric phase is also reduced (vagus stimulates G cells to release gastrin)

C. Only the gastric phase — vagal innervation only reaches the stomach

D. Cephalic phase only — the gastric phase is entirely mediated by gastrin without vagal input

E. Basal acid output only — stimulated acid secretion is preserved after vagotomy

✓ Correct: B — Cephalic phase abolished + gastric phase significantly reduced

Vagotomy cuts vagal efferent fibers to the stomach. Effects: (1) Cephalic phase is completely abolished — the entire cephalic response depends on vagal efferents to parietal cells and G cells. (2) Gastric phase is significantly reduced — vagal stimulation amplifies gastrin release from G cells during the gastric phase; without this, gastrin release is reduced (~50% of gastric phase preserved via local mechanisms). Side effects of truncal vagotomy: gastroparesis (requires pyloroplasty to ensure gastric emptying), diarrhea (from disrupted intestinal motility regulation).

Question 26

During a "fight-or-flight" response, sympathetic nervous system activation occurs. Which of the following correctly describes the sympathetic effects on the GI tract and the physiologic rationale?

A. Sympathetic activation increases GI motility and secretion to maximize nutrient absorption during stress

B. Sympathetic activation decreases GI motility and secretion and causes splanchnic vasoconstriction, redirecting blood to vital organs (heart, brain, skeletal muscle)

C. Sympathetic activation selectively increases gastric acid secretion while decreasing intestinal motility

D. Sympathetic activation has no effect on the GI tract; only the ENS controls GI function during stress

E. Sympathetic activation increases gastric emptying to quickly absorb nutrients for "fight-or-flight"

✓ Correct: B — SNS decreases GI function + splanchnic vasoconstriction → blood redistribution

Sympathetic activation (T5–L2 → splanchnic nerves → celiac/SMA/IMA ganglia → NE release) produces coordinated suppression of GI function: ↓peristalsis, ↑sphincter tone, ↓secretion, and most importantly, splanchnic vasoconstriction. This diverts blood from the GI vasculature (which normally receives 25–30% of cardiac output) to skeletal muscle, heart, and brain for the "fight-or-flight" response. This is the physiologic basis of the classic advice "don't swim right after eating" — vigorous exercise activates the SNS, diverting blood away from the GI tract just when it needs blood for post-prandial digestion, causing cramping.

Question 27

A researcher performs experiments demonstrating that merely showing food to a hungry dog causes immediate increased gastric secretion. This is replicated in human studies where subjects secrete significant gastric acid simply by smelling food. Which neural pathway mediates this response?

A. Olfactory nerve (CN I) → hypothalamus → phrenic nerve → diaphragm → mechanical compression of stomach

B. Olfactory/visual cortex → hypothalamus → dorsal motor nucleus of vagus (CN X) → vagal efferents → parietal cells + G cells → ↑HCl + gastrin

C. Glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX) → medulla → sympathetic chain → splanchnic nerves → stomach

D. Trigeminal nerve (CN V) → pons → ENS → parietal cells

E. Olfactory receptors → ENS directly via blood-borne signals

✓ Correct: B — Cortex/hypothalamus → dorsal motor nucleus of vagus → vagal efferents → stomach

The cephalic phase involves: sensory stimuli (sight, smell, taste, thought of food) → activation of cerebral cortex and hypothalamus → activation of the dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus nerve (CN X) in the medulla → preganglionic vagal fibers travel to the stomach → synapse on ENS postganglionic neurons → ACh release → stimulates both parietal cells (HCl via muscarinic M3) and G cells (gastrin release). This accounts for ~20–30% of total gastric acid secretion in response to a meal. Pavlov demonstrated this with his conditioned reflex experiments, for which he received the Nobel Prize in 1904.

Question 28

A clinical pharmacologist is designing a drug to increase GI motility for patients with gastroparesis without causing CNS side effects. The drug would need to act peripherally on the ENS. Which combination of receptor actions would BEST achieve this goal?

A. Central 5-HT3 agonist + peripheral muscarinic antagonist

B. Peripheral 5-HT4 agonist (↑ACh release from ENS excitatory neurons) + peripheral dopamine D2 antagonist (removes dopaminergic inhibition of ENS)

C. Peripheral α2-adrenergic agonist (mimics sympathetic activation) + central vagal stimulator

D. Peripheral μ-opioid agonist (stimulates ENS prokinetic pathways) + 5-HT3 antagonist

E. Peripheral GABA agonist + peripheral VIP receptor agonist

✓ Correct: B — 5-HT4 agonist + peripheral D2 antagonist

This is the mechanism of metoclopramide (and the newer prucalopride, which is more selective for 5-HT4). 5-HT4 agonism → increases ACh release from ENS excitatory motor neurons → ↑peristalsis + ↑gastric emptying. Dopamine D2 antagonism → removes dopaminergic inhibition of ENS → further increases motility. Metoclopramide's CNS side effects (extrapyramidal symptoms, tardive dyskinesia) occur because it crosses the blood-brain barrier and blocks D2 receptors in the basal ganglia. Peripheral D2 antagonists (domperidone) don't cross the BBB — reduced CNS side effects. Prucalopride is a highly selective 5-HT4 agonist approved for chronic idiopathic constipation.
